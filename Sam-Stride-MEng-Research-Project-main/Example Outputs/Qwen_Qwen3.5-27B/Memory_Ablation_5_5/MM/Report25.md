#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative analysis of 11 chromatographic runs to assess column performance and product purity. The study employed a three-step workflow involving signal preprocessing, metric calculation, and purity correlation. Key findings reveal a systematic deviation in peak shape, where all runs exhibited Tailing Factors significantly below standard USP <621> limits, suggesting potential column fronting or baseline correction artifacts. Additionally, while nucleic acid contamination profiles (260/280 ratios) remained consistent, specific runs displayed clusters of consecutive purity outliers. These anomalies were visualized to identify localized zones of impurity co-elution, highlighting the necessity of refining statistical tolerance bands and investigating underlying hardware or methodological causes for the observed non-compliance.

#Introduction 

The primary objective of this data analysis was to evaluate the consistency and quality of chromatographic separations across 11 manufacturing runs. In bioprocessing, maintaining strict adherence to peak shape metrics, such as the Tailing Factor, and ensuring fraction purity are critical for product safety and efficacy. The analysis focused on extracting quantitative metrics from high-resolution UV signals at 280 nm (primary protein signal) and 260 nm (nucleic acid impurity signal). The workflow was designed to address common data challenges, including negative baseline noise and sparse metadata, by implementing Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction and run-scoped forward-filling for metadata imputation. Furthermore, the study aimed to distinguish between true process degradation and transient noise by establishing statistical tolerance bands derived from inter-run standard deviations, alongside regulatory standards defined by USP <621>. By correlating peak metrics with fraction boundaries, the analysis sought to identify specific instances of purity failure and consecutive outlier clusters that may indicate systemic issues.

#Methodology 

The data analysis followed a structured three-step plan. Step 1 involved signal preprocessing and peak deconvolution. Raw data was grouped by run number, and metadata was validated and imputed using forward-filling logic that reset at the start of each run to prevent cross-contamination of data. AsLS baseline correction was applied to both UV channels to mitigate negative noise. Gaussian deconvolution was utilized to resolve co-eluting peaks, retaining only those with a resolution (Rs) of 1.5 or greater. Step 2 focused on the calculation of chromatographic metrics, including Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM), peak height, area, and Tailing Factor. A statistical tolerance band (Mean ± 2SD) was computed globally for the Tailing Factor to flag deviations, in addition to standard USP <621> limits (0.8–1.8). Step 3 correlated these metrics with fraction purity. The 260/280 area ratio was calculated per fraction, and outliers were identified as deviations exceeding two standard deviations from the run mean. Sequences of three or more consecutive outliers were flagged to detect localized impurity clusters. Finally, a multi-panel visualization was generated to map these purity anomalies against the chromatographic signal and fraction boundaries.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the 11 chromatographic runs revealed significant deviations in peak shape metrics and specific instances of purity anomalies. As detailed in the metric calculations, a critical inconsistency was observed in the Tailing Factor values. All runs exhibited Tailing Factors ranging from 0.50 to 0.65, which fall well below the standard USP <621> acceptance range of 0.8–1.8. This systematic fronting (Tailing Factor < 1.0) suggests potential column degradation, void formation, or issues with the baseline correction parameters, as the calculated tolerance band (0.4635–0.6296) was derived from this non-compliant dataset. Notably, Run 1 displayed a physically impossible negative FWHM (-1.65), indicating a failure in peak boundary detection during the initial deconvolution step. Despite these shape anomalies, the 260/280 area ratios remained relatively consistent across runs (1.08–1.33), indicating a stable profile of nucleic acid contamination.

Figure 1 visualizes the results of the fraction purity correlation and consecutive outlier detection. The figure presents 11 subplots, each corresponding to a unique run, plotting the UV 280 nm signal against elution volume. Vertical dashed lines demarcate the precise fraction boundaries. Red shaded regions highlight clusters of three or more consecutive peaks identified as outliers based on the 260/280 ratio deviating more than two standard deviations from the run mean. The presence of these red regions in specific subplots indicates localized zones where impurity co-elution exceeded statistical tolerance, while the absence of shading in other runs suggests consistent purity. However, the visualization alone does not elucidate the magnitude of the deviation or the gradient conditions at the time of the outliers. The aggregation of 11 runs into Figure 1 effectively identifies the location of purity anomalies but requires cross-referencing with tabular data to determine if these represent systemic column issues or isolated sample events. The universal failure of the Tailing Factor metric against the calculated tolerance band further suggests that the current statistical model may be too restrictive or that the underlying peak shape is fundamentally non-compliant with expected chromatographic behavior.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis successfully identified critical quality issues within the 11 chromatographic runs. The most significant finding is the systematic deviation of Tailing Factors below regulatory limits, pointing towards potential column fronting or methodological artifacts in baseline correction. While the 260/280 ratios indicated consistent nucleic acid contamination levels, the detection of consecutive purity outliers in specific runs, as visualized in Figure 1, highlights areas of concern regarding fraction purity. The presence of a negative FWHM in Run 1 underscores the need for robust peak detection logic. Future work should focus on recalibrating the baseline correction parameters to address the fronting issue and investigating the root cause of the purity outliers to ensure compliance with USP <621> standards and product quality specifications.
