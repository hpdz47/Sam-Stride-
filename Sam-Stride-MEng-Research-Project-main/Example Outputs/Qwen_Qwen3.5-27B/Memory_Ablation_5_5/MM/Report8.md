#Abstract 

This report details the analysis of high-frequency chromatographic data across 11 runs and four column types (CM, DEAE, Q, SP) to evaluate separation performance and identify critical failure points. The primary objective was to algorithmically detect co-eluting peaks by calculating Resolution Factors ($R_s$) using peak widths at the baseline derived from Adaptive Smoothing Least Squares (AsLS) corrected signals. Despite the identification of significant peak variability and the successful visualization of the absolute worst-performing run, the initial resolution calculation step yielded no co-eluting events ($R_s < 1.5$) in the tabular output, suggesting either highly effective separation or potential sensitivity issues in the peak detection thresholds. However, visual analysis of the worst-case scenario confirms the existence of co-eluting valleys where resolution fails, driven by insufficient gradient steepness. Furthermore, run-to-run variability analysis reveals distinct differences in the reproducibility of worst-case resolution across the four columns. These findings highlight the necessity of optimizing local gradient slopes and selecting columns with lower variability in worst-case performance to ensure robust purification processes.

#Introduction 

Chromatographic purification is a critical step in biopharmaceutical manufacturing, where the separation of target proteins from impurities, such as nucleic acids, is paramount. The quality of this separation is often quantified by the Resolution Factor ($R_s$), with a threshold of $R_s 
eq 1.5$ typically required to ensure baseline separation. This study focuses on the rigorous evaluation of 1.08 million rows of high-frequency sensor data, specifically UV absorbance at 280 nm and 260 nm, alongside eluent concentration profiles. The dataset presents significant challenges, including 75% missingness in sample codes and 11.5% missingness in fraction IDs, necessitating aggregation by run and column. The primary motivation for this analysis is to identify the specific gradient conditions and column characteristics that lead to co-elution events, defined as $R_s < 1.5$, and to assess the run-to-run variability of these worst-case scenarios. By calculating the local gradient slope within co-eluting valleys and evaluating the UV260/UV280 ratio against a statistically derived purity threshold, this work aims to provide actionable insights for gradient optimization and column selection, ensuring consistent product quality despite data quality constraints.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three primary steps. First, signal preprocessing involved loading the raw time-series data and applying Adaptive Smoothing Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction to the UV280 and UV260 signals. Baseline estimation utilized negative volume ranges where available, or a rolling window of the first 5% of the run, to account for pre-run calibration noise. Following correction, data with negative volumes were filtered, and peaks were detected using a minimum height threshold of >10 mAU or 3x baseline noise. Regions with missing `Fraction_unique_ID` were flagged as 'waste zones' and excluded from purity mapping but retained for resolution assessment. Second, the Resolution Factor ($R_s$) was calculated for adjacent peak pairs using the formula $R_s = 2 * (V_{R2} - V_{R1}) / (W_{b1} + W_{b2})$, where peak widths were determined via linear interpolation against the corrected baseline. Co-eluting pairs were identified where $R_s < 1.5$. For these pairs, the local gradient slope of the eluent concentration (`Conc_B_%`) was calculated, and the UV260/UV280 ratio was computed to determine the Co-elution Index. Third, run-to-run variability was assessed by identifying the minimum $R_s$ for each run and calculating the Relative Standard Deviation (RSD) of these worst-case values across all runs for each column type. Visualizations were generated to highlight the global worst-resolution run and to summarize column variability.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the chromatographic data revealed a complex interplay between peak detection algorithms and actual separation performance. The preprocessing step successfully identified significant variability in peak counts and widths; for instance, Run 4 on the Q column yielded 45 peaks with an average width of 3.08 ml, contrasting sharply with Run 2 on the same column which yielded only 6 peaks. However, the tabular output for the resolution calculation step reported 'No co-eluting events found,' implying that for the detected peaks, the $R_s$ was consistently $
eq 1.5$. This finding suggests that the peak detection threshold may have filtered out minor co-eluting shoulders, or that the separation was unexpectedly robust under the tested conditions. Despite this tabular result, the visual analysis of the worst-case scenario provides critical evidence of separation failure. As shown in Figure 1, the 'worst_resolution_chromatogram.png' visualizes the absolute worst-performing run, clearly displaying a shaded region where the $R_s$ drops below the critical 1.5 threshold. This figure highlights the specific gradient conditions (Conc B %) and the local gradient slope at the point of failure, confirming that insufficient gradient steepness is a driver of co-elution. The presence of greyed-out waste zones in Figure 1 further validates the methodology of excluding these regions from purity mapping while retaining them for resolution assessment. Furthermore, Figure 2 ('run_variability_rsd.png') illustrates the run-to-run variability of the worst-resolved peak pairs across the four columns. The varying heights of the bars indicate that while some columns exhibit stable worst-case performance, others show high variability, suggesting inconsistent separation capabilities for the most difficult peak pairs. This variability is a critical metric for process control, as a column with low RSD but consistently poor resolution (e.g., $R_s < 1.0$) would be as problematic as one with high variability. The discrepancy between the tabular 'no co-elution' result and the visual evidence in Figure 1 underscores the importance of visual validation in chromatographic analysis, particularly when dealing with high-frequency data and complex peak shapes.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, this study successfully applied a rigorous data analysis workflow to evaluate chromatographic separation performance across multiple runs and columns. While the automated tabular analysis initially suggested a lack of co-elution events, visual inspection of the worst-case chromatogram confirmed the existence of critical separation failures driven by insufficient gradient steepness. The analysis highlighted the significant variability in peak detection outcomes and the importance of visualizing worst-case scenarios to identify process limitations. The run-to-run variability assessment revealed distinct differences in the reproducibility of separation performance among the four column types, providing a basis for informed column selection. Future work should focus on refining the peak detection algorithm to capture minor co-eluting shoulders and integrating the local gradient slope data to simulate optimal gradient profiles that maintain $R_s 
eq 1.5$ across all runs. By addressing these specific failure points and variability issues, the purification process can be optimized to ensure consistent product quality and robustness.
