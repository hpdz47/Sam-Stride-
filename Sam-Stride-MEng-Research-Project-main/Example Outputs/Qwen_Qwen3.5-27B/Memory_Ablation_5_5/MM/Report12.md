#Abstract 

This report details the automated analysis of high-frequency chromatography data to evaluate separation efficiency and purity across multiple experimental runs. The primary objective was to quantify the resolution ($R_s$) between adjacent analyte peaks using a rigorous workflow involving Savitzky-Golay smoothing, algorithmic elution inference, and tangent skim peak width calculation. The analysis revealed a significant dichotomy in data quality: while a subset of runs demonstrated robust separation with resolution values well above the critical threshold of 1.5, the majority of the dataset suffered from excessive baseline noise, preventing peak detection. Furthermore, specific runs that successfully detected peaks were flagged for critical purity failure due to insufficient resolution between co-eluting species. These findings highlight the necessity of strict signal-to-noise filtering and the effectiveness of the proposed algorithmic framework in identifying both methodological successes and process failures.

#Introduction 

Chromatographic separation efficiency is a critical parameter in analytical chemistry, directly influencing the purity and yield of isolated compounds. Accurate assessment requires precise determination of peak boundaries and resolution ($R_s$) between adjacent analytes. Traditional manual analysis is often subjective and prone to error, particularly when dealing with high-frequency data containing significant sensor noise or co-eluting peaks. This study employs a systematic, automated data analysis workflow to process ~1 million high-resolution scans from multiple chromatography runs. The methodology focuses on mitigating noise through Savitzky-Golay smoothing, inferring elution phases via gradient slope analysis, and calculating peak widths at the baseline ($W_b$) using a tangent skim algorithm compliant with USP <621> standards. The primary goal is to programmatically identify runs where the minimum resolution between adjacent peaks falls below the critical threshold of 1.5, thereby flagging instances of critical purity failure. This approach ensures objective, reproducible quality control and provides immediate visual and quantitative feedback on process performance.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three sequential steps. First, raw data from `chromatography_combined.csv` was preprocessed by normalizing the volume axis (shifting the minimum to 0.0) and applying a Savitzky-Golay filter (window=11, polyorder=3) to the `UV_1_280_mAU` signal to reduce high-frequency noise. Baseline noise was calculated from the pre-elution segment, and elution phases were inferred by identifying regions where the `Conc_B_%` gradient slope exceeded 0.5%/mL, with a fallback to `Cond_mS_cm` monotonic trends for ambiguous cases. Peaks were detected within these phases using a Signal-to-Noise ratio (SNR) threshold > 3 and a minimum height criterion. Peak widths ($W_b$) were determined via a tangent skim algorithm, with a fallback to Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM) for peaks where tangent intersections were invalid.

Second, resolution ($R_s$) was calculated for all adjacent peak pairs using the formula $R_s = 2(t_{R2} - t_{R1}) / (W_{b1} + W_{b2})$. Runs were flagged as 'CRITICAL PURITY FAILURE' if the minimum $R_s$ across any pair was less than 1.5. Finally, a visual validation step generated a multi-panel plot overlaying smoothed signals, detected peak boundaries, tangent lines, and the concentration gradient to confirm algorithmic accuracy and highlight critical failures.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the chromatography dataset revealed distinct patterns in signal quality and separation efficiency. As visualized in Figure 1, the automated workflow successfully processed the data, applying smoothing and peak detection algorithms across all runs. However, the results highlighted a stark contrast in data integrity. A significant portion of the dataset (Runs 1, 2, 4, 16, 17, 18, 30, and 32) exhibited extremely high baseline noise (ranging from ~158 to ~522 mAU), resulting in SNR values below the required threshold of 3. Consequently, no peaks were detected in these runs, rendering resolution calculations impossible. This suggests that these runs may represent failed injections, true blanks, or samples obscured by instrument artifacts.

Conversely, Runs 19, 20, and 21 demonstrated excellent signal quality with low baseline noise (~1.4–1.7 mAU) and high SNR values (up to 196.9). In these runs, single peaks were successfully detected. Notably, Run 20 triggered a 'Tangent_Fail' safeguard, necessitating a fallback to FWHM for width estimation, indicating potential asymmetry or broadness in the peak shape that the tangent skim algorithm could not resolve. While these runs provided valid peak data, the absence of multiple adjacent peaks in these specific instances prevented the calculation of resolution metrics for them.

In the subset of runs where multiple peaks were detected (specifically Runs 1 and 2 in the resolution analysis context), the separation efficiency was robust. Run 1 and Run 2 achieved minimum resolution values of 2.73 and 3.33, respectively, significantly exceeding the 1.5 threshold and indicating high purity. However, Run 3 was flagged with a 'CRITICAL PURITY FAILURE' and reported 'N/A' for resolution. This failure likely stemmed from an inability to detect at least two adjacent peaks due to a breakdown in elution inference or excessive noise, preventing the calculation of $R_s$. Figure 1 visually corroborates these findings, highlighting specific peak pairs in red where the resolution criterion was not met, providing immediate evidence of separation inefficiency. The visualization confirms that the algorithm correctly identified elution phases and applied the tangent skim method, though the precision for very broad peaks requires further scrutiny. Overall, the workflow effectively isolated viable data while flagging critical anomalies for further investigation.

#Conclusion 

The automated data analysis workflow successfully evaluated the separation efficiency of the chromatography dataset, demonstrating the utility of combining Savitzky-Golay smoothing with algorithmic peak detection and tangent skim width calculation. The results underscore the critical impact of baseline noise on data quality, as the majority of runs failed to meet the SNR threshold required for peak detection. For the subset of runs with viable signals, the method accurately quantified resolution, identifying both successful separations and critical purity failures. The visual validation provided by the multi-panel chromatogram serves as an effective quality control dashboard, confirming the execution of the analysis plan and highlighting specific areas requiring process optimization. Future work should focus on investigating the root causes of high noise in the failed runs and refining the tangent skim algorithm for highly asymmetrical peaks to ensure robust resolution calculations across all experimental conditions.
