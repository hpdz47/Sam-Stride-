#Abstract 

This report details a comprehensive data analysis workflow designed to quantify Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) and assess baseline stability across four chromatography columns (CM, DEAE, Q, SP) and multiple runs. The primary objective was to distinguish genuine analyte peaks from instrumental noise and baseline drift by applying rigorous offset correction and rolling standard deviation calculations. The analysis successfully established noise thresholds (3σ and 5σ) for both UV channels (280 nm and 260 nm), revealing significant variability in baseline stability, particularly within the Q column. While the SNR analysis highlighted unit-specific performance degradation and run-to-run instability, the secondary investigation into conductivity correlations yielded statistically ambiguous results for two columns due to methodological anomalies. These findings underscore the necessity of column-stratified monitoring to detect early signs of unit degradation and the importance of refining statistical validation protocols for drift analysis.

#Introduction 

High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) data analysis requires precise differentiation between true analyte signals and background noise to ensure accurate quantification and limit of detection (LOD) determination. In complex datasets characterized by high missingness in sample metadata, stratifying analysis by hardware units (columns) becomes critical for identifying unit-specific degradation or variability. This study focuses on quantifying the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) and baseline stability for two UV detection channels: `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU`. The `UV_2_260_mAU` channel, typically more susceptible to nucleic acid variability, exhibited higher inherent noise, necessitating independent threshold calculations. The motivation for this analysis was to establish a robust methodology for peak detection using 3σ and 5σ thresholds while correcting for baseline offsets derived from pre-injection equilibration phases. Furthermore, the study aimed to explore the relationship between buffer conductivity and baseline drift to determine if buffer-induced artifacts were contributing to signal instability. By isolating constant gradient regions and excluding transition zones, this workflow seeks to provide a clear metric of system performance across different column units and operational runs.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a three-step plan utilizing the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset. Step 1 involved Baseline Stability & Noise Threshold Calculation. Data was filtered for pre-injection regions (`volume_ml` < 0) where buffer concentration (`Conc_B_%`) was within ±0.5% of 0.0% or 100.0%, excluding the first five scans to remove startup transients. Valid groups required at least 25 scans. The mean of these stable regions was calculated to determine the `Offset_Corrected_Mean`, which was subtracted from the signal. A rolling standard deviation (window=25) was applied to the offset-corrected signal to compute global noise thresholds at 3σ and 5σ levels for each column and channel combination.

Step 2 focused on Column-Stratified SNR & Peak Detection. Using the offsets from Step 1, valid baseline regions were redefined to include post-injection data (`volume_ml` > 0) while strictly excluding gradient transitions (where `Conc_B_%` change > 0.5%) and potential peak regions (signal > 3σ). Local noise was recalculated using a rolling standard deviation on these refined regions. SNR was calculated as (Peak_Height - Offset_Corrected_Mean) / Local_Noise_Std. Results were aggregated by `column` and `run_no`, with visualizations highlighting the top three runs exhibiting the highest variance.

Step 3 conducted Conductivity Correlation & Drift Analysis. Linear regression was performed on constant gradient regions to calculate drift rates (mAU/mL). Pearson correlation coefficients were then computed between these drift rates and buffer conductivity (`Cond_mS_cm`) to assess buffer-induced instability. Statistical significance was evaluated via p-values.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of baseline stability (Step 1) confirmed that all evaluated groups for runs 1, 2, and 4 across the four columns contained sufficient data for robust statistical calculation. However, significant baseline drift was observed; for instance, the Q column's 280 nm offset shifted from +1.32 mAU in Run 1 to -0.18 mAU in Run 2, validating the necessity of the offset correction step. Noise thresholds varied markedly, with the Q column exhibiting the highest noise levels (3σ up to 0.36 mAU at 260 nm) compared to the CM and SP columns (3σ ~0.04–0.05 mAU), suggesting potential flow instability or instrumental noise specific to the Q unit.

As illustrated in Figure 1, the SNR analysis reveals that performance is not uniform across columns or runs. The grouped bar plot demonstrates distinct SNR characteristics for the 260 nm and 280 nm channels, confirming that independent thresholding was required. The visualization explicitly labels the top three runs with the highest variance, indicating specific instances of instability or degradation within certain column units. The separation of UV channels in the figure highlights that the 260 nm channel generally exhibits different SNR profiles compared to the 280 nm channel, likely due to the higher inherent variability of nucleic acid detection.

The conductivity correlation analysis (Step 3) yielded mixed results. While drift rates were successfully quantified, the statistical validity of the correlation for the DEAE and Q columns was compromised. These columns displayed perfect correlation coefficients (r = ±1.0000) accompanied by p-values of 1.0000, a statistical contradiction suggesting a calculation error, such as zero variance in one of the variables. In contrast, the CM and SP columns showed non-significant correlations (p > 0.60) with low correlation magnitudes, indicating that baseline drift in these units is not linearly driven by conductivity changes. Notably, the SP column exhibited a massive positive drift at 260 nm (2860 mAU/mL), suggesting unit-specific detector issues unrelated to buffer conductivity. Consequently, while the workflow successfully isolated column-specific SNR metrics, the conductivity correlation data for half the dataset requires further methodological review before definitive conclusions can be drawn regarding buffer-induced drift.

#Conclusion 

This data analysis successfully established a robust framework for quantifying SNR and baseline stability in chromatography data, effectively stratifying results by column to identify unit-specific performance issues. The Q column was identified as a primary concern, exhibiting significantly higher noise levels and baseline drift compared to other units. The visualization of SNR by column and run (Figure 1) provided clear evidence of non-uniform performance, highlighting specific runs with high variance that warrant operational investigation. While the offset correction and noise thresholding methodology proved effective for peak detection, the conductivity correlation analysis revealed statistical anomalies for the DEAE and Q columns, preventing a definitive conclusion on the role of buffer conductivity in baseline drift for these units. Future work should focus on resolving the statistical calculation errors in the drift analysis and expanding the dataset to include missing runs to assess long-term degradation trends. The current findings confirm that column-stratified analysis is essential for maintaining data integrity in high-throughput chromatography workflows.
