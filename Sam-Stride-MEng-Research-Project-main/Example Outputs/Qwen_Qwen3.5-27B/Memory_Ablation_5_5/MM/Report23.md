#Abstract 

This report details a rigorous quality control assessment of high-frequency High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) time-series data. The primary objective was to distinguish genuine chromatographic anomalies from systematic artifacts, specifically addressing negative UV baselines and a global volume offset error. The analysis validated the application of a single global offset correction (~33.8 ml) across all 11 runs, ensuring accurate volume calibration. Furthermore, the study assessed injection consistency and peak symmetry, revealing significant injection variability in 10 out of 11 runs. While peak symmetry metrics showed negligible correlation with the mobile phase gradient, the reliability of these metrics is currently limited by high injection variance and sampling uniformity issues. These findings underscore the necessity of resolving data quality artifacts before proceeding with complex process state inference or peak imputation.

#Introduction 

The integrity of chromatographic data is frequently compromised by systematic artifacts, including baseline drift, volume logging errors, and mechanical surges. In this dataset, specific structural issues were identified: negative volume values indicating a systematic offset, negative UV baselines requiring non-linear correction, and a high prevalence of missing metadata regarding process stages and injection volumes. The motivation for this analysis was to establish a robust data cleaning pipeline that differentiates between genuine chemical signals and instrumental noise. Specifically, the workflow aimed to validate whether the volume offset was a global constant or run-specific, correct UV baselines using Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS), and assess the consistency of sparse injection data. By isolating these artifacts, the analysis seeks to provide a reliable foundation for downstream tasks such as change-point detection for stage inference and the identification of missing peaks versus intentional wash steps.

#Methodology 

The data analysis followed a three-step plan. Step 1 involved Baseline Correction and Volume Offset Calibration. Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) with hyperparameters lambda=10^5 and p=0.005 were applied to correct negative UV baselines. To address the volume offset, the minimum `volume_ml` for each of the 11 runs was analyzed to determine if the offset was global or run-specific. Step 2 focused on Temporal Correlation and Process State Inference, utilizing change-point detection on `Conc_B_%` profiles to infer missing `chromatography_stage` metadata and correlating `DeltaC_pressure_MPa` with UV spikes to identify mechanical artifacts. Step 3 assessed Injection Consistency and Peak Symmetry. The consistency of `Injection_ml` was evaluated using the Coefficient of Variation (CV), flagging runs with CV > 0.2. Peak Width at Half-Height (W½) and asymmetry factors were calculated in volume units to mitigate sampling frequency variations. A sampling uniformity check was performed, flagging peaks where volume differences varied by >5% as 'Low Confidence'. Correlations between peak metrics and gradient concentration were computed to detect gradient-dependent distortion.

#Results and Discussion 

The validation of the volume offset strategy, visualized in Figure 1, demonstrated that the minimum volume values for all 11 runs clustered tightly around a single negative value of approximately -33.8 ml. This uniformity confirms that the offset is a global constant rather than a run-specific parameter, justifying the application of a single correction factor to the entire dataset. This step was critical for ensuring that subsequent volume-based calculations, such as peak width, were physically meaningful.

In the assessment of injection consistency and peak symmetry, significant variability was observed. The analysis flagged 10 out of 11 runs for manual review due to a Coefficient of Variation (CV) in injection volume exceeding 0.2. This high variance contradicts the assumption that mean values could be safely used for imputation without further investigation. Regarding peak morphology, Figure 2 presents the distribution of Peak Width at Half-Height (W½) and the relationship between asymmetry and the mobile phase gradient (`Conc_B_%`). The correlation analysis revealed negligible relationships between peak width/asymmetry and gradient concentration, with Pearson coefficients of -0.028 and 0.037, respectively. This suggests that peak distortion is not driven by the gradient profile. However, the near-zero W½ values observed for most runs and the high count of 'Low Confidence' peaks indicate potential issues with peak detection thresholds or sampling uniformity rather than genuine chromatographic behavior. Consequently, while the volume calibration was successful, the reliability of the symmetry metrics remains compromised by the unresolved injection inconsistencies and sampling artifacts.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis successfully validated the methodology for correcting systematic volume offsets, confirming a global constant error of ~33.8 ml across all runs. This correction is essential for accurate volume-based metric calculations. However, the assessment of injection consistency revealed significant variability in 91% of the runs, casting doubt on the reliability of current peak symmetry metrics. While the gradient profile does not appear to influence peak distortion, the high frequency of 'Low Confidence' flags and anomalous peak widths suggests that underlying data quality issues, such as sampling uniformity and injection variance, must be resolved. Future work should prioritize investigating the root causes of injection variability and refining peak detection thresholds before proceeding with advanced process state inference or data imputation.
