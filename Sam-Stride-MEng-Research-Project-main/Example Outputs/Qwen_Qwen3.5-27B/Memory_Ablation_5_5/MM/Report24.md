#Abstract 

This report details a rigorous quality assessment workflow applied to High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) time-series data to determine fitness for downstream modeling. The analysis focused on three critical stages: data sanitization and metadata reconstruction, signal integrity validation against USP <621> standards, and dual-path outlier classification. Key findings indicate that while the signal processing pipeline successfully differentiated high-integrity chromatograms from those with low signal-to-noise ratios (SNR), methodological deviations occurred during baseline correction due to insufficient pre-run data points. Specifically, the intended linear trend fit for dynamic baseline correction was replaced by a zero-baseline fallback in specific runs, potentially introducing bias. Furthermore, the dual-path outlier logic successfully processed the dataset, confirming sufficient valid runs per column for consistency assessment, though granular counts of artifacts versus process anomalies remain unquantified in the summary outputs. The study confirms the efficacy of adaptive Savitzky-Golay smoothing and derivative-based peak detection in filtering poor-quality data, despite challenges in metadata reconstruction and redundant column filtering.

#Introduction 

The reliability of downstream predictive modeling in chromatography is contingent upon the integrity of the input time-series data. This analysis addresses significant data quality challenges inherent in the provided dataset, including severe ingestion errors characterized by erroneous volume proxy columns (e.g., `Conc_B_ml`, `pH_ml`) and a substantial absence of event-based metadata (`chromatography_stage`), with approximately 76% of values missing. The primary objective was to establish a robust pipeline to sanitize data, reconstruct process phases, and validate sensor signals against regulatory standards. Specifically, the workflow aimed to apply dynamic baseline correction using linear trend fits on negative volume data to account for sensor warm-up drift, a critical step for accurate Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) calculation. Additionally, the study sought to distinguish between statistical artifacts and genuine process anomalies using a dual-path classification logic that correlates UV spikes with conductivity and pressure changes. By enforcing USP <621> compliance criteria—specifically Tailing Factors between 0.8 and 1.8 and Resolution values ≥ 1.5—the analysis intended to filter out runs compromised by noise or poor peak symmetry, ensuring that only high-fidelity data proceeds to consistency modeling.

#Methodology 

The data analysis followed a three-step plan. Step 1 involved data sanitization, where columns ending in `_ml` (excluding `volume_ml` and `Conc_B_ml`) were targeted for removal to eliminate duplicate volume proxies. Metadata reconstruction was performed by forward-filling `chromatography_stage` within run groups, imputing missing initial rows based on `Conc_B_%` thresholds (<5% defined as 'Equilibration'), and validating transitions against step-changes (>5% within 1 mL). Dynamic baseline correction was applied to `UV_1_280_mAU` and `Cond_mS_cm` signals; the protocol dictated a linear trend fit on negative `volume_ml` rows if the count exceeded five, otherwise defaulting to a zero baseline. Step 2 focused on Signal Integrity Assessment. Valid runs underwent Savitzky-Golay smoothing (Order 3) with an adaptive window size based on estimated peak width. Runs were flagged as 'Low SNR' and excluded if the Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE) between raw and smoothed signals exceeded 1.0%. Derivative-based peak detection and Gaussian fitting were used to calculate Tailing Factor and Resolution. Step 3 executed a Dual-Path Outlier Classification, distinguishing statistical artifacts (>3 sigma) from process-correlated anomalies (Pearson correlation > 0.7 between UV spikes and conductivity/pressure changes). Finally, a per-column consistency assessment calculated the Coefficient of Variation (CV) for peak area and retention time for columns with at least two valid runs.

#Results and Discussion 

The execution of the analysis plan yielded mixed results regarding data integrity and methodological adherence. As visualized in Figure 1, the signal processing pipeline effectively applied derivative-based peak detection and Gaussian fitting, clearly defining peak shapes and quantifying parameters. The figure confirms that the adaptive smoothing logic successfully identified runs where noise reduction compromised signal fidelity, correctly flagging them as 'Low SNR' (MAPE > 1.0%) for exclusion. This demonstrates the robustness of the filtering mechanism for poor-quality chromatograms. However, the visual evidence does not explicitly confirm the success of the `Cond_mS_cm` drift analysis or the `Conc_B_%` gradient correlation, which were integral parts of Step 2 but likely reported in separate textual outputs. 

Regarding Step 1, the markdown reports indicate a critical deviation: the sanitization logic failed to drop the redundant `_ml` suffixed columns (e.g., `pH_ml`), contradicting the plan's explicit instructions. While metadata reconstruction successfully identified and flagged 3 rows as 'Unverified' due to mismatches between forward-filled values and `Conc_B_%` step-changes, the baseline correction logic encountered limitations. Due to an insufficient count of negative `volume_ml` rows (NegCount=2, below the threshold of 5), the system relied on a 'Fallback' mechanism, setting baselines to 0.0000 rather than applying the planned linear trend fit. This deviation may introduce bias in SNR calculations if pre-run sensor drift was non-zero. 

In Step 3, the dual-path outlier classification was successfully implemented across 6 entries and 2 columns. The absence of excluded groups due to insufficient data suggests that upstream filtering effectively removed runs with zero peaks or invalid tailing factors, leaving a robust subset for consistency analysis. However, the summary output lacks granular data regarding the specific counts of artifacts versus anomalies and the actual CV values, preventing a definitive assessment of the magnitude of process variability. Despite these limitations, the data quality appears sufficient for the visualized UV analysis, with clear peak separation and baseline stability in non-flagged panels, indicating that the dynamic baseline correction was effective for the majority of runs where the linear fit could be applied.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis workflow successfully established a framework for filtering low-quality HPLC data and validating signal integrity against USP <621> standards. The visualization in Figure 1 confirms that the adaptive smoothing and peak detection algorithms effectively distinguish between high-integrity and low-SNR runs. However, the study highlights significant methodological deviations that warrant attention in future iterations. The failure to remove redundant volume proxy columns and the reliance on zero-baseline fallbacks due to insufficient pre-run data points represent compromises that could affect the precision of downstream modeling. While the dual-path outlier logic confirmed the dataset's suitability for per-column consistency assessment, the lack of granular reporting on anomaly types and CV metrics limits the depth of the variability analysis. Future work should focus on refining the baseline correction logic to handle sparse pre-run data and ensuring strict adherence to column sanitization protocols to guarantee the highest fidelity of input data for predictive modeling.
