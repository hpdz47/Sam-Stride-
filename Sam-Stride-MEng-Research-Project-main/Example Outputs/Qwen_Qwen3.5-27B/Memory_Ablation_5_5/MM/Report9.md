#Abstract 

This report details the comparative analysis of 11 high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) runs, focusing on the identification, integration, and normalization of chromatographic peaks. The primary objective was to quantify the relative proportions of compounds per run by calculating the ratio of individual peak areas to the total valid peak area. The methodology involved rigorous data preprocessing, including Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction and the inference of elution stages from gradient data due to significant metadata missingness. Results indicate successful normalization across all runs, with relative proportions summing to unity. However, significant heterogeneity was observed in peak detection outcomes, with total valid areas ranging from approximately 28,000 to 113,000 mAU·ml and peak counts varying from 459 to over 11,000. These findings highlight both the robustness of the normalization pipeline and the variability in chromatographic profiles across the dataset.

#Introduction 

The analysis of high-frequency HPLC time-series signals is critical for understanding the composition and consistency of chromatographic separations. In this study, the focus was on processing UV absorbance data at 280 nm (`UV_1_280_mAU`) to identify and quantify compounds across 11 distinct experimental runs. A significant challenge in this dataset was the absence of critical metadata, specifically the `chromatography_stage`, which was missing for approximately 75% of the data rows. This necessitated the development of an inference strategy based on the gradient of the mobile phase concentration (`Conc_B_%`) to identify elution windows. Furthermore, the presence of pre-injection artifacts, represented by negative `volume_ml` values, required robust baseline correction to ensure accurate peak integration. The ultimate goal was to generate a normalized, run-level comparison of compound distribution, expressed as the relative proportion of each peak's area to the total valid area, thereby facilitating the assessment of run-to-run reproducibility and compound dominance.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three sequential steps. First, data preprocessing and baseline correction were performed using Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) with parameters lambda=1e6 and p=0.001, utilizing the pre-injection region (`volume_ml` between -5.0 and 0.0) to estimate the baseline. Rows corresponding to the solvent front (`volume_ml` < 1.0) were subsequently excluded. To address missing process stage metadata, the `chromatography_stage` was inferred by calculating the rolling slope of `Conc_B_%`, normalized by system flow, and comparing it against local noise thresholds; runs meeting these criteria were labeled as 'Elution'. Second, peak detection was executed on the baseline-corrected signals within the inferred elution windows. Peaks were identified using a height threshold of three times the baseline noise standard deviation and integrated to calculate areas at 280 nm and 260 nm. Purity ratios were derived from these areas, and peaks were assigned to fractions based on signal contribution. Third, a run-level comparative analysis was conducted. The total valid area for each run was calculated by summing the `Area_280` of all detected peaks. Individual peak areas were then normalized by this total to determine relative proportions. For visualization, runs with more than 10 peaks were categorized into 'Major', 'Minor', and 'Trace' groups to generate a comparative stacked bar chart.

#Results and Discussion 

The comparative analysis of the 11 chromatographic runs revealed distinct patterns in compound distribution and detection robustness. As illustrated in Figure 1, the stacked bar chart successfully visualizes the relative proportions of peaks, with each bar summing to 1.0, confirming the mathematical integrity of the normalization step. The visualization highlights variability in peak dominance; some runs are characterized by a single dominant 'Major' component, while others exhibit a more heterogeneous distribution of 'Minor' and 'Trace' components. Quantitative analysis of the summary data further elucidates this heterogeneity. While the sum of relative proportions was consistently 1.0 across all runs, the absolute metrics varied significantly. Run 21 exhibited the highest total valid area (113,076.79) with a relatively low peak count (1,498), suggesting the presence of fewer but significantly larger or broader peaks. In stark contrast, Run 19 was a distinct outlier with a drastically reduced total valid area (28,354.23) and an extremely low peak count (459). Other runs, such as Run 1 and Run 2, displayed high peak counts (ranging from 8,850 to 10,878) with moderate total areas. This wide variance in peak counts raises questions regarding the uniformity of the peak detection thresholds across different signal-to-noise environments. Additionally, the preprocessing log indicated that the elution stage was successfully inferred for only two runs (16 and 17), while the remaining nine runs showed no inferred elution stage despite having valid peak data. This discrepancy suggests that while the gradient-based inference may have been too strict for some runs, the peak detection algorithm successfully identified features in the raw signal, or that the elution phases in those runs did not meet the specific slope criteria defined in the plan. The successful normalization despite these methodological variances provides a robust high-level summary of run-to-run reproducibility, though the extreme differences in peak counts warrant further investigation into run-specific tuning of detection parameters.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis workflow successfully processed 11 HPLC runs, overcoming significant challenges related to missing metadata and baseline artifacts. The application of AsLS baseline correction and gradient-based elution inference allowed for the extraction of meaningful peak data, which was subsequently normalized to provide a clear comparison of relative compound proportions. The results demonstrate that while the normalization methodology is robust, the dataset exhibits substantial heterogeneity in peak abundance and detection counts. The identification of outliers, such as Run 19 and Run 21, underscores the importance of validating detection parameters across diverse chromatographic profiles. Future work should focus on refining the elution stage inference criteria to ensure consistency across all runs and investigating the root causes of the extreme variance in peak counts to determine if they reflect biological differences or methodological limitations.
