#Abstract 

This report details a rigorous quality control assessment of high-frequency High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) time-series data. The primary objective was to distinguish genuine chromatographic anomalies from systematic artifacts, specifically addressing negative UV baselines and physically impossible negative volume offsets. The analysis employed Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) for baseline correction and evaluated the nature of volume offsets to determine the appropriate calibration strategy. While initial visualizations suggested a global volume offset, detailed quantitative analysis revealed a run-specific variation necessitating individual corrections. Furthermore, the study assessed injection consistency and peak symmetry, identifying significant data integrity issues in specific experimental runs, including zero-width peak calculations and high variance in injection counts. These findings highlight critical propagation errors from baseline correction to peak detection, underscoring the need for refined thresholding and validation protocols in automated chromatographic analysis.

#Introduction 

The integrity of chromatographic data is paramount for accurate process monitoring and quality assurance. In this study, we address two critical data quality artifacts prevalent in the provided HPLC dataset: negative UV baseline values and systematic negative volume offsets. The presence of negative volume values (ranging from -5.01 to -33.82 ml) is physically impossible and suggests a deterministic data processing error, likely stemming from baseline subtraction or sensor calibration drift. Additionally, approximately 75% of the dataset lacks explicit `chromatography_stage` metadata, and 11.5% of rows are missing `Fraction_unique_ID`, complicating the identification of genuine peaks versus intentional wash steps. The motivation for this analysis is to validate detector integrity and experimental consistency by applying non-linear baseline correction and determining whether the volume offset is a global constant or requires run-specific calibration. By establishing a robust correction methodology, we aim to enable reliable downstream analysis of peak symmetry and injection consistency, which are essential for assessing column performance and gradient-dependent distortion.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three primary steps. First, baseline correction and volume offset calibration were performed. Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) optimization was applied to the `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` signals using hyperparameters `lambda`=10^5 and `p`=0.005 to correct negative baselines. Concurrently, the minimum `volume_ml` value for each `run_no` was calculated to determine if the negative offset was a global constant or varied by run. Second, temporal correlation and process state inference were conducted. A rolling median of `Conc_B_%` was computed to identify wash steps based on gradient slope stability, and change-point detection was applied to infer missing `chromatography_stage` metadata. Mechanical artifacts were isolated by correlating `DeltaC_pressure_MPa` surges with UV spikes within a fixed lag window. Third, injection consistency and peak symmetry were analyzed. The consistency of sparse `Injection_ml` values was checked per run. Peaks were identified in the baseline-corrected UV signal, and Peak Width at Half-Height (W½) and asymmetry factors were calculated in volume units (ml) to mitigate sampling frequency variations. A sampling uniformity check was implemented to flag peaks where the volume difference varied by more than 5% as 'Low Confidence'.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of volume offsets revealed a critical discrepancy between visual inspection and quantitative data. As shown in Figure 1, the distribution of minimum volume values across the 11 experimental runs appears tightly clustered around -33.8 ml, initially suggesting a global constant offset. However, the detailed markdown analysis contradicts this visual impression, revealing that the offset varies significantly by run (e.g., Run 18 at -25.02 ml versus Run 1 at -5.01 ml). This finding necessitates a 'Run-Specific' correction strategy rather than a single global shift to ensure physical validity of the volume data. The visual clustering in Figure 1 likely masks the underlying variability due to the scale of the plot or the dominance of specific runs in the dataset. 

Regarding peak symmetry and injection consistency, Figure 2 presents the distribution of Peak Width at Half-Height (W½) and the relationship between asymmetry and mobile phase concentration (`Conc_B_%`). The results indicate significant inconsistencies in data integrity. While Runs 1, 2, 4, 16, and 17 demonstrated consistent injection counts and low variance, Runs 18, 19, 20, 21, and 30 exhibited drastically reduced injection counts and extremely high variance. Notably, the average W½ was reported as zero for several runs (2, 4, 18, 19, 20, 21), which contradicts the presence of chromatographic peaks. This suggests a failure in the peak detection logic, potentially caused by the AsLS baseline correction not normalizing the signal sufficiently for the defined thresholds. Furthermore, Run 30 displayed a high count of 'Low Confidence' peaks (3,486) alongside near-zero average widths, indicating severe sampling uniformity issues or algorithmic failure in volume-based width calculations. The scatter plots in Figure 2, while intended to show gradient-dependent distortion, are compromised by these detection failures, rendering the symmetry analysis for the affected runs unreliable without further investigation into the peak detection thresholds and the efficacy of the baseline correction.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis successfully identified and characterized systematic artifacts within the HPLC dataset, specifically the run-specific nature of the negative volume offset and the propagation of baseline correction errors into peak detection. While the visual validation of volume offsets suggested a global constant, quantitative analysis confirmed the necessity of a run-specific correction strategy to restore physical validity. However, the downstream analysis of peak symmetry and injection consistency revealed significant data quality issues, with several runs exhibiting zero-width peaks and high variance, likely due to inadequate baseline normalization or thresholding errors. These findings underscore the critical interdependence of preprocessing steps; errors in baseline correction directly compromise the reliability of peak detection and symmetry metrics. Future work must focus on refining the AsLS hyperparameters and peak detection thresholds to ensure robustness across all experimental runs, particularly those with high variance or low injection counts. Only after resolving these foundational data integrity issues can the symmetry analysis and process state inference be considered reliable for quality control purposes.
