#Abstract 

This report details the automated analysis of high-frequency HPLC UV time-series data to identify and classify trace impurities in chromatography runs. The workflow addressed significant data quality challenges, including missing metadata and baseline drift, through rigorous sanitization and Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) correction. Statistical peak detection utilizing a 3-sigma local noise threshold identified 2,145 peaks across 11 runs, predominantly classified as protein impurities (1,897) and nucleic acid contaminants (144). While the detection and classification algorithms performed robustly, the subsequent fraction alignment step revealed a critical limitation: all identified critical impurities were currently mapped as 'Uncollected/Lost' in the generated reports. This suggests a potential disconnect in the fraction mapping logic or a successful exclusion of contaminants from collected fractions, necessitating further validation to ensure product safety compliance.

#Introduction 

The purification of biopharmaceutical products via chromatography requires the precise detection of trace impurities and degradation products to ensure product safety and regulatory compliance. In this context, high-frequency UV data at 280 nm and 260 nm serves as a primary indicator of protein and nucleic acid presence, respectively. However, raw chromatographic data often suffers from baseline drift, sensor artifacts, and missing metadata, which can obscure minor peaks indicative of contaminants. The primary objective of this analysis was to develop a robust pipeline capable of distinguishing these trace impurities from background noise and classifying them based on spectral properties. Specifically, the workflow aimed to correct baseline artifacts using AsLS, infer missing chromatography stages using heuristic rules based on buffer concentration, and detect peaks exceeding a statistically defined noise threshold. Furthermore, the analysis sought to classify contaminants as either protein impurities or nucleic acid contaminants based on the 280/260 UV ratio and map these findings to specific fraction collection windows to prioritize critical risk zones.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three sequential steps. First, data sanitization and baseline correction were performed on the raw dataset (`chromatography_combined.csv`). Pre-injection data (volume < 0) and artifact columns were removed. Missing `Sample_Code` metadata was imputed by grouping by run number, and the `chromatography_stage` was inferred using state-persistent heuristics based on `Conc_B_%` thresholds (Load < 5%, Elute > 20%, Wash otherwise). Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction was applied to both UV channels (lambda=1e6, p=0.001) to remove drift while preserving minor peaks. 

Second, statistical peak detection and contaminant classification were executed. Local noise standard deviation was estimated using a 50-point rolling window. Peaks were detected where the signal height exceeded three times the local noise standard deviation. The 280/260 UV ratio was calculated at the apex of each detected peak; peaks with a ratio > 1.8 were classified as 'Nucleic_Acid_Contaminant', while those with a ratio ≤ 1.8 were classified as 'Protein_Impurity'. A mandatory filter removed 'Wash' stage peaks with heights below five times the local noise. 

Third, fraction alignment and critical zone prioritization were attempted. Detected peaks were mapped to `Fraction_unique_ID` using volume overlap and a constrained nearest-neighbor logic, allowing for a maximum volume gap based on the average system flow rate. Peaks failing to map were marked as 'Uncollected/Lost'. Risk prioritization flagged peaks as 'Critical' if they were nucleic acid contaminants or located in the 'Elute' stage.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully processed 11 chromatography runs, identifying a total of 2,145 peaks. The majority of these were classified as protein impurities (1,897), with a smaller but significant population of nucleic acid contaminants (144). The distribution of these impurities varied by chromatography stage; protein impurities were heavily concentrated in the Flow-Through (FT) stage (958 peaks), indicating effective removal of bulk contaminants during the initial load. Conversely, nucleic acid contaminants were more prevalent in early elution stages (E4, E5, E6). 

Figure 1 visualizes the output of the peak detection algorithm for the run with the highest peak count. The plot demonstrates the efficacy of the AsLS baseline correction, showing a flat baseline near zero which effectively removed the negative offsets and drift observed in the raw data. The 3-sigma local noise threshold is clearly visible as a horizontal line situated well above the residual noise floor, ensuring that only statistically significant excursions were flagged as peaks. The color-coding in Figure 1 confirms the classification logic, with red markers denoting nucleic acid contaminants and blue markers indicating protein impurities. The visual separation of these peaks from the baseline suggests high data quality post-correction. 

However, a critical discrepancy was observed in the 'Critical Impurity Report' generated during the fraction alignment step. Despite the detection of nucleic acid contaminants and peaks in the elution stage, all 11 entries in the report were marked as 'Uncollected/Lost' with 'Standard' risk levels. This outcome contradicts the prioritization logic, which should flag nucleic acid contaminants as 'Critical'. This suggests a potential failure in the fraction alignment algorithm, where the volume mapping logic failed to associate these peaks with collected fractions, or it indicates that the impurities were indeed successfully excluded from the collection windows. Without successful mapping, the current output cannot definitively confirm whether these critical impurities pose a risk to the final product or were effectively discarded. Further validation of the fraction mapping parameters and the `Fraction_unique_ID` data integrity is required to resolve this ambiguity.

#Conclusion 

The implemented data analysis pipeline successfully demonstrated the capability to sanitize complex chromatographic data, correct baseline drift, and statistically detect trace impurities with high sensitivity. The classification of contaminants based on UV ratios provided a clear distinction between protein impurities and nucleic acid contaminants, with the latter showing a specific distribution in early elution stages. The visualization in Figure 1 confirms the robustness of the peak detection and baseline correction methodologies. However, the workflow's final objective of mapping these impurities to collected fractions to assess product risk was not fully realized in the current output. The 'Uncollected/Lost' status of all critical impurities highlights a critical gap in the fraction alignment logic or data availability. Future work must focus on refining the nearest-neighbor mapping algorithm and validating the fraction collection metadata to ensure that critical impurities are accurately tracked and their impact on product safety is definitively assessed.
