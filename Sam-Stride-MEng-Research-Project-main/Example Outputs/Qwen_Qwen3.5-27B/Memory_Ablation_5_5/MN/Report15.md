#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative analysis of chromatography data across 11 experimental runs to isolate, align, and classify biomolecular species. Utilizing Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction, the study successfully resolved negative sensor drift and identified distinct elution peaks. A gradient-based alignment strategy normalized elution positions across runs, revealing consistent compound elution profiles despite run-to-run variations. Empirical thresholding of UV 280/260 nm ratios was employed to distinguish protein, nucleic acid, and mixed species. While the methodology successfully identified dominant compounds and flagged unassigned peaks due to metadata gaps, the analysis revealed significant challenges in classification purity, with approximately 90% of peaks categorized as 'Mixed'. Furthermore, methodological inconsistencies were observed regarding negative volume handling and baseline over-correction in specific gradient regions, necessitating further refinement of peak detection parameters.

#Introduction 

The primary objective of this data analysis workflow was to characterize biomolecular elution profiles across 11 chromatography runs, focusing on the quantitative comparison of UV absorbance intensities at 280 nm and 260 nm. Given the high frequency of missing metadata (75.7% missing `Sample_Code` and 11.5% missing `Fraction_unique_ID`), the analysis was restricted to the `run_no` and `column` level, employing a robust flagging protocol for unassigned peaks rather than discarding data. The workflow addressed three critical challenges: correcting baseline drift caused by sensor noise, aligning peaks across runs using elution gradients to account for flow variations, and classifying compounds based on spectral signatures. By integrating Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) correction, gradient-based binning, and empirical ratio thresholding, this study aimed to rank dominant compounds and detect multivariate outliers to ensure data integrity.

#Methodology 

The analysis proceeded in three sequential steps. First, raw time-series data underwent ALS baseline correction to remove negative drift, followed by peak detection. Peaks were flagged as 'unassigned' if `Fraction_unique_ID` was missing at the apex or within the Full-Width-at-Half-Maximum (FWHM). Second, detected peaks were aligned across runs by mapping elution volumes to buffer concentration (`Conc_B_%`) bins of 2% width, allowing for the aggregation of integrated areas and ranking of compounds by abundance and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR). Third, empirical thresholds for the 280/260 nm ratio were derived using the 5th and 95th percentiles to classify peaks as Protein, Nucleic Acid, or Mixed. Multivariate outlier detection was subsequently performed using Isolation Forest on intensity, ratio, and concentration variables to identify anomalous data points.

#Results and Discussion 

The application of ALS baseline correction successfully flattened the signal, removing negative drift and enabling the clear identification of distinct elution peaks, as visualized in Figure 1. However, the initial peak detection in the pre-gradient region identified a high density of low-intensity peaks with a consistent 280/260 ratio of ~0.73, suggesting the detection of noise or nucleic acid-like artifacts rather than pure protein species. The gradient-based alignment, illustrated in Figure 2, confirmed reproducible elution profiles across the 11 runs, with dominant vertical bands indicating consistent compound elution at specific `Conc_B_%` levels. Quantitative analysis revealed that the most abundant compounds eluted at high salt concentrations (68–70% and 48–50% `Conc_B_%`), though these regions exhibited notably low SNR values (0.35) and, critically, negative average 280/260 ratios in specific bins (e.g., -12.28 at 52–54%), indicating potential baseline over-correction or integration of noise artifacts.

The classification of compounds based on empirical ratio thresholds (0.628 and 1.888) yielded a distribution where approximately 90% of the 937,974 detected peaks were categorized as 'Mixed', with only ~5% classified as Protein or Nucleic Acid. This overwhelming dominance of the 'Mixed' category, shown in Figure 3, suggests that the percentile-based thresholds were insufficient to cleanly separate distinct biomolecular species, or that the dataset contains a high prevalence of co-eluting impurities. Furthermore, multivariate outlier detection identified 46,879 anomalies, with a concerning clustering in Run 16, including a negative volume outlier (-4.86 mL). This indicates a failure in the preprocessing pipeline to exclude negative volumes from the final analysis, as these values were intended solely for baseline characterization. The presence of negative ratios and the high proportion of mixed classifications highlight the need for refined peak detection parameters and a stricter review of the volume filtering logic to distinguish true compound signals from baseline artifacts.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis workflow successfully demonstrated the feasibility of gradient-based cross-run alignment and the utility of ALS baseline correction in resolving sensor artifacts. The visualization of elution profiles confirmed high reproducibility across runs, and the flagging of unassigned peaks preserved data integrity in the face of missing metadata. However, the study identified significant limitations in the current classification and outlier detection protocols. The high prevalence of 'Mixed' classifications and the occurrence of physically impossible negative ratios suggest that the empirical thresholding logic and peak integration parameters require optimization. Additionally, the inclusion of negative volume outliers in the final dataset underscores a critical gap in the preprocessing pipeline. Future iterations of this analysis should focus on refining the peak detection sensitivity, implementing bimodal thresholding strategies, and enforcing strict volume filtering to ensure that only valid elution data contributes to compound classification and ranking.
