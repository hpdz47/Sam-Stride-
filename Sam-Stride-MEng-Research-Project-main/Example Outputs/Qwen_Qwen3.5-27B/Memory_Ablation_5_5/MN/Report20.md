#Abstract 

This report details the quality control and noise floor estimation for two chromatography runs, focusing on the segmentation of high-activity peaks against baseline regions. The analysis utilized a dynamic thresholding approach based on rolling standard deviations and fixed signal limits to distinguish analytical activity from background noise. While the algorithm successfully identified one peak per run with average heights of 5.0 and 4.5 mAU against a low noise floor (95th percentile: 0.1–0.2 mAU), a critical methodological limitation was identified regarding baseline continuity. Both runs were flagged for 'Fragmented Baselines,' likely due to high missingness (~76%) in the `chromatography_stage` variable and the subsequent reliance on a fallback baseline strategy. Although no contamination events exceeding the 5% threshold were detected, the instability in baseline definition necessitates a manual review of the segmentation logic to ensure the validity of peak boundaries and integrated areas.

#Introduction 

High-frequency chromatography time-series data requires robust algorithmic segmentation to distinguish inert baseline regions from significant analytical activity. The primary objective of this analysis was to optimize data storage and ensure quality control by isolating flat baseline sections from high-activity peaks. The workflow was designed to treat rows with missing `chromatography_stage` data as system baselines, unless gradient changes in `Conc_B_%` indicated active elution or high-salt wash steps. A key challenge in this dataset is the high rate of missing metadata (~76%), which complicates the identification of stable gradient periods required for accurate baseline correction. Furthermore, the presence of mislabeled variables and potential baseline offsets necessitated a rigorous validation and correction protocol. This report evaluates the performance of the analysis pipeline, specifically examining the efficacy of the dynamic threshold calculation, the integrity of baseline segmentation, and the detection of potential nucleic acid contamination via A280/A260 ratios.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a three-step plan. First, data validation and baseline correction were performed. Variables with `_ml` suffixes were screened and excluded if they contained volume-like statistics rather than signal intensity. Conductivity values were validated against a dynamic range to flag outliers. Baseline correction was calculated using a rolling median of `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` during stable `Conc_B_%` periods (change < 0.5%). In the absence of stable periods, a fallback strategy utilized the first 5% of rows per run, provided the signal variance was low. A dynamic activity threshold was established as `Corrected_Signal > (Rolling_Median + 3 * Rolling_StdDev)` or a fixed threshold of >50 mAU, with the rolling window size dynamically adjusted based on system flow rate.

Second, peak segmentation and contamination flagging were executed. Time-series data were segmented into 'High Activity' and 'Baseline' regions. Peak boundaries were expanded to include transition phases where `Fraction_unique_ID` was null, provided the signal remained above the dynamic threshold. Peaks were split only if the signal dipped below the threshold for a duration exceeding 0.5 minutes. Contamination was flagged where the A280/A260 ratio was < 1.5, and high-salt wash steps were reclassified based on `Conc_B_%` > 80%.

Third, run-level noise floor estimation and quality control summaries were generated. This involved verifying baseline continuity, estimating noise floors (median and 95th percentile) in baseline regions, and calculating peak statistics (AUC, height, width). Runs with fragmented baselines (>50% in segments <10 rows) were flagged for manual review.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the two chromatography runs yielded mixed results regarding data quality and algorithmic performance. Quantitatively, the noise floor estimation indicated a low background noise environment, with a mean of 0.15 mAU and a standard deviation of 0.05 mAU across the runs. The 95th percentile of the noise floor ranged between 0.1 and 0.2 mAU. Against this low background, the algorithm successfully identified one distinct peak per run, with average peak heights of 5.0 mAU and 4.5 mAU, respectively. The anomaly report confirmed that no contamination events exceeded the 5% threshold, and the 'High_Salt_Wash_%' was recorded as 0.0%, suggesting that high-salt wash steps were either absent or correctly excluded from the high-activity calculations.

However, a significant methodological concern emerged from the 'Baseline Integrity' assessment. Both Run 1 and Run 2 were flagged for 'Fragmented Baselines.' This indicates that the algorithm failed to identify continuous baseline regions, a failure likely attributed to the high missingness (~76%) in the `chromatography_stage` variable. Consequently, the system relied on the fallback 'first 5%' baseline strategy. While the noise floor statistics suggest low background noise, the fragmented baseline flag implies that the dynamic threshold calculation may have been unstable or that the baseline correction logic could not be robustly applied due to the lack of stable gradient periods. This fragmentation compromises the validity of the peak boundaries and the exclusion of non-peak regions. Although the peak detection appears numerically clean, the inability to define a continuous baseline necessitates a manual review of the segmentation logic for these specific runs to ensure that the reported peak heights and areas are accurate representations of the analytical activity.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis successfully identified analytical peaks and confirmed the absence of significant nucleic acid contamination in the two chromatography runs. The low noise floor statistics support the reliability of the signal detection in terms of background interference. However, the analysis is limited by the 'Fragmented Baselines' issue, which undermines the confidence in the precise segmentation of peak boundaries. This issue stems from the high volume of missing `chromatography_stage` data, forcing the algorithm to rely on a fallback baseline strategy that proved insufficient for defining continuous baseline regions. Future analyses should prioritize the imputation or acquisition of `chromatography_stage` data to enable more robust baseline correction. Until this is resolved, the peak statistics for these runs should be treated with caution and validated through manual inspection of the raw chromatograms.
