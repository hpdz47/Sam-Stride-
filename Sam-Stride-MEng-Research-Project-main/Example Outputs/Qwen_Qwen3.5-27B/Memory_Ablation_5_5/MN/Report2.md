#Abstract 

This study presents a comprehensive quantitative analysis of High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) data to assess system stability, gradient alignment, and product purity. The workflow processed raw UV signals (280 nm and 260 nm) from 11 chromatographic runs, employing Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction to mitigate negative signal drift and excluding non-physical pre-run intervals. Key findings indicate successful system stability with distinct pressure anomaly detection, though a significant stratification in purity profiles was observed based on column chemistry. While Cation Exchange (CM, SP) columns demonstrated low 260/280 ratios indicative of high protein purity, Anion Exchange (DEAE, Q) columns exhibited elevated ratios, flagging 479 high-risk fractions for potential nucleic acid contamination. These results underscore the necessity of column-specific purity monitoring and validate the robustness of the integrated area-based quantification method.

#Introduction 

The accurate quantification of biomolecules via HPLC relies heavily on the integrity of UV absorbance signals and the stability of the chromatographic system. In this analysis, the primary objective was to derive robust abundance and purity metrics from high-frequency signal data, specifically focusing on the 280 nm wavelength for protein abundance and the 260 nm wavelength for nucleic acid contamination assessment. A critical challenge in such datasets is the presence of baseline drift and non-physical data points, such as negative volume intervals representing pre-run phases. Furthermore, the dataset exhibited a significant metadata gap, with approximately 75% of rows lacking specific sample codes, necessitating an aggregation strategy based on run numbers and column types rather than individual samples. This report details the methodology used to preprocess these signals, integrate peak areas, and evaluate system health, ultimately aiming to identify systematic purity issues across different chromatographic modes.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three primary steps. First, signal preprocessing involved filtering the dataset to exclude all rows with negative `volume_ml` values, effectively removing pre-run intervals. Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction was applied to both `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` signals using parameters $\	ext{lambda} \approx 10^6\text{--}10^7$ and $p \approx 10^{-4}\text{--}10^{-5}$ to correct for a documented negative drift of approximately -7.95 mAU. Rows with missing `Fraction_unique_ID` were retained for system stability monitoring but excluded from fraction-specific integration. Second, peak integration was performed using the trapezoidal method on the corrected signals. Purity was assessed by calculating the ratio of integrated areas ($\text{Area}_{260} / \text{Area}_{280}$), with a threshold of 0.6 established to flag high-risk fractions. Abundance was normalized by both total run area and fraction volume. Third, system stability and gradient alignment were visualized by plotting corrected UV signals, gradient profiles (`Conc_B_%`), and system pressure (`DeltaC_pressure_MPa`). Pressure anomalies were identified as points exceeding the mean $\pm 3$ standard deviations, and gradient alignment was verified by checking peak apexes against elution windows.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of 11 chromatographic runs, comprising 285 unique samples, revealed distinct patterns in system performance and product purity. As illustrated in Figure 1, the application of AsLS baseline correction successfully flattened the pre- and post-peak regions of the UV signals, confirming the removal of negative drift. The gradient profiles displayed clear step changes, and the alignment of UV peaks with these gradient steps validated the elution windows. System pressure remained largely stable, with specific anomalies highlighted in Figure 1 where pressure exceeded the $\text{mean} \pm 3\sigma$ threshold, primarily occurring during waste phases or initial low-volume segments. 

Quantitative analysis of the 260/280 purity ratios yielded a global mean of 1.03, with a maximum outlier of 3.11. A critical finding was the identification of 479 high-risk fractions exceeding the 0.6 purity threshold. This issue was not uniform; it showed a strong correlation with column chemistry. Samples processed on Cation Exchange columns (CM and SP) exhibited low mean ratios (0.53–0.63), suggesting effective separation of proteins from nucleic acids. In contrast, Anion Exchange columns (DEAE and Q) demonstrated significantly higher mean ratios (1.1–1.6), with a substantial number of fractions flagged as high-risk. This suggests that anion exchange steps may be co-eluting nucleic acids or that the target protein exhibits a naturally higher absorbance ratio under these conditions. Additionally, Flow Through (FT) fractions across all column types consistently showed ratios between 1.2 and 1.3, consistent with unbound material containing nucleic acids. The visualization in Figure 1 confirms that while the system hardware operated within stable parameters, the chemical selectivity of the anion exchange columns requires further investigation to mitigate purity risks.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis workflow successfully processed complex HPLC data, overcoming challenges related to baseline drift and incomplete metadata through robust preprocessing and aggregation strategies. The results confirm the stability of the chromatographic system, as evidenced by the corrected UV baselines and controlled pressure profiles shown in Figure 1. However, the analysis highlights a significant purity concern specific to Anion Exchange chromatography, where 479 fractions were flagged for potential nucleic acid contamination. The stark contrast in 260/280 ratios between Cation and Anion Exchange columns indicates that purity profiles are highly dependent on the chromatographic mode employed. Future work should focus on optimizing gradient conditions for Anion Exchange steps to reduce nucleic acid co-elution and further investigate the source of the elevated purity ratios in these specific runs.
