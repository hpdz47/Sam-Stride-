#Abstract 

This report details the analysis of high-frequency High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) data from multiple manufacturing runs to assess product purity and process consistency. The workflow employed Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction, derivative-based peak detection, and Gaussian fitting to catalog chromatographic peaks. A statistical elution window for the target product was established using DBSCAN clustering on normalized retention volumes. Subsequent multi-channel correlation analysis between protein (280 nm) and nucleic acid (260 nm) absorbance signals revealed a strong linear relationship for the primary eluting species, confirming stable target profiles across runs. However, the analysis identified distinct categories of anomalies: systematic artifacts in specific runs and potential nucleic acid contaminants in others. These findings highlight the robustness of the target identification methodology while flagging specific runs requiring further investigation into data processing limits and impurity profiles.

#Introduction 

The consistent production of high-purity biopharmaceuticals relies on the rigorous monitoring of chromatographic profiles during manufacturing. Variations in elution profiles can indicate process deviations, equipment malfunctions, or the presence of impurities such as nucleic acids. This study focuses on analyzing HPLC sensor data characterized by variable system flow rates and significant baseline drift. The primary objective was to develop a robust data analysis pipeline capable of normalizing retention volumes, accurately detecting peaks despite noise, and distinguishing the target product from impurities. By correlating protein absorbance at 280 nm with nucleic acid absorbance at 260 nm, the analysis aims to quantify purity and identify co-eluting contaminants. Furthermore, the study seeks to categorize anomalies into 'critical' artifacts versus 'potential' impurities to refine quality control protocols and ensure the reliability of the manufacturing process across different runs.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three sequential steps. First, raw HPLC data was pre-processed using Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction with parameters lambda=10^5 and p=0.001 to remove negative drift while preserving sharp peak features. Derivative-based peak detection was applied to the corrected signals, followed by Gaussian curve fitting to calculate peak area, retention volume, and Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM). Peaks with a Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) below 3 were filtered out. Second, target identification was performed by normalizing retention volumes based on system flow rates and applying DBSCAN clustering to high-intensity peaks present in at least 80% of runs. This established a statistical elution window defined by the mean and standard deviation of the target cluster. Peaks were categorized as 'Target', 'Critical Anomaly', or 'Potential Impurity' based on their position relative to this window and the gradient profile. Finally, a multi-channel correlation analysis was conducted by aligning the 260 nm and 280 nm signals using a FWHM-dependent tolerance window. The ratio of integrated areas (AUC_260/AUC_280) was calculated for each peak to assess purity, and a scatter plot was generated to visualize the correlation across all manufacturing runs.

#Results and Discussion 

The target product was successfully identified across all analyzed runs, establishing a stable statistical elution window with a normalized volume mean of 198.14 and a standard deviation of 55.48. This consistency confirms the efficacy of the DBSCAN clustering approach in isolating the primary product peak despite flow rate variations. However, the anomaly detection phase revealed a bimodal distribution in the data. Runs 1, 2, 16, and 17 exhibited a high volume of 'Critical Anomalies' clustered at a normalized volume of -5.0. This uniformity suggests a systematic artifact, likely a baseline correction floor or a data processing limit, rather than genuine chromatographic impurities. In contrast, Runs 18, 19, 20, and 21 displayed anomalies with variable normalized volumes ranging from -0.3 to -33.7, indicating distinct, potentially real impurities or process deviations occurring outside the target window. Runs 4, 30, and 32 reported no anomalies, suggesting either highly clean runs or potential detection logic failures for those specific datasets. As illustrated in Figure 1, the multi-channel correlation analysis demonstrates a strong positive linear correlation between protein and nucleic acid signals for the majority of data points. This indicates that the primary eluting species contains both components or that peaks are co-eluting. The color-coding by run number confirms that the target product elution profile is stable across the 11 runs analyzed. However, Figure 1 also highlights points deviating from the main trend line, specifically those with high 260 nm signal relative to 280 nm. These deviations identify potential nucleic acid contaminants that do not co-elute perfectly with the main protein peak. While the plot effectively distinguishes between pure protein peaks and potential impurities, the lack of explicit retention volume labels on the visual itself necessitates cross-referencing with the peak catalog to pinpoint the specific chromatographic fractions containing these anomalies.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis successfully characterized the chromatographic behavior of the target product across multiple manufacturing runs, confirming a stable elution profile and robust peak detection methodology. The correlation analysis between protein and nucleic acid signals provided critical insights into product purity, identifying specific instances of potential nucleic acid contamination. However, the study also uncovered significant discrepancies in anomaly detection, distinguishing between systematic data artifacts in certain runs and genuine process deviations in others. These findings underscore the importance of refining anomaly categorization logic to better differentiate between processing limits and actual impurities. Future work should focus on investigating the root cause of the systematic artifacts observed in Runs 1, 2, 16, and 17, and further validating the detection of variable anomalies in Runs 18 through 21 to ensure comprehensive quality control.
