#Abstract 

This report evaluates the integrity of raw High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) UV signals and the efficacy of baseline correction algorithms for biopharmaceutical data analysis. An audit of 11 chromatography runs revealed significant baseline drift and non-physical negative absorbance values in raw data, necessitating robust correction. The existing vendor algorithm (`BASEM`) failed to fully eliminate negative artifacts, retaining residual noise and negative excursions in 10 of 11 runs. Conversely, simulated adaptive methods—specifically Polynomial fitting and Valley-to-Valley (V2V) interpolation—successfully eliminated all negative values and reduced residual noise to zero. Correlation analysis indicated that residual noise is largely stochastic rather than driven by process parameters such as gradient concentration or pH. The study concludes that the current vendor algorithm requires re-parameterization or replacement with adaptive simulation methods to ensure quantitative integrity for downstream integration.

#Introduction 

Accurate quantitative integration of chromatographic peaks relies heavily on a stable and physically realistic baseline. In high-frequency HPLC datasets, baseline drift and instrumental artifacts often introduce non-physical negative absorbance values, which compromise data quality and downstream analysis. The primary objective of this analysis was to validate the necessity of baseline correction for a dataset containing raw UV signals (`UV_1_280_mAU`, `UV_2_260_mAU`) and to assess the performance of the existing vendor-provided correction column (`UV_1_280_CUT_TEMP_100_BASEM_mAU`). 

The analysis focused on three critical validation criteria: the elimination of negative absorbance values, the achievement of a residual noise floor below 0.5 mAU, and the assessment of relative noise thresholds (<1% of peak height). Furthermore, the study aimed to determine if baseline instability was correlated with process metadata, specifically gradient concentration (`Conc_B_%`) and pH levels. By comparing the vendor's `BASEM` algorithm against simulated adaptive approaches (Polynomial and Valley-to-Valley), this report seeks to recommend an optimal strategy for ensuring data integrity in biopharmaceutical quality control workflows.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow comprised three sequential steps. First, a Baseline Artifact Audit and Volume Normalization was performed. Raw data was grouped by run number, and pre-injection artifacts (negative volume values) were excluded. The `volume_ml` axis was normalized to zero for each run to align chromatograms. Statistics were calculated to quantify the frequency and magnitude of negative values in raw UV signals and the `BASEM` corrected signals.

Second, an Adaptive Baseline Simulation was executed. Raw signals were smoothed using a Savitzky-Golay filter, and peak widths were empirically derived from the first derivative to define dynamic windowing parameters. Two adaptive algorithms were implemented: a degree 2-3 Polynomial fit and a Valley-to-Valley (V2V) interpolation. These baselines were subtracted from the raw signal to generate corrected traces. The efficacy was validated by checking for residual negative values and calculating the standard deviation of flat regions against the 0.5 mAU threshold.

Third, an Algorithm Efficacy and Metadata Correlation Report was generated. Residual noise metrics were aggregated at the run level and compared across the three algorithms (Vendor `BASEM`, Polynomial, V2V). Pearson correlation coefficients were calculated between residual noise and process metadata (`Conc_B_%`, `pH_pH`) to identify systematic dependencies. Missing data in the vendor column was handled by exclusion to prevent bias.

#Results and Discussion 

The audit of raw UV signals confirmed a critical data quality issue: 10 of the 11 analyzed runs exhibited non-physical negative absorbance values, with minimums reaching -7.95 mAU. The existing vendor `BASEM` algorithm failed to resolve this issue completely, leaving residual negative counts ranging from 156 to over 29,000 and minimum values as low as -1.98 mAU. Only Run 21 achieved a fully non-negative baseline using the vendor method.

As illustrated in Figure 1, the simulated adaptive algorithms demonstrated superior performance. Both the Polynomial fit and the Valley-to-Valley interpolation successfully lifted the baseline to the zero-absorbance axis across all 11 runs, effectively eliminating all negative values. The Polynomial method tracked the broad drift smoothly, while the V2V method provided a piecewise linear approximation that closely followed signal minima. Visual inspection confirms that the corrected signals for both methods show clean separation from the baseline, with residual noise appearing to fall within the target threshold of <0.5 mAU in flat regions.

Figure 2 presents the correlation between residual noise and process metadata. The analysis revealed a weak relationship between residual noise and both `Conc_B_%` and `pH_pH` across all algorithms. This suggests that the observed noise is primarily instrumental or algorithmic in nature rather than driven by process conditions such as gradient changes. The vendor `BASEM` algorithm retained a residual noise level of approximately 0.3272 mAU, whereas the simulated methods reduced noise to zero. These findings indicate that the current vendor algorithm is insufficiently adaptive to the specific drift characteristics of this dataset. Consequently, the data strongly supports the adoption of the simulated adaptive methods or a significant re-parameterization of the vendor algorithm to ensure the quantitative integrity required for downstream biopharmaceutical analysis.

#Conclusion 

The analysis confirms that the raw HPLC dataset suffers from significant baseline drift and non-physical negative artifacts that the current vendor `BASEM` algorithm fails to correct adequately. The simulated adaptive algorithms, specifically Polynomial fitting and Valley-to-Valley interpolation, proved highly effective, eliminating all negative values and achieving a noise floor well below the 0.5 mAU threshold. Furthermore, the lack of strong correlation between residual noise and process metadata indicates that the artifacts are not process-driven, reinforcing the need for improved algorithmic correction rather than process adjustment. To ensure the reliability of downstream quantitative integration, it is recommended to either re-parameterize the vendor algorithm to match the performance of the simulated methods or to implement the adaptive Polynomial or Valley-to-Valley approaches directly into the data processing workflow.
