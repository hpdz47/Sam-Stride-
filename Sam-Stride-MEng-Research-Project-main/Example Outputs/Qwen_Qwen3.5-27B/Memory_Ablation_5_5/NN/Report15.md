#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative analysis of chromatographic data across 11 runs to isolate, classify, and rank dominant compounds. The workflow addressed significant data quality challenges, including negative sensor artifacts and missing metadata, by employing Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction and gradient-based cross-run alignment. Key findings indicate that the vast majority of the dataset (~98%) consists of 'Mixed' species, suggesting extensive co-elution or narrow empirical thresholds for pure protein and nucleic acid classification. While baseline correction successfully resolved signal drift, the gradient alignment revealed a highly skewed distribution where dominant signals were concentrated at 0% Buffer B, raising concerns regarding the detection of retained compounds. Multivariate outlier detection identified specific anomalies in pre-run equilibration phases and distinct runs, highlighting the need for refined thresholding strategies to better distinguish pure species from background noise.

#Introduction 

The primary objective of this data analysis was to characterize the composition of chromatographic eluates by comparing UV absorbance intensities at 280 nm and 260 nm against cumulative volume. The dataset, comprising 11 runs across different columns, presented specific challenges: negative absorbance values indicating sensor drift, a 75.7% missingness rate in `Sample_Code` preventing sample-level attribution, and 11.5% of records lacking `Fraction_unique_ID`. Consequently, the analysis was restricted to the `run_no` and `column` level. The methodology focused on three core pillars: (1) signal processing via ALS baseline correction to isolate true peaks from noise; (2) gradient-based alignment to normalize elution conditions across runs using `Conc_B_%`; and (3) empirical classification of compounds based on 280/260 nm ratios to distinguish proteins from nucleic acids. This report synthesizes the results of these steps to evaluate the purity of eluted fractions and the consistency of compound elution profiles.

#Methodology 

The analysis proceeded in three sequential steps. First, raw time-series data was processed using Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) to correct baseline drift, utilizing negative volume regions for noise estimation. Peaks were detected using `scipy.signal.find_peaks` with height and prominence thresholds set relative to baseline standard deviation. Peaks in regions with missing `Fraction_unique_ID` were flagged as 'unassigned' rather than discarded. Second, detected peaks were mapped to elution strength (`Conc_B_%`) to perform cross-run alignment. Adaptive binning was applied to group peaks by gradient concentration, followed by integration of peak areas and calculation of Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) to rank compounds. Finally, empirical ratio thresholding was applied to the entire dataset. The 280/260 nm ratio was calculated for valid data points, and thresholds were derived from the 1st and 99th percentiles to classify data as 'Nucleic Acid', 'Protein', or 'Mixed'. Multivariate outlier detection was performed using an Isolation Forest on log-transformed intensity, ratio, and gradient concentration to identify anomalous data points.

#Results and Discussion 

The application of ALS baseline correction successfully resolved negative sensor artifacts, resulting in a stable baseline near zero and distinct positive peaks as visualized in Figure 1. This processing step was critical for accurate peak detection, particularly given the initial presence of negative absorbance values. The gradient profile (Figure 2) confirmed stable pump performance and reproducible gradient delivery, a prerequisite for the subsequent alignment. However, the gradient alignment heatmap (Figure 3) and associated tabular data revealed a critical finding: the vast majority of significant signal was concentrated at 0% Buffer B. While the heatmap shows distinct vertical bands indicating consistent elution windows, the tabular output suggests that the adaptive binning logic may have grouped the majority of peaks into the 0.00-0.00% range, with negligible signal detected at higher gradient concentrations. This implies that either the peak detection parameters were too stringent for retained compounds or that the dataset is dominated by unretained species and void volume artifacts.

The classification of compounds based on the 280/260 ratio (Figure 4) highlighted a significant heterogeneity in the dataset. Approximately 98% of the data points were classified as 'Mixed', falling between the empirically derived 1st and 99th percentile thresholds. This high prevalence of mixed species suggests that pure protein or nucleic acid fractions are rare in this dataset, or that the percentile-based thresholds are too narrow to effectively isolate pure species. The scatter plot clearly distinguishes 'Protein' species at high ratios and 'Nucleic Acid' species at low ratios, consistent with biochemical expectations. Multivariate outlier detection identified specific anomalies, including negative volume points in Run 19, which correspond to pre-run equilibration artifacts flagged by the Isolation Forest due to their deviation from the main elution cluster. The presence of 'unassigned' peaks, particularly in the Q column, warrants further investigation to determine if they represent genuine low-abundance species or artifacts of the fraction collection logic.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis successfully implemented a robust workflow for baseline correction, peak detection, and compound classification. The ALS method effectively mitigated sensor drift, and the gradient alignment confirmed reproducible experimental conditions. However, the results indicate that the current dataset is dominated by 'Mixed' species and signals concentrated at the start of the gradient (0% Buffer B). The high proportion of mixed classifications and the lack of significant peaks at higher gradient concentrations suggest that the empirical thresholds may require refinement or that the chromatographic conditions resulted in extensive co-elution. Future work should focus on optimizing peak detection parameters to capture retained compounds and refining the ratio thresholds to better distinguish pure species. Additionally, the 'unassigned' peaks and multivariate outliers identified in specific runs require targeted investigation to rule out systematic artifacts.
