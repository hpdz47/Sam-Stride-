#Abstract 

This report details a rigorous quality assessment of High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) time-series data to determine its fitness for downstream modeling. The analysis addressed critical data ingestion errors, including redundant volume columns and missing process metadata, while validating sensor integrity against USP <621> standards. Key findings reveal a significant data quality gap where 62% of the dataset failed metadata reconstruction due to flat gradient profiles, limiting process phase classification. Furthermore, a dual-path outlier classification identified a cluster of 18 high-severity artifacts exclusively in Run R32 on the SP column, indicative of localized sensor noise rather than process anomalies. Consistency metrics highlight the SP column as the least robust, exhibiting the highest variability in peak area and retention time. These results underscore the necessity of dynamic baseline correction and artifact filtering before proceeding with predictive modeling.

#Introduction 

The reliability of downstream modeling in chromatographic processes is contingent upon the integrity of the input data. This study focuses on a dataset characterized by severe ingestion errors, such as erroneous duplicate columns (e.g., `pH_ml` with 66% missing values) and a lack of event-based metadata (`chromatography_stage`), which was missing in approximately 76% of records. The primary objective was to sanitize the data, reconstruct missing process phases, and validate sensor signals against regulatory standards. Specifically, the analysis aimed to distinguish between genuine process anomalies and sensor artifacts using a dual-path detection logic that correlates UV absorbance with conductivity and pressure data. Additionally, the study sought to assess run-to-run consistency per column to identify potential degradation or fouling. By applying dynamic baseline correction and Savitzky-Golay smoothing, the workflow aimed to ensure that UV and conductivity signals met the required Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) thresholds and USP <621> peak shape criteria.

#Methodology 

The data analysis followed a three-step pipeline. First, data sanitization involved removing redundant columns, specifically targeting `pH_ml` and other `_ml` suffixed duplicates, while retaining valid volume and concentration data. Missing `chromatography_stage` metadata was reconstructed using a forward-filling logic validated against `Conc_B_%` step-changes (>5% within a 1 mL window). A fallback mechanism marked rows as 'failed' if the gradient was flat or noisy. Dynamic baseline correction was applied to `UV_1_280_mAU` and `Cond_mS_cm` by fitting a linear trend to negative `volume_ml` regions to account for sensor drift. Second, signal integrity was assessed by isolating peaks within reconstructed stage boundaries, applying Savitzky-Golay smoothing (Order 3), and calculating USP <621> metrics such as Tailing Factor and Resolution. Third, a dual-path outlier classification was performed. This involved statistical limits (>3 sigma) for artifact detection and a process-correlation logic where UV spikes were correlated with `Cond_mS_cm` and `DeltaC_pressure_MPa`. Outliers were classified as 'Anomalies' only if the correlation exceeded 0.8 and the signal-to-noise ratio was >3.0; otherwise, they were treated as noise. Finally, per-column consistency was assessed by calculating the Coefficient of Variation (CV) for peak area and retention time across runs.

#Results and Discussion 

The data sanitization phase successfully removed 11 redundant columns, resolving the ingestion error associated with the `pH_ml` duplicate. However, the metadata reconstruction revealed a critical limitation: while 1,350 rows were successfully assigned a `chromatography_stage`, 671,577 rows (approximately 62% of the total dataset) failed to be classified. This high failure rate indicates that the forward-filling logic could not detect necessary step-changes in `Conc_B_%` due to extensive regions of flat or noisy gradient data, leaving a significant portion of the dataset without process context. Baseline correction parameters confirmed significant sensor drift, with UV slopes varying from -0.2271 to 0.0810 across different run/column combinations, validating the necessity of the dynamic linear trend correction.

The outlier classification identified a critical data quality issue: a cluster of 18 high-severity outliers (severity > 1671) exclusively associated with Run R32 on the SP column. These outliers were classified as 'Artifacts' with a correlation of 0, indicating they are uncorrelated sensor noise or ingestion errors rather than genuine process anomalies linked to conductivity or pressure changes. The volume values for these artifacts (approx. 25.7–35.2 mL) suggest a localized failure or spike during a specific phase of the run. Despite the dual-path logic correctly isolating these as artifacts, the magnitude of the severity scores implies a significant deviation from the baseline that warrants immediate investigation of the raw data for Run R32.

Regarding consistency, the Coefficient of Variation (CV) metrics revealed that the SP column exhibits the highest variability in both peak area (CV_Area = 147.69) and retention time (CV_RT = 25.95) compared to the CM, DEAE, and Q columns. This elevated variability, combined with the severe artifact cluster in Run R32, suggests potential column degradation, fouling, or a specific operational failure unique to that run/column combination. The SP column is currently the least robust in this dataset, potentially compromising downstream modeling if not addressed. #Conclusion 

The analysis confirms that while the data sanitization and baseline correction methodologies are valid, the dataset suffers from significant metadata gaps and specific column-level instabilities. The failure to reconstruct `chromatography_stage` for 62% of the data limits the efficacy of downstream peak isolation and compliance checks. The identification of 18 high-severity artifacts in Run R32 on the SP column highlights a localized sensor or operational failure that must be resolved. Furthermore, the SP column's high variability in peak area and retention time suggests it is the least reliable component in the current workflow. Future work should focus on refining the metadata reconstruction logic to handle flat gradients and conducting a physical inspection of the SP column to rule out degradation. Addressing these issues is essential before the data can be deemed fit for regulatory-grade modeling.
