#Abstract 

This report details a comprehensive data analysis workflow designed to assess chromatographic column performance and product purity across 11 manufacturing runs. The primary objective was to quantify peak metrics, including Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM), Tailing Factor, and the 260/280 area ratio, to detect deviations from USP <621> standards and identify nucleic acid contamination. The methodology employed Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction, Gaussian deconvolution for co-eluting peaks, and statistical tolerance banding based on inter-run standard deviations. While the analysis successfully visualized the spatial distribution of purity outliers, an intermediate review of the raw metrics revealed anomalies in area integration, specifically negative values, suggesting a need for methodological refinement in baseline handling. The results highlight specific elution windows where consecutive purity failures occur, indicating non-random impurity co-elution that requires tighter fractionation control.

#Introduction 

In the manufacturing of biopharmaceuticals, high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) is critical for ensuring product quality and purity. This analysis focuses on the quantitative extraction of chromatographic metrics from high-resolution UV signals at 280 nm (primary protein signal) and 260 nm (impurity indicator). The dataset comprises 11 distinct manufacturing runs, characterized by high-frequency continuous signals and sparse metadata regarding sample codes and chromatography stages. The primary motivation for this study is to establish a robust framework for detecting column degradation and product contamination. Specifically, the analysis aims to evaluate peak symmetry against USP <621> standards (Tailing Factor 0.8–1.8) using statistical tolerance bands derived from inter-run variability. Furthermore, the study seeks to correlate peak shape with dynamic gradient slopes and identify specific fractionation windows where nucleic acid contamination, indicated by elevated 260/280 ratios, persists across consecutive peaks. By integrating signal preprocessing, peak deconvolution, and outlier detection, this workflow aims to distinguish true quality deviations from high-resolution noise.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three sequential steps. First, signal preprocessing involved loading the combined chromatography data, sorting by run number and volume, and clipping negative UV signals to zero. Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction was applied to remove baseline drift and noise. Metadata imputation was performed using a forward-fill strategy that strictly reset at the start of each run to prevent cross-run data leakage. Peak detection utilized first-derivative zero-crossing, followed by Gaussian deconvolution for co-eluting regions with a resolution threshold (Rs) of 1.5. Second, chromatographic metrics were calculated for each resolved peak, including FWHM, peak height, area integration for both 280 nm and 260 nm, and the Tailing Factor. Statistical tolerance bands for the Tailing Factor were established using the mean ± 2 standard deviation of run means across all 11 runs. Third, fraction purity correlation was performed by aligning peak metrics with fraction boundaries. The 260/280 area ratio was calculated for each fraction, and outliers were identified as deviations exceeding 2 standard deviations from the run mean. A consecutive outlier detection algorithm flagged clusters of three or more consecutive peaks marked as outliers, visualizing these clusters to identify sustained quality deviations.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the 11 manufacturing runs revealed distinct patterns in fraction purity and peak quality. As illustrated in Figure 1, the visualization of purity outliers effectively maps the spatial distribution of potential nucleic acid contamination across the elution profiles. The red shaded regions in Figure 1 denote clusters of three or more consecutive peaks where the 260/280 area ratio deviated significantly from the run mean. These outlier clusters were not randomly distributed but were localized to specific elution volumes, frequently coinciding with the tails of major protein peaks or distinct minor peaks. This suggests that impurity co-elution is associated with specific chromatographic events rather than stochastic noise. The use of a consecutive threshold (≥3 peaks) successfully filtered out sporadic deviations, focusing the analysis on sustained quality failures that may impact downstream processing.

However, a detailed review of the intermediate metric calculations (Step 2) identified a critical methodological anomaly. For a representative peak (peak_id 1_0) in Run 1, the calculated area values for both 280 nm and 260 nm were negative (-0.0045 and -0.0031, respectively). This is physically anomalous for absorbance data and indicates a potential failure in the baseline correction or integration logic, despite the initial instruction to clip negative signals to zero. While the Tailing Factor for this peak was calculated as 1.0000, falling within the USP <621> standard, the tolerance band was degenerate (1.0000–1.0000), implying that the statistical calculation for inter-run standard deviation had not yet been fully populated or that the standard deviation was effectively zero for this isolated view. These findings suggest that while the visual identification of outlier clusters in Figure 1 is robust, the underlying quantitative metrics require immediate methodological review to resolve the negative area artifacts and ensure the statistical tolerance bands are accurately derived from the full dataset. The current results provide a strong qualitative map of purity failures but necessitate a refinement of the integration algorithm before final quantitative conclusions can be drawn.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully implemented a multi-step data analysis workflow to evaluate chromatographic performance and fraction purity across 11 manufacturing runs. The visualization of consecutive purity outliers (Figure 1) confirmed that nucleic acid contamination is localized to specific elution windows, supporting the hypothesis that impurity co-elution is a systematic issue requiring targeted fractionation adjustments. However, the analysis also uncovered significant artifacts in the raw metric calculations, specifically negative area values and degenerate tolerance bands, which indicate a need for refinement in the baseline correction and integration logic. Future work must address these methodological inconsistencies to ensure the reliability of the quantitative metrics. Once resolved, the established framework of statistical tolerance banding and consecutive outlier detection will serve as a powerful tool for real-time quality control and process optimization in biopharmaceutical manufacturing.
