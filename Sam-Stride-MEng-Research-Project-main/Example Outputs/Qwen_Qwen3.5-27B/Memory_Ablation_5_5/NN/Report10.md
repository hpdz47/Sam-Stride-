#Abstract 

This study aimed to evaluate detector instability and column degradation across 11 chromatography manufacturing runs by establishing empirical baselines and inferring process stages deterministically. The analysis workflow involved rigorous data sanitization, flow validation, baseline stability assessment, and multivariate drift clustering. However, the primary objective was critically compromised during the initial data sanitization phase. A strict flow validation criterion, requiring less than 2% of data points to deviate by more than 2% from the run mean, resulted in the exclusion of 100% of the dataset. Consequently, no valid runs remained for downstream analysis, including baseline correlation checks, gradient inference, or multivariate clustering. While the methodology for baseline stability and drift detection was theoretically sound and visualized in preliminary outputs, the total data loss renders the specific results regarding column aging and detector drift inconclusive. This report details the methodology, the catastrophic failure in data retention, and the implications for future data quality control strategies.

#Introduction 

High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) processes require rigorous monitoring to distinguish between instrument drift, detector instability, and actual column degradation. In the absence of explicit quality thresholds or process stage labels, empirical baselines must be derived from the data itself. The primary motivation for this analysis was to detect statistical outliers in baseline drift and signal noise using Z-score and Interquartile Range (IQR) methods, while simultaneously inferring process stages through the detection of step-changes and linear gradients in concentration profiles. The workflow was designed to validate the temporal proxy (`volume_ml`) against flow rates, exclude runs with significant flow deviations, and utilize dual-wavelength correlation to identify buffer contamination. Furthermore, the analysis sought to isolate column aging by normalizing peak retention times to an inferred gradient start point and employing Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to separate detector drift from process variations using pressure and conductivity data. The ultimate goal was to provide a robust, data-driven assessment of system health across the 11 manufacturing runs.

#Methodology 

The data analysis followed a three-step plan. Step 1 involved Data Sanitization, Flow Validation, and Baseline Truncation. The dataset was loaded, and corrupted columns ending in '_ml' (excluding `volume_ml`) were removed. Runs were grouped, and the mean system flow was calculated. A strict exclusion criterion was applied: any run where more than 2% of rows deviated by more than 2% from the run mean flow was discarded. Remaining runs were truncated to a common negative start point based on the minimum `volume_ml` value. Step 2 focused on Baseline Stability and Gradient Inference. For valid runs, the pre-run baseline region (`volume_ml` < 0) was analyzed for UV statistics using Z-score and IQR methods stratified by column. Dual-wavelength correlation (Pearson r) was calculated, flagging runs with r < 0.95. Process stages were inferred by applying Savitzky-Golay smoothing to the `Conc_B_%` profile and detecting the gradient start volume via derivative analysis. Step 3 entailed Multivariate Drift Clustering. Features including UV absorbance, pressure, and conductivity were aggregated and standardized. PCA was performed on these features, followed by K-Means clustering (k=2) incorporating gradual baseline elevation to identify column-specific aging trends. Normalized retention shifts were calculated relative to the inferred gradient start.

#Results and Discussion 

The execution of the data analysis plan revealed a critical failure in the initial data sanitization phase that precluded the generation of valid results for the subsequent steps. As detailed in the markdown analysis reports, Step 1 resulted in the exclusion of all 11 chromatography runs (run_no 1, 2, 4, 16, 17, 18, 19, 20, 21, 30, 32). The 'Flow_Excluded_Flag' was marked as 'EXCLUDED' for every entry, with 'Final_Rows' counting zero for the entire dataset. This indicates that the strict flow validation criterion (>2% of rows deviating >2% from the mean) was triggered universally, likely due to systemic sensor noise, data recording artifacts, or an unrealistically stringent tolerance threshold for the specific instrument. Consequently, the downstream analyses described in the image reports could not be performed on actual data. Figure 1 (baseline_analysis.png) and Figure 2 (multivariate_drift.png) represent the visual outputs of the analysis pipeline's logic and structure but do not reflect the results of the 11 runs, as the dataset was empty prior to these steps. The boxplots in Figure 1 and the PCA scatter plot in Figure 2 illustrate the intended methodology for identifying baseline drift and column aging, but the absence of data points in the actual workflow means no statistical conclusions regarding detector instability or column degradation can be drawn. The total data loss suggests that the flow deviation threshold requires immediate re-evaluation or that the raw flow data contains high-frequency noise necessitating preprocessing before validation.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the data analysis workflow failed to produce actionable insights regarding detector instability or column degradation due to a total exclusion of the dataset during the flow validation step. The strict criterion of excluding runs with >2% flow deviation proved too severe for the current dataset, resulting in 100% data loss. While the methodology for baseline stability assessment, gradient inference, and multivariate clustering was robust and clearly defined, it could not be applied to the manufacturing runs. Future efforts must focus on revisiting the flow validation parameters, potentially relaxing the tolerance threshold or applying noise filtering to the `System_flow_ml_min` data prior to exclusion. Without valid data passing the initial sanitization step, the empirical baselines and drift detection mechanisms remain theoretical. The visualizations provided serve as a blueprint for the intended analysis but do not reflect the actual state of the chromatography system.
