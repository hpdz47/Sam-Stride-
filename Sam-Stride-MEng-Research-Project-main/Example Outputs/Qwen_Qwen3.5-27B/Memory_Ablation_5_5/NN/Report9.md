#Abstract 

This report details the analysis of high-frequency HPLC time-series data across 11 chromatographic runs to quantify the relative proportions of detected compounds. The workflow employed Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction, gradient-based inference of elution stages, and rigorous peak detection thresholds. While the normalization pipeline successfully calculated relative proportions summing to unity for all runs, a critical methodological failure in Step 1 prevented the inference of 'Elution' stages, resulting in an empty peak detection dataset in Step 2. Consequently, the comparative analysis in Step 3 revealed significant heterogeneity in total signal intensity between two distinct groups of runs, yet the specific compound identities and purity ratios could not be validated due to the lack of populated peak metrics. The findings highlight a divergence in data quality or experimental conditions that requires immediate investigation into the stage inference logic and raw chromatograms.

#Introduction 

The primary objective of this data analysis was to process UV absorbance signals (UV_1_280_mAU) from 11 distinct chromatographic runs to identify, integrate, and normalize peaks, ultimately calculating the relative proportion of compounds per run. The dataset presented significant challenges, including 75% missingness in the `chromatography_stage` metadata and the presence of pre-injection artifacts in negative volume regions. To address these issues, the analysis plan mandated the use of Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) for baseline correction and the inference of process stages based on `Conc_B_%` gradient slopes. The ultimate goal was to generate a comparative visualization of compound distribution across runs while validating peak purity using the 280/260 nm ratio. However, the successful execution of this workflow relies heavily on the accurate inference of elution windows, which serves as the gatekeeper for subsequent peak detection and integration.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a three-step protocol. Step 1 involved data preprocessing, where AsLS baseline correction (lambda=1e6, p=0.001) was applied to the pre-injection region to remove artifacts, followed by the exclusion of the solvent front (volume_ml < 1.0). Crucially, this step required inferring the `chromatography_stage` by calculating the rolling slope of `Conc_B_%` normalized by system flow; regions exceeding a dynamic threshold were labeled 'Elution', while others were labeled 'Unknown'. Step 2 focused on peak detection within the inferred 'Elution' windows. Peaks were identified using `scipy.signal.find_peaks` with thresholds of height > 3x baseline noise standard deviation and width > 0.5 ml. Integration of Area_280 and Area_260 was performed to calculate purity ratios. Step 3 involved run-level normalization, where the relative proportion of each peak was calculated as the ratio of its area to the total valid area of the run. Outliers were flagged if the sum of proportions deviated by more than 0.05 from 1.0.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis results reveal a dichotomy between successful normalization logic and a critical failure in upstream data processing. The preprocessing log (Step 1) confirms that baseline correction and solvent front exclusion were executed correctly, removing approximately 1.7% to 15.2% of data rows per run. However, the 'Inferred Elution' and 'Labeled Unknown' counts were uniformly zero across all runs. This indicates a complete failure in the gradient-based logic intended to identify elution stages, likely due to unmet slope thresholds or a logic error. Consequently, the Peak Metrics Summary (Step 2) is entirely empty, containing no detected peaks, start/end volumes, or purity ratios. Without valid 'Elution' labels, the peak detection algorithm had no data to process, rendering the purity validation and specific compound identification impossible.

Despite the lack of peak-level data, the Run-Level Normalization summary (Step 3) and Figure 1 provide insight into the raw signal characteristics. The summary table indicates that while the mathematical normalization was applied (Sum_of_Relative_Proportions = 1.0000 for all runs), the underlying data exhibits significant heterogeneity. Runs 1, 2, 4, 16, 17, 30, and 32 displayed high total valid areas (28M–39M) and a high number of detected peaks (159–479). In stark contrast, Runs 18, 19, 20, and 21 showed a dramatic reduction in total area (5.3M–16.3M) and peak count (71–199). 

Figure 1 visualizes these proportions, assuming the normalization logic was applied to the available signal. While the chart confirms that the relative abundance calculation is mathematically consistent (bars sum to 1.0), the absence of a legend mapping colors to specific Peak_IDs and the lack of actual peak data prevent a definitive interpretation of compound identity or reproducibility. The observed drop in signal intensity for Runs 18–21 suggests potential issues with sample loading, column performance, or detection sensitivity, or that these runs represent a different experimental condition. The current results serve as a high-level quality control check, confirming that the normalization pipeline functions mathematically but highlighting that the upstream stage inference must be corrected to yield valid scientific conclusions regarding compound composition.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the data analysis workflow successfully executed the mathematical components of baseline correction and run-level normalization but failed at the critical stage of process inference. The inability to label 'Elution' stages resulted in a complete absence of detected peaks, preventing the calculation of purity ratios and the identification of specific compounds. While the comparative analysis in Figure 1 demonstrates that the normalization logic is robust, the significant disparity in total signal intensity between the two groups of runs (High vs. Low signal) warrants further investigation. Future work must prioritize debugging the `Conc_B_%` slope inference logic to correctly identify elution windows. Once corrected, the pipeline will be able to generate the necessary peak metrics to validate the compositional consistency and purity of the chromatographic runs.
