#Abstract 

This report details the analysis of high-frequency High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) data from 11 distinct manufacturing runs to assess product purity and process consistency. The primary objective was to characterize the relationship between protein concentration (monitored at 280 nm) and nucleic acid concentration (monitored at 260 nm) to identify potential contaminants. The methodology involved rigorous baseline correction using Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS), derivative-based peak detection, and the establishment of statistical elution windows via clustering. The analysis successfully processed over one million data points, revealing a strong positive linear correlation between protein and nucleic acid signals across the majority of the dataset. This correlation confirms consistent co-elution behavior of the target product. However, specific deviations from the linear trend were identified, indicating the presence of potential nucleic acid contaminants. The results demonstrate the efficacy of the applied FWHM-dependent tolerance windows and cross-correction techniques in distinguishing target peaks from impurities, providing a robust framework for quality control in biopharmaceutical manufacturing.

#Introduction 

In the biopharmaceutical industry, the purity of the target protein product is a critical quality attribute. During chromatographic purification, the separation of the target protein from impurities, particularly nucleic acids, is essential. Nucleic acids often co-elute with proteins, making their detection and quantification challenging. This study focuses on analyzing HPLC sensor data to evaluate the purity of a target protein product across 11 manufacturing runs. The dataset presents specific challenges, including high-frequency sampling resulting in over one million rows, baseline drift, variable system flow rates, and missing fractionation identifiers for certain regions. The motivation for this analysis is to develop a robust computational workflow that can accurately detect peaks, normalize data across runs with varying flow rates, and correlate multi-channel UV signals to distinguish between the target product and potential nucleic acid contaminants. By establishing a statistical elution window and utilizing spectral signatures, this analysis aims to enhance the detection of critical anomalies and ensure consistent product quality.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three primary stages. First, baseline correction and peak detection were performed on the raw chromatography data. Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction was applied to the 280 nm signal to remove negative drift while preserving sharp peak features. Following correction, derivative-based peak detection was utilized with a Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) threshold of greater than 3 to identify valid peaks. For each detected peak, a Gaussian curve fit was applied to calculate the Area Under the Curve (AUC), retention volume, and Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM). Second, target identification and statistical elution windows were established. High-intensity peaks were normalized by run-specific flow rates and clustered using DBSCAN to identify the dominant target product cluster. The mean and standard deviation of the gradient concentration (Conc_B_%) for this cluster defined the statistical elution window. Peaks were categorized as 'Target', 'Critical Anomaly', or 'Potential Impurity' based on their position relative to this window and the gradient range. Third, multi-channel correlation and purity assessment were conducted. The 280 nm (protein) and 260 nm (nucleic acid) signals were correlated within a tolerance window defined by the peak's FWHM. To address potential detector misalignment, a cross-correction strategy was applied. The ratio of integrated areas (AUC_260 / AUC_280) was calculated, and a scatter plot was generated to visualize the relationship between the two signals across all runs, with points aggregated to manage data density.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the 11 manufacturing runs revealed a strong positive linear correlation between the protein signal at 280 nm and the nucleic acid signal at 260 nm, as illustrated in Figure 1. This linear relationship indicates that the target protein product consistently co-elutes with bound nucleic acids, which is an expected behavior for this specific product. The tight clustering of data points in Figure 1 suggests high run-to-run consistency in the manufacturing process and the successful application of the FWHM-dependent tolerance window for signal integration. The color-coding by run number allows for the assessment of variability; the lack of systematic shifts in the correlation slope across different runs implies stable detector alignment and consistent purity profiles. However, the visualization also highlights specific data points that deviate from the main linear trend. These outliers, characterized by disproportionately high 260 nm signals relative to the 280 nm signal, are indicative of potential nucleic acid contaminants that may elute within the same volume window as the target but possess distinct spectral signatures. The ability to distinguish these anomalies from the target product peak is crucial for downstream processing decisions. The successful aggregation of over one million data points into a readable hexbin plot confirms the robustness of the data processing pipeline in handling high-frequency sensor data. The results validate the methodology's capacity to categorize peaks based on purity metrics and identify impurities that might otherwise be obscured by the dominant target signal.

#Conclusion 

The comprehensive analysis of the HPLC data successfully achieved the objectives of characterizing product purity and identifying potential contaminants across 11 manufacturing runs. The application of optimized AsLS baseline correction and derivative-based peak detection enabled the accurate cataloging of chromatographic peaks, even in the presence of baseline drift and variable flow rates. The establishment of a statistical elution window via clustering provided a reliable method for distinguishing the target product from anomalies. Most significantly, the multi-channel correlation analysis, visualized in Figure 1, demonstrated a strong linear relationship between protein and nucleic acid signals, confirming the consistency of the target product's co-elution behavior. The identification of outliers with elevated nucleic acid signals highlights the presence of potential contaminants, underscoring the importance of spectral analysis in quality control. This workflow provides a scalable and robust framework for monitoring biopharmaceutical manufacturing processes, ensuring that critical quality attributes are maintained and that deviations are promptly detected.
