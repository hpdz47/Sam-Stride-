#Abstract 

This report details the algorithmic analysis of high-frequency chromatographic data to detect and classify peak anomalies indicative of column degradation or matrix effects. Utilizing Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction and USP <621> Tailing Factor calculations, the workflow processed 447,507 data rows corresponding to the 'Elution' stage. The analysis identified 20 anomalous peaks across five distinct runs involving CM, DEAE, Q, and SP columns. While the pipeline successfully differentiated anomalies based on multi-wavelength correlation, a critical methodological inconsistency was observed where the majority of flagged 'Column Degradation' cases exhibited Tailing Factors within the normal range, suggesting a potential inversion in classification logic or threshold application. The visualization of the run with the highest anomaly count confirms the spatial distribution of these deviations, highlighting the need for refined threshold interpretation in future assessments.

#Introduction 

Chromatographic performance is critical for ensuring the purity and yield of protein purification processes. Deviations in peak symmetry, characterized by tailing or fronting, often signal underlying issues such as column degradation, mobile phase incompatibility, or sample matrix effects. The primary objective of this analysis was to automate the detection of such anomalies within high-frequency time-series data, specifically focusing on the `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` signals. The methodology aimed to distinguish between 'Matrix Effects'—where sample components interfere with the separation—and 'Column Degradation'—where the stationary phase integrity is compromised. This distinction was achieved by correlating the Tailing Factor ($T$) with the Pearson correlation coefficient between the 280nm and 260nm channels. Peaks with a Tailing Factor outside the range of 0.8 to 1.8 were flagged as abnormal; those with a correlation coefficient below 0.85 were classified as matrix effects, while others were attributed to column degradation. This report synthesizes the results of a three-step analysis plan involving data preprocessing, anomaly classification, and run-level visualization.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three sequential steps. First, data preprocessing involved filtering the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset to retain only rows corresponding to the 'Elution' stage, resulting in 447,507 rows after dropping 636,562 non-elution entries. Gaps in `Fraction_unique_ID` were bridged if the volume gap was less than 0.5 mL and signal continuity was maintained. Baseline correction was applied using Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) with a fixed lambda of 800,000 to ensure non-negative absorbance values. Peak detection utilized Savitzky-Golay smoothing (window=5) to identify local maxima, merging adjacent peaks within 0.2 mL, while injection spikes with a slope exceeding 50 mAU/mL were removed.

Second, peak symmetry and anomaly classification were performed. The USP <621> Tailing Factor ($T$) was calculated at 5% peak height. Simultaneously, the Pearson correlation between the 280nm and 260nm profiles was computed for each peak. Anomalies were defined as peaks with $T < 0.8$ or $T > 1.8$. Classification logic dictated that abnormal peaks with a correlation coefficient $< 0.85$ be labeled 'Matrix Effect', while those with higher correlation were labeled 'Column Degradation'.

Third, run-level aggregation identified the single run with the highest total anomaly count for visualization. A composite chromatogram was generated, highlighting the top five anomalous peaks to illustrate the spatial distribution of deviations.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully identified 20 anomalous peaks distributed across runs 1, 2, 4, 16, and 17, involving CM, DEAE, Q, and SP columns. Figure 1 visualizes the run with the highest anomaly count, displaying the baseline-corrected `UV_1_280_mAU` signal against `volume_ml`. The plot clearly delineates five specific regions highlighted with shaded areas, representing the most severe anomalies detected by the algorithm. These regions are color-coded to distinguish between 'Matrix Effect' and 'Column Degradation' classifications.

However, a significant discrepancy was observed in the classification results. While the plan defined abnormality as a Tailing Factor ($T$) outside the 0.8–1.8 range, 19 of the 20 flagged peaks were classified as 'Column Degradation' despite having Tailing Factors between 0.5 and 0.75. This range typically indicates peak fronting but falls within the lower bound of the defined 'Abnormal' threshold ($T < 0.8$), suggesting that the classification logic may have been applied to peaks that were technically 'abnormal' by the strict definition but physically represent fronting rather than the severe tailing often associated with degradation. Conversely, the single peak classified as 'Matrix Effect' (Run 16, SP column) exhibited a negative correlation coefficient of -0.5963, which strongly validates the correlation-based differentiation logic for identifying matrix interference. The high correlation coefficients (>0.9) observed in most other peaks indicate consistent signal behavior between the two wavelengths, supporting the data quality for peak detection. The visualization in Figure 1 confirms the successful application of AsLS baseline correction, showing a flat, non-negative baseline and smooth signal profiles outside the highlighted peaks. Nevertheless, the predominance of 'Column Degradation' labels for peaks with $T \approx 0.6$ warrants a re-evaluation of the classification thresholds to ensure they accurately reflect the physical state of the column versus sample matrix interactions.

#Conclusion 

The automated data analysis workflow effectively processed high-frequency chromatographic data to identify potential quality issues, successfully filtering for elution stages and applying robust baseline correction. The visualization of the most anomalous run (Figure 1) provides a clear overview of peak deviations. However, the results highlight a critical inconsistency in the anomaly classification logic, where the majority of flagged 'Column Degradation' cases did not align with the expected Tailing Factor thresholds for severe degradation. While the correlation-based method for identifying 'Matrix Effects' proved valid, the current thresholds for 'Column Degradation' require refinement to distinguish between fronting, tailing, and true column failure. Future iterations of this analysis should adjust the Tailing Factor thresholds or incorporate additional symmetry metrics to improve the accuracy of column health assessments.
