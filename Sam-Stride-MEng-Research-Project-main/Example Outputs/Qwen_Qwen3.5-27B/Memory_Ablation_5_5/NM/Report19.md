#Abstract 

This report evaluates the structural integrity and process consistency of high-frequency HPLC sensor data to determine its viability for downstream modeling. The analysis focused on reconstructing missing chromatographic stage metadata, segmenting cleaning cycles, assessing peak purity via UV260/UV280 ratios, and quantifying retention volume variability against USP standards. While visual analysis of signal integrity and gradient correlation confirms robust data quality and consistent chromatographic mechanisms across runs, a critical methodological failure was identified in the retention volume calculation. The quantitative analysis yielded an anomalous Relative Standard Deviation (RSD) of 812.4% and a mean retention volume near zero, indicating that the peak selection algorithm erroneously identified noise artifacts rather than the main product peak. Consequently, while the raw sensor data appears suitable for modeling, the current computational pipeline for retention volume extraction requires immediate re-evaluation before valid process compliance conclusions can be drawn.

#Introduction 

The primary objective of this data analysis workflow was to rigorously assess the signal-to-noise ratio and structural integrity of continuous HPLC sensor streams, specifically `UV_1_280_mAU`, `UV_2_260_mAU`, `Cond_mS_cm`, and `pH_pH`. The dataset, comprising over one million high-frequency rows indexed by `volume_ml`, presents significant challenges, including the presence of negative volume values representing pre-run equilibration and a 76% missingness rate in the `chromatography_stage` metadata. To address these challenges, the analysis aimed to infer the chromatographic mechanism through gradient-peak correlation, reconstruct stage boundaries using conductivity and buffer concentration profiles, and segment Clean-in-Place (CIP) cycles from process data based on pH stability. Furthermore, the study sought to validate peak purity using UV260/UV280 ratios and quantify run-to-run retention volume variability against strict USP standards (±2.0%) to identify potential column degradation or failed runs. Establishing the viability of this data for downstream modeling is contingent upon resolving these structural ambiguities and ensuring that peak detection algorithms accurately distinguish true elution events from baseline noise.

#Methodology 

The analysis was executed in three distinct steps. Step 1 involved Signal Integrity and Stage Reconstruction. Baseline Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) were calculated using the standard deviation of UV signals in the -33 to -5 mL range. Chromatographic stages were reconstructed by detecting inflection points in `Cond_mS_cm` and `Conc_B_%` using derivative thresholding (>0.5 mS/cm or 1% change), with a fallback to 'Unknown' if reconstruction failed. CIP regions were segmented based on pH thresholds (>10 or <2). Step 2 focused on Peak Purity and Gradient Correlation. The UV260/UV280 ratio was calculated for peaks exceeding the baseline threshold, with a target purity of 1.8 ± 0.2. Cross-correlation analysis was performed between `Conc_B_%` and `UV_1_280_mAU` to quantify the lag and strength of the gradient-peak relationship. Step 3 assessed Retention Volume Variability. The main product peak was defined as the peak with the largest UV280 area, excluding runs with peak heights < 5 mAU or SNR < 3.0. The apex volume was calculated, and the Relative Standard Deviation (RSD) was determined against a mean retention volume, with compliance flagged for deviations exceeding ±2.0%.

#Results and Discussion 

The visual analysis of the sensor streams indicates high data quality and successful execution of the initial processing steps. Figure 1 demonstrates that the signal integrity is robust, with clear separation between baseline, peak, and CIP regions. The reconstruction of `chromatography_stage` boundaries via derivative thresholding appears effective, as evidenced by the synchronized transitions in conductivity and buffer concentration profiles. Similarly, Figure 2 confirms a strong temporal alignment between the rising `Conc_B_%` gradient and the subsequent UV280 elution peaks, validating the chromatographic mechanism. The UV260/UV280 ratios in the peak regions cluster around the expected purity target of 1.8, suggesting that the raw data supports valid purity assessments. However, a critical discrepancy arises when comparing these visual findings with the quantitative results of the retention volume analysis. While Figure 3 visually depicts a tight clustering of data points around the mean, suggesting low run-to-run variability, the accompanying quantitative analysis reports a catastrophic failure with an RSD of 812.4% and a mean retention volume of 0.0460 mL. This extreme dispersion, coupled with deviations reaching 6500% and -100%, indicates that the algorithm failed to identify the true main product peak. It is highly probable that the peak selection logic erroneously selected noise artifacts or baseline fluctuations, particularly given the inclusion of negative pre-run volumes. Consequently, while the visual evidence in Figures 1 and 2 supports the dataset's suitability for modeling, the retention volume metrics in Figure 3 cannot be trusted in their current form and highlight a fundamental breakdown in the peak identification methodology.

#Conclusion 

The analysis confirms that the underlying HPLC sensor data possesses high structural integrity, with clear signal separation, successful stage reconstruction, and consistent gradient-peak coupling. The visual evidence strongly supports the conclusion that the chromatographic runs exhibit acceptable peak purity and stable process boundaries. However, the quantitative assessment of retention volume variability is invalid due to a critical failure in the peak detection algorithm, which resulted in erroneous mean values and extreme RSD. This discrepancy underscores the necessity of re-evaluating the peak selection criteria, specifically regarding the handling of negative volume pre-run data and the distinction between noise and true elution events. Until the retention volume calculation logic is corrected, no definitive conclusions regarding USP compliance or column degradation can be drawn. Future work must prioritize refining the peak identification algorithm to ensure that downstream modeling is based on accurate, physically meaningful retention metrics.
