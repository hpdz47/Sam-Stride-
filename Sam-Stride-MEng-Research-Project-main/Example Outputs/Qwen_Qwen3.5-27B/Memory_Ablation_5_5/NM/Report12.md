#Abstract 

This report details the automated analysis of chromatographic data from 11 experimental runs to assess separation efficiency and purity. The workflow employed Savitzky-Golay smoothing for noise reduction, followed by algorithmic peak detection based on Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) thresholds and elution phase inference via gradient slopes. The primary objective was to calculate the resolution ($R_s$) between adjacent peaks using the tangent skim method for peak width determination ($W_b$) and flag runs with $R_s < 1.5$ as critical purity failures. While the visualization pipeline successfully generated chromatograms with annotated resolution values, the quantitative analysis revealed a systemic anomaly: 10 out of 11 runs reported a minimum resolution of 0.00, and 6 runs detected zero peaks entirely. These results suggest a failure in the upstream peak detection logic or parameterization rather than a physical lack of separation, necessitating a review of the detection thresholds and handling of single-peak scenarios.

#Introduction 

High-resolution chromatography is critical for ensuring the purity of analytical samples, where the separation of co-eluting compounds is quantified by the resolution ($R_s$). In this study, we analyzed a dataset comprising approximately one million high-frequency scans across 11 distinct experimental runs. The data included UV absorbance at 280 nm, eluent B concentration, and conductivity. The primary challenge involved processing raw time-series data that contained pre-injection artifacts and lacked explicit stage labels for ~76% of the data points. Consequently, the analysis required the inference of elution phases based on the slope of the eluent gradient and the application of robust smoothing techniques to mitigate sensor noise. The specific goal was to programmatically identify analyte peaks, calculate their widths using the tangent skim method to adhere to USP <621> standards, and determine the minimum resolution between adjacent peaks. A run was designated as a 'CRITICAL PURITY FAILURE' if the minimum $R_s$ fell below 1.5. This report synthesizes the methodological approach and the unexpected results derived from the automated analysis pipeline.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three sequential steps. First, signal preprocessing involved loading the raw data and normalizing the volume axis by shifting the minimum volume to 0.0 mL, explicitly excluding negative pre-injection values. The UV absorbance signal was smoothed using a Savitzky-Golay filter (window size 11, polynomial order 3) to reduce high-frequency noise. Elution phases were inferred by calculating the rolling slope of the eluent B concentration; segments with a slope greater than 0.5%/mL were identified as active elution zones. If no such segment was found, a fallback mechanism checked for a monotonic increase in conductivity. Peak detection was performed on the smoothed signal within these phases using a derivative-based algorithm, retaining only peaks with an SNR > 3 and a height exceeding 5% of the maximum signal. Second, peak width at the baseline ($W_b$) was calculated using the 'tangent skim' method, which involves fitting tangents at the inflection points of the peak to the baseline. Third, resolution ($R_s$) was calculated for all adjacent peak pairs using the formula $R_s = 2(t_{R2} - t_{R1}) / (W_{b1} + W_{b2})$. The minimum $R_s$ per run was aggregated, and runs with values below 1.5 were flagged. Finally, a consolidated visualization was generated to validate the detection logic and resolution annotations.

#Results and Discussion 

The visual validation presented in Figure 1 confirms the successful application of Savitzky-Golay smoothing, resulting in clean UV traces with reduced noise. The figure effectively overlays the tangent skim boundaries and the eluent B gradient, validating the methodological approach for isolating elution phases. However, a critical discrepancy exists between the visual representation and the quantitative metrics derived in the resolution analysis. The markdown analysis of Step 1 revealed that 6 out of 11 runs (Runs 1, 2, 4, 16, 17, and 32) yielded zero detected peaks, suggesting that the signal-to-noise ratio or height thresholds were not met, or that the elution phase inference failed for these batches. Conversely, Runs 18 through 21 and Run 30 showed successful detection, with Run 30 exhibiting the highest complexity (3 peaks) and Run 19 showing a high average SNR. Despite successful detection in some runs, the Step 2 resolution analysis reported a minimum $R_s$ of 0.00 for 10 runs, with Run 19 marked as 'N/A'. An $R_s$ of 0.00 implies either perfectly co-eluting peaks or, more likely, a failure in the adjacent pair logic when only a single peak is detected per run. The uniformity of the 0.00 result across multiple runs suggests a systemic issue where the algorithm initialized the minimum resolution to 0 and failed to update it when no adjacent pairs were found, or where the peak detection algorithm failed to resolve distinct peaks despite the visual appearance of features in Figure 1. Consequently, while the 'CRITICAL PURITY FAILURE' flags are technically correct based on the $R_s < 1.5$ rule, the underlying cause appears to be a methodological breakdown in the peak pairing or detection logic rather than a physical purity issue. The visualization in Figure 1 highlights specific peak pairs in red, but the quantitative data suggests these annotations may be based on flawed resolution calculations or default values.

#Conclusion 

The automated analysis pipeline successfully processed the chromatographic data, applied smoothing, and generated visualizations that confirm the presence of analyte signals and the validity of the elution phase inference. However, the quantitative results indicate a significant failure in the peak detection and resolution calculation logic. The prevalence of zero detected peaks in half the dataset and the ubiquitous reporting of $R_s = 0.00$ in the remaining runs suggest that the current SNR thresholds or peak pairing algorithms are not robust enough for this specific dataset. The 'CRITICAL PURITY FAILURE' flags, while mathematically consistent with the $R_s < 1.5$ criterion, likely reflect a software logic error regarding single-peak scenarios rather than actual chromatographic failure. Future work must focus on recalibrating the peak detection parameters, specifically the SNR and height thresholds, and implementing robust error handling for runs with fewer than two detected peaks to ensure accurate resolution metrics.
