#Abstract 

This report details the analysis of high-frequency High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) data across 11 manufacturing runs to assess product purity and identify critical anomalies. The workflow employed optimized Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction to resolve signal drift, followed by derivative-based peak detection and Gaussian fitting to catalog chromatographic features. A primary objective was to correlate protein (280 nm) and nucleic acid (260 nm) signals to evaluate the purity profile of the target product. The analysis successfully identified a strong positive linear correlation between the two signals, confirming the presence of both components in the majority of detected peaks. However, specific deviations from the expected correlation trend were observed, indicating the presence of nucleic acid contaminants in certain peaks. These findings establish a baseline for purity assessment and highlight specific runs requiring further investigation regarding impurity profiles.

#Introduction 

In the manufacturing of biological products, maintaining high purity is critical for efficacy and safety. Chromatographic analysis serves as a primary tool for monitoring product quality, yet the interpretation of high-frequency sensor data presents challenges due to baseline drift, variable flow rates, and the co-elution of impurities. This study focuses on the analysis of 11 manufacturing runs to characterize the elution behavior of a target protein product and assess its purity relative to nucleic acid contaminants. The dataset, characterized by variable system flow rates and the absence of explicit time columns, necessitated a robust analytical approach using volume-based retention metrics. The primary motivation for this analysis was to develop a comprehensive peak catalog that distinguishes between the target product, critical anomalies, and potential impurities, including those in non-fractionated regions. By correlating UV absorbance at 280 nm (protein) and 260 nm (nucleic acid), this work aims to quantify the purity profile across manufacturing batches and identify specific deviations that may indicate process inconsistencies or contamination.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three sequential steps designed to process raw HPLC data and extract meaningful quality metrics. First, baseline correction and peak detection were performed on the raw chromatograms. Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) algorithms were applied to correct for negative baseline drift in the 280 nm signal, with parameters tuned to preserve sharp peak features. Derivative-based peak detection was subsequently applied to the corrected signals, utilizing a Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) threshold of >3 to filter noise. Detected peaks were fitted with Gaussian curves to calculate the Area Under the Curve (AUC), retention volume, and Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM). Second, target identification and statistical elution windows were established. High-intensity peaks were normalized by run-specific flow rates and clustered using DBSCAN to identify the dominant target product cluster. This allowed for the definition of a statistical elution window based on the mean and standard deviation of the mobile phase concentration (Conc_B_%). Peaks were categorized as 'Target', 'Critical Anomaly', or 'Potential Impurity' based on their position relative to this window and the gradient range. Finally, multi-channel correlation and purity assessment were conducted. For each detected peak, the integrated signal intensity of nucleic acids (260 nm) was correlated against proteins (280 nm) within a tolerance window defined by the peak's FWHM to account for potential detector misalignment. This correlation was visualized using hexbin aggregation to manage data density across the 11 runs.

#Results and Discussion 

The multi-channel correlation analysis, visualized in Figure 1, reveals a strong positive linear correlation between the integrated protein signal (280 nm) and the nucleic acid signal (260 nm) across the 11 manufacturing runs. This linear trend indicates that the majority of detected peaks contain both protein and nucleic acid components, which is consistent with the expected composition of crude or partially purified biological samples. The clustering of data points suggests a stable baseline purity profile, with a consistent ratio of 260 nm to 280 nm absorbance maintained across the dataset. The successful application of the FWHM-dependent tolerance window is evidenced by the clear definition of these clusters, demonstrating effective alignment of the two detector channels despite potential variations in flow rates. However, the analysis also identified scattered points deviating from the main trend line, specifically those exhibiting elevated 260 nm signals relative to 280 nm. These outliers likely represent the 'potential impurities' or nucleic acid contaminants targeted in the analysis plan. While Figure 1 effectively demonstrates the correlation methodology and highlights runs with potential purity deviations, the lack of explicit axis labels or a regression line in the visualization limits the precise quantification of the purity ratio for individual outliers. Consequently, definitive categorization of specific peaks as 'critical anomalies' requires cross-referencing with the underlying peak catalog data to confirm their retention volumes and SNR values. The presence of these deviations underscores the importance of the multi-channel correlation approach in detecting subtle impurity profiles that might be overlooked in single-channel analysis.

#Conclusion 

The analysis of the 11 manufacturing runs successfully established a robust framework for assessing product purity and identifying anomalies in HPLC data. The application of optimized AsLS baseline correction and FWHM-dependent multi-channel correlation provided a clear visualization of the relationship between protein and nucleic acid signals. The results confirm a generally stable purity profile across the dataset, characterized by a strong linear correlation between the two UV signals. However, the identification of specific outliers with elevated nucleic acid content highlights the presence of potential impurities that warrant further investigation. These findings validate the utility of the proposed analytical workflow in distinguishing between target product peaks and contaminants. Future work should focus on integrating the visual correlation data with the detailed peak catalog to precisely quantify the purity ratios of outlier peaks and correlate these findings with specific manufacturing parameters. This comprehensive approach ensures a deeper understanding of process consistency and product quality.
