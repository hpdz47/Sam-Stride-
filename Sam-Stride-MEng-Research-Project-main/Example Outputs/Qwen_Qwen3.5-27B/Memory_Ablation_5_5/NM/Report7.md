#Abstract 

This report evaluates the efficacy of a data analysis workflow designed to quantify the baseline noise floor and establish the Limit of Detection (LOD) for UV signals (`UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU`) in chromatography data. The workflow successfully implemented dynamic gradient stability thresholding and data sanitization, retaining approximately 88-99% of valid data across 11 experimental runs. However, the subsequent LOD validation failed catastrophically, yielding a simulated False Positive Rate (FPR) of 100.00% against a target of <1%. This failure suggests that the current noise characterization model, which assumes Gaussian distribution without transformation, is insufficient to distinguish signal from the observed noise floor, particularly given the presence of physically impossible negative UV values. The report details the methodology, highlights the critical discrepancy in noise modeling, and discusses the necessity for non-Gaussian noise transformation and rigorous baseline offset correction to achieve a valid LOD.

#Introduction 

The accurate determination of the Limit of Detection (LOD) is paramount in chromatographic analysis, as it defines the lowest concentration of an analyte that can be reliably distinguished from background noise. In this study, the primary objective was to programmatically define stable gradient regions within high-frequency time-series data and quantify the baseline noise floor for `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` signals. The dataset presented significant challenges, including physically impossible negative UV values (down to -7.95 mAU), corrupted data columns, and a lack of explicit metadata for approximately 76% of the rows. To address these issues, the analysis plan focused on inferring baseline regions through the derivative analysis of `Conc_B_%` and excluding pre-run equilibration artifacts characterized by negative `volume_ml` values. The ultimate goal was to derive an LOD using a 3σ multiplier on baseline-corrected signals, ensuring a False Positive Rate (FPR) of less than 1% when validated against synthetic peaks. This report synthesizes the results of the data sanitization, noise characterization, and validation steps to assess the current state of the LOD derivation.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three distinct steps. Step 1 involved data sanitization and dynamic baseline region identification. The dataset `chromatography_combined.csv` was processed to drop corrupted columns ending in `_ml` (excluding flow variables) and filter out rows with negative `volume_ml`. A rolling derivative of `Conc_B_%` was calculated to identify stable regions, defined as areas where the absolute derivative fell below the 95th percentile threshold and `Fraction_unique_ID` was null. This step successfully generated a `stable_region_mask` for 11 runs. Step 2 focused on noise characterization and LOD derivation. Signals within the stable regions were extracted to calculate the baseline offset (mean) and standard deviation (σ). The plan specified a normality test (Shapiro-Wilk) and potential Box-Cox transformation for non-Gaussian noise, followed by the calculation of LOD as 3 * σ_corrected. Step 3 involved a False Positive Rate simulation. Synthetic Gaussian noise matching the derived statistics was generated, and synthetic peaks were injected at amplitudes ranging from 0.5x to 2x the calculated LOD. The detection algorithm was applied to determine the FPR, with the target set at <1%.

#Results and Discussion 

The execution of the analysis plan yielded mixed results. Step 1 was highly effective, confirming the robustness of the dynamic thresholding logic. The sanitization process successfully removed negative volume artifacts, and the derivative-based method identified stable baseline regions with a high retention rate of 88-99% of cleaned rows across all 11 runs. The consistent derivative threshold of 0.0107 for `Conc_B_%` indicates that the gradient stability inference is reliable for this dataset. However, Step 3 revealed a critical failure in the LOD validation. The simulated False Positive Rate was 100.00%, with a calculated LOD of 0.6544 mAU. This result implies that the derived LOD is significantly lower than the actual noise floor, causing every synthetic peak injection to be flagged as a false positive. The discrepancy likely stems from the noise characterization in Step 2. The presence of physically impossible negative UV values and high standard deviation suggests that the noise distribution is non-Gaussian. The analysis indicates that the assumption of Gaussian noise without applying a Box-Cox transformation led to an underestimated standard deviation or an incorrect baseline offset correction. Consequently, the current LOD of 0.6544 mAU is insufficient to distinguish signal from noise. To rectify this, the noise characterization must be re-evaluated to account for non-Gaussian distributions and ensure the baseline offset is correctly subtracted prior to calculating σ.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, while the data sanitization and dynamic baseline identification components of the workflow are robust and effective, the current Limit of Detection (LOD) derivation is invalid due to a fundamental mismatch between the assumed noise model and the empirical data characteristics. The 100% False Positive Rate in the validation step confirms that the calculated LOD of 0.6544 mAU fails to separate signal from noise. The primary cause is the failure to account for non-Gaussian noise distributions and the potential mismanagement of baseline offsets in the presence of negative UV artifacts. Future iterations of this analysis must prioritize the application of Box-Cox transformations to normalize the noise distribution and rigorously verify baseline offset subtraction before calculating the standard deviation. Only by addressing these statistical assumptions can a valid LOD be established that meets the target False Positive Rate of <1%.
