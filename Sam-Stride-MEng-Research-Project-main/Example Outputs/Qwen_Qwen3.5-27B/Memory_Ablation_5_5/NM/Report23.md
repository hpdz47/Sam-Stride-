#Abstract 

This report details a comprehensive data analysis workflow designed to quantify baseline stability and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) in chromatography systems across four distinct columns (CM, DEAE, Q, SP) and two UV detection channels (280 nm and 260 nm). The primary objective was to distinguish genuine analyte peaks from artifacts by establishing robust, unit-specific noise thresholds and correcting for baseline offsets. The methodology involved a three-step process: calculating baseline stability and noise thresholds using rolling standard deviations, performing column-stratified SNR analysis, and investigating the correlation between baseline drift and conductivity. Results indicate significant heterogeneity in baseline stability across columns, with the Q column exhibiting the highest noise floor and the SP column showing a strong, conductivity-dependent baseline drift. These findings underscore the necessity of independent noise threshold calculations per channel and column to ensure accurate peak detection and quality control.

#Introduction 

High-performance chromatography relies on the precise detection of analyte peaks against a stable baseline. However, baseline instability, driven by factors such as detector noise, buffer incompatibility, and column degradation, can obscure genuine signals or generate false positives. This analysis addresses these challenges by quantifying the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) and baseline characteristics for two UV detection channels, `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU`, across four chromatography columns. The dataset presents specific challenges, including higher variability in the 260 nm channel and potential unit-specific degradation. The motivation for this study is to establish a rigorous, data-driven framework for peak detection that accounts for these variabilities. By stratifying analysis by column and run, and by correlating baseline drift with conductivity, this work aims to identify specific sources of instability and validate the efficacy of independent noise thresholding strategies (3σ and 5σ) for distinguishing true analyte signals from background noise.

#Methodology 

The data analysis followed a structured three-step plan. First, baseline stability and noise thresholds were calculated using pre-injection data (`volume_ml` < 0), excluding initial transients and focusing on constant gradient regions where `Conc_B_%` was within ±0.5% of 0.0 or 100.0. Global mean offsets were determined for each column and signal combination, followed by the computation of a rolling standard deviation (window=25) on offset-corrected signals to derive 3σ and 5σ noise thresholds. Second, column-stratified SNR and peak detection were performed. Local offsets were recalculated for each run, and valid post-injection regions were identified. Peaks were detected where the signal exceeded 3σ and 5σ thresholds relative to the local noise. Mean SNR and standard deviation were aggregated by column across 11 experimental runs. Third, conductivity correlation and drift analysis were conducted by filtering data to constant gradient regions and performing linear regression of signal versus volume to calculate drift rates. These rates were correlated with log-transformed conductivity (`Cond_mS_cm`) using Pearson correlation to assess the impact of buffer ionic strength on baseline stability.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis revealed significant variations in baseline stability and noise characteristics across the four chromatography columns. The baseline analysis (Step 1) identified substantial baseline offsets ranging from -1.59 mAU to +1.49 mAU, validating the necessity of the applied offset correction strategy. Notably, the Q column exhibited the highest noise floor, with 3σ thresholds approximately 4–5 times higher than those observed for the CM and SP columns. This suggests potential unit-specific degradation or detector sensitivity issues specific to the Q column. Furthermore, the 260 nm channel consistently demonstrated slightly higher noise thresholds than the 280 nm channel across all columns, aligning with the known higher variability of the 260 nm signal.

Figure 1 visualizes the column-stratified SNR results, illustrating the run-to-run stability and channel-specific performance. The plot confirms the successful differentiation of SNR performance between the 280 nm and 260 nm channels. The consistency of error bars across runs indicates a robust noise estimation, though the elevated noise in the Q column implies that higher detection thresholds may be required to avoid false positives compared to other units. The stratification effectively isolates the signal from baseline noise, fulfilling the objective of distinguishing genuine analyte peaks from artifacts based on the defined 3σ and 5σ thresholds.

In the drift analysis (Step 3), a stark contrast in baseline stability was observed. The SP column exhibited a highly significant, strong negative correlation between drift and conductivity (r ≈ -0.90, p ≈ 0.002) with substantial drift rates, indicating that baseline instability in this unit is strongly driven by buffer conductivity changes. In contrast, the CM, DEAE, and Q columns showed weak, statistically non-significant correlations with conductivity, suggesting their drift is driven by other factors such as temperature fluctuations or intrinsic detector noise. These findings highlight the importance of unit-specific monitoring and the potential need for conductivity-dependent corrections in specific columns like SP.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully quantified baseline stability and SNR across multiple chromatography columns and UV channels, demonstrating the critical importance of unit-specific and channel-specific noise profiling. The Q column's elevated noise floor and the SP column's conductivity-dependent drift highlight the heterogeneity present within the system, necessitating tailored detection thresholds rather than a one-size-fits-all approach. The methodology of using rolling standard deviations and constant gradient region identification proved effective in isolating genuine signals from artifacts. Future work should focus on investigating the root causes of the Q column's high noise and the SP column's conductivity sensitivity, potentially leading to improved buffer formulations or maintenance protocols. Overall, the established framework provides a robust basis for quality control and accurate peak detection in complex chromatographic datasets.
