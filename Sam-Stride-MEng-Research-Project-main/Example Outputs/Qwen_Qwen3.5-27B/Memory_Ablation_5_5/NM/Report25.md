#Abstract 

This report details a rigorous quality assessment of High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) time-series data to determine its fitness for downstream modeling. The analysis focused on filtering data ingestion artifacts, validating sensor integrity against USP <621> standards, and distinguishing between sensor noise and genuine process anomalies. A three-stage pipeline was executed: data sanitization and metadata reconstruction, signal integrity assessment, and dual-path outlier classification. While the data sanitization and signal smoothing steps successfully addressed baseline drift and redundancy issues, the final consistency assessment revealed critical gaps in peak metric extraction. Specifically, while 20 potential anomalies were identified, the inability to calculate Coefficient of Variation (CV) metrics per column prevents a definitive conclusion on column-specific degradation. The study highlights the necessity of robust fallback mechanisms in automated chromatographic data analysis pipelines.

#Introduction 

The reliability of downstream process modeling in chromatography is contingent upon the quality of the input time-series data. This dataset presented significant challenges, including severe ingestion errors where columns such as `Conc_B_ml` and `pH_ml` were erroneous duplicates of `volume_ml`, and approximately 76% of critical event-based metadata (`chromatography_stage`) was missing. Furthermore, sensor warm-up drift and potential gradient delivery failures necessitated a comprehensive validation strategy. The primary objective of this analysis was to ensure that the `UV_1_280_mAU` and `Cond_mS_cm` signals met regulatory-grade Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) thresholds. This was achieved by applying Savitzky-Golay smoothing to achieve a Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE) of less than 1.0% while preserving peak width, utilizing negative `volume_ml` data for dynamic baseline correction, and assessing conductivity drift against theoretical gradient profiles. Additionally, the study aimed to reconstruct missing process phases and perform a per-column run-to-run consistency assessment to isolate specific column degradation trends from general run-level variability.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a three-step plan. Step 1 involved Data Sanitization, Metadata Reconstruction, and Baseline Correction. Redundant columns ending in `_ml` (except `volume_ml` and `Conc_B_ml`) were dropped, specifically removing `pH_ml` due to 66% missing values. Missing `chromatography_stage` metadata was reconstructed via forward-filling within each run, with a fallback mechanism to flag rows where `Conc_B_%` signals were flat or noisy. Dynamic baseline correction was performed by fitting a linear trend to the negative `volume_ml` region for both UV and conductivity signals. Step 2 focused on Signal Integrity Assessment and USP <621> Compliance Validation. Peaks were isolated within reconstructed stage boundaries, and Savitzky-Golay filtering (Order 3, adaptive window) was applied. Gaussian fitting was used to calculate Tailing Factor and Resolution. Conductivity drift was analyzed by comparing observed slopes against the theoretical `Conc_B_%` gradient, flagging deviations exceeding 5% slope variance or 0.5 mS/cm/min. Step 3 utilized Dual-Path Outlier Classification. Outliers were categorized as 'Artifacts' or 'Anomalies' based on statistical limits (>3 sigma) and process-correlation logic (Pearson correlation > 0.8 between UV spikes and process variables). A fallback mechanism attempted to extract peak area and retention time to calculate Coefficient of Variation (CV) per column.

#Results and Discussion 

The data sanitization phase successfully removed redundant columns and reconstructed `chromatography_stage` metadata for 12 rows, while correctly flagging 20 rows where the fallback mechanism prevented incorrect stage assignment due to flat or noisy signals. The dynamic baseline correction effectively utilized the negative `volume_ml` region to remove sensor warm-up drift. As visualized in Figure 1, the Savitzky-Golay smoothing successfully suppressed high-frequency noise while preserving the shape of major elution peaks, indicating that the adaptive windowing strategy maintained the target MAPE < 1.0%. The visual consistency of peak shapes across runs suggests good run-to-run reproducibility, although specific numerical USP <621> metrics (Tailing Factor and Resolution) could not be confirmed from the visual annotations alone. Figure 2 illustrates the gradient-referenced conductivity drift analysis. The overlay of empirical conductivity signals with the theoretical gradient profile shows characteristic step-changes and linear ramps, suggesting the system is monitoring gradient delivery fidelity. However, the absence of explicit deviation markers in the visualization limits the ability to definitively confirm if any runs exceeded the 5% slope variance or 0.5 mS/cm/min drift thresholds. In the outlier classification phase, the top 20 outliers were identified as 'Anomalies' with deviations ranging from 43.46 to 45.63, clustered around 19.5–20.5 mL. Critically, the correlation values between UV spikes and process variables were low (0.09–0.22), falling below the 0.8 threshold required to confirm process-correlated anomalies. This suggests the anomalies may be statistical artifacts rather than genuine process events. Furthermore, the per-column consistency assessment failed to produce valid CV metrics for peak area and retention time, resulting in 'nan' values. This indicates a failure in the fallback mechanism to extract peak metrics, preventing a valid assessment of column-specific degradation.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis workflow successfully addressed the initial data ingestion artifacts and implemented effective signal smoothing and baseline correction strategies. The visual evidence from Figure 1 and Figure 2 suggests that the chromatographic runs generally exhibit stable baselines and expected gradient behavior. However, the analysis revealed significant limitations in the automated outlier classification and consistency assessment modules. The low correlation scores for identified anomalies suggest a potential misalignment in the dual-path classification logic, while the complete failure to calculate CV metrics indicates a breakdown in the peak detection fallback mechanism. Consequently, while the data quality regarding signal continuity and noise reduction is high, the current results are insufficient to definitively quantify column-specific degradation or confirm USP <621> compliance numerically. Future iterations of the analysis pipeline must prioritize robust peak detection algorithms and refined correlation thresholds to enable accurate consistency assessments and anomaly verification.
