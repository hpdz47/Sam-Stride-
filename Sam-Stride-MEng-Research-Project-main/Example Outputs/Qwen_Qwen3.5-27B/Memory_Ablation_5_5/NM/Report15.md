#Abstract 

This study presents a comprehensive data analysis workflow designed to isolate, classify, and rank chromatographic compounds across 11 experimental runs. The primary motivation was to address significant sensor artifacts, specifically negative absorbance drift, and to establish a robust method for cross-run alignment in the absence of complete sample metadata. By employing Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction, the workflow successfully resolved signal noise, enabling the detection of distinct elution peaks. Subsequent gradient-based alignment normalized elution volumes to buffer concentration percentages (`Conc_B_%`), revealing a highly skewed distribution where the flow-through phase (0% `Conc_B_%`) dominated the signal intensity. Finally, empirical thresholding of the 280/260 nm UV absorbance ratio facilitated the classification of compounds into protein, nucleic acid, and mixed categories. The analysis identified that the majority of the dataset (~98%) consists of mixed species, while multivariate outlier detection flagged specific data points associated with pre-run equilibration artifacts. These findings provide a validated framework for compound profiling at the run and column level, despite high missingness in sample identifiers.

#Introduction 

Chromatographic data analysis often faces challenges related to baseline drift, run-to-run variability, and the lack of granular sample metadata. In this dataset, 75.7% of `Sample_Code` entries were missing, necessitating an analysis strategy restricted to the `run_no` and `column` level. Furthermore, raw UV absorbance signals exhibited negative values indicative of sensor noise and pre-run equilibration artifacts, which could obscure true compound peaks. The primary objective of this analysis was to develop a reproducible pipeline to correct these baselines, align peaks across multiple runs based on elution gradients, and classify compounds based on spectral ratios. Specifically, the workflow aimed to: (1) apply Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) correction to isolate true peaks from noise; (2) utilize the elution gradient (`Conc_B_%`) to normalize peak positions across 11 runs; and (3) apply empirical thresholds to the 280/260 nm ratio to distinguish between protein and nucleic acid species. Additionally, the study sought to flag peaks occurring in regions with missing fraction identifiers and detect multivariate outliers to ensure data integrity.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a three-step sequential plan. Step 1 involved baseline correction and peak detection. Raw time-series data were grouped by run and column. Negative `volume_ml` values were utilized to estimate baseline noise (mean and standard deviation) before being excluded from peak integration. Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) was applied to the `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` signals to remove drift and sensor artifacts. Peaks were detected using `scipy.signal.find_peaks` with parameters set to a minimum height of 3x baseline standard deviation, a minimum distance of 5 data points, and a prominence of 2x baseline standard deviation. Peaks lacking a `Fraction_unique_ID` were flagged as 'unassigned', and the 280/260 ratio was calculated for each detected peak. Step 2 focused on gradient-based cross-run alignment. Detected peaks were mapped to their corresponding `Conc_B_%` values. Adaptive binning was used to group peaks by concentration, and compounds were ranked by total integrated area and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR). Step 3 performed empirical ratio thresholding and outlier detection. Data points with non-positive 260 nm absorbance were filtered. The 1st and 99th percentiles of the 280/260 ratio distribution were calculated to define thresholds for 'Nucleic Acid' and 'Protein' classifications, respectively, with the remainder classified as 'Mixed'. Multivariate outlier detection was performed using an Isolation Forest algorithm on the log-transformed intensity, ratio, and gradient concentration.

#Results and Discussion 

The application of ALS baseline correction successfully flattened the baseline, eliminating negative drift artifacts and revealing distinct positive peaks across the elution volume range (Figure 1). The gradient profiles for all 11 runs demonstrated high consistency in the step-wise increase of Buffer B concentration, validating the use of `Conc_B_%` as a robust normalization factor (Figure 2). However, the gradient-based alignment revealed a critical data characteristic: the signal intensity was overwhelmingly dominated by the 0% `Conc_B_%` range (flow-through), which accounted for approximately 1.19 billion mAU*min with a high average SNR of 25.80 (Figure 3). In contrast, elution ranges between 3% and 49% showed drastically lower intensities and poor reproducibility, with most bins represented by only 1 or 2 runs. This suggests that the majority of the detected signal in the 0% range may represent baseline drift or uncorrected system noise rather than specific eluting compounds, or that the gradient elution was ineffective in separating significant quantities of material. 

Regarding compound classification, the empirical thresholding of the 280/260 ratio (1st percentile: 0.4010; 99th percentile: 3.0951) classified approximately 98% of the 942,571 data points as 'Mixed' (Figure 4). Only 9,425 points were classified as pure 'Protein' and an equal number as 'Nucleic Acid'. This high proportion of mixed species indicates significant spectral overlap or co-elution of compounds, rather than a dataset dominated by pure fractions. The 0% range exhibited a low average ratio of 0.40, consistent with nucleic acid-rich or non-protein baseline signals, while mid-range elutions (e.g., 13-33%) showed higher ratios (~0.70-0.73), indicative of protein presence. Multivariate outlier detection identified anomalies primarily at extreme volume values, including negative volumes corresponding to the pre-run equilibration phase, confirming that these points represent sensor noise rather than biological anomalies. While the methodology successfully isolated peaks and aligned runs, the dominance of the 0% signal and the prevalence of 'Mixed' classifications highlight the need for further validation of the gradient elution efficiency and the potential limitations of using scan-level data for pure compound identification.

#Conclusion 

This analysis successfully implemented a robust pipeline for processing complex chromatographic data, overcoming challenges related to baseline drift and missing metadata. The ALS baseline correction effectively isolated true signal peaks, and the gradient-based alignment confirmed the reproducibility of the elution process across 11 runs. However, the results highlight a significant skew in the data, where the flow-through phase (0% `Conc_B_%`) dominates the signal intensity, potentially masking the elution of specific compounds. Furthermore, the classification of compounds revealed that the vast majority of the dataset consists of mixed species, with very few pure protein or nucleic acid fractions identified. The multivariate outlier detection effectively flagged pre-run artifacts, ensuring data quality. Future work should focus on refining the gradient elution parameters to improve separation in the 3-49% range and investigating the nature of the dominant 0% signal to distinguish between baseline artifacts and genuine flow-through compounds. Despite these limitations, the established workflow provides a solid foundation for run-level compound profiling and quality control in chromatographic analysis.
