#Abstract 

This report details the automated analysis of high-frequency High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) UV time-series data to identify and classify trace impurities. The workflow processed over 1.05 million data points from 11 chromatography runs, employing Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction and statistical peak detection to distinguish genuine contaminants from sensor noise. While the methodology successfully isolated major protein impurities in the top intensity peaks, a significant discrepancy was observed in the aggregate classification, where the majority of detected 'impurities' were flagged as nucleic acids despite low signal intensity. This suggests potential over-sensitivity of the UV ratio classification threshold to baseline noise in the 260 nm channel. The analysis validates the detection of trace signals but highlights the need for refined noise filtering to prevent false positives in nucleic acid identification.

#Introduction 

The rigorous processing of HPLC UV data is critical for ensuring product safety and quality compliance in biopharmaceutical manufacturing. The primary objective of this analysis was to distinguish trace impurities and degradation products from baseline drift and sensor artifacts within a dataset containing approximately 1.1 million rows. A significant challenge in this workflow was the absence of ~76% of sample-specific metadata, necessitating the inference of chromatography stages (Load, Wash, Elute) based on heuristic rules derived from buffer concentration gradients. Furthermore, the data exhibited critical quality issues, including pre-injection artifacts with negative volumes and negative UV offsets indicating baseline drift. The analysis aimed to implement a robust pipeline involving data sanitization, AsLS baseline correction, local noise estimation, and UV ratio-based classification to generate a validated list of potential impurities mapped to critical process zones.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three primary steps. First, data sanitization was performed by filtering extreme outliers (volume < -1.0 mL) and excluding irrelevant columns, resulting in a final dataset of 1,056,044 rows. Chromatography stages were inferred using `Conc_B_%` thresholds: 'Load' (<5%), 'Elute' (>20%), and 'Wash' (intermediate). Second, Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction was applied to UV 280 nm and 260 nm signals using lambda=1e6 and p=0.001 to remove drift while preserving minor peaks. Local noise was estimated using a rolling window of 50 points, and peaks exceeding 3 times the local standard deviation were detected. Contaminants were classified based on the UV 280/260 ratio at the peak apex: ratios >1.8 were classified as 'Nucleic_Acid_Contaminant', while others were labeled 'Protein_Impurity'. Third, a critical impurity report was generated, prioritizing findings in the 'Elute' stage or those classified as nucleic acids. Visualization was performed on a single run selected for high peak density to validate the detection algorithm.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully processed 11 chromatography runs, correctly categorizing the majority of data into 'Load' (66%), 'Elute' (28%), and 'Wash' (6%) stages. A total of 2,104 peaks were identified exceeding the 3-sigma local noise threshold. As illustrated in Figure 1, the AsLS baseline correction effectively removed negative offsets, allowing the signal to hover near zero during non-eluting phases. The 3-sigma threshold line served as a statistical gate, clearly distinguishing detected peaks from background noise. In the visualized run, peaks are color-coded to indicate classification: red points denote 'Nucleic_Acid_Contaminant' and blue points denote 'Protein_Impurity'.

However, a critical discrepancy emerged when comparing the top intensity peaks with the aggregate findings. The top 20 peaks by height were predominantly classified as 'Protein_Impurity' with UV 280/260 ratios between 0.70 and 0.93, indicating a lack of nucleic acid contamination in the most significant signals. Conversely, the aggregate 'Critical Impurity Report' flagged 946,447 critical findings, with 654,020 classified as 'Nucleic_Acid' and 292,427 as 'Unknown'. This suggests that while the algorithm effectively isolates major protein impurities, the classification logic is over-sensitive to noise in the 260 nm channel. The high volume of nucleic acid classifications likely stems from the UV ratio threshold (>1.8) being triggered by baseline drift or noise in the 260 nm channel where signal intensity is lower, rather than representing true contaminants. Consequently, while Figure 1 validates the visual detection of trace impurities, the operational context regarding the true nature of the vast majority of 'nucleic acid' flags requires further validation of the classification threshold to distinguish true contaminants from artifacts.

#Conclusion 

The implemented data analysis workflow successfully demonstrated the capability to sanitize large-scale HPLC datasets, infer missing process metadata, and detect trace impurities using statistical thresholds. The visualization in Figure 1 confirms that the AsLS baseline correction and 3-sigma peak detection logic are effective in isolating minor peaks from background noise. However, the analysis revealed a significant limitation in the contaminant classification strategy. The overwhelming number of 'Nucleic_Acid' classifications in the aggregate data, contrasted with the protein-dominated top intensity peaks, indicates that the current UV ratio threshold is prone to false positives driven by noise in the 260 nm channel. Future iterations of this workflow must refine the classification logic, potentially by incorporating signal-to-noise ratios or dynamic thresholds, to ensure that critical impurity reports accurately reflect true product risks rather than sensor artifacts.
