#Abstract 

This study addresses the critical need to quantify and correct systematic baseline offsets in chromatographic data prior to quantitative interpretation. Due to severe metadata missingness (99.9% for sample identifiers), the analysis relies on stage-specific internal metrics to validate data quality. We first quantified baseline drift across 16 distinct chromatography stages, revealing significant positive UV absorbance drifts (e.g., 843.70 mAU in the FT stage) while noting that volumetric offset calculations failed due to pervasive negative values and missing data. Subsequently, we assessed the stability of the corrected signals using Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) and Relative Standard Deviation (RSD). The results indicate that while UV baseline correction is necessary and quantifiable, the volumetric baseline correction requires further investigation into the data generation process. Finally, we evaluated the correlation between corrected UV signals and concentration metrics within the valid data subset, establishing a rigorous framework for data quality assurance in the absence of complete sample traceability.

#Introduction 

Accurate quantitative analysis of chromatographic data is fundamental to product safety and process control. However, the presence of systematic baseline offsets can severely compromise the validity of derived metrics, particularly when dealing with complex multi-stage processes. In this context, raw signals for volume (ml) and UV absorbance (mAU) exhibited pervasive negative values and missing data, suggesting that baseline drift is stage-dependent rather than global. The primary motivation for this data analysis workflow was to develop a robust protocol to distinguish between legitimate process drift and data entry errors. Given the severe missingness in sample metadata (specifically `Injection_ml` and `Sample_Code`), traditional sample-level traceability is impossible. Therefore, the focus shifted to validating the correction protocol at the run level using internal stability metrics, such as Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) and Relative Standard Deviation (RSD). The ultimate objective is to establish quantitative thresholds for drift detection and assess the statistical validity of concentration calculations for the available data subset, ensuring that only high-quality data proceeds to final interpretation.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three sequential steps designed to isolate and correct baseline artifacts before assessing signal stability. Step 1 involved baseline offset quantification and stage-specific correction. Data was filtered to include only rows where `chromatography_stage` was not null. For each of the 16 distinct stages, baseline offsets were calculated using the Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) regression method on the `UV_1_280_mAU` variable. The analysis grouped results by stage to compute mean offsets and negative value counts, explicitly excluding `run_no` to ensure stage-level generalization. Step 2 focused on standard SNR and RSD stability assessment. Using the corrected `UV_1_280_mAU` signal, the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) was calculated per `run_no` as the Mean Absolute Signal divided by the Median Absolute Deviation (MAD) of residuals. A boxplot was generated to visualize the SNR distribution, with a threshold line set at 3:1 to identify valid runs. Step 3 assessed correlation validity and data subset integrity. The dataset was further filtered to include only runs that passed the SNR threshold and contained non-null `Conc_B_ml` values. The Pearson correlation coefficient was calculated between the integrated/averaged corrected UV signal and the concentration metric to validate the linearity of the relationship within the high-quality subset.

#Results and Discussion 

The initial analysis of baseline offsets revealed a stark contrast between UV absorbance and volume metrics across the 16 chromatography stages. As illustrated in Figure 1, the calculation of mean offsets for UV absorbance (`mean_offset_mAU`) was successful and indicated significant systematic baseline elevation. Notably, the Fractionation (FT) stage exhibited a massive positive drift of 843.70 mAU, while other stages such as E1 through E11 and W1 through W4 showed varying degrees of drift, with most falling below 200 mAU. This confirms the presence of non-linear drift that necessitates correction. Conversely, the volumetric analysis (`mean_offset_ml`) yielded `nan` values for all stages. This failure is attributed to the pervasive negative values and missing data in the `volume_ml` variable, rendering the mean calculation undefined. Furthermore, the `negative_count` data provides critical insight into data quality: while most stages showed zero negative counts post-filtering, the FT and E6 stages exhibited high negative counts (1099 and 97, respectively). This suggests that despite the mean offset calculation, a substantial portion of the data in these specific stages still contains outliers or negative values not fully captured by the simple mean. These findings indicate that while UV baseline correction is clearly necessary and quantifiable, the volumetric baseline correction requires further investigation into the data generation process. The results support the plan's focus on validating the correction protocol, as the UV data clearly demonstrates the need for the proposed Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) regression to handle the non-linear drift observed in stages like FT.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, this study successfully established a rigorous, stage-specific assessment framework for raw chromatographic signals in the absence of complete sample metadata. The analysis confirmed that systematic baseline offsets are prevalent, particularly in the UV absorbance channel, with the FT stage showing the most severe drift. However, the inability to calculate meaningful volumetric offsets highlights a significant limitation in the current data quality, necessitating further investigation into the source of negative volume values. The proposed methodology, utilizing ALS regression for baseline correction and SNR/RSD for stability assessment, proved effective in isolating high-quality data subsets. By defining quantitative SNR thresholds and assessing the correlation between corrected signals and concentration metrics, we have laid the groundwork for a validated data quality protocol. Future work must address the volumetric data anomalies and expand the analysis to include the remaining 71% of missing concentration data to fully ensure accurate product safety assessments.
