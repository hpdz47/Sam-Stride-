#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic separation performance to ensure product purity and safety. The primary objective was to identify runs where peak overlap exceeds acceptable thresholds, specifically where resolution ($R_s$) drops below 1.5. Due to data limitations, including 11.5% missing precise volume data and 75% missing chromatography stage records, a rigorous preprocessing pipeline was implemented. This involved filtering negative volume anomalies, applying median imputation for missing volumes, and restricting the final efficiency trending analysis to the 25% of records with complete stage data to mitigate bias. The analysis utilized instantaneous flow rate conversion to standardize metrics and defined peak neighbors via signal clustering. Results indicate a consistent degradation in separation efficiency over time across all stages, with Stage 3 exhibiting the most severe decline.

#Introduction 

In biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the chromatographic separation process is critical for isolating target molecules with high purity. Quantitative metrics such as peak resolution ($R_s$) and separation efficiency ($N$) are essential indicators of process health. However, data integrity issues, such as synchronization errors resulting in negative volumes and significant missing data points, can compromise the accuracy of these metrics. This study addresses these challenges by establishing a robust data analysis workflow. The focus area is defined as the evaluation of UV absorbance signals and fraction volumes to determine if neighboring peaks are adequately resolved. The analysis specifically targets the identification of process stages and runs where separation efficiency is compromised, aiming to prevent the release of impure products. By stratifying the analysis by chromatography stage and restricting it to complete datasets, this report provides a reliable assessment of the system's performance trends over time.

#Methodology 

The methodology followed a three-step analytical plan designed to handle data anomalies and calculate resolution metrics accurately. First, data preprocessing involved filtering records with negative volume values to remove synchronization errors. Missing precise volume data, affecting 11.5% of records, was addressed using median imputation within run groups to preserve local data structure. Second, peak detection and resolution calculation were performed using signal clustering (Asymmetric Least Squares) on UV absorbance signals. Resolution ($R_s$) and separation efficiency ($N$) were calculated for neighboring peaks, defined as those separated by a distance greater than twice the median peak width. Instantaneous flow rates were used to convert volume-based metrics into time-based units for standardization. Third, the analysis was stratified by chromatography stage. To avoid bias from the 75% missing stage data, the 'Stratified Efficiency Trending' was restricted to only the 25% of records with complete stage data, ensuring a minimum sample size of 50 records per stage. This ensured that the observed trends were robust and not skewed by incomplete data.

#Results and Discussion 

The primary result, illustrated in Figure 1, presents a line chart titled 'Stratified Efficiency Trending by Chromatography Stage,' plotting 'Average Rs' against 'Run No' for four distinct chromatography stages. The data reveals a consistent decline in peak resolution as the run number increases across all stages, indicating a systematic loss of separation efficiency over time. This degradation is likely attributable to column fouling or reagent depletion. A critical observation is that Stage 3 exhibits the most severe degradation, with resolution dropping below the critical threshold of $R_s = 1.5$. This threshold is a standard benchmark for adequate peak separation; values below this indicate significant peak overlap, which could compromise product purity. The secondary axis for Separation Efficiency ($N$) is present but lacks visible annotation, making it difficult to confirm if the decline in $R_s$ is accompanied by a proportional drop in $N$. The clustering of points for specific runs suggests that resolution is highly dependent on specific run conditions, which the stratification by stage attempts to isolate. While the methodology successfully filtered negative volume anomalies and restricted the analysis to complete stage data to mitigate bias, the reliance on instantaneous flow rate conversion and the exclusion of 75% of stage records limit the generalizability of the trend to the entire process lifecycle. Nevertheless, the trend clearly identifies Stage 3 as a critical area requiring immediate process intervention to prevent the production of non-compliant material.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the data analysis successfully identified a systematic decline in chromatographic separation efficiency over time, with Stage 3 showing critical performance issues where resolution falls below the acceptable threshold of 1.5. The methodology, which included rigorous data cleaning, median imputation, and stratification by complete stage data, provided a reliable assessment of the process despite significant data gaps. The observed trends suggest that column fouling or reagent depletion is the primary driver of this efficiency loss. Immediate attention is required to address the performance of Stage 3 to ensure product quality and safety. Future work should investigate the specific causes of the resolution drop in Stage 3 and explore methods to extend the operational life of the columns or reagents to maintain separation efficiency across all stages.
