#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic separation performance derived from a long-form time-series dataset containing UV absorbance signals and fraction volume data. The primary objective was to identify runs and process stages where peak overlap exceeds acceptable thresholds ($R_s < 1.5$), which could compromise product purity. A critical limitation of the dataset was identified: 11.5% of records lacked precise volume boundaries, and 75% of records were missing `chromatography_stage` metadata. To address data quality, a preprocessing pipeline was implemented involving the filtering of negative volume anomalies and the application of median imputation for missing volume data within run groups. The analysis methodology included signal clustering for peak detection, instantaneous flow rate conversion to standardize metrics, and the calculation of resolution ($R_s$) and separation efficiency ($N$). Due to the significant data gaps, the 'Run-to-Run Efficiency Trending' was strictly restricted to the 25% of records possessing complete `chromatography_stage` data to prevent bias. Results indicate that while resolution metrics were successfully calculated and stratified, the absence of error bars limits the assessment of stability. Furthermore, the exclusion of the majority of stage data means the observed trends are inherently biased toward specific process stages that had complete metadata, preventing a comprehensive assessment of the full process lifecycle.

#Introduction 

In biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the chromatographic purification steps are critical for ensuring the purity and safety of the final product. Quantitative metrics such as peak resolution ($R_s$), separation efficiency ($N$), and selectivity ($\alpha$) are essential for determining whether neighboring peaks are adequately resolved. This analysis focuses on a dataset comprising UV absorbance signals (`UV_1_280_mAU`, `UV_2_260_mAU`) and associated fraction volumes to evaluate separation performance across multiple chromatography stages. The primary motivation for this analysis was to identify specific runs or process stages where peak overlap exceeds the critical threshold of $R_s = 1.5$, indicating a potential loss of separation efficiency that could compromise product quality. However, the data presented significant challenges, including synchronization errors resulting in negative volume values and substantial missing data regarding precise fraction volumes and chromatography stages. This report outlines the methodology employed to handle these data quality issues and the resulting findings regarding the stability and consistency of the chromatographic process.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a three-step methodology designed to ensure data integrity and accurate metric calculation. First, data preprocessing and imputation were performed on the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset. Records with negative volume values (`volume_ml` < 0 or `fraction_volume_ml_min_precise` < 0) were filtered out to remove synchronization errors. For the 11.5% of records with missing precise volume data, median imputation was applied within `run_no` groups to preserve the local data structure, as opposed to KNN imputation. Second, peak detection and resolution calculation were executed. Peak maxima were identified using signal clustering (Asymmetric Least Squares) on the `UV_1_280_mAU` signal. Records with missing precise volumes were excluded from peak width calculations, utilizing the average of adjacent complete records as a fallback. Neighboring peaks were defined as those separated by a distance greater than 2x the median peak width. Resolution ($R_s$) and separation efficiency ($N$) were calculated for these neighbors and aggregated by `run_no` and `column`. Third, stratified efficiency trending was conducted. The analysis was stratified by `chromatography_stage`, but strictly restricted to the 25% of records with complete stage metadata to avoid bias. A minimum sample size threshold of 50 records per stage was enforced, and efficiency metrics were aggregated by `run_no` and `column` before plotting.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis yielded visualizations that confirm the successful calculation of resolution metrics but highlight significant limitations regarding data completeness and stability assessment. Figure 1 presents the 'Stratified Efficiency Trending by Chromatography Stage,' plotting 'Average Rs' against 'Run No.' The chart displays distinct lines for different chromatography stages, confirming the stratification logic. However, the visual evidence reveals a critical methodological inconsistency: the dataset report indicates that 75% of `chromatography_stage` records are missing, leaving only 25% for analysis. Consequently, the visible lines represent trends restricted to these complete records, as intended to avoid bias. While the Y-axis shows Resolution values fluctuating, the absence of the 75% missing data in the plot does not resolve the underlying data quality issue; the results are inherently biased toward the specific stages that happened to have complete volume and stage metadata. The high variance in UV signals noted in the dataset context likely contributes to the observed fluctuations in Rs, but without the full dataset, the true stability of the process across all stages cannot be confirmed. Figure 2, titled 'Peak Resolution by Column and Run,' visualizes chromatographic separation performance with data points color-coded by 'Column' and annotated to mark points where Rs < 1.5, indicating unresolved peaks. The visual evidence confirms that resolution metrics were successfully calculated and stratified by both run and column, correctly identifying runs with inadequate separation. However, the plot lacks explicit error bars or shaded regions to represent the variance within each run, which is critical for assessing the stability of separation efficiency. Furthermore, while the plan mandated restricting analysis to the 25% of records with complete `chromatography_stage` data, the figure does not visually indicate this stratification or the exclusion of the remaining 75% of stage data, making it impossible to verify if the plotted trends are biased toward specific process stages. The presence of annotated points below the Rs = 1.5 threshold confirms the analysis correctly identified runs with inadequate separation, but the absence of confidence intervals or inter-run variability metrics limits the robustness of the conclusions regarding process consistency.

#Conclusion 

This analysis successfully quantified chromatographic separation performance, identifying specific runs with inadequate resolution ($R_s < 1.5$) and stratifying efficiency metrics by column and chromatography stage. The methodology effectively handled data quality issues through filtering of negative anomalies and median imputation of missing volume data. However, the findings are constrained by significant data gaps. The exclusion of 75% of the `chromatography_stage` data means the 'Run-to-Run Efficiency Trending' is biased toward a specific subset of the process lifecycle, preventing a comprehensive assessment of overall process stability. Additionally, the lack of visual indicators for missing data and the absence of error bars in the resolution plots limit the robustness of conclusions regarding process consistency. Future analyses should prioritize the recovery of missing `chromatography_stage` metadata and the inclusion of variance metrics (e.g., confidence intervals) to provide a more accurate and unbiased evaluation of chromatographic performance across the entire process.
