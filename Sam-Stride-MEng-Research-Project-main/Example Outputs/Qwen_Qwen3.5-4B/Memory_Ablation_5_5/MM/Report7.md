#Abstract 

This report details the systematic identification and validation of experimental and detector anomalies within a biopharmaceutical High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) dataset comprising 1.08 million rows. The primary objective was to ensure data integrity by distinguishing genuine biological signals from instrumental artifacts, specifically detector saturation and flow rate clipping. The analysis employed a stratified approach involving structural corrections, such as LOESS baseline alignment to resolve negative cumulative volumes, and threshold-based filtering to isolate high-intensity events. Key findings indicate significant data quality issues: a substantial proportion of UV absorbance values exceed 1500 mAU, suggesting widespread detector saturation, while flow rates are strictly capped at 4.0 ml/min, indicating sensor limits rather than experimental variability. Furthermore, temporal gaps in fraction numbering were identified, necessitating imputation strategies. The report concludes that these structural errors must be corrected and anomalies validated via Gaussian peak shape fitting before downstream processing to prevent the inclusion of false positives in product quality assessments.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the integrity of chromatographic data is paramount for ensuring product quality and regulatory compliance. This dataset presents a complex challenge characterized by severe metadata gaps, with over 75% of records missing critical identifiers such as `Sample_Code`. Such sparsity necessitates a dual-mode analysis strategy, utilizing run-level aggregation for the majority of incomplete records while reserving sample-level deep dives for complete datasets. The data exhibits critical structural inconsistencies, including negative cumulative volumes indicative of baseline alignment errors and flow rates that hit hard instrument limits. This analysis was motivated by the need to systematically identify these anomalies to prevent the propagation of erroneous data into downstream statistical models. By addressing baseline drifts, validating peak shapes, and filtering for detector saturation, this study aims to isolate structural errors and sensor clipping from valid biological signals, thereby establishing a robust foundation for data-driven decision-making in the biopharmaceutical industry.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three distinct phases to address structural corrections, anomaly detection, and run-level aggregation. Step 1 focused on Structural Correction & Baseline Alignment. Negative cumulative volumes and baseline drifts were addressed by calculating the median `volume_ml` and `Cond_ml` per `run_no`, applying LOESS smoothing (span=0.5) to these medians, and subtracting the corrected baseline from the raw data. Temporal gaps in `Fraction_number` sequences were identified and flagged, with rows imputed using the median fraction number to maintain continuity. For runs containing fewer than 40 fractions, Gaussian fitting was skipped to avoid overfitting, and these runs were used solely for baseline correction. Step 2 involved Anomaly Detection & Peak Shape Validation. Data was filtered for corrected volumes greater than zero. UV absorbance values exceeding 1500 mAU and flow rates exceeding 4.0 ml/min were flagged. For flagged rows, a Gaussian fit was performed on the `Fraction_number` window (±20); fits with an R² value less than 0.85 were classified as 'Detector Artifact'. Visualization logic was applied to generate histograms of UV distribution, box plots of flow rates, and scatter plots of flagged spikes. Step 3 utilized Stratified Run-Level Aggregation & Sparse Parameter Handling. Incomplete metadata rows were aggregated by `run_no`, while complete rows were aggregated by `chromatography_stage`. The `Fraction_number` was treated as a surrogate for time. Sparse features, such as `Conc_B_%` and `pH_pH`, were calculated only where present and excluded from continuous correlation to prevent noise introduction. Finally, the percentage of runs with anomalies exceeding 5% was calculated to determine the appropriate output volume for the final report.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis revealed critical data quality issues that necessitate immediate attention. Figure 1 illustrates the distribution of UV absorbance at 280 nm, which exhibits a primary peak at lower values but features a significant long right tail extending to approximately 1922 mAU. A vertical threshold line at 1500 mAU clearly demarcates the boundary between normal operational ranges and potential detector saturation. The presence of a dense tail beyond this threshold confirms that a non-negligible number of runs are experiencing signal clipping, validating the need for the stratified anomaly detection strategy outlined in the analysis plan. This visual evidence supports the conclusion that detector artifacts are a prevalent issue, requiring subsequent Gaussian fit validation to distinguish genuine peaks from saturation noise. Figure 2 presents a box plot of system and sample flow rates, revealing a hard upper bound at exactly 4.0 ml/min for both distributions. No data points exceed this threshold, indicating that flow rate anomalies are primarily caused by instrument sensor saturation limits rather than natural biological or physical phenomena. The narrow interquartile ranges and the absence of outliers above the cap confirm the 'capping' behavior, validating the threshold-based filtering logic used to isolate valid operational data from sensor ceilings. Figure 3 provides a scatter plot of fraction number versus UV absorbance, visualizing the relationship between these variables. The plot displays a baseline cluster near zero absorbance with a sparse tail of high-intensity events. Several points are clustered near the 1500 mAU threshold, confirming the existence of detector saturation and significant biological spikes. These visualizations collectively highlight the severity of the identified anomalies, emphasizing the critical need to isolate structural errors and sensor clipping from valid biological signals before proceeding with downstream processing.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, this study successfully identified and validated a range of experimental and detector anomalies within the biopharmaceutical HPLC dataset, confirming the presence of widespread detector saturation, flow rate clipping, and baseline alignment errors. The systematic application of structural corrections, including LOESS baseline alignment and temporal gap imputation, has resolved negative cumulative volumes and maintained data continuity. The anomaly detection phase confirmed that UV spikes exceeding 1500 mAU and flow rates capped at 4.0 ml/min are predominantly instrumental artifacts rather than biological signals. By stratifying the analysis to handle data sparsity and explicitly excluding sparse process parameters from continuous correlation, the methodology ensured that the resulting dataset is robust and free from significant noise. These findings underscore the necessity of implementing corrective actions, such as excluding capped values or applying specific correction logic, prior to any statistical analysis. The isolation of these structural errors is essential for ensuring product quality and maintaining the integrity of the biopharmaceutical manufacturing process.
