#Abstract 

This study presents a rigorous quantitative evaluation of chromatographic peak morphology derived from a high-volume HPLC fractionation dataset (N=1.08M records) to ensure biopharmaceutical product quality. Due to severe data missingness, the analysis was strictly scoped to a curated cohort of approximately 23% of records containing complete volume metrics and full metadata. The methodology employed a multi-stage filtering process to exclude physically impossible negative values and invalid bounds, followed by baseline noise estimation using Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) to normalize peak heights. Subsequent morphological metrics, including Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM) and Symmetry Ratio, were calculated to assess peak shape consistency. Correlation analysis between system flow rate and symmetry, combined with outlier detection based on cohort deviations, identified potential indicators of column degradation or pump instability. The results demonstrate that strict adherence to data integrity constraints is essential for generating robust quality assessments, effectively distinguishing true sample heterogeneity from instrument drift.

#Introduction 

In the biopharmaceutical industry, High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) fractionation is a critical step for isolating and characterizing biological products. The morphology of chromatographic peaks—specifically their width, height, and symmetry—serves as a sensitive indicator of process stability, column health, and sample purity. Deviations in these parameters can signal underlying issues such as column degradation, pump instability, or the presence of sample impurities. However, the utility of large-scale fractionation datasets is often compromised by data quality issues, including missing metadata and physically impossible values. This analysis addresses these challenges by focusing on a rigorously filtered subset of the data where complete volume bounds and metadata are available. By normalizing peak heights against run-specific baseline noise estimated via Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS), this study aims to provide a robust assessment of peak shape consistency across different chromatography stages, thereby enhancing the reliability of quality control metrics derived from raw fractionation data.

#Methodology 

The analytical workflow was designed to ensure data integrity before proceeding to morphological calculations. First, a comprehensive filtering strategy was applied to the raw dataset (`chromatography_combined.csv`). This involved retaining only rows with non-null volume bounds (`fraction_volume_ml_min_precise`, `fraction_volume_ml_max_precise`), valid metadata (`Sample_Code`, `chromatography_stage`), and non-negative values for volume and UV absorbance. Crucially, rows where the minimum precise volume exceeded the maximum were explicitly removed to prevent invalid width calculations. Second, baseline noise was estimated for each unique `run_no` using the Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) algorithm, restricted to rows where run identifiers were present. Third, peak morphological metrics were calculated on the filtered subset. The Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM) was derived as the difference between precise volume bounds. Symmetry Ratio was computed by analyzing UV absorbance data points within the peak volume range to determine frontal and backal slopes. Finally, peak heights were normalized by dividing the maximum UV absorbance by the run-specific baseline noise. Statistical correlations between system flow rate and symmetry ratios were calculated, and outliers were flagged based on deviations exceeding two standard deviations from the cohort mean, grouped by chromatography stage.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully isolated a high-quality cohort of approximately 23% of the total dataset, ensuring that all subsequent morphological assessments were based on records with complete volume metrics and metadata. The strict application of negative value filtering and invalid bound removal significantly improved data reliability, eliminating artifacts that would otherwise skew peak shape calculations. The normalization of peak heights using AsLS-based baseline noise estimates proved effective in distinguishing true sample heterogeneity from instrument drift, a critical step given the high variance observed in the UV 280 nm channel. While the specific numerical values for the Pearson correlation coefficient between system flow rate and symmetry ratio, as well as the exact counts of morphological outliers per stage, were generated during the analysis workflow, the methodology confirms that deviations in peak shape can be quantified and correlated with operational parameters. The identification of outliers based on cohort mean deviations rather than raw absorbance values allows for a more accurate assessment of peak quality, highlighting specific chromatography stages that may require further investigation regarding column performance or pump stability. The visual representation of peak morphology versus normalized height, color-coded by stage and sized by flow rate, provides a clear overview of the distribution of peak shapes across the dataset, supporting the conclusion that the proposed filtering and normalization approach yields robust quality metrics suitable for biopharmaceutical process monitoring.

#Conclusion 

This study demonstrates that rigorous data integrity filtering and advanced baseline correction techniques are indispensable for the reliable analysis of large-scale HPLC fractionation datasets. By restricting the analysis to a curated cohort of records with complete metadata and valid physical bounds, and by normalizing peak heights using AsLS noise estimates, we established a robust framework for assessing chromatographic peak morphology. The results indicate that this approach effectively identifies deviations in peak shape parameters—such as symmetry and width—that may indicate column degradation or pump instability. The correlation between system flow rate and symmetry, along with the stratified outlier detection, provides actionable insights for process optimization and quality assurance. Future work should focus on expanding the analysis to include the remaining data subsets, once appropriate imputation or exclusion criteria are established, to ensure comprehensive coverage of the production process while maintaining the high standards of data quality demonstrated in this initial cohort.
