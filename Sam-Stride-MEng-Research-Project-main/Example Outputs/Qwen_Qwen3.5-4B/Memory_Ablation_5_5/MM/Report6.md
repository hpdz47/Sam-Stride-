#Abstract 

This report details the quality assurance analysis of high-frequency time-series chromatographic data, specifically focusing on the integrity of the UV_1_280_mAU signal. The primary objective was to quantify Signal-to-Noise (SNR) ratios, assess baseline stability, and detect data artifacts such as sensor drift and negative volume outliers that could compromise product safety and process reliability. The analysis involved a rigorous preprocessing workflow including baseline correction, missing data filtering, and outlier identification. Results indicate that while the UV_1_280_mAU signal exhibits high completeness (~94%), significant intra-run noise variance and temporal drift were detected across the first five runs. Furthermore, specific runs were flagged as outliers due to excessive noise and the presence of negative volume_ml values exceeding -5 mL. These findings highlight critical data integrity issues requiring attention before downstream fraction collection and quality control decisions can be made.

#Introduction 

In the context of High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC), the accuracy of fraction collection and subsequent quality control decisions relies heavily on the stability and integrity of the chromatographic data. This analysis focuses on evaluating the quality of time-series data generated during a specific analytical run. The primary signal of interest, UV_1_280_mAU, was selected due to its superior data completeness (~94%) compared to the UV_2_260_mAU signal, which suffered from significant missingness (~70%). Critical process parameters, such as injection volume, were largely unavailable, necessitating a reliance on internal signal metrics for variability analysis. The main focus of this study was to identify excessive noise, sensor drift, and data artifacts—specifically negative baseline values and severe missingness—that could compromise the accuracy of the analysis. By quantifying the Signal-to-Noise (SNR) ratio and assessing baseline stability across available HPLC UV signals, this report aims to ensure product safety and process reliability.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three primary phases: Data Integrity & Preprocessing, Noise Variance Analysis & Drift Detection, and Outlier Run Identification. First, the raw data from 'chromatography_combined.csv' underwent preprocessing. Rows where 'Fraction_unique_ID' was missing were filtered out to prevent skewing metrics. Baseline correction was applied to the 'UV_1_280_mAU' variable, while 'UV_2_260_mAU' was explicitly excluded from general quality metrics. Negative 'volume_ml' values less than -5 mL were flagged. Second, noise variance was calculated as the standard deviation of baseline segments for each 'run'. Temporal drift was assessed by plotting noise variance against 'Fraction_number' for the first five runs sorted by 'run_no'. The Signal-to-Noise (SNR) ratio was calculated using the median peak height versus baseline noise. Finally, runs were identified as outliers if their intra-run noise variance exceeded the mean plus two standard deviations. These flagged runs were then cross-referenced with counts of negative 'volume_ml' outliers. All outputs were generated to provide a comprehensive view of data quality, including summary statistics, visualizations of noise trends, and tables of anomalous runs.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis revealed significant variations in data quality across the experimental runs. As illustrated in Figure 1, the top subplot displays the intra-run noise variance against the fraction number for the first five runs. The data indicates a clear temporal drift in noise levels, with variance increasing over time in several runs, suggesting sensor instability or baseline instability. The bottom subplot of Figure 1 presents a boxplot of the Signal-to-Noise (SNR) ratio grouped by run, highlighting substantial variability in signal quality. Runs with lower SNR values are associated with higher noise variance, directly impacting the reliability of peak detection and integration. Furthermore, the outlier identification phase flagged specific runs where the noise variance exceeded the statistical threshold (mean + 2*std). These flagged runs contained a notable number of negative 'volume_ml' values, indicating potential data acquisition errors or sensor saturation issues. The exclusion of 'UV_2_260_mAU' was justified by its high missingness rate, ensuring that the quality metrics were derived from a robust dataset. The baseline correction applied to 'UV_1_280_mAU' effectively stabilized the signal for the initial analysis, yet the presence of negative outliers and drift suggests that further calibration or data filtering may be required for downstream applications.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully evaluated the integrity of the chromatographic data, confirming that while the UV_1_280_mAU signal is the most reliable source due to high completeness, it is susceptible to temporal drift and increased noise variance over time. The identification of outlier runs characterized by high noise and negative volume artifacts underscores the necessity of strict data quality controls before fraction collection. The methodology employed, including baseline correction and variance-based outlier detection, provided a robust framework for assessing data reliability. Future work should focus on mitigating the identified sensor drift and investigating the causes of negative volume readings to ensure the long-term accuracy and safety of the analytical process.
