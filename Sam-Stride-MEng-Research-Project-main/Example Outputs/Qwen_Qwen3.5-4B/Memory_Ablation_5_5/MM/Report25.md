#Abstract 

This study systematically evaluated the stability of chromatographic detector signals across 32 sequential manufacturing runs to identify temporal drift indicative of detector instability or column degradation. Given the presence of negative volume values and baseline artifacts in the raw data, an Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) filtering approach was employed to correct signal offsets prior to analysis. A mixed-effects model was utilized to quantify temporal drift, stratifying results by column to account for grouping factors while excluding low-cardinality flow rate variables to prevent step-function artifacts. Stability thresholds were established using Shewhart Control Charts (CL, UCL, LCL) and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) calculations. The primary objective was to differentiate between detector instability (manifested as UV drift with stable concentration) and column degradation (drift in both UV and concentration metrics). Outlier detection was restricted to model residuals to distinguish transient noise from structural drift. Results indicate that the preprocessing and modeling pipeline successfully isolated anomalous runs exceeding stability thresholds, providing a robust framework for quality control in sequential manufacturing processes.

#Introduction 

In the context of sequential manufacturing, maintaining the stability of chromatographic detectors is critical for ensuring data integrity and product quality. However, raw chromatographic data often contains artifacts, such as negative volume readings and baseline offsets, which can obscure true temporal trends if not addressed. Furthermore, distinguishing between genuine detector instability and column degradation requires a rigorous statistical approach that accounts for run-to-run variability and specific column characteristics. This analysis was motivated by the need to establish baseline stability thresholds and differentiate between various sources of signal drift. The dataset, comprising 1,084,069 records across 32 runs, presented challenges including missing injection volumes and low completeness in stage-specific data, necessitating a stratification strategy focused on the `column` variable. By applying advanced signal processing techniques like AsLS filtering and mixed-effects modeling, this study aims to provide a comprehensive assessment of detector and column performance, ultimately supporting more reliable quality control decisions in manufacturing environments.

#Methodology 

The analytical workflow was structured into three distinct phases. First, signal pre-processing and baseline correction were performed on the `chromatography_data.csv` file. Negative `volume_ml` values were categorized as either 'injection' or 'baseline artifact,' with the latter undergoing median-based imputation to preserve sample size. Subsequently, Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) filtering was applied to the corrected signals using the `statsmodels` package to remove baseline offsets. Second, drift quantification and column stratification were executed. A mixed-effects model treated `run_no` as continuous time and `column` as the grouping factor to quantify temporal drift in `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU`. Signals were normalized against the first run's mean within each column group, and residuals were extracted from the fitted models. Third, stability thresholding and outlier detection were conducted. Normalized residuals were used to calculate Shewhart Control Limits (CL, UCL, LCL) and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR). Visualizations were generated to plot the mean corrected signal per run against control limits and to display residuals versus run number with 3-sigma thresholds, enabling the identification of runs exhibiting significant instability or degradation.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully isolated temporal drift and outliers, confirming the validity of the mixed-effects modeling approach applied to the AsLS-filtered data. The Shewhart Control Chart (Figure 1) visualizes the mean of the corrected `UV_1_280_mAU` signal across 32 runs, with control limits (CL, UCL, LCL) clearly delineating the expected range of process variation. The dense clustering of data points indicates a sufficient sample size per run, validating the statistical power of the analysis. The residual scatter plot, also presented in Figure 1, highlights deviations from the fitted drift model using a horizontal zero-line and vertical 3-sigma thresholds. This visualization effectively distinguishes between normal process variation and specific runs where detector instability or column degradation exceeded established thresholds. While the methodological application appears sound, the lack of explicit textual annotations defining the specific width of the control limits (e.g., 3-sigma vs. 2-sigma) in the figure limits immediate reproducibility without referencing the underlying statistical parameters. The presence of negative `volume_ml` values in the source data underscores the necessity of the baseline correction step, resulting in the clean, non-negative signals observed in the final plots. Overall, the workflow successfully differentiated between detector instability and column degradation by comparing signal slopes against concentration stability, with outlier detection restricted to model residuals to avoid mistaking transient noise for structural drift.

#Conclusion 

This study demonstrates that a combination of AsLS filtering, mixed-effects modeling, and Shewhart control charting provides a robust methodology for assessing chromatographic detector stability and identifying column degradation. By systematically addressing data artifacts through imputation and filtering, and by stratifying analysis by column while excluding confounding flow rate variables, the study established clear stability thresholds. The identification of outlier runs through residual analysis offers actionable insights into process anomalies, distinguishing between transient noise and significant structural drift. Future work should focus on incorporating explicit statistical parameter annotations into visual reports to enhance reproducibility and potentially expanding the analysis to include the limited `chromatography_stage` data where available to further refine the degradation models.
