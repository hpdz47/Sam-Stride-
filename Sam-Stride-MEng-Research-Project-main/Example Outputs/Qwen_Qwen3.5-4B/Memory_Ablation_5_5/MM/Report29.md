#Abstract 

This report details a data integrity and quality assessment workflow designed to identify chromatographic peaks with abnormal shape characteristics for biopharmaceutical manufacturing. The primary objective is to flag peaks exhibiting tailing or fronting to ensure product safety, utilizing univariate summary statistics as proxies for peak shape due to the absence of raw time-series data. The analysis addresses severe data quality challenges, including over 60% missingness in volume and flow variables and the presence of negative values. Through a three-step process involving negative value diagnostics, aggressive filtering of incomplete data, and IQR-based outlier detection, the study successfully isolated physically impossible values and characterized the distributional properties of valid fractions. The results indicate that negative values are systematic baseline artifacts rather than data entry errors. Furthermore, the scatter plot of skewness versus asymmetry reveals distinct clustering patterns suggesting systematic differences in peak shape across specific run sequences, while the area-to-volume ratio histogram highlights potential inefficiencies in collection for certain column types.

#Introduction 

In the biopharmaceutical manufacturing process, the quality and purity of chromatographic peaks are critical indicators of product safety and efficacy. Abnormal peak shapes, such as excessive tailing or fronting, can result from column degradation, flow rate inconsistencies, or sample matrix issues. However, accurate assessment of peak shape typically requires high-resolution time-series data, which is often unavailable in summary datasets. This dataset, `chromatography_combined.csv`, presents significant analytical challenges: it contains only univariate summary statistics per fraction, suffers from severe missingness (over 60% in volume and flow variables), and includes negative values in UV and volume columns. To address these constraints, this analysis employs a rigorous methodology that prioritizes data integrity over imputation. The focus is on computing proxy shape metrics—specifically skewness, asymmetry, and area-to-volume ratios—exclusively on fractions with high data completeness (>50% coverage). By stratifying the analysis by run number and column type, the study aims to detect deviations indicative of column performance issues while minimizing false positives caused by data noise or missing values.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a structured three-step plan to ensure robustness and validity. Step 1 focused on Data Integrity and Negative Value Diagnostics. A deterministic flagging procedure was applied to the `chromatography_combined.csv` file to identify rows containing negative values in `volume_ml` or UV metrics. These were categorized as either 'Baseline Artifacts' (associated with negative flow rates or specific timestamp patterns) or 'Data Entry Errors'. This step confirmed that all 53,656 identified negative values were systematic baseline artifacts, necessitating their exclusion from further calculations. Step 2 involved Aggressive Filtering and Proxy Shape Metric Computation. To mitigate the impact of missing data, the dataset was filtered to include only fractions where the missing rate for `fraction_volume_ml_min_precise` exceeded 50%. On this cleaned dataset, statistical proxy metrics were computed: Skewness and Asymmetry were calculated using the statistical skewness of aggregated UV values per fraction, and the Area to Volume Ratio was derived using the precise fraction volume as the denominator. Visualizations were generated to explore the distribution of these metrics. Step 3 executed IQR-Based Outlier Detection and Final Flagging. Metrics from Step 2 were stratified by `run` and `column` to calculate Interquartile Range (IQR) bounds. Values falling outside the [Q1 - 1.5*IQR, Q3 + 1.5*IQR] range were flagged as outliers, providing a run-specific threshold to detect potential column degradation or anomalous peak shapes without relying on the missing `chromatography_stage` variable.

#Results and Discussion 

The initial data integrity check revealed a systematic issue with negative values. All 53,656 instances of negative values in volume or UV metrics were classified as 'Baseline Artifacts,' indicating a consistent subtraction artifact rather than random data entry errors. This finding validates the exclusion of these rows before proceeding to shape metric calculations. The subsequent analysis of proxy shape metrics, visualized in Figure 1, provides critical insights into peak quality. The scatter plot of Skewness versus Asymmetry, colored by `run_no`, displays a dominant diagonal cluster where both metrics are positive and correlated. This pattern suggests consistent tailing behavior across the majority of the runs. However, a secondary, smaller cluster is visible in the lower-left quadrant, representing runs with significantly lower skewness and asymmetry. This separation implies distinct operational conditions or column behaviors between batches, warranting further investigation into the specific runs within this lower cluster for potential quality anomalies. Additionally, the histogram of the Area to Volume Ratio, stratified by column type, exhibits a right-skewed distribution with a long tail towards higher ratios. This indicates that for specific columns, the integrated area relative to the collected volume is disproportionately high, which may suggest peak broadening or inefficient collection processes. These visual patterns confirm the plan's objective of identifying abnormal shape characteristics, with the identified clusters serving as key indicators for downstream quality control decisions.

#Conclusion 

This analysis successfully navigated the complexities of a low-quality chromatographic dataset to identify potential product quality risks. By rigorously filtering out negative baseline artifacts and excluding fractions with excessive missing data, the study established a reliable foundation for shape metric computation. The results demonstrate that univariate summary statistics can effectively serve as proxies for peak shape when raw time-series data is unavailable. The distinct clustering observed in the skewness-asymmetry plot highlights systematic variations in peak shape across different run sequences, while the area-to-volume ratio distribution flags potential inefficiencies in specific column types. These findings support the conclusion that the current manufacturing batches exhibit varying degrees of peak shape deviation, with specific runs and columns requiring closer scrutiny. Future work should focus on investigating the specific conditions associated with the lower-skewness cluster and the high area-to-volume ratios to determine their impact on final product purity and safety.
