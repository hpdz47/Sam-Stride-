#Abstract 

This study addresses the challenge of assessing retention time reproducibility in a chromatography dataset lacking explicit timestamps and suffering from significant data missingness. Due to the absence of precise time data and high missing rates in flow rate variables (~66%), the analysis approximates reproducibility by evaluating the consistency of cumulative volume profiles across 11 distinct runs. The primary objective was to differentiate between random noise and systematic process drift, which are critical indicators of system stability and product quality. By imputing missing flow rates using run-specific means and calculating the slope of cumulative volume versus fraction number, we established a proxy for flow rate consistency. The results indicate substantial inter-run variability in these slopes, suggesting inconsistent system performance. Statistical classification using Z-score thresholds further categorizes these variations, providing a quantitative assessment of whether observed fluctuations represent minor random noise or significant systematic drift requiring process intervention.

#Introduction 

In biopharmaceutical manufacturing, maintaining consistent separation conditions is paramount for product quality. Retention time reproducibility serves as a key metric for this consistency; however, precise calculation requires accurate timestamps and reliable flow rate data. This dataset presents a unique analytical challenge: it contains 11 experimental runs but lacks an explicit time column, rendering direct retention time calculation impossible. Furthermore, critical variables such as `System_flow_ml_min` exhibit high missingness rates (~66%), and categorical variables like `Sample_Code` have over 75% missingness, precluding sample-specific analysis. Additionally, the presence of negative values in cumulative volume columns suggests potential data entry errors that must be addressed prior to statistical comparison. Consequently, this study adopts a volume-based approach to approximate retention time reproducibility. By assuming constant flow rates within specific runs, we utilize the slope of cumulative volume profiles as a proxy for flow rate consistency. The motivation for this analysis is to determine if the observed variability in fraction volumes is attributable to random experimental noise or systematic process drift, which would indicate a failure in maintaining the required separation conditions.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three primary steps to ensure data integrity and accurate reproducibility assessment. First, a data cleaning and integrity verification phase was conducted on the `chromatography_combined.csv` file. Invalid data points were removed, specifically filtering for negative values in the `volume_ml` column to eliminate entry errors. Given the high missingness of `Sample_Code` and `System_flow_ml_min`, these variables were excluded from filtering logic to prevent errors. Instead, the mean and standard deviation of `System_flow_ml_min` were calculated per run to facilitate imputation. Second, a flow rate imputation and cumulative profile reconstruction phase was executed. Missing flow rate values were imputed using the run-specific mean derived from the first step. The slope of `volume_ml` versus `Fraction_number` was then calculated for each of the 11 runs, serving as the actual flow rate proxy. A line plot visualizing these profiles was generated, with each line annotated by its calculated slope. Third, an inter-run variance analysis was performed to differentiate noise from drift. The slopes from all runs were analyzed to compute the Coefficient of Variation (CV). A Z-score threshold approach was applied: slopes within 2 standard deviations of the mean were classified as 'random noise' (stable), while those exceeding this threshold were classified as 'systematic drift' (unstable). This method avoided inappropriate clustering techniques like K-Means for univariate scalar data, focusing instead on statistical deviation from the mean.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the cumulative volume profiles reveals significant inter-run variability in the calculated slopes, which serve as proxies for the system's flow rate. As illustrated in Figure 1, the 11 distinct experimental runs display a wide range of slopes, indicating inconsistent flow rates or system performance across the dataset. This visual evidence supports the analysis objective of differentiating between random noise (minor slope fluctuations) and systematic drift (large deviations in slope), which is critical for assessing retention time reproducibility in the absence of explicit timestamps. The plot demonstrates that while some runs may exhibit minor fluctuations consistent with random noise, others show large deviations that suggest systematic drift. This variability implies that the system may not be maintaining consistent separation conditions required for biopharmaceutical product quality. The visual confirmation of these discrepancies underscores the necessity of the subsequent statistical classification using Z-score thresholds to formally categorize runs as 'stable' or 'drifting'. Without this statistical rigor, the visual data alone cannot definitively confirm the nature of the variability observed in the cumulative volume profiles.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, this study successfully approximated retention time reproducibility in a dataset characterized by missing timestamps and high data missingness. By utilizing cumulative volume profiles and imputed flow rates, we were able to quantify inter-run variability and distinguish between random noise and systematic drift. The results indicate substantial inconsistency in the system's flow rate across the 11 runs, with significant deviations from the mean slope suggesting potential systematic drift. This finding is critical for quality assurance, as it indicates that the separation conditions were not consistently maintained, which could impact the quality and reproducibility of the biopharmaceutical product. Future work should focus on identifying the root causes of the systematic drift and implementing corrective measures to stabilize the flow rate. Additionally, further investigation into the missing data patterns and the impact of imputation on the final statistical conclusions is recommended to enhance the robustness of the reproducibility assessment.
