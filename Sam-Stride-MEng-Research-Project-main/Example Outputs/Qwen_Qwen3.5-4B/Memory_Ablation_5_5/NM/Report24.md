#Abstract 

This study quantitatively evaluates the separation efficiency of a High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) system using UV absorbance signals at 280 nm and 260 nm. The primary objective was to determine the system's ability to resolve target compounds from impurities across multiple experimental batches and chromatography columns. Data derived from 32 runs involving four distinct column types were subjected to rigorous cleaning, including the capping of negative artifacts in volume and absorbance values. Subsequent analysis involved peak detection, calculation of peak widths at half-height ($W_{1/2}$), and computation of resolution metrics ($R_s$) between adjacent peaks. The results indicate that while the system generally achieves baseline separation, significant batch-to-batch variance exists, particularly when stratified by column type. Outlier detection identified specific runs where resolution metrics deviated substantially from the mean, suggesting potential operational inconsistencies or column degradation. These findings underscore the necessity of monitoring $R_s$ and $W_{1/2}$ as key quality indicators to ensure product purity and safety in biopharmaceutical manufacturing.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the quality of the final product is intrinsically linked to the efficiency of the purification steps, specifically chromatography. The resolution ($R_s$) between neighboring peaks is a critical parameter that dictates the purity of the target compound and the removal of impurities. However, HPLC data is often collected as discrete fractions, introducing a 'binning effect' that can obscure the true width of peaks and underestimate resolution. Furthermore, data integrity issues, such as negative values resulting from alignment errors, must be addressed prior to analysis. This study focuses on a dataset comprising 32 chromatographic runs across four different column types. The motivation for this analysis is to move beyond simple visual inspection to provide a quantitative assessment of separation efficiency. By calculating $R_s$ and $W_{1/2}$ while stratifying by run number and column type, we aim to identify systematic variations in performance that could compromise downstream quality. Understanding these variations is essential for optimizing process parameters and establishing robust quality control protocols.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three distinct phases to ensure data integrity and accurate metric calculation. First, data cleaning and signal preprocessing were performed on the raw `chromatography_combined.csv` file. Negative values in the `volume_ml` and UV absorbance columns were capped at zero to correct alignment artifacts. Rows with missing `run_no` or `column` identifiers were excluded, and the `chromatography_stage` variable was explicitly ignored for stratification to focus on batch and column effects. Second, peak detection was conducted on the continuous `volume_ml` axis using local maxima and minima identification. To mitigate the binning effect, peak widths at half-height ($W_{1/2}$) were calculated via interpolation between data points. Resolution ($R_s$) was then computed between adjacent peaks for each run. Third, batch variance and outlier detection were performed. Mean and standard deviation of $R_s$ and $W_{1/2}$ were calculated for each `run_no` and `column` combination. The Interquartile Range (IQR) method was applied to identify outliers in these metrics. All analyses were stratified by `run_no` (1–32) and `column` (4 unique types) to account for batch effects and column-specific performance differences.

#Results and Discussion 

The initial data cleaning phase successfully removed artifacts, capping negative values and retaining valid records for all 32 runs. As illustrated in Figure 1, the elution profiles for representative runs (Run 1 and Run 16) exhibit distinct peaks corresponding to target compounds and potential impurities. The interpolation method used for $W_{1/2}$ calculation effectively mitigated the binning effect, providing a more accurate representation of peak width than simple fraction counting. The scatter plot reveals that while the peak positions are consistent across runs, the peak widths show slight variability, which is critical for resolution calculations. Figure 2 presents the distribution of resolution metrics ($R_s$) stratified by column type. The box plots indicate that while all four column types achieve baseline separation ($R_s > 1.5$), there is a notable spread in the interquartile ranges. Specifically, two column types demonstrate higher variability in resolution compared to the other two, suggesting potential differences in column packing or age. The mean $R_s$ values across all runs generally exceed the threshold for acceptable purity, but the presence of outliers in specific batch-column combinations warrants further investigation. These results confirm that while the system is capable of effective separation, operational consistency and column selection play pivotal roles in maintaining high resolution standards.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully quantified the separation efficiency of the HPLC system across 32 experimental runs and four column types. The methodology, combining rigorous data cleaning with interpolation-based peak width calculation, provided a robust assessment of resolution ($R_s$) that accounts for the inherent binning effects of fractionated data. The results demonstrate that the system effectively separates target compounds from impurities, with mean resolution values generally meeting quality standards. However, the analysis also revealed significant batch-to-batch variance and column-specific differences in separation efficiency. Outlier detection identified specific instances where resolution metrics deviated from the norm, highlighting the need for continuous monitoring of $R_s$ and $W_{1/2}$ parameters. In conclusion, while the chromatographic process is viable, the observed variability underscores the importance of strict process control and regular column validation to ensure consistent product purity and safety in future manufacturing batches.
