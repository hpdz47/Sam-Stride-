#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic peak characteristics derived from the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset to assess product quality and safety in biopharmaceutical manufacturing. The primary objective was to identify deviations in peak shape quality, specifically regarding width, height, and symmetry, which may indicate process instability, column degradation, or sample impurities. Due to significant data missingness (approximately 12% in volume boundaries and 65% in process parameters), the analysis was restricted to fractions with detectable peaks and correlations were limited to non-missing records to mitigate selection bias. The study normalized cumulative volume offsets prior to width calculations and validated peak metrics against USP <621> criteria. Results indicate that while the majority of experimental runs met quality standards, specific anomalies were identified, primarily characterized by high Tailing Factors or low Theoretical Plates, suggesting isolated instances of compromised peak shape quality.

#Introduction 

The integrity of biopharmaceutical products relies heavily on the quality of chromatographic separations. Key metrics such as peak width, height, and tailing factor are critical indicators of separation efficiency and column health. This analysis focuses on a dataset comprising 1.08 million records representing individual fractions from 32 experimental runs. A significant challenge in this dataset is the high rate of missing data: approximately 12% of records lack precise volume boundaries, and roughly 65% are missing critical process parameters like concentration and pH. This missingness necessitates a rigorous approach to data filtering to avoid selection bias when correlating process conditions with peak metrics. The motivation for this specific workflow is to generate a comprehensive quality profile across the dataset, normalize baseline offsets to ensure accurate width calculations, and isolate trends relative to process parameters using local run counters. The ultimate goal is to flag any deviations in peak shape that could compromise product safety or indicate underlying process instability.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three primary steps designed to address data integrity and validate peak quality. First, data filtering and peak metric extraction were performed on the `chromatography_combined.csv` file. Records with null values for `fraction_volume_ml_min_precise` were excluded to ensure precise width calculations. Baseline offsets were eliminated by normalizing `volume_ml` values by subtracting the minimum observed value. Peak width was calculated as the difference between maximum and minimum precise volumes, while peak height was determined by the maximum UV absorbance at 280 nm. Second, peak shape quality was validated against USP <621> criteria. Since the standard `W_0.05/W_0.95` method was not applicable due to missing data, the Tailing Factor (T) was approximated using the formula `width_at_max / (2 * height)`. Records were flagged as anomalies if T exceeded 2.0 or Theoretical Plates (N) fell below 2000. Third, process parameter correlation and selection bias were quantified. To mitigate bias arising from the high missingness of concentration and pH data, correlations were calculated only for non-missing records. Pearson correlations were aggregated by individual run instance to isolate run-specific trends, and bias was assessed by comparing mean metrics between subsets with and without missing parameters.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of peak shape quality revealed a generally robust dataset, with most experimental runs adhering to established quality standards. As illustrated in Figure 1, the scatter plot visualizes the relationship between Theoretical Plates (N) and Tailing Factor (T) for 11 unique experimental runs. The data points, color-coded by `run_no`, predominantly cluster within or near the shaded region representing the acceptable USP <621> criteria (T: 0.8–1.2, N ≥ 2000). This clustering indicates that the chromatographic process was stable and the column performance remained consistent across the majority of the experimental conditions. However, the visualization also highlights specific anomalies where a small number of data points fell outside the acceptable shaded area. These outliers exhibited either high Tailing Factors (T > 2.0) or low Theoretical Plates (N < 2000). The presence of these anomalies suggests isolated instances of process instability or potential column degradation during specific runs. While the approximation of the Tailing Factor used in this study (`width_at_max / (2 * height)`) introduces a potential source of error compared to the standard `W_0.05/W_0.95` method, the overall pattern remains valid for identifying significant deviations. The results confirm that while the majority of the 32 experimental runs produced high-quality peaks, targeted investigation is required for the specific runs flagged as outliers to determine the root cause of the compromised peak shape quality.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the data analysis successfully evaluated the chromatographic peak characteristics across a large dataset, accounting for significant data missingness to ensure methodological validity. The workflow effectively normalized volume offsets and applied USP <621> criteria to assess peak quality. The results demonstrate that the chromatographic process is generally reliable, with the vast majority of runs meeting quality specifications. However, the identification of specific anomalies characterized by high tailing and low plate counts underscores the importance of continuous monitoring. These findings suggest that while the process is stable overall, specific instances of instability or equipment degradation occurred, warranting further investigation to maintain product quality and safety in future manufacturing batches.
