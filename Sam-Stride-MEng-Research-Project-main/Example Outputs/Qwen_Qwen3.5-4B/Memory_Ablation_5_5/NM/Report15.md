#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic separation performance and compound quantification derived from High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) signals. The primary objective was to establish a robust concentration profile across the manufacturing process by normalizing peak areas to determine the relative proportion of detected compounds. The analysis focused on validating process consistency and identifying Critical Quality Attributes (CQAs) such as purity, impurity levels, and retention time stability. Key methodological steps included Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction to eliminate negative absorbance artifacts, peak area normalization to derive relative ratios, and a comprehensive Retention Time (RT) drift analysis to detect system degradation. Results indicate that the baseline correction algorithm was uniformly successful across all analyzed runs, eliminating all negative absorbance values without introducing new artifacts. However, a critical deviation was identified in Run 21, which exhibited a statistically significant positive RT drift of 33.29 minutes. This extreme deviation suggests potential column degradation, mobile phase composition changes, or severe system pressure issues, necessitating immediate investigation to ensure product safety and efficacy standards are met.

#Introduction 

The quantitative evaluation of chromatographic data is fundamental to ensuring the quality and consistency of biopharmaceutical products. In this context, raw absorbance signals (UV_1_280_mAU) must be rigorously processed to extract meaningful concentration profiles and identify deviations from expected process parameters. The primary objective of this data analysis workflow was to normalize peak areas to determine the relative proportion of detected compounds within each fraction, thereby enabling the identification of Critical Quality Attributes (CQAs). Specifically, the analysis aimed to establish a robust concentration profile across the manufacturing process, converting raw absorbance units into normalized ratios to facilitate the assessment of purity, impurity levels, and yield distribution. This analysis is critical for validating process consistency; deviations in compound proportions can indicate upstream process failures or instrumental degradation. The dataset under review consists of a large time-series chromatographic run containing approximately 1.1 million rows, presenting challenges such as severe missingness in flow rate and pressure variables and the presence of negative absorbance values indicative of baseline drift. This report outlines the methodology employed to address these challenges and presents the findings regarding signal integrity, normalization efficacy, and retention time stability.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three distinct analytical phases designed to progressively refine the chromatographic data. The first phase, Signal Integrity and Baseline Correction, utilized the Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) algorithm to correct baseline drift. This step involved filtering out rows with absorbance values below -5.0 mAU to remove sensor noise prior to correction. The AsLS method was applied per run group, and the efficacy was verified by ensuring post-correction minimum values were non-negative. The second phase, Peak Area Normalization and Fraction Aggregation, calculated the sum of UV absorbance for each unique fraction ID and normalized individual values by this sum. This normalization allowed for the comparison of relative compound proportions across different runs. The third phase, Impurity Profiling and Retention Time Drift, identified the primary compound peak for each run based on maximum normalized ratios. Retention Time (RT) drift was calculated by mapping fraction order to volume and comparing primary peak RTs across runs. Runs exhibiting a primary peak RT shift greater than 0.5 minutes were flagged as significant deviations. This multi-step approach ensured that data quality was established before proceeding to complex profiling and quality attribute assessment.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of signal integrity and baseline correction yielded robust results, confirming the methodological soundness of the AsLS algorithm. As detailed in the baseline correction report, the application of AsLS was uniformly successful across all analyzed runs (R1 through R32). In every instance, the post-correction minimum UV absorbance value was exactly 0.00, indicating that the algorithm effectively eliminated all negative absorbance values associated with baseline drift without introducing new artifacts. The metric 'Rows_Flipped_Positive' quantified the volume of data corrected, ranging from 1,412 to 29,844 rows per run, suggesting that raw data contained significant baseline drift, particularly in runs R32 and R4. A total of 363 rows with negative values were removed pre-correction, resulting in a clean dataset for subsequent normalization steps. No methodological issues were observed in this phase, validating the data quality for further analysis. However, the assessment is limited to this correction step and does not evaluate subsequent normalization or impurity profiling. 

Conversely, the impurity profiling and retention time drift analysis revealed a critical quality attribute (CQA) deviation. The analysis identified a total of one flagged run, Run 21, which exhibited a positive RT drift of 33.29 minutes. This deviation is statistically significant and far exceeds the threshold of 0.5 minutes used for flagging. Such a substantial shift in retention time likely indicates column degradation, mobile phase composition changes, or severe system pressure issues. While the summary table confirms this critical deviation, it does not provide the raw signal data required to diagnose the specific root cause or to validate the purity calculation against the full chromatographic profile. The data quality for the summary table itself appears robust, with complete records for the analyzed variables. However, the missing context of the underlying 1.1M row dataset means that while the flag is confirmed, the specific chemical or instrumental cause of the 33-minute shift remains undetermined without access to the full time-series data. This finding underscores the necessity of immediate investigation into the instrumentation and process conditions during Run 21 to ensure product safety and efficacy.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the data analysis workflow successfully validated the efficacy of the AsLS baseline correction method, ensuring that all negative absorbance artifacts were removed across the dataset. This robust data cleaning process provided a reliable foundation for subsequent normalization and profiling steps. However, the analysis identified a critical anomaly in Run 21, characterized by an extreme retention time drift of 33.29 minutes. This deviation suggests significant instrumental or process instability, potentially compromising the integrity of the chromatographic separation and the resulting product quality. While the summary data confirms the presence of this deviation, further investigation utilizing the full raw time-series data is required to determine the root cause. Until the specific cause of the drift is resolved and the affected run is re-evaluated, the process consistency and product safety standards may be compromised. Future analyses should prioritize the retrieval of raw signal data for flagged runs to facilitate a comprehensive diagnosis of the underlying issues.
