#Abstract 

This report details the systematic analysis of high-resolution High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) fraction-level data to ensure product quality and safety. The primary objective was to extract, validate, and characterize chromatographic peaks by detecting signal maxima at 260nm and 280nm while rigorously filtering data artifacts. Due to the absence of injection volume metadata, the analysis prioritized Peak Area over Peak Height for quantitative validation. The workflow involved three critical phases: data cleaning and baseline subtraction to address negative volumes and UV offsets; signal-to-noise (S/N) ratio calculation and peak detection using a rolling window approach; and a multi-wavelength quality control assessment. The results confirm the successful generation of a robust peak inventory, distinguishing valid chromatographic events from baseline noise with high confidence.

#Introduction 

The analysis of chromatographic data is fundamental to verifying product quality and safety in high-resolution HPLC workflows. This specific dataset represents time-series absorbance profiles collected at the fraction level, requiring precise identification of signal maxima to characterize elution events. A significant challenge in this dataset is the presence of data quality artifacts, including negative cumulative volumes and negative UV absorbance readings, which indicate baseline noise or detector offsets. Furthermore, critical operational metadata such as injection volume and sample codes are largely missing, necessitating a methodology that relies on signal characteristics rather than correlating peak areas with specific injection conditions. The motivation for this analysis was to generate a comprehensive inventory of detected peaks with precise retention times (proxied by cumulative elution volume) and fraction identifiers, ensuring that the analysis accounts for these critical data quality issues before proceeding to quantitative integration. The focus area mandates the detection of all significant signal maxima while rigorously filtering out artifacts to produce a dataset that distinguishes valid chromatographic events from baseline noise using S/N ratios and baseline subtraction.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three primary steps designed to ensure data integrity and accurate peak detection. First, Data Cleaning and Signal Preprocessing were performed on the raw `chromatography_combined_data.csv`. This involved filtering out rows with negative `volume_ml` values and normalizing UV signals by subtracting run-specific medians to handle baseline offsets. Second, Signal-to-Noise Ratio Calculation and Peak Detection were executed on the cleaned dataset. A 5-fraction rolling window was used to calculate S/N ratios, with peaks identified as local maxima. Integration was performed using the trapezoidal rule, and peaks were flagged based on S/N thresholds (S/N < 3.0 filtered out; S/N < 5.0 marked as low confidence). Third, Multi-Wavelength Correlation and Quality Control Assessment were conducted on the resulting peak inventory. This step calculated the 260/280 absorbance ratio, grouped results by chromatography stage, and generated visualizations and text reports to assess signal quality and consistency across runs.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully identified and characterized chromatographic peaks across all runs, effectively distinguishing valid signal events from baseline noise. As illustrated in Figure 1, the Signal-to-Noise ratios at 280nm show distinct distributions across the different chromatography stages, indicating consistent detection performance. The boxplot reveals that the majority of peaks fall well above the S/N threshold of 3.0, confirming the robustness of the peak detection algorithm in filtering out low-quality data. Figure 2 demonstrates a strong positive correlation between the absorbance areas at 260nm and 280nm, with the data points clustering tightly around the expected linear relationship for each stage. This correlation validates the multi-wavelength approach used for peak characterization, ensuring that detected peaks represent genuine chromatographic events rather than artifacts. The quality control assessment further confirmed that the baseline subtraction and negative volume filtering steps were effective, resulting in a clean dataset where peak areas can be reliably used for validation in the absence of injection volume metadata. Overall, the workflow produced a comprehensive peak inventory that meets the requirements for product quality and safety assessment.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the data analysis workflow successfully achieved its primary objective of extracting and validating chromatographic peaks from complex HPLC fraction-level data. By implementing rigorous data cleaning to address negative volumes and baseline offsets, followed by a robust signal-to-noise based peak detection strategy, the analysis generated a reliable inventory of chromatographic events. The multi-wavelength correlation and quality control assessments confirmed the integrity of the detected peaks, demonstrating high consistency across different chromatography stages. Despite the absence of injection volume metadata, the reliance on Peak Area and signal characteristics proved sufficient for accurate characterization. This report provides a solid foundation for downstream quality assurance processes, ensuring that only valid, high-confidence chromatographic events are considered for product safety and quality determinations.
