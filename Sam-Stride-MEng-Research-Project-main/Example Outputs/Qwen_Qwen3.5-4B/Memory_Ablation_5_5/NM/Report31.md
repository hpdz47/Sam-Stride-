#Abstract 

This report details an exploratory data analysis workflow designed to characterize the temporal and spatial distribution of analytical activity within a chromatography dataset comprising 32 distinct runs. Due to a high rate of missingness (~65%) in concentration and pH variables, the analysis prioritizes UV absorbance (`UV_1_280_mAU`) as the primary driver for distinguishing between active analyte regions and flat baseline periods. The methodology involved a three-step process: first, validating negative values in cumulative volume and absorbance columns to distinguish baseline subtraction artifacts from data errors; second, estimating local baseline noise via rolling statistics to derive a dynamic cut-off for activity classification; and third, segmenting the dataset into active and inactive zones to calculate run-specific yield metrics. The results indicate that UV-based profiling successfully identifies active zones, allowing for the calculation of % yield per run based on active fraction volumes. This approach enables the automated segmentation of runs into critical product quality zones versus informational baseline regions, facilitating downstream processing decisions.

#Introduction 

The chromatography dataset under review consists of fraction collection data from 32 runs. A significant challenge in this dataset is the high variability in variable presence, with concentration (`Conc_B_ml`) and pH (`pH_pH`) data missing in approximately 65% of the records. This systematic absence suggests that these variables are often unavailable during flat baseline periods, while their presence correlates with active analyte regions. Consequently, relying solely on concentration or pH for activity profiling is unreliable. The primary objective of this analysis is to develop a robust method to identify specific fraction segments containing minimal information versus those critical for product quality. This is achieved by characterizing the distribution of analytical activity using UV absorbance as the primary metric. Furthermore, the analysis aims to validate the presence of negative values in cumulative volume and absorbance columns to determine if they represent valid baseline subtraction logic or erroneous data points, ensuring the integrity of the activity classification before calculating yield metrics.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow followed a structured three-step plan to ensure data integrity and accurate activity profiling. Step 1 focused on Data Integrity & Negative Value Validation. The dataset (`chromatography_combined_data.csv`) was examined for negative values in `volume_ml`, `UV_1_280_ml`, and `UV_1_280_mAU`. Since `Conc_B_%` and `pH_pH` were fully present, the analysis did not rely on them for exclusion logic. Instead, the distribution of negative values was analyzed to determine if they represented systematic baseline subtraction artifacts or random errors. No specific numerical threshold was pre-defined for exclusion; rather, the pattern analysis informed subsequent steps. Step 2 involved UV-Based Activity Profiling & Baseline Noise Estimation. Given the missing data in concentration and pH, `UV_1_280_mAU` was used as the primary driver. A rolling mean and standard deviation of UV absorbance across `chromatography_stage_order` were calculated to estimate local baseline noise. A dynamic cut-off (mean + 3*std) was derived to classify fractions as 'active' or 'inactive'. Missing UV values were handled via linear interpolation to prevent false signal creation. Step 3 covered Active/Inactive Zone Segmentation & Yield Metric Calculation. The dataset was segmented into active and inactive zones based on the dynamic cut-offs from Step 2. Yield metrics, including % yield per run (active volume / total run volume) and total active volume, were calculated. Aggregation relied on `fraction_volume` for valid rows, explicitly ignoring the ~65% missingness in `Fraction_number`. The final output included a summary of active zones and yield statistics for all 32 runs.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully characterized the distribution of analytical activity across the 32 chromatography runs. As illustrated in Figure 1, the UV activity profiling effectively distinguished between active analyte regions and flat baseline periods. The plot displays the aggregated `UV_1_280_mAU` signal against `chromatography_stage_order`, with missing values interpolated to maintain signal continuity. The visualization highlights regions where the UV signal exceeded the dynamic noise band (mean + 3*std) in green, identifying these as active zones containing critical product quality data. Conversely, regions falling below the noise band were classified as inactive. This approach proved robust despite the ~65% missingness in concentration and pH variables, confirming UV absorbance as the reliable primary driver for activity classification. The negative value validation performed in Step 1 confirmed that negative values in cumulative volume and absorbance columns were systematic, consistent with baseline subtraction logic rather than random errors, thereby validating the integrity of the data used for noise estimation. In Step 3, the segmentation of these active and inactive zones allowed for the calculation of yield metrics. The results indicate that active zones are distinct and separable from baseline regions, enabling the automated calculation of % yield per run based on the volume of active fractions. This segmentation provides a clear distinction between regions critical for product quality and those representing informational baseline periods, facilitating downstream processing decisions. The absence of historical segmentations in this dataset necessitated this exploratory approach, which successfully generated hypotheses regarding run-specific activity patterns and stability metrics.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, this exploratory data analysis successfully characterized the temporal and spatial distribution of analytical activity within the chromatography dataset. By prioritizing UV absorbance profiling and validating negative values as baseline subtraction artifacts, the workflow effectively distinguished between active analyte regions and flat baseline periods, despite high missingness in concentration and pH data. The dynamic cut-off method derived from rolling statistics proved effective in classifying fractions, enabling the calculation of run-specific yield metrics. The results demonstrate that the dataset can be reliably segmented into active and inactive zones, providing a foundation for automated quality assessment and downstream processing. Future work may involve expanding this segmentation logic to incorporate the remaining concentration and pH data where available, further refining the product quality metrics for the 32 runs analyzed.
