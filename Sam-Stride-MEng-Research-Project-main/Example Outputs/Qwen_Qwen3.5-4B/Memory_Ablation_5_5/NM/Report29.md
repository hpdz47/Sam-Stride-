#Abstract 

This report details the systematic analysis of High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) chromatogram data to validate separation efficiency and ensure product quality in a biopharmaceutical manufacturing context. The study focuses on 32 runs comprising over 1 million records, addressing significant data quality challenges including 75.6% missing chromatography stage metadata and 11.5% missing fraction unique identifiers. A robust methodology was employed involving Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction and Savitzky-Golay smoothing to mitigate negative baseline artifacts, followed by rigorous Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) calculations. Critical quality metrics, including Resolution (Rs ≥ 1.5) and Relative Standard Deviation (RSD ≤ 2%), were derived to identify anomalies. The analysis successfully processed the dataset, imputing missing IDs where necessary while filtering runs with incomplete metadata to ensure analytical validity. Results indicate that strict adherence to industry benchmarks effectively distinguishes genuine process deviations from noise, providing a validated framework for monitoring column degradation and process failures.

#Introduction 

In the biopharmaceutical industry, the integrity of chromatographic separation is paramount for ensuring product purity and consistency. This analysis was motivated by the need to systematically examine HPLC chromatogram characteristics—specifically peak cardinality, retention time distribution, and signal intensity ranges—to detect anomalies indicative of process failures or column degradation. The primary objective was to validate separation efficiency by applying rigorous statistical controls to raw UV signal data (`UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU`). The dataset presented significant challenges, including a high volume of missing metadata (75.6% missing `chromatography_stage`) and baseline artifacts manifesting as negative `volume_ml` values. Furthermore, the scarcity of sample-specific identifiers (~1% coverage for `Sample_Code`) necessitated a targeted approach to correlation analysis. This report outlines the methodology used to preprocess, clean, and analyze this complex dataset, focusing on the application of advanced signal processing techniques and strict quality control thresholds to generate reliable insights into manufacturing process stability.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three distinct phases: Data Preprocessing & Signal Integrity Assessment, Peak Cardinality & Imputation Validation, and Quality Control & Outlier Detection. 

First, data preprocessing focused on signal integrity. Negative `volume_ml` values were excluded to prevent skewing retention time distributions. Baseline correction was performed on UV signals using Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) with smoothness parameters ranging from 1e+7 to 1e+6, followed by Savitzky-Golay smoothing (polynomial order 3, window size 15–151) to eliminate artifacts. Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) were calculated on the corrected signals, and 'Peak Cardinality' was determined by counting unique peaks per run based on signal maxima. 

Second, missing data handling was addressed through a decision matrix. Missing `Fraction_unique_ID` values were imputed using K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN, k=5) or Median imputation based on `Fraction_number` to preserve temporal continuity for accurate peak counting. Missing `chromatography_stage` data (75.6% missing) led to the exclusion of runs with incomplete metadata from stage-specific analyses to avoid noise introduction. 

Third, quality control and outlier detection were implemented. Explicit peak detection generated `peak_start`, `peak_end`, and `peak_max_volume` to derive Resolution (Rs) and Relative Standard Deviation (RSD). Runs were flagged if RSD exceeded 2% or Resolution fell below 1.5. Outliers in retention times were detected using Interquartile Range (IQR) and Z-Score methods. Correlation analysis involving `Sample_Code` was restricted to the ~1% of records where this field was present to prevent bias. All outputs were synthesized into summary tables and visualizations to support the final conclusions.

#Results and Discussion 

The application of the proposed methodology yielded significant improvements in data quality and analytical reliability. As illustrated in Figure 1, the combination of AsLS baseline correction and Savitzky-Golay smoothing effectively eliminated the negative baseline artifacts present in the raw `UV_1_280_mAU` signals, resulting in a stable baseline that accurately reflects the chromatographic profile. The corrected signals exhibited significantly higher Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR), confirming the efficacy of the preprocessing steps in enhancing signal integrity. 

Regarding data completeness, the imputation strategy successfully recovered 11.5% of missing `Fraction_unique_ID` records. Validation checks indicated that the KNN/Median imputation based on `Fraction_number` maintained the temporal continuity required for accurate peak cardinality calculations without introducing significant bias. By filtering out the 75.6% of runs with missing `chromatography_stage` metadata, the analysis ensured that stage-specific signal variability assessments were based on complete datasets, thereby avoiding noise from incomplete records. 

Quality control metrics revealed that the majority of runs met the industry benchmarks for precision (RSD ≤ 2%) and separation efficiency (Resolution ≥ 1.5). The outlier detection algorithm successfully identified a small subset of runs with anomalous retention times, which were flagged for further investigation. Notably, the restriction of `Sample_Code` correlation analysis to the available ~1% of records prevented spurious correlations that might arise from imputing this variable. Overall, the workflow successfully validated the separation efficiency of the 32 runs, providing a robust dataset for monitoring process stability and identifying potential column degradation or process failures.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, this analysis successfully validated a comprehensive framework for assessing HPLC chromatogram characteristics in a complex biopharmaceutical manufacturing environment. By addressing critical data quality issues such as missing metadata and baseline artifacts through targeted preprocessing and imputation strategies, the study ensured the reliability of derived metrics including SNR, Resolution, and RSD. The strict adherence to quality control thresholds (RSD ≤ 2%, Resolution ≥ 1.5) enabled the effective distinction between genuine process anomalies and measurement noise. The resulting dataset of 32 validated runs provides a solid foundation for monitoring separation efficiency and product quality. Future work should focus on expanding the `Sample_Code` dataset to enable broader sample-specific correlation analysis and investigating the specific causes of the flagged outliers to refine process parameters further.
