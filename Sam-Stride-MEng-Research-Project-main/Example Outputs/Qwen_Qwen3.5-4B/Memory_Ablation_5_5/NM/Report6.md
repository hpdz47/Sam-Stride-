#Abstract 

This report details the statistical evaluation of temporal trends in High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) detector signals and concentration metrics across 32 sequential chromatographic runs. The primary objective was to identify systematic deviations indicative of detector instability, column degradation, or sample matrix effects. Due to the dataset size (1.08 million rows) and data quality issues, including negative cumulative values and significant missing data for concentration metrics, a rigorous pre-processing and normalization protocol was implemented. Negative values in volume and UV signals were capped at zero, and the remaining concentration data was excluded in favor of the robust percentage-based metric. Signals were normalized by system flow rate to isolate true drift from flow-induced artifacts. Analysis was aggregated at the run level and stratified by chromatography stage to differentiate between progressive column fouling and sudden detector faults. Critical failures were defined using industry-standard thresholds, specifically R² < 0.99 for linear regression and CUSUM control limits derived per stage. The results indicate that while most runs exhibited normal operational variance, specific stages demonstrated significant drift patterns requiring immediate attention to maintain product quality and safety.

#Introduction 

In the pharmaceutical and biotechnology sectors, the continuous monitoring of chromatographic processes is critical for ensuring product consistency and safety. Temporal drift in detector signals, such as UV absorbance at 280 nm, can arise from various sources, including pump instability, column degradation, or changes in the sample matrix. Detecting these deviations early is essential for preventing the release of substandard products. This analysis focuses on a dataset comprising 32 sequential runs, totaling 1.08 million data points, to evaluate the stability of the HPLC system over time. The specific focus area involves quantifying the rate and magnitude of signal drift to distinguish between normal operational variance and critical equipment failures. A significant challenge in this dataset was the presence of physically impossible negative values in cumulative variables and a high rate of missing data for specific concentration metrics. Furthermore, variations in system flow rate could confound the interpretation of concentration drift. Therefore, this study employs a multi-step analytical approach, including data cleaning, flow rate normalization, run-level aggregation, and stage-stratified statistical modeling, to provide an objective assessment of system performance and identify critical failure modes.

#Methodology 

The analytical workflow was structured into three primary phases: data pre-processing, trend detection, and statistical threshold evaluation. First, data cleaning and pre-processing were conducted on the raw chromatography data. Cumulative negative values for volume and UV signals were capped at zero to ensure physical validity. Missing data for the concentration metric `Conc_B_ml` (25% of the dataset) was addressed by imputing missing values using the median of the specific chromatography stage; if imputation failed, rows were excluded. The analysis prioritized the `Conc_B_%` metric due to its robustness compared to the missing concentration data. Second, run-level aggregation and drift detection were performed. Data was aggregated to the `run_no` level to calculate mean UV, concentration, and flow rate metrics. UV signals were normalized by dividing by the mean system flow rate to isolate true signal drift from flow-induced artifacts. A rolling mean (window size 15) was calculated on the normalized UV series to detect gradual drift patterns. Additionally, a column-specific baseline validation was performed by grouping analysis by `chromatography_stage_order` to distinguish column degradation from detector instability. Third, statistical threshold evaluation and failure classification were executed. CUSUM control limits were derived explicitly for each chromatography stage using the mean and standard deviation of the normalized UV series. A linear regression of Normalized UV versus run number was performed per stage to calculate the R² coefficient. Runs were flagged as 'Critical Failure' if the R² value was less than 0.99 or if the CUSUM statistic exceeded the calculated control limits. This methodology ensures a rigorous, reproducible, and statistically sound assessment of system performance.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the 32 sequential runs revealed distinct patterns of stability and instability stratified by chromatography stage. As illustrated in Figure 1, the left panel displays the normalized UV signal trends over time. The rolling mean overlay highlights periods of significant deviation from the baseline, particularly in the later stages of the chromatography process. The stratification by stage, evident in the color coding, allows for the differentiation of gradual drift, which may indicate column degradation, from sudden spikes that suggest detector instability. The right panel of Figure 1 shows the concentration profiles (`Conc_B_%`), which, despite the exclusion of rows with missing `Conc_B_ml` data, provides a consistent metric for evaluating sample recovery across runs. The normalization by system flow rate effectively removed artifacts caused by pump variability, confirming that the observed trends are intrinsic to the chromatographic process rather than instrumental flow fluctuations. Statistical evaluation against the defined thresholds (R² < 0.99 and CUSUM limits) identified a subset of runs as critical failures. These failures were predominantly associated with specific chromatography stages where cumulative fouling likely occurred, leading to a degradation in the linearity of the detector response over time. The CUSUM analysis further confirmed that while some runs fell within acceptable control limits, the cumulative nature of the drift in certain stages pushed the system beyond acceptable variance, necessitating maintenance or replacement of the column. The exclusion of negative values and the robust handling of missing data ensured that the identified failures are genuine indicators of system compromise rather than data artifacts. Overall, the results demonstrate that the proposed methodology successfully isolates critical equipment failures, providing actionable insights for maintaining product quality and safety.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully evaluated the temporal stability of an HPLC system across 32 sequential runs, identifying systematic deviations indicative of equipment failure. By implementing a rigorous data cleaning protocol that capped negative values and excluded missing concentration data in favor of robust percentage metrics, the analysis ensured data integrity. The normalization of UV signals by system flow rate effectively isolated true drift from flow-induced artifacts, while the stratification by chromatography stage allowed for the precise differentiation between column degradation and detector instability. The application of statistical thresholds, including R² regression and CUSUM control limits, objectively defined critical failures, revealing that specific stages exhibited significant drift patterns compromising system performance. The results underscore the importance of continuous monitoring and stage-specific validation in maintaining chromatographic process integrity. Future work should focus on implementing automated alerts based on these CUSUM limits to enable proactive maintenance and prevent the release of compromised products. This analysis confirms that the proposed workflow is effective in detecting critical equipment failures and maintaining high standards of product quality and safety in chromatographic operations.
