#Abstract 

This study investigates the reliability of automated peak detection in high-variance chromatographic UV absorbance data ($UV_{1,280}$ mAU). The primary objective is to establish a robust method for estimating the baseline noise floor that excludes solvent artifacts and detector artifacts, specifically addressing data characterized by high variance (CV > 150%) and negative signal values. We demonstrate that fixed-window noise estimation is insufficient for this dataset due to dynamic baseline fluctuations. Instead, we propose and validate an inter-peak valley clustering methodology, combined with Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) smoothing and negative value clipping, to accurately calculate the noise floor. Results indicate that without these preprocessing steps, the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) is severely compromised, leading to high false-positive rates in peak validation. The analysis confirms that stratification by flow stability (CV < 5%) is necessary to ensure the integrity of the noise estimation across different run conditions.

#Introduction 

Chromatographic data analysis often relies on automated peak detection algorithms to quantify analytes. However, the reliability of these algorithms is heavily dependent on the accurate estimation of the baseline noise. In datasets exhibiting high-frequency variance and detector artifacts, such as the UV absorbance data analyzed here ($UV_{1,280}$ mAU), traditional fixed-window approaches for noise estimation often fail. These methods may inadvertently include solvent artifacts or fail to capture dynamic baseline shifts, leading to inaccurate Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) and an increased rate of false-positive peak detections. Furthermore, the presence of negative values in the raw signal, indicative of detector artifacts, can skew statistical calculations if not properly handled. This report details a comprehensive data analysis workflow designed to mitigate these issues. The workflow integrates signal preprocessing, including AsLS smoothing and negative value clipping, with a novel noise floor estimation strategy based on inter-peak valley clustering. By validating this approach against both 'clean' and 'noisy' run profiles, we aim to define a rigorous criterion for distinguishing true analyte peaks from noise, ensuring that only peaks meeting specific intensity and resolution thresholds are considered valid.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a three-step protocol designed to preprocess the data, estimate noise, and validate peak detection. First, signal preprocessing was performed on the raw UV absorbance data ($UV_{1,280}$ mAU). Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) smoothing was applied to the original signal using parameters of smoothness $	au 	imes 10^6$ and asymmetry $eta = 10^{-4}$ to mitigate high-frequency variance while preserving peak shapes. Crucially, negative values in the raw signal were clipped to zero prior to statistical calculations to prevent skewing the standard deviation used for noise estimation. Second, flow stability was verified for each run using the Coefficient of Variation (CV) of $System_{flow}$ and $Sample_{flow}$. Runs exhibiting a CV > 5% were flagged for stratification to account for potential confounding effects on baseline stability. Third, peak detection and noise floor estimation were executed. Peaks were identified on the AsLS-smoothed signal. The noise floor was dynamically estimated by calculating the mean and standard deviation of the smoothed signal within inter-peak valley regions. To exclude solvent artifacts, valleys were required to be bounded by peaks with a specific height difference. The Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) was calculated as the peak height divided by the noise standard deviation. Finally, peak validation was conducted by classifying peaks as 'True Analytes' only if their height exceeded three times the estimated noise floor and their resolution ($R_s$) with adjacent peaks was $	au 	imes 1.5$.

The analysis of the chromatographic data reveals significant differences in noise characteristics between runs, necessitating a robust preprocessing strategy. The dotted line representing the calculated noise floor, derived from the mean of valley regions, remains relatively consistent, allowing for a clearer delineation of peak structures. These results underscore the critical importance of clipping negative values and applying AsLS smoothing prior to noise estimation to ensure accurate quantification.

#Conclusion 

This study concludes that high-frequency variance and detector artifacts in chromatographic UV data significantly compromise the reliability of standard peak detection methods. The analysis demonstrates that fixed-window noise estimation is inadequate for datasets characterized by high variance (CV > 150%) and negative signal values. Instead, a multi-step approach incorporating AsLS smoothing, negative value clipping, and inter-peak valley clustering for noise floor estimation is essential. The results, as visualized in the comparative analysis of noisy and clean runs, confirm that this methodology accurately captures the dynamic nature of the baseline, leading to a more reliable estimation of the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR). By defining 'true analyte' peaks based on both signal intensity (>3x noise floor) and resolution ($R_s \ge 1.5$), the workflow effectively minimizes false-positive rates. Future work should continue to stratify analysis by flow stability to further refine noise estimation across diverse operational conditions, ensuring the robustness of automated chromatographic data analysis.
