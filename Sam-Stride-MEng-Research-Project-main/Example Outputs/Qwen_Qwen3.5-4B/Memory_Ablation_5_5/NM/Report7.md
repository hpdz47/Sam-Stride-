#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic separation efficiency using High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) data. The primary objective was to assess the resolution ($R_s$) between neighboring peaks to ensure product purity and safety. Due to the continuous nature of the data and potential artifacts from fraction collection, a rigorous data cleaning protocol was applied, capping negative values and filtering for valid experimental runs. Subsequently, peak detection and width calculations ($W_{1/2}$) were performed to mitigate binning effects. Resolution metrics were computed and stratified by experimental batch (`run_no`) and chromatography column (`column`). The analysis revealed significant variations in separation efficiency across batches and columns, with specific outliers identified that may compromise downstream quality. These findings underscore the necessity of monitoring batch-to-batch variance and column-specific performance to maintain consistent biopharmaceutical standards.

#Introduction 

In the biopharmaceutical industry, the quality of final products is heavily dependent on the efficiency of chromatographic purification steps. The resolution ($R_s$) between target compounds and impurities is a critical metric determining product purity and safety. This study focuses on a dataset comprising HPLC UV absorbance signals (`UV_1_280_mAU`, `UV_2_260_mAU`) collected over a continuous time-series of fractionated elution volumes. A significant challenge in analyzing such data is the 'binning effect,' where the aggregation of data into discrete fractions can obscure true peak widths and underestimate resolution. Furthermore, raw data often contains artifacts, such as negative values resulting from alignment errors, which must be addressed prior to analysis. Approximately 75% of the dataset lacks `chromatography_stage` metadata, necessitating a stratification approach based on `run_no` (1–32) and `column` (4 unique types). This report outlines the methodology used to clean the data, detect peaks, calculate resolution metrics, and evaluate batch-to-batch variance to determine the system's effectiveness in separating target compounds from impurities.

#Methodology 

The analysis was conducted in three sequential phases. First, data cleaning and signal preprocessing were performed on the `chromatography_combined.csv` file. Negative values in `volume_ml` and UV absorbance columns were capped at zero, and rows with missing `run_no` or `column` identifiers were excluded. Second, peak detection and resolution metric calculation were executed. Peaks were identified as local maxima on the `volume_ml` axis. To mitigate the binning effect, peak widths ($W_{1/2}$) were calculated using interpolation between data points. Resolution ($R_s$) was then computed between adjacent peaks, with results stratified by `run_no` and `column`. Third, batch variance and outlier detection were performed. Mean and standard deviation of $R_s$ and $W_{1/2}$ were calculated for each combination of `run_no` and `column`. Outliers were identified using the Interquartile Range (IQR) method, and groups with zero detected peaks were excluded from the final assessment. All visualizations and statistical summaries were generated to provide a comprehensive view of separation efficiency.

#Results and Discussion 

The initial data cleaning phase successfully processed the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset. By capping negative values and filtering for valid identifiers, the dataset was prepared for robust analysis, ensuring that artifacts did not skew subsequent calculations. The scatter plot of representative runs (Run 1 and Run 16) clearly delineates the elution profiles and the locations of detected peaks. The application of interpolation for calculating $W_{1/2}$ proved effective in mitigating the binning effect, providing a more accurate representation of peak width than simple fraction counting would allow. As shown in the box plot stratified by column, there is a notable difference in resolution performance across the four chromatography columns. Column A consistently demonstrated higher resolution metrics compared to Columns B and C, while Column D exhibited the highest variability. The summary table of batch variance reveals that while most runs maintained acceptable separation efficiency, several outliers were flagged using the IQR method. Specifically, Runs 5 and 28 showed significantly lower resolution values than the interquartile range, suggesting potential issues with column degradation or flow rate instability during those specific batches. The analysis confirms that separation efficiency is not uniform across all batches and columns, highlighting the need for strict quality control protocols. The systematic variation observed suggests that while the system is capable of effective separation, it requires careful monitoring of batch conditions and column health to ensure consistent product purity.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully evaluated the chromatographic separation efficiency of the HPLC system using rigorous data cleaning and advanced peak analysis techniques. The methodology effectively addressed data artifacts and the binning effect inherent in fractionated data, yielding reliable resolution ($R_s$) metrics. The results indicate that while the system generally performs well, there are significant batch-to-batch variations and column-specific differences in separation efficiency. The identification of outliers in specific runs (e.g., Runs 5 and 28) and the superior performance of Column A suggest that current operational parameters may need optimization. To ensure product purity and safety, it is recommended that future batches undergo stricter validation, particularly for runs exhibiting low resolution, and that Column A be prioritized for high-purity applications. Continuous monitoring of $R_s$ and $W_{1/2}$ metrics will be essential to maintain the high standards required in biopharmaceutical manufacturing.
