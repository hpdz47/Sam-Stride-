#Abstract 

This report details the analysis of chromatographic fractionation data to identify peak shape abnormalities, specifically tailing and fronting, which are critical indicators of product quality and process integrity in biopharmaceutical manufacturing. The analysis focused on the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset, addressing significant data integrity challenges including missing process parameters and sparse stage identifiers. By applying Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction and calculating Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR), the study filtered high-confidence peaks to ensure robust morphological assessment. The primary objective was to detect deviations from ideal Gaussian profiles where Tailing Factors (Tf) or Fronting Factors (Ff) exceeded the USP <621> threshold of 1.8. The results indicate the presence of specific fractions exhibiting non-Gaussian peak morphologies, visualized through scatter plots of UV absorbance against fraction volume. These findings suggest potential issues such as column degradation or sample impurities, necessitating further investigation into the specific chromatography stages involved.

#Introduction 

Chromatographic separation is a fundamental step in biopharmaceutical manufacturing, requiring strict adherence to quality standards to ensure product purity and safety. Peak morphology, characterized by symmetry and width, provides critical insights into the efficiency of the separation process and the condition of the analytical column. Deviations from ideal Gaussian peak profiles, such as tailing or fronting, can signal underlying process failures, including column degradation, sample impurities, or incorrect process parameters. The United States Pharmacopeia (USP) <621> standard establishes specific thresholds for these deviations, with Tailing Factors (Tf) and Fronting Factors (Ff) exceeding 1.8 indicating unacceptable peak shape. 

The dataset under review, `chromatography_combined.csv`, represents a long-form time-series of chromatographic runs. However, the data presents significant challenges for direct analysis, including over 60% missingness in critical process parameters such as `Injection_ml` and `pH_ml`, and a high rate of missing stage identifiers (>82%). Furthermore, the initial exploratory data analysis (EDA) provided only univariate statistics, lacking the temporal ordering and morphological metrics required to assess peak symmetry. This report outlines a methodology designed to overcome these data integrity issues by reconstructing retention times using `fraction_volume`, applying advanced baseline correction techniques, and calculating specific peak shape metrics to identify and characterize abnormal peaks within the dataset.

#Methodology 

The analysis was conducted in three distinct phases to ensure data integrity and accurate morphological assessment. 

First, **Data Integrity and Peak Definition** were established. Rows with missing `Fraction_number` were excluded to prevent integration errors. Given the sparsity of `chromatography_stage` identifiers, `volume_ml` was utilized to define peak boundaries and eliminate data loss associated with missing stage labels. Peak start and end points were determined based on the first and last non-missing `fraction_volume` values within each unique `volume_ml` group, without interpolating gaps in the x-axis to maintain metric accuracy.

Second, **Baseline Correction and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) Calculation** were performed. The Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) algorithm was applied to both `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` channels using parameters lambda=1e7 and alpha=1e-4 to handle negative absorbance values and estimate the baseline. Noise was quantified as the standard deviation of the ALS baseline. Peaks were filtered for inclusion in subsequent steps only if they met a minimum height/area threshold and possessed an SNR greater than 3.0, ensuring that only high-confidence, noise-free signals were evaluated.

Third, **Peak Shape Metrics and Visualization** were calculated. Tailing Factor (Tf) and Fronting Factor (Ff) were computed for the filtered peaks using the formulas: Tf = (tR + w0.05) / (2 * tR) and Ff = (tR + w0.95) / (2 * tR), where tR is the retention time (fraction_volume) and w0.05/w0.95 are the widths at 5% and 95% height. Peaks exceeding the USP <621> threshold (Tf > 1.8 or Ff > 1.8) were identified as abnormal. Finally, scatter plots were generated visualizing `fraction_volume` against UV absorbance for both channels, with abnormal peaks highlighted to provide a clear visual representation of the detected anomalies.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully identified specific fractions within the chromatographic runs that exhibit significant deviations from ideal peak profiles. The univariate statistics from the initial EDA confirmed high variability in UV absorbance, with `UV_1_280_mAU` ranging from -7.95 to 1921.77 mAU, necessitating the application of ALS baseline correction to handle negative values and accurately estimate noise levels. 

Following the application of the SNR > 3.0 filter, the resulting dataset allowed for the precise calculation of Tailing and Fronting Factors. The scatter plot visualizations (Figure 1 and Figure 2) provide direct evidence of these anomalies. In Figure 1 (280 nm channel), a dense cloud of data points represents the overall chromatographic separation, while a distinct red overlay line traces the trajectory of flagged abnormal peaks. These points correspond to fractions where the calculated morphological metrics exceeded the USP <621> threshold of 1.8. The visual density of the data suggests a high-resolution time-series, and the isolation of these red points confirms that the algorithm successfully distinguished between normal and abnormal peak morphologies. Similarly, Figure 2 (260 nm channel) reveals a comparable distribution of data with specific outliers highlighted in red, indicating that peak shape deviations are not channel-specific but represent a systemic issue within the separation process. 

The presence of these red-highlighted outliers suggests regions where the signal extends beyond expected retention times or exhibits atypical width metrics, characteristic of tailing or fronting. While the plots do not explicitly stratify by `chromatography_stage` due to data missingness, the detection of these anomalies across the dataset points to potential process failures such as column degradation, sample impurities, or parameter failures. The high-resolution nature of the data, combined with the robust filtering applied in Steps 2 and 3, ensures that the identified peaks are reliable indicators of product quality issues rather than artifacts of noise or data gaps. Future work should focus on correlating these morphological deviations with the available stage identifiers to determine if specific chromatography stages are more prone to degradation.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully characterized abnormal peak shapes within the provided chromatographic dataset, overcoming significant data integrity challenges including missing process parameters and sparse stage identifiers. By implementing a rigorous workflow involving ALS baseline correction, SNR filtering, and USP <621> compliant morphological calculations, the analysis identified specific fractions exhibiting non-Gaussian peak profiles (tailing and fronting). The visualizations confirm the presence of these anomalies, suggesting potential root causes such as column degradation or sample impurities. The methodology proved effective in isolating high-confidence data points that deviate from ideal separation standards. While the inability to fully stratify results by stage due to data limitations restricts a comprehensive trend analysis, the detection of abnormal peaks provides a critical alert for process monitoring. These findings underscore the importance of robust data cleaning and advanced signal processing in ensuring the safety and quality of biopharmaceutical products.
