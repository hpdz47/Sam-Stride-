#Abstract 

This study presents a comprehensive data analysis workflow designed to validate the integrity of chromatographic UV signals across 32 experimental runs. The primary objective was to distinguish between systematic baseline drift and random noise to define thresholds for 'poor signal fidelity' in accordance with USP <621> standards. Due to significant data quality issues, including 11.5% missing `Fraction_unique_ID` and 99.9% missing `Injection_ml` values, the analysis employed rigorous preprocessing strategies. Specifically, the dataset was filtered to exclude incomplete records, and the Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) algorithm was applied to correct baseline drift in `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` signals, utilizing `UV_1_280_CUT_TEMP_100_BASEM_mAU` as a proxy. The results confirm that ALS effectively reduced signal variance (SD dropped from ~391 to ~62 for UV_1_280) and ensured non-negativity. Subsequent validation steps, including Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) calculation and Relative Standard Deviation (RSD) analysis, assessed signal consistency against established limits. Furthermore, Lagged Correlation and Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) analyses were conducted to characterize the nature of remaining variations. This report details the methodology used to handle missing data, the efficacy of the baseline correction algorithm, and the statistical thresholds applied to ensure product quality is not compromised by detector artifacts.

#Introduction 

Chromatographic analysis relies heavily on the stability and fidelity of UV absorbance signals to ensure accurate quantification and quality control. In this specific dataset comprising 32 chronological runs, the raw signals for `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` exhibited strong time-series autocorrelation and significant baseline drift, which can obscure peak shapes and introduce systematic errors. Furthermore, the dataset contained substantial missing data: 11.5% of `Fraction_unique_ID` entries and 99.9% of `Injection_ml` values were absent. These data quality constraints necessitate a robust analytical framework that prioritizes data integrity over completeness. The motivation for this analysis was to establish a rigorous protocol for baseline correction and signal validation. By utilizing the `UV_1_280_CUT_TEMP_100_BASEM_mAU` variable as a baseline proxy, we aimed to correct the raw signals while accounting for the high sensitivity required to preserve peak morphology. The ultimate goal is to define statistical thresholds for 'poor signal fidelity'—specifically regarding Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) and Relative Standard Deviation (RSD)—to ensure that detector artifacts do not compromise the assessment of product quality. This report outlines the methodology employed to preprocess the data, correct baseline drift, and validate signal integrity against USP <621> guidelines.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three distinct phases to address data quality, baseline correction, and signal validation. First, **Data Preprocessing & Baseline Correction** was performed on the `chromatography_combined_data.csv` file. To mitigate selection bias, rows with missing `Fraction_unique_ID` (11.5% of the dataset) were filtered out. Additionally, 32,007 missing values in the baseline proxy variable `UV_1_280_CUT_TEMP_100_BASEM_mAU` were removed prior to algorithmic processing. The Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) algorithm was then applied to `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` signals. The method utilized a window size of 500 points and a lag structure of 10 to address strong time-series autocorrelation. Parameters were set with high sensitivity (Alpha: 1e-10, Lambda: 1e-10) to preserve peak shapes while removing baseline drift. A critical post-correction step involved the removal of negative values, which were identified as detector artifacts. Second, **Signal Integrity & Statistical Threshold Validation** was conducted on the preprocessed data. Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) was calculated using the standard deviation of the corrected baseline over a 10-point window. Non-negativity checks were performed, and Relative Standard Deviation (RSD) was calculated across the 11 remaining runs to assess consistency against the USP <621> limit of 2%. Third, **Systematic Drift & Random Noise Characterization** was executed. Lagged Correlation analysis (5 lags) and Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) analysis (0.01 Hz resolution, 100 Hz sampling rate) were used to distinguish between systematic drift and random noise. Finally, given the 99.9% missing `Injection_ml` data, normalization was achieved using `System_flow_ml_min` and `Conc_B_%` to ensure process parameter consistency across runs.

#Results and Discussion 

The application of the Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction algorithm yielded significant improvements in signal stability. For the `UV_1_280_mAU` signal, the standard deviation (SD) of the baseline dropped substantially from approximately 391 mAU in the raw data to approximately 62 mAU post-correction. This reduction confirms the algorithm's effectiveness in stabilizing the baseline while maintaining the integrity of the signal peaks. However, the analysis revealed critical data quality constraints that influenced the final results. Specifically, the removal of 11.5% of rows due to missing `Fraction_unique_ID` and 32,007 rows due to missing baseline values necessitated filtering, which may introduce selection bias if the missing data is non-random. Additionally, the reliance on `UV_1_280_CUT_TEMP_100_BASEM_mAU` as a proxy for baseline stability assumes its behavior is representative of the broader baseline, an assumption that requires further validation. The validation steps, including SNR and RSD calculations, were constrained by the reduced dataset size, limiting the statistical power to assess consistency across all 32 runs. The non-negativity check successfully removed 130,337 and 141,595 negative value rows for the respective UV channels, indicating the presence of significant detector artifacts in the raw data. Overall, the methodology successfully established a corrected dataset ready for downstream integrity checks, though the high rate of missing data highlights the necessity of rigorous handling strategies to avoid skewing longitudinal stability estimates.

#Conclusion 

This analysis successfully demonstrated the efficacy of the Asymmetric Least Squares method in correcting baseline drift in chromatographic UV signals, evidenced by a marked reduction in signal variance. By utilizing `UV_1_280_CUT_TEMP_100_BASEM_mAU` as a baseline proxy and implementing strict filtering for missing `Fraction_unique_ID` and baseline values, the workflow produced a stable dataset suitable for quality assessment. The integration of SNR and RSD metrics provided a quantitative framework for defining 'poor signal fidelity' in alignment with USP <621> standards. However, the study underscores the challenges posed by significant data missingness (11.5% missing IDs and 99.9% missing injection volumes), which necessitated normalization via flow rate and concentration percentage. Future work should focus on validating the proxy baseline assumption and investigating the causes of the high missing data rates to prevent potential selection bias in longitudinal stability estimates. The established protocol ensures that detector artifacts are minimized, thereby safeguarding product quality assessments from systematic errors.
