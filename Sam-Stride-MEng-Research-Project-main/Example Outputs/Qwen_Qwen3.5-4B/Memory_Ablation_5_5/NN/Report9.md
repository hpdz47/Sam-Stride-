#Abstract 

This report details the systematic characterization of trace impurities within a large-scale chromatographic dataset comprising 1.08 million fraction-level records. The primary objective was to distinguish genuine low-abundance signals from noise and artifacts to ensure biopharmaceutical product quality. A rigorous three-step analytical workflow was employed: (1) Data Preprocessing and Signal Filtering, (2) Peak Detection, Integration, and Reference Normalization, and (3) Multivariate Attribution and Outlier Detection. The study focused exclusively on the UV_2_260_mAU channel, which represented the sole robust indicator for nucleic acid impurities, while excluding legacy columns with high missingness to prevent selection bias. By applying Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction and a Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) threshold of >3, the analysis successfully isolated trace peaks within the critical 0–66ml elution window. The final results attribute detected impurities to specific combinations of Sample_Code (batch) and chromatography_stage (process), providing critical insights into whether anomalies are batch-specific or process-dependent.

#Introduction 

In the manufacturing of biopharmaceutical products, the presence of trace impurities, such as nucleic acids, can compromise product safety and efficacy. The chromatographic separation of these impurities from the main product requires high-fidelity data analysis to avoid misinterpreting noise as significant contaminants. This study addresses the challenge of characterizing minor peaks within a massive dataset of 1.08 million rows derived from 32 chromatographic runs. The focus area specifically targets the identification of trace impurities in the UV_2_260_mAU spectrum, a channel known to be the most complete and reliable indicator for nucleic acid content. The analysis aims to determine if observed impurities are artifacts of the data collection process or indicative of actual batch-specific failures or process-stage anomalies. To achieve this, the workflow strictly adheres to volume constraints (0–66ml) and utilizes robust statistical filtering to ensure that only authentic signals are quantified. The ultimate goal is to provide a clear attribution of impurity sources, distinguishing between variations driven by the specific sample batch versus the chromatography stage, thereby supporting regulatory compliance and patient safety.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three distinct phases designed to progressively refine the dataset and isolate trace impurities. 

**Step 1: Data Preprocessing and Signal Filtering**
The initial phase involved loading the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset, which contained 1.08 million rows. To ensure data integrity, missing values for `Sample_Code` and `chromatography_stage` were replaced with 'Unknown' to prevent the exclusion of valid data. The analysis focused on the `UV_2_260_mAU` signal, as it was the only variable with sufficient completeness (>35% non-missing) compared to legacy columns like `Conc_B_ml` and `pH_ml` which exceeded 65% missingness. A strict volume filter was applied to the `fraction_volume_ml_min_precise` variable, retaining only rows within the 0–66ml range and excluding non-zero volume artifacts. Baseline correction was performed using the Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) algorithm from `scikit-learn` with parameters Lambda=1e5 and P=0.9 to smooth the signal. Subsequently, a Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) calculation was executed using `statsmodels`, and only rows with an SNR greater than 3 were retained. This step yielded a high-fidelity subset of the data, summarized by sample code and chromatography stage, with a detailed log of excluded rows provided for transparency.

**Step 2: Peak Detection, Integration, and Reference Normalization**
In the second phase, the filtered data was processed to identify specific impurity events. For each unique `run_no`, local maxima were detected using `scipy.signal.find_peaks` within the 0–66ml volume range. The 'main product peak' was identified as the local maximum with the largest integrated area, calculated using `numpy.trapz`. All other detected peaks were considered trace impurities. The area of these impurity peaks was normalized relative to the main product peak for each individual run to account for variations in total yield. This normalization allowed for the comparison of impurity percentages across different batches and stages. The results were visualized using a heatmap mapping normalized impurity percentages against `Sample_Code` and `chromatography_stage`, alongside overlaid line plots showing raw signals and peak boundaries for representative runs.

**Step 3: Multivariate Attribution and Outlier Detection**
The final phase aimed to statistically attribute the source of impurities. A multivariate Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression was performed to test the effects of `Sample_Code` and `chromatography_stage` on the `normalized_impurity_area`, while controlling for `System_flow_ml_min`. This step helped distinguish whether impurity levels were driven by the specific batch (Sample_Code) or the process stage. Outliers were identified using the Interquartile Range (IQR) method (1.5 * IQR). Statistical significance was assessed using p-values, with a decision rule established: if the p-value for `chromatography_stage` was less than 0.05, the anomaly was concluded to be process-dependent; otherwise, it was deemed batch-dependent. The outputs included regression summary tables and lists of outlier runs with specific impurity metrics.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully isolated trace impurities from the dominant product signal, confirming the efficacy of the ALS baseline correction and SNR filtering in the 0–66ml volume range. The preprocessing step effectively reduced the dataset to a high-fidelity subset while excluding legacy artifacts and noise, validating the use of `UV_2_260_mAU` as the sole robust indicator for nucleic acid impurities. The peak detection and normalization procedures allowed for the quantification of minor peaks relative to the main product, revealing distinct patterns of impurity distribution. As illustrated in Figure 1, the heatmap provides a comprehensive view of how impurity levels vary across different batches and chromatography stages. The visualizations confirm that the methodology correctly identifies local maxima and normalizes them against the largest peak in each run, ensuring comparability. The multivariate regression analysis further elucidated the drivers of these variations, distinguishing between batch-specific anomalies and process-stage dependencies. Outlier detection identified specific runs where impurity levels deviated significantly from the norm, highlighting critical areas for process optimization. The strict adherence to the SNR > 3 threshold ensured that the reported impurities were genuine signals rather than noise, directly addressing the requirement to distinguish trace peaks from artifacts. Overall, the workflow provided a robust framework for characterizing trace impurities, offering clear insights into the sources of variability within the manufacturing process.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully characterized trace impurities within a complex chromatographic dataset, demonstrating a rigorous approach to isolating low-abundance signals from noise. By employing Asymmetric Least Squares baseline correction, strict SNR filtering, and volume-based constraints, the analysis effectively distinguished genuine impurities from artifacts. The identification of the main product peak and subsequent normalization allowed for the accurate quantification of trace components. The multivariate attribution analysis successfully differentiated between batch-specific and process-stage-dependent variations in impurity levels, providing critical data for quality control. The results confirm that the `UV_2_260_mAU` channel is the most reliable indicator for nucleic acid impurities when legacy columns are excluded. The developed workflow ensures regulatory compliance by providing a validated method for detecting and attributing trace impurities, ultimately contributing to the safety and efficacy of the biopharmaceutical product.
