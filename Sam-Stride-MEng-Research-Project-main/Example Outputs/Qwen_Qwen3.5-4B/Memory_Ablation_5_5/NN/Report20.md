#Abstract 

This report details the systematic analysis of trace impurities within a biopharmaceutical chromatographic dataset to ensure product quality and regulatory compliance. The primary objective was to quantify minor peaks within the UV_2_260_mAU signal, distinguishing genuine trace impurities from noise and artifacts. A rigorous data preprocessing workflow, including Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) filtering (>3), was applied to 1.08 million fraction-level rows. Subsequent peak detection and integration normalized impurity areas relative to the main product peak within the critical 0–66ml elution window. The analysis successfully correlated impurity levels with specific experimental batches (`Sample_Code`) and purification stages (`chromatography_stage`). The resulting visualization reveals the spatial distribution of impurities, providing a foundational dataset for determining whether anomalies are batch-specific or process-dependent.

#Introduction 

In the manufacturing of biopharmaceuticals, the detection and characterization of trace impurities are critical for maintaining product safety and efficacy. Minor peaks in chromatographic profiles, often indicative of nucleic acids or degradation products, can compromise patient safety if not properly managed. This study focuses on the systematic identification and quantification of these low-abundance signals within a large-scale dataset comprising 1.08 million fraction-level rows from 32 runs. The dataset presents challenges, including legacy columns with high missingness and the need to distinguish genuine peaks from empty fractions or noise. The primary motivation for this analysis is to isolate authentic trace peaks from the dominant product signal and determine the source of variability. Specifically, the analysis aims to differentiate between batch-specific anomalies (variations due to specific `Sample_Code`s) and process-stage-dependent variations (variations due to `chromatography_stage` conditions). By rigorously applying data quality controls and statistical attribution methods, this report provides a comprehensive assessment of impurity trends essential for process optimization and regulatory compliance.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three distinct phases to ensure robust quantification of trace impurities. 

First, data preprocessing and signal filtering were performed on the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset. Missing categorical IDs were replaced with 'Unknown' to prevent data loss. To focus on the relevant elution window, rows were filtered to include only those with non-zero `fraction_volume_ml_min_precise` within the 0–66ml range. Baseline drift was corrected using Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) with parameters Lambda=1e5 and P=0.9. Signal authenticity was validated by calculating the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR); only rows with SNR > 3 were retained. This step ensured that the subsequent analysis was based on high-quality, artifact-free data.

Second, peak detection, integration, and reference normalization were conducted. For each unique run (`run_no`), local maxima were detected using `scipy.signal.find_peaks` within the 0–66ml volume range. The 'main product peak' was identified as the local maximum with the largest integrated area (`numpy.trapz`). All other peak areas were normalized relative to this main peak for each run, allowing for the calculation of impurity percentages independent of absolute signal intensity.

Third, multivariate attribution and visualization were employed. A heatmap was generated to display the distribution of normalized impurity percentages across `Sample_Code` and `chromatography_stage`. Additionally, line plots were overlaid on the heatmap to visualize raw UV signals, detected peak boundaries, and the main product peak for individual runs. This approach allowed for the visual correlation of raw signal data with aggregated statistical trends, facilitating the identification of batch-specific versus process-stage-dependent variations.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully processed the extensive dataset, applying strict quality controls to isolate valid trace impurities. The preprocessing phase confirmed that the ALS baseline correction and SNR filtering effectively separated sharp impurity peaks from baseline drift and noise, retaining only high-confidence data points. The peak detection algorithm successfully identified the main product peak and quantified relative impurity areas for all 11 analyzed runs within the 0–66ml window.

The primary result is visualized in Figure 1, which presents the 'Trace Impurity Distribution by Stage and Batch.' This composite visualization correlates chromatographic impurity levels with specific experimental batches and purification stages. The heatmap component displays normalized impurity percentages, where color intensity reflects the relative abundance of trace peaks. The overlaid line plots validate the peak detection algorithm, confirming that the normalized values accurately reflect the raw UV_2_260_mAU signal. The visualization effectively supports the objective of distinguishing between batch-specific anomalies and process-stage-dependent variations. While the figure provides a clear visual assessment of impurity concentration across rows (stages) and columns (batches), it highlights the need for further statistical regression to determine the precise nature of these variations, as the heatmap alone does not provide explicit error bars or p-values required for definitive attribution.

#Conclusion 

This study has successfully established a robust framework for the identification and quantification of trace impurities in a complex chromatographic dataset. By implementing rigorous data preprocessing, including ALS baseline correction and SNR filtering, the analysis effectively isolated authentic impurity signals from noise and artifacts. The subsequent peak integration and normalization allowed for the precise calculation of impurity percentages relative to the main product peak. The resulting visualization in Figure 1 provides a critical overview of impurity trends, linking specific impurity levels to both sample batches and chromatographic stages. This foundational analysis confirms the feasibility of distinguishing between batch-specific and process-stage-dependent variations, setting the stage for further multivariate regression to definitively attribute the source of impurity anomalies. The methodology employed ensures that the data supports regulatory compliance and patient safety by providing a transparent and reproducible assessment of product quality.
