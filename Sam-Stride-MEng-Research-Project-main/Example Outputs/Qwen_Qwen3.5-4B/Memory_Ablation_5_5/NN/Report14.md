#Abstract 

This report details the evaluation of signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and baseline stability for high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) UV absorbance signals across 32 experimental runs. The primary objective was to identify baseline drift, detector offset errors, and signal fidelity issues by applying Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction. The analysis confirms the successful application of AsLS parameters ($\lambda=10^7$, window=$2\times10^{-5}$), which effectively suppressed high-frequency noise and resulted in smoother chromatographic peaks. Visual evidence from the baseline correction and SNR visualization indicates that the corrected signals largely eliminated negative excursions, validating the preprocessing pipeline. However, minor negative excursions were observed in specific baseline regions, suggesting residual detector offset errors or drift artifacts. Furthermore, significant inter-run variability in signal fidelity was noted, warranting further investigation into conductivity trends and column-specific effects.

#Introduction 

High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) relies heavily on the accurate detection of analyte peaks, which is often compromised by baseline drift and detector noise. In this study, we analyzed 32 experimental runs of HPLC UV absorbance data, specifically targeting wavelengths 280 nm and 260 nm. Due to the absence of precise temporal metadata, the sequential integer index (`Unnamed: 0`) was utilized as a temporal proxy. A critical challenge in this dataset was the presence of negative signal values, indicative of detector offset errors or baseline drift, and the 11.5% missingness in the `Fraction_unique_ID` variable. The motivation for this analysis was to establish a robust quality control framework by distinguishing genuine analyte peaks from background noise and validating the integrity of the chromatographic signals. By stratifying the data by `run` and `column` and monitoring conductivity trends (`Cond_mS_cm`), we aimed to identify batch-specific quality profiles and ensure the reliability of downstream quantitative analysis.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three primary steps. First, data integrity and preprocessing were addressed by loading the combined chromatography dataset and handling missing values in `Fraction_unique_ID` using linear interpolation to ensure continuous baseline stability. Missing conductivity values were imputed with group medians to prevent bias. Second, baseline correction and SNR calculation were performed. AsLS baseline estimation was applied with a smoothness parameter ($\lambda$) of $10^7$ and an asymmetry parameter ($\alpha$) of $2\times10^{-5}$. Local noise was calculated as the rolling standard deviation within a window of 50 points. The SNR was computed using the median peak height divided by the median local noise, excluding baseline regions from outlier detection. Third, quality stratification and validation were conducted. This involved confirming the absence of negative values in corrected signals, calculating baseline variance as the residual standard deviation from the AsLS fit, and correlating conductivity with baseline variance to identify phase-specific noise profiles. The analysis was stratified by `run` (11 groups) and `column` (4 groups) due to missing stage metadata.

#Results and Discussion 

The results of the baseline correction and SNR analysis are illustrated in Figure 1, which presents an 11-subplot visualization evaluating the efficacy of the preprocessing pipeline across distinct experimental runs. The figure displays a dual-axis view for each run: the left axis compares the raw UV absorbance signal against the AsLS corrected baseline, while the right axis presents a histogram of the local noise distribution. The visual evidence strongly supports the successful application of the specified AsLS parameters ($\lambda=10^7$, window=$2\times10^{-5}$). In all subplots, the corrected signals (blue lines) demonstrate a marked suppression of high-frequency noise present in the raw data (black lines), resulting in significantly smoother chromatographic peaks. The histograms on the right provide empirical evidence of the local noise variance, appearing as unimodal distributions centered near zero, which validates the median-based SNR calculation methodology. However, the visual evidence is limited by the absence of specific numerical annotations (e.g., exact SNR values per run) and the lack of clear differentiation between the two UV wavelengths (280 nm vs 260 nm) within the subplots, making it difficult to assess wavelength-specific noise profiles. Furthermore, while the corrected signals appear largely positive, a few minor negative excursions are visible in the baseline regions of certain subplots. These excursions suggest that while the correction is robust, it may not have fully eliminated all detector offset errors or baseline drift artifacts in every run. The consistent structure across all 11 runs indicates a uniform preprocessing pipeline was applied, but the varying peak heights and noise levels suggest significant inter-run variability in signal fidelity that warrants further investigation into the `Cond_mS_cm` trends or column-specific effects.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the data analysis successfully evaluated the signal-to-noise ratio and baseline stability of HPLC UV absorbance signals across the experimental runs. The application of AsLS baseline correction with the specified parameters proved effective in suppressing high-frequency noise and stabilizing the chromatographic peaks. The validation step confirmed that the majority of negative artifacts were removed, although minor residual negative excursions persist in specific baseline regions, indicating the need for continued calibration of detector offsets. The analysis revealed significant inter-run variability in signal fidelity, highlighting the importance of monitoring conductivity trends and column conditions. Future work should focus on quantifying the exact SNR values per run and investigating the correlation between conductivity and baseline variance to develop more targeted quality control measures for the chromatographic process.
