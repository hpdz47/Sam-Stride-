#Abstract 

This study reconstructs and quantifies compound abundance across 11 distinct logical chromatography runs by aggregating UV absorbance data at 260 nm. Due to significant data integrity issues, including cumulative volume offsets and high missingness in critical process variables, a rigorous methodology was employed. The analysis corrected volume offsets by +33.8 ml and applied Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction to ensure signal accuracy. Compounds were quantified using weighted peak area calculations to account for varying fraction volumes. Results indicate that Run R32 and R30 exhibited significantly higher compound yields (~50.1 million mAU) compared to the remaining batches. However, due to over 60% missingness in process conditions, no correlations between signal intensity and operational parameters could be established, limiting the ability to attribute yield differences to specific process stages.

#Introduction 

Chromatographic data analysis requires precise reconstruction of elution profiles from raw fractionation records. In this dataset, 1.08 million records span 32 sequential run numbers but only 11 logical experimental batches. Comparisons must be performed strictly by the logical `run` identifier to ensure batch validity. A primary challenge involves data integrity: cumulative volume variables exhibit negative minimums (-33.8 ml), indicating uncorrected offsets that must be addressed prior to integration. Furthermore, critical process variables such as buffer concentration (`Conc_B_ml`) and chromatography stage are missing for over 60-75% of records, precluding standard normalization or correlation analysis. The objective of this analysis was to reconstruct individual chromatograms, quantify relative abundance using weighted peak areas, and assess the impact of data quality on the ability to link performance to process conditions.

#Methodology 

The analysis proceeded in three distinct steps. First, data integrity and baseline correction were performed. The cumulative volume offset was corrected by adding 33.8 to the `volume_ml` variable. The UV signal (`UV_2_260_mAU`) underwent Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction using a lambda parameter of 1,000,000 and an asymmetry parameter of 0.00001 to handle high noise levels. Second, weighted aggregation and peak area calculation were executed. Corrected UV signals were aggregated by logical `run` and fraction number. Absolute Peak Area was calculated by multiplying the mean signal intensity by the corresponding `fraction_volume`. This weighting strategy was essential to minimize integration bias given the wide range of fraction volumes (0.008 to 66.0 ml). Third, compound ranking and correlation assessment were conducted. Runs were ranked by total weighted peak area. Correlations between signal intensity and process variables (`chromatography_stage`, `Conc_B_ml`) were attempted only if missingness was below 60%. Given the high missingness (>60%), these correlations were explicitly skipped to avoid spurious results.

#Results and Discussion 

The reconstructed chromatograms, visualized in Figure 1, display the weighted peak area profiles across the 11 logical run groups. The visualization confirms the successful application of data integrity corrections; the negative volume offsets have been eliminated, and the profiles are cleaner, suggesting the AsLS baseline correction was effective. The primary finding is a distinct stratification in compound yield. As shown in the aggregated data, Run R32 and Run R30 exhibit significantly higher total peak areas, approximately 50.1 million mAU, compared to the remaining runs which cluster between 38 million and 5.8 million mAU. This indicates a substantial difference in compound concentration or yield between these top two batches and the rest. However, the analysis reveals a critical limitation regarding process optimization. The correlation assessment failed completely due to >60% missingness in `Conc_B_ml` and `chromatography_stage`. Consequently, the high yields observed in R32 and R30 cannot be definitively linked to specific chromatography stages or buffer concentrations. The data supports the conclusion that while the weighted aggregation strategy successfully reconstructed interpretable chromatograms, the current dataset lacks the necessary process variable completeness to determine the drivers of the observed performance differences.

#Conclusion 

This analysis successfully reconstructed and quantified compound abundance across 11 logical chromatography runs, correcting for data integrity issues such as volume offsets and applying robust baseline corrections. The results demonstrate that Runs R32 and R30 produced significantly higher yields than the other batches. However, the high missingness in critical process variables prevented the establishment of correlations between operational parameters and signal intensity. Future analyses should prioritize the collection of complete process metadata to enable a deeper understanding of the factors driving the high yields observed in R32 and R30.
