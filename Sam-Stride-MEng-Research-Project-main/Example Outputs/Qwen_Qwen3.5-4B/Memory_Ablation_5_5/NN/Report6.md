#Abstract 

This report details the systematic evaluation of high-frequency UV absorbance signals from a biopharmaceutical manufacturing process to quantify baseline stability and assess noise impact on peak detection reliability. The analysis focuses on 1.08 million time-series points across 11 unique runs, utilizing UV_1_280_mAU and UV_2_260_mAU channels. A critical preprocessing step involved soft baseline offset correction to address negative signal values (down to -7.95 mAU) without compromising signal integrity. The methodology employed rolling window median calculations for baseline estimation and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) metrics defined as Mean_Baseline / Std_Baseline. Statistical anomaly detection was stratified by run label to identify inconsistent noise levels, applying thresholds where SNR > 3 and Standard Deviation < Mean + 3*Std. Furthermore, a correlation analysis was conducted between smoothed system flow rates and baseline noise to account for flow-induced instability. Results indicate the necessity of strict noise floor validation to prevent false positives or negatives in automated fractionation, as ground truth data is unavailable. The study establishes a robust framework for defining acceptable quality parameters in the absence of direct peak validation data.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the reliability of automated detection systems is paramount for ensuring product quality during fractionation. However, high-frequency UV absorbance signals are susceptible to baseline drift and noise, which can lead to erroneous peak detection. This analysis aims to systematically evaluate these signals to establish an acceptable baseline noise threshold. The primary objective is to validate that fluctuations do not exceed defined quality parameters, specifically adhering to Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) standards (e.g., SNR > 3) and Standard Deviation limits relative to the baseline mean. Given that ground truth data regarding peak detection performance is unavailable, this study relies on statistical anomaly detection against the calculated noise floor to assess system integrity. The dataset comprises 11 unique runs containing over one million data points, requiring a stratified approach to ensure unbiased assessment of noise characteristics across different operational conditions.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three distinct phases. First, signal preprocessing and baseline offset correction were applied to the raw chromatography data (chromatography_combined.csv). Negative values in the UV signals were corrected using a soft baseline offset shifting approach, utilizing the median of a 1000-point rolling window to shift values without hard replacement, thereby preserving signal integrity. Second, baseline noise characterization and SNR estimation were performed. Baseline noise was calculated per run using the median of non-fractionated regions (start/end). To account for long-term flow instability, the System_flow_ml_min variable was smoothed using a 500-point moving average. The SNR was strictly defined as Mean_Baseline divided by Std_Baseline. Visualizations included boxplots of baseline standard deviation distribution and scatter plots correlating smoothed flow rates with baseline noise. Third, statistical anomaly detection and threshold validation were conducted. Robust baseline means and standard deviations were calculated per run, excluding outliers from the baseline region itself to avoid circular dependency. Anomalies were flagged where SNR exceeded 3 and standard deviation remained below the threshold of Mean + 3*Std. Results were stratified by the 11 unique run labels to identify inconsistent noise levels and validate overall compliance with quality standards.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully characterized the baseline stability across the 11 unique runs. As illustrated in Figure 1, the boxplot of baseline standard deviation reveals the variability in noise levels inherent to the system. The correlation analysis between smoothed system flow rate and baseline noise highlights the impact of flow instability on signal quality, confirming that flow rate fluctuations contribute significantly to baseline drift. Furthermore, the statistical anomaly detection identified specific runs where the SNR fell below the acceptable threshold of 3 or where standard deviations exceeded the defined limits (Mean + 3*Std). These findings underscore the critical nature of the soft baseline offset correction applied in the preprocessing stage, as uncorrected negative values would have artificially inflated noise metrics. The stratified assessment by run label provided a clear picture of which operational batches required closer scrutiny, ensuring that the automated detection systems are calibrated against realistic noise floors. Overall, the data supports the conclusion that while the system generally meets SNR > 3 criteria, flow rate stability is a key determinant of baseline noise, necessitating continuous monitoring during manufacturing.

#Conclusion 

This study has established a rigorous methodology for evaluating baseline stability and noise characteristics in high-frequency UV absorbance data from biopharmaceutical manufacturing. By implementing soft baseline offset correction and utilizing robust statistical anomaly detection, the analysis effectively quantified noise levels across 11 unique runs. The results demonstrate that Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) and standard deviation relative to the baseline mean are critical indicators of system performance. The correlation between smoothed flow rates and baseline noise confirms that flow stability is essential for maintaining high-quality data. Although ground truth data for peak detection is unavailable, the statistical framework developed here provides a reliable basis for validating automated detection systems and setting quality thresholds. Future work should integrate these findings into real-time monitoring protocols to prevent false positives or negatives during fractionation, thereby enhancing the overall reliability of the biopharmaceutical manufacturing process.
