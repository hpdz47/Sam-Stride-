#Abstract 

This report details the analysis of chromatographic separation consistency across 32 experimental runs, where direct retention time metadata was unavailable. Due to significant data sparsity, including the absence of injection volumes and sample codes, the analysis relied on inferring retention times from cumulative fractionation volumes and variable flow rates. Rigorous data integrity checks identified that 11.5% of rows lacked unique fraction IDs and confirmed that flow rates varied significantly between run categories, necessitating run-specific time calculations. The primary objective was to assess system stability by detecting systematic drifts or outliers in the inferred retention times. Visual analysis of the inferred retention time distributions reveals the spread and central tendency across run categories, providing a foundational assessment of process consistency despite the lack of direct temporal data.

#Introduction 

In biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the consistency of chromatographic separation is a critical quality attribute. This analysis focuses on quantifying the reproducibility of retention times across 32 experimental runs to ensure product quality and safety. A significant challenge in this dataset is the absence of direct time metadata and injection-specific variables, which typically allow for precise retention time determination. Consequently, this study employs an indirect method to infer retention times by calculating the time required to elute fractions based on cumulative volumes and measured flow rates. The analysis specifically addresses the complexity introduced by high variance in system flow rates and data gaps, aiming to identify any systematic drifts or outliers that might indicate instrument instability or process deviations. By focusing on run-level consistency, the study provides essential insights into the stability of the chromatographic process under varying operational conditions.

#Methodology 

The analytical workflow was executed in three distinct phases. First, a data integrity and pre-processing phase was conducted on the raw chromatography data. This involved filtering out 125,153 rows (11.5%) missing the critical `Fraction_unique_ID` to ensure valid baseline analysis. The dataset was further processed to handle negative values in volume columns, interpreting them as cumulative deltas, and to verify the mapping between `run_no` and `run` categories, which revealed 32 distinct run numbers mapped to only 11 unique run categories. Second, a run-specific retention time inference phase was performed. Since a constant flow rate assumption was invalid due to high variance in `System_flow_ml_min` (ranging from 0.50 to 1.06 mL/min), retention times were calculated individually for each fraction using the formula: RT = Cumulative Volume / Mean System Flow Rate for that specific run. Peak retention times were identified for each run based on maximum UV absorbance. Third, a systematic drift and outlier detection phase was initiated to evaluate the consistency of these inferred times. This involved calculating the mean and standard deviation of peak retention times across the 11 run categories to visualize the distribution and identify any potential systematic trends or deviations from expected performance.

#Results and Discussion 

The primary result of this analysis is visualized in Figure 1, which presents a boxplot of inferred retention times grouped by run category, overlaid with a line plot representing the mean retention time across all runs. The boxplot illustrates the distribution of peak retention times for each of the 11 run categories, where the boxes denote the interquartile range (IQR), the internal line represents the median, and the whiskers extend to 1.5 times the IQR. The overlay of the mean line is critical for assessing the central tendency and identifying any systematic drift or consistent offset in retention times between the different run categories. The visualization allows for an immediate assessment of the variability (spread) and consistency (alignment of medians and means) across the experimental runs. While the figure provides a clear view of the distribution, it highlights the necessity of accounting for the significant heterogeneity in flow rates observed during pre-processing. The presence of the mean line serves as a reference to determine if specific run categories exhibit significant deviation from the expected performance, which would indicate potential instrument instability or process deviation. However, the current visualization does not quantify the magnitude of the drift or the statistical significance of the differences between groups, nor does it explicitly flag outliers calculated via Z-scores. Nevertheless, the visual evidence underscores the importance of comparing the position and spread of the boxes relative to the mean line to evaluate the overall stability of the chromatographic separation process. The data integrity report confirms that the pre-processing successfully established a valid baseline, with the exclusion of missing IDs and the validation of run mappings supporting the reliability of the inferred retention times presented in Figure 1.

#Conclusion 

This analysis successfully quantified the consistency of chromatographic separation performance across 32 experimental runs by inferring retention times from fractionation data and variable flow rates. The rigorous pre-processing steps, including the exclusion of 11.5% of incomplete data and the correction for flow rate variance, ensured the validity of the inferred metrics. The visualization of retention time distributions by run category (Figure 1) provides a clear assessment of process stability, highlighting the spread and central tendency of retention times. While the current analysis relies on run-level consistency due to missing sample-specific data, it effectively identifies the need for run-specific flow rate calculations and reveals potential areas of deviation. Future analyses should incorporate the systematic drift and outlier detection steps outlined in the plan to quantify the slope of any drift and identify specific outlier runs, thereby providing a more comprehensive evaluation of instrument stability and process control.
