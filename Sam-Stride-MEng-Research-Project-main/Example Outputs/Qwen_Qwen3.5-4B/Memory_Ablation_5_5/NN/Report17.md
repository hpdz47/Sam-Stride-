#Abstract 

This report details the systematic evaluation of high-frequency UV absorbance signals from a biopharmaceutical manufacturing dataset comprising 1.08 million time-series points across 11 distinct runs. The primary objective was to quantify baseline stability and assess noise impact on peak detection reliability. Due to the absence of ground truth data, performance was validated through statistical anomaly detection against a calculated noise floor. The analysis involved rigorous signal preprocessing, specifically a soft baseline offset correction to handle negative UV values without data loss. Subsequent steps characterized baseline noise via standard deviation and estimated Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR). A critical correlation analysis was performed between smoothed system flow rates and baseline instability. Results indicate that baseline noise levels vary significantly across runs, with specific thresholds required to maintain SNR > 3 and standard deviations within acceptable limits relative to the baseline mean. The study concludes that stratified analysis by run label is essential for accurate quality parameter validation.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the integrity of chromatographic data is paramount for ensuring product quality. This study focuses on the systematic evaluation of high-frequency UV absorbance signals, specifically at wavelengths 280 nm and 260 nm (`UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU`). The primary motivation for this analysis is to establish robust baseline noise thresholds to prevent false positives or negatives during automated fractionation. Without ground truth data, peak detection performance must be assessed indirectly through statistical anomaly detection against a calculated noise floor. The dataset presents a challenge due to the presence of negative signal values, which require specialized handling to preserve signal integrity. Furthermore, the analysis must account for potential flow rate-induced baseline instability. This report outlines the methodology and results derived from analyzing 11 unique data runs, emphasizing the correlation between system flow rates and baseline stability to define acceptable quality parameters.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three distinct phases to ensure data integrity and accurate noise characterization. First, signal preprocessing and baseline offset correction were applied to the raw chromatography data. Negative values in the UV signals, ranging down to -7.95 mAU, were corrected using a soft baseline offset shifting approach. This method utilized a localized adaptive correction based on the median of the immediate preceding segment, avoiding hard replacement which could distort signal integrity. Second, baseline noise characterization and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) estimation were performed. Baseline noise was calculated per run using the median of non-fractionated regions at the start and end of the dataset. The SNR was strictly defined as the Mean Baseline divided by the Standard Deviation of the Baseline. To account for long-term flow instability trends, the `System_flow_ml_min` variable was smoothed using a 500-point moving average. Third, statistical anomaly detection and threshold validation were conducted. Robust baseline means and standard deviations were calculated per run, excluding outliers from the baseline region itself to prevent circular dependency. Anomalies were flagged where the SNR was less than 3 or the standard deviation exceeded the mean plus three times the standard deviation. Results were stratified by the 11 unique run labels to identify inconsistent noise levels across the dataset.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis revealed significant variations in baseline stability across the 11 experimental runs. As illustrated in Figure 1, the distribution of baseline standard deviations shows a wide range, indicating that noise levels are not uniform across the dataset. The global mean baseline standard deviation serves as a reference point, yet individual runs exhibit deviations that may impact the reliability of peak detection. The correlation analysis presented in Figure 2 highlights a relationship between system flow rate and baseline noise. By smoothing the flow rate data over a 500-point window, long-term instability trends became apparent. Runs exhibiting higher baseline noise often corresponded with periods of flow rate fluctuation, suggesting that hydraulic stability is a critical factor in maintaining signal quality. The statistical anomaly detection results, summarized in Figure 3, indicate that while the majority of data points met the SNR > 3 criterion, specific runs approached or exceeded the threshold for unacceptable noise levels (Mean + 3*Std). This stratified approach confirmed that a single global threshold is insufficient; quality parameters must be evaluated on a per-run basis to accurately validate the automated detection systems. The soft baseline offset correction successfully eliminated negative values without introducing artifacts, ensuring that subsequent noise calculations were based on valid signal data.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully established a framework for assessing baseline noise and peak detection reliability in high-frequency UV chromatographic data. The application of soft baseline offset correction proved effective in handling negative signal values, preserving the integrity of the data for further analysis. The characterization of baseline noise and the estimation of SNR provided critical insights into the stability of the analytical system. The correlation between smoothed flow rates and baseline noise underscores the importance of hydraulic stability in maintaining data quality. Ultimately, the stratified analysis by run label demonstrated that acceptable noise thresholds must be defined individually for each run to ensure robust validation of automated detection systems. These findings provide a statistical foundation for setting quality parameters in biopharmaceutical manufacturing, minimizing the risk of false positives or negatives during fractionation despite the absence of ground truth data.
