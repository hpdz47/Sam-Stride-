#Abstract 

This report summarizes the data analysis workflow designed to assess chromatographic performance from a High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) dataset comprising approximately 1.1 million fractions across 32 runs. The primary objective was to identify significant peaks using a dynamic signal-to-noise ratio and characterize their performance metrics according to United States Pharmacopeia (USP) standards. Due to the absence of explicit timestamps and concentration data in over 60% of rows, the analysis relied on reconstructing retention times from ordinal fraction numbers and calculating shape-based metrics such as Resolution, Tailing Factor, and Theoretical Plates. The results indicate a critical systemic failure in peak separation or column performance, as 100% of the 20 successfully characterized peaks failed to meet USP criteria for Resolution (Rs < 1.5) or Tailing Factor (Tf > 1.8). Furthermore, data integrity checks revealed significant heterogeneity in run duration and data density, alongside systematic gaps in unique identifiers that may introduce bias. These findings suggest that the current experimental parameters or instrument configuration require immediate adjustment to ensure product quality and safety.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the systematic identification and characterization of chromatographic peaks are essential for ensuring product quality and safety. This study focuses on a dataset representing High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) time-series data, characterized by a high volume of fractions (~1.1 million) and a lack of explicit timestamps. The primary challenge addressed in this analysis is the derivation of accurate retention times and the assessment of peak performance in the absence of concentration data (`Conc_B_ml`), which is missing in over 60% of the dataset rows. Consequently, the analytical strategy shifted from quantitative purity and yield assessment to qualitative evaluation based on chromatographic shape metrics, specifically Resolution, Tailing Factor, and Theoretical Plates, as defined by USP and IUPAC standards. The analysis aims to detect significant absorbance events using a dynamic signal-to-noise ratio and to evaluate the robustness of the data collection process, addressing issues such as negative volume corrections and missing unique identifiers.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three distinct phases. First, a data integrity and preprocessing step was conducted on the `chromatography_combined.csv` file. This involved filtering rows with missing UV absorbance data, correcting negative `volume_ml` values to prevent integration errors, and reconstructing the time-series by filling missing `Fraction_unique_ID`s using the ordinality of `Fraction_number`. Retention times were derived for each fraction using the formula: `Retention_Time = (Fraction_number * Mean_Fraction_Duration) + Run_Offset`, where Mean_Fraction_Duration was calculated from individual fraction volumes and system flow rates. Second, a dynamic signal-to-noise ratio assessment was performed to detect peaks. The baseline was defined as absorbance values below 20 mAU, and noise was calculated as the standard deviation within a 50-fraction rolling window. Peaks were flagged where the signal height exceeded three times the dynamic noise. Third, peak shape and performance metrics were calculated for the top 20 peaks. This phase involved computing baseline width, Resolution, Tailing Factor, and Theoretical Plates using standard chromatographic formulas. Purity ratios were calculated conditionally where dual-wavelength data (`UV_2_260_mAU`) was available. The final output included a summary of USP criterion failures and a detailed table of performance metrics.

#Results and Discussion 

The data integrity analysis revealed substantial variability in the experimental runs. While Runs 1, 2, 4, and 16 exhibited high data density with approximately 130,000 fractions and long retention times extending up to 9,345 minutes, Runs 18, 19, 20, and 21 showed drastically reduced data counts (42,000–74,000 fractions) and compressed retention times (maximum 590 minutes). This heterogeneity suggests potential incomplete data collection or shifts in experimental parameters such as flow rate or fractionation strategy. Additionally, systematic data gaps ranging from 21 to 82 fractions were observed across multiple runs, indicating possible instrument interruptions or alignment errors that could bias peak detection. The reconstruction of unique IDs from ordinal fraction numbers was successful, but the wide standard deviations in retention time distributions underscore the complexity of the underlying data structure. 

Regarding peak performance, the analysis of the top 20 identified peaks yielded critical findings regarding chromatographic quality. The results indicate that 100% of these peaks failed to meet USP criteria, specifically failing the Resolution threshold (Rs < 1.5) or the Tailing Factor requirement (Tf > 1.8). This universal failure suggests a systemic issue with peak separation or column performance rather than isolated anomalies. The calculation of Theoretical Plates and Purity Ratios was largely impeded by missing data; specifically, the absence of `UV_2_260_mAU` data for the aggregated peak regions prevented purity calculations, and insufficient peak width data in the first 9 rows led to failed calculations for Resolution and Theoretical Plates. Consequently, the analysis relied solely on shape metrics which were insufficient to validate the product quality against established standards. The failure rate contradicts the initial goal of characterizing performance via USP standards, necessitating a review of the chromatographic method and instrument configuration.

#Conclusion 

The comprehensive analysis of the HPLC dataset highlights significant challenges in data integrity and chromatographic performance. While the methodology successfully reconstructed the time-series and identified peaks using a dynamic signal-to-noise approach, the results demonstrate a critical failure in meeting quality standards. The 100% failure rate for USP Resolution and Tailing Factor criteria across all analyzed peaks indicates that the current experimental setup is unable to achieve the required separation or column efficiency. Furthermore, the observed heterogeneity in run durations and the presence of systematic data gaps suggest that the data collection process itself is inconsistent, potentially introducing further bias into the analysis. Given the absence of concentration data, the reliance on shape metrics alone is insufficient to guarantee product quality. Immediate corrective actions are required, including an investigation into the causes of data gaps, a review of the fractionation strategy to ensure consistent run parameters, and potential optimization of the chromatographic method to improve peak resolution and tailing characteristics.
