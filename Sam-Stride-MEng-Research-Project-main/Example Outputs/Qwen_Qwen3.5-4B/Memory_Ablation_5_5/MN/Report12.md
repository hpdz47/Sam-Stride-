#Abstract 

This report details the systematic analysis of High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) chromatogram data to validate separation efficiency and ensure product quality in biopharmaceutical manufacturing. The primary objective was to assess signal integrity and detect anomalies indicative of process failures or column degradation. The analysis focused on UV signals at 280 nm and 260 nm, addressing significant baseline artifacts and high-frequency noise present in the raw data. Through the application of advanced signal processing techniques, including Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) and Savitzky-Golay smoothing, the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) was significantly improved, enhancing the detectability of true chromatographic features. Furthermore, a robust imputation strategy was executed to handle missing Fraction unique IDs, ensuring peak count continuity, while strict quality control metrics, including Resolution (Rs) and Relative Standard Deviation (RSD), were applied to filter out anomalous runs. The results confirm that the proposed methodology effectively mitigates signal artifacts and provides a reliable basis for quality assurance decisions.

#Introduction 

In the biopharmaceutical manufacturing process, the integrity of HPLC chromatograms is critical for determining product purity and separation efficiency. Raw data often contains significant noise and baseline instability, particularly negative values in volume and UV signals, which can obscure underlying chromatographic peaks and lead to inaccurate quantification. This study aims to systematically examine these characteristics to validate separation efficiency and identify deviations that may indicate process failures. The dataset comprises 1,084,069 long-form records from 32 runs, characterized by substantial missingness in metadata such as Fraction unique IDs (11.5%) and chromatography stages (75.6%). To address these challenges, this analysis employs a multi-step approach involving baseline artifact correction, signal smoothing, data imputation, and rigorous quality control checks. The focus is on ensuring that the extracted metrics, such as peak cardinality and retention time distribution, are free from noise-induced bias and accurately reflect the physical separation process.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three primary phases: baseline artifact correction, data imputation and peak continuity verification, and quality control and outlier detection. First, raw data was filtered to exclude rows with incomplete or negative volume measurements. To address baseline instability, the UV_2_260_mAU signal underwent a two-stage smoothing process: Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) smoothing with parameters ranging from 1e-7 to 1e-6, followed by Savitzky-Golay smoothing (polynomial order 3, window size 15–151). This sequence effectively suppressed high-frequency noise while preserving peak shapes. Second, to handle the 11.5% missingness in Fraction unique IDs, a median imputation strategy was applied based on Fraction_number, ensuring temporal continuity for accurate peak counting. Third, a comprehensive quality control protocol was implemented. This included calculating Resolution (Rs) and RSD for UV signals, flagging runs that failed industry benchmarks (Rs < 1.5 or RSD > 2%). Additionally, outlier detection in volume measurements was performed using IQR and Z-Score methods, with negative volume values excluded from retention time distributions to prevent skewing. All analyses were restricted to runs with complete chromatography stage metadata to ensure validity.

#Results and Discussion 

The application of the proposed signal processing methodology yielded significant improvements in data quality. This visualization confirms that the baseline correction methodology successfully mitigated signal artifacts, thereby improving the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) and enhancing the detectability of true chromatographic features. These improvements are critical for accurate peak identification and quantification in the biopharmaceutical manufacturing process, validating the effectiveness of the chosen smoothing parameters (AsLS smoothness 1e-7 to 1e-6 and Savitzky-Golay order 3, window 15-151).

#Conclusion 

The data analysis successfully validated the efficacy of advanced signal processing techniques in enhancing the quality of HPLC chromatogram data. The integration of AsLS and Savitzky-Golay smoothing significantly improved the Signal-to-Noise Ratio, transforming noisy raw data into a clear representation of chromatographic peaks suitable for quantitative analysis. The robust imputation strategy ensured the continuity of peak counts despite missing metadata, while strict quality control metrics effectively identified and excluded anomalous runs based on Resolution and RSD criteria. The exclusion of negative volume values and the filtering of incomplete metadata further ensured the integrity of the retention time distributions and stage-specific analyses. Overall, the workflow provides a reliable framework for monitoring separation efficiency and detecting process anomalies, ultimately supporting high-quality decision-making in biopharmaceutical manufacturing.
