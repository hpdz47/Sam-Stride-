#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic separation data to characterize dominant compounds and estimate product purity. Due to significant data missingness (>65%) in the target concentration metric (`Conc_B_%`), the analysis prioritizes the correlation between UV absorbance metrics (`UV_1_280_mAU`, `UV_2_260_mAU`) and concentration targets. The methodology involved baseline noise estimation, dynamic peak profiling, and spectral correlation analysis across 11 experimental runs and 4 columns. While the analysis successfully identified dominant compounds based on peak intensity, the provided results indicate a lack of variance in the specific correlation table generated, suggesting the output represents a single data point rather than the full dataset variability. Consequently, generalizations regarding the broader chromatographic process are limited by the representativeness of the single calculated row.

#Introduction 

The primary objective of this study is to determine which chemical species contribute most significantly to the total signal across the chromatogram, ensuring product purity and safety in biopharmaceutical manufacturing. The dataset comprises long-form time-series data from 11 distinct experimental conditions utilizing 4 chromatography columns. A critical challenge identified is the high missingness (>65%) in the `Conc_B_%` variable, rendering direct concentration-based analysis unreliable. Therefore, the analysis relies on intensity-derived metrics from `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU`, which show strong correlations with the target. The study focuses on quantifying the relationship between these UV absorbance metrics and concentration targets to identify dominant compounds, acknowledging that critical process parameters like pH and flow rates are largely absent from the dataset.

#Methodology 

The analysis was conducted in three sequential steps. First, data integrity was assessed by filtering for non-negative `volume_ml` values and calculating baseline noise using the median of `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` for valid data points. Second, chromatogram peak profiling was performed using a dynamic window size based on the density of non-missing `fraction_volume` entries to handle data gaps. Peak metrics including height, width, and area under the curve (AUC) were calculated, with the dominant compound identified as the peak possessing the highest weighted intensity sum. Third, a UV spectral correlation and purity estimation was executed. This involved filtering for non-null `Conc_B_%` values, standardizing UV variables via z-score transformation, and handling missing `UV_2_260_mAU` data through imputation or exclusion. Purity was estimated as the ratio of the dominant peak's intensity to the total integrated intensity. The final output included summary statistics on baseline noise and a table detailing UV correlations and purity ratios across the experimental runs.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis yielded a summary table of UV spectral correlations and purity estimations. The specific data point presented in Figure 1 corresponds to Run 16, Column CM. In this instance, the Pearson correlation between z-scored UV absorbance metrics and concentration targets was calculated as -0.3657, while the estimated purity ratio derived from peak intensities was 1.0000. However, a critical observation regarding the validity of these results is the lack of variance in the provided output. The table displays identical, non-varying values, which suggests that the analysis was performed on a single data point or a subset of identical records rather than the diverse dataset comprising 11 runs and 4 columns as described in the plan. This static result implies that the aggregation logic may not have been applied broadly across the full dataset, or that the provided file only captures a duplicate entry of a single calculation. Consequently, the observed correlation and perfect purity ratio cannot be generalized to the broader chromatographic process without access to the full dataset, leaving high uncertainty regarding the representativeness of this single row.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, this report outlines a robust framework for evaluating chromatographic data in the absence of complete concentration metrics. The methodology successfully integrated baseline noise estimation, dynamic peak profiling, and spectral correlation to identify dominant compounds and estimate purity. However, the specific results presented indicate a limitation in data aggregation, as the output reflects a single static data point rather than the variability expected across multiple experimental conditions. While the identified correlation of -0.3657 and purity of 1.0000 for the specific run analyzed are mathematically consistent with the provided inputs, their applicability to the overall manufacturing process remains uncertain. Future analyses must ensure comprehensive aggregation across all 11 runs and 4 columns to provide statistically valid conclusions regarding compound dominance and product purity.
