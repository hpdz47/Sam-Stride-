#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic separation performance and compound quantification derived from High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) signals. The primary objective was to establish a robust concentration profile by normalizing peak areas to determine the relative proportion of Compound B within each fraction. The analysis addressed critical data quality issues, specifically severe baseline drift indicated by negative absorbance values, through the application of Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) correction. Subsequent steps involved aggregating corrected signals into peak areas, normalizing these areas to relative abundance ratios, and validating the quantification method against observed concentration data. The results confirm that AsLS correction effectively stabilizes the baseline, enabling accurate peak area calculation. However, the analysis identified specific outlier fractions where signal saturation or residual baseline errors may have compromised the normalized ratio, highlighting the necessity of rigorous pre-processing for reliable Critical Quality Attribute (CQA) assessment.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic separation is essential for validating process consistency and ensuring product safety and efficacy. This analysis focuses on a large time-series dataset comprising approximately 1.1 million rows of HPLC data, where the primary signal of interest is the UV absorbance at 280 nm (`UV_1_280_mAU`). The core challenge in this dataset is the presence of significant baseline drift, evidenced by negative absorbance values, which can severely skew peak area calculations if uncorrected. Furthermore, the dataset suffers from substantial missingness in auxiliary variables such as flow rate and pressure, limiting the ability to correlate elution conditions with concentration profiles. The motivation for this specific workflow is to convert raw absorbance units into normalized ratios, thereby enabling the identification of Critical Quality Attributes (CQAs) such as purity, impurity levels, and yield distribution. By accurately quantifying Compound B across fractions, this analysis aims to provide a reliable metric for assessing whether the manufacturing process meets established safety and efficacy standards, or if deviations indicate upstream process failures.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three distinct phases to ensure data integrity and accurate quantification. First, Signal Pre-processing and Baseline Correction were applied to the raw `UV_1_280_mAU` data using the Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) algorithm. This step was critical to remove the non-constant baseline drift characterized by upward slopes and negative values. The correction was visualized by comparing the raw signal against the corrected signal across representative fractions to verify the stabilization of the baseline near zero during elution phases. Second, Peak Area Normalization and Fraction Aggregation were performed. Corrected signals were aggregated by `Fraction_unique_ID` to calculate total peak area. These areas were then normalized relative to the global sum to generate a `Normalized_Ratio_%`, ranging from 0% to 100%. This normalized metric was cross-referenced with observed concentration data (`Conc_B_%`) to validate the quantification method. Third, Quality Attribute Validation and Purity Assessment were conducted by grouping data by `run` batch. This involved calculating mean purity and yield per batch, handling low cardinality data using jittered points for visualization, and identifying outliers using the Interquartile Range (IQR) method. The entire process strictly utilized the `UV_1_280_mAU` variable, avoiding ambiguous intensity variables, and accounted for missing fraction IDs to ensure accurate proportion calculations.

#Results and Discussion 

The initial phase of the analysis successfully demonstrated the efficacy of the AsLS baseline correction. As illustrated in Figure 1, the raw signal exhibited significant baseline drift, characterized by a gradual upward slope and negative values at the start of the chromatographic run. These artifacts would otherwise lead to inaccurate peak area calculations if summed directly. The application of the AsLS correction, represented by the blue line in Figure 1, effectively removed this non-constant baseline, stabilizing the signal closer to zero during elution phases where no analyte was present. This visual confirmation validates the necessity of the pre-processing step before proceeding to quantification. However, while the correction appears successful, the absence of annotated algorithm parameters in the figure suggests that further methodological documentation regarding smoothing factors and lambda values is required for full reproducibility. In the subsequent aggregation and normalization phase, the data revealed a distinct separation between low-concentration fractions, which showed negligible or zero concentration, and high-concentration fractions exhibiting a strong, linear correlation between peak area and observed concentration. The normalization process proved robust for the majority of the dataset, with normalized ratios increasing proportionally with observed concentration up to approximately 70% for the highest purity fractions. A critical finding, however, was the identification of outlier data points, such as fraction R16_3H10, which displayed a high observed concentration but a significantly suppressed normalized ratio. This discrepancy suggests potential baseline correction errors or signal saturation issues in that specific fraction, indicating that while the overall methodology is feasible, specific fractions require closer inspection to ensure data fidelity. Overall, the analysis confirms that `UV_1_280_mAU` is a viable signal for quantification, provided that baseline drift is adequately corrected and outliers are managed.

#Conclusion 

This analysis successfully established a robust framework for the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic separation performance. By applying AsLS baseline correction, the study effectively mitigated the impact of severe baseline drift and negative absorbance values, enabling the accurate calculation of peak areas and the derivation of normalized purity ratios. The validation against observed concentration data confirmed the feasibility of using UV absorbance at 280 nm for quantifying Compound B, demonstrating a strong linear correlation in the majority of high-concentration fractions. These results are critical for validating process consistency and ensuring that the biopharmaceutical product meets safety and efficacy standards. However, the identification of specific outlier fractions highlights the need for continued monitoring and potential refinement of the baseline correction parameters or signal processing thresholds. Future work should focus on investigating the causes of the identified outliers and expanding the purity assessment to cover the full chromatographic run to ensure comprehensive CQA validation across all manufacturing batches.
