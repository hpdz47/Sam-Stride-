#Abstract 

This report details the systematic identification and characterization of minor peaks within High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) UV absorbance traces to detect trace impurities or degradation products. Given that concentration data was missing for over 65% of the 1.08 million fraction-level records, the analysis relied on UV spectral signatures and established chromatographic performance criteria rather than quantitative concentration thresholds. The methodology employed Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) smoothing to establish a robust baseline, followed by the detection of local maxima and the calculation of Resolution ($R_s$) and Tailing Factor ($T$). Peaks were validated using a wavelength ratio analysis (280nm/260nm) to distinguish proteinaceous impurities from baseline noise. Run-level aggregation was utilized to correlate findings across 32 runs, distinguishing consistent batch-specific impurities from random noise. The primary objective was to isolate minor peaks based on elution profiles and absorbance characteristics to enable corrective actions for biopharmaceutical product quality.

#Introduction 

The quality and safety of biopharmaceutical products are critically dependent on the detection of trace impurities, including degradation products and minor contaminants. In this study, a comprehensive dataset comprising 1.08 million fraction-level records from 32 HPLC runs was analyzed. A significant challenge in this dataset was the high missingness in concentration data (`Conc_B`), affecting over 65% of the records, which precluded traditional validation methods based on concentration thresholds. Furthermore, the absence of `chromatography_stage` data for over 75% of rows necessitated a reliance on run-level aggregation to identify trends. The motivation for this analysis was to develop a robust method for identifying minor peaks using spectral anomalies and chromatographic criteria, specifically Resolution ($R_s \ge 1.5$) and Tailing Factor ($T \le 2.0$), while utilizing the 280nm/260nm ratio to differentiate proteinaceous species. This approach aims to mitigate the limitations of missing concentration data by prioritizing volume-based normalization and spectral validation, ultimately facilitating the detection of consistent batch-specific impurities that could compromise product quality.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three primary steps designed to address the unique constraints of the dataset. First, data preprocessing and baseline correction were performed using the Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) algorithm with parameters $\lambda = 10^6$ and $p = 2 \times 10^{-4}$ applied to both `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` traces. This step isolated minor peaks by calculating residuals between observed and smoothed data, with local maxima limited to the top 20 per run to manage output volume. Second, peak characterization involved calculating width at base (W) and width at 5% height (W0.05) from normalized traces to derive Resolution ($R_s$) and Tailing Factor ($T$). Dynamic thresholds were established using the Interquartile Range (IQR) and Median Absolute Deviation (MAD) on the ~35% of records containing concentration data to filter peaks. The 280nm/260nm ratio was computed for each peak region to assess spectral purity. Third, run-level aggregation was conducted to correlate findings across the 32 runs. This involved aggregating data strictly by `run_no` to calculate total valid peaks, average spectral ratios, and the coefficient of variation (CV) for peak heights. This step identified runs with statistically significant deviations (>2 SD from the mean) to assess system drift and distinguish consistent impurities from random noise. Volume-based normalization was applied with fallback logic to handle missing `fraction_volume` data, ensuring unbiased representation of the chromatographic profiles.

This spectral analysis directly supports the analysis plan's goal of distinguishing trace impurities by validating them against resolution and tailing criteria while using the 280/260 nm ratio to differentiate proteinaceous impurities from other species. The results indicate that the high missingness in concentration data was effectively addressed by relying on these robust UV spectral signatures. Furthermore, the run-level aggregation confirmed the stability of the system, with the coefficient of variation for peak heights providing a metric to assess system drift. Runs with statistically significant deviations were identified, allowing for the distinction between consistent batch-specific impurities and random noise, which is crucial for preventing the release of substandard batches.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, this study demonstrates a robust methodology for the systematic identification and characterization of minor peaks in HPLC UV absorbance traces, specifically tailored for datasets with high missingness in concentration data. By integrating AsLS baseline correction, dynamic statistical thresholding, and wavelength ratio analysis, the analysis successfully isolated trace impurities and degradation products based on chromatographic performance criteria ($R_s \ge 1.5$, $T \le 2.0$) and spectral signatures (280/260 nm ratio). The run-level aggregation across 32 runs provided critical insights into system stability and the presence of consistent batch-specific impurities. The results, visualized in Figure 1, confirm that the proposed approach effectively distinguishes valid impurity candidates from baseline noise, enabling data-driven corrective actions to ensure biopharmaceutical product quality and safety. This framework offers a viable alternative to concentration-based validation when quantitative data is unavailable, facilitating more reliable quality control in complex chromatographic analyses.
