#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic peak characteristics to assess product quality and safety during biopharmaceutical manufacturing. The analysis focuses on a filtered subset of approximately 88% of records from the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset, where precise volume boundaries are available. The primary objective was to identify deviations in peak shape quality, specifically width and symmetry, which may indicate process instability, column degradation, or sample impurities. Methodology involved data normalization of negative volume offsets, calculation of peak width, and the application of IQR-based Z-score outlier detection. Results indicate significant variability in peak widths across experimental runs, with several runs exhibiting long right tails and numerous outliers. These findings suggest that the current process stability may be compromised in specific runs, necessitating further investigation into column condition and sample heterogeneity.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the integrity of chromatographic peaks is a critical indicator of product purity and process robustness. This analysis aims to quantify peak characteristics—specifically width, height, and symmetry—derived from high-resolution time-series data. The dataset under review, `chromatography_combined.csv`, contains over 1.08 million long-form records. However, critical peak shape metrics are only available for fractions containing detectable peaks, resulting in a significant selection bias where flat baseline data is excluded. This report addresses the challenge of assessing stability using only peak-specific metrics while acknowledging the limitations imposed by missing data for 125,153 records. The analysis strictly adheres to a protocol that normalizes negative `volume_ml` values to zero and distinguishes between global run indices and local fraction counters to ensure accurate aggregation. By employing robust statistical methods, including IQR-based outlier detection, this study seeks to generate actionable insights on peak integrity across different runs and columns.

#Methodology 

The analytical workflow was structured into three distinct phases to ensure data integrity and statistical rigor. First, a data filtering and normalization phase was conducted. Rows were filtered to retain only records where both `fraction_volume_ml_min_precise` and `fraction_volume_ml_max_precise` were non-null, ensuring that only fractions with precise volume metrics were analyzed. Negative values in `volume_ml` were normalized to zero to correct for cumulative offsets or noise artifacts. Peak width was calculated as the difference between the maximum and minimum precise volumes, with rows exhibiting widths less than 0.001 excluded. Second, a peak shape quality and outlier detection phase was executed. The Tailing Factor was calculated using the formula (R + 2*W)/2R, where R is the minimum volume and W is the maximum volume. Simultaneously, Z-scores for `fraction_volume` were computed using the Interquartile Range (IQR) method (1.5 * IQR) to identify extreme values. Third, a multivariate scoring approach was conceptualized for future expansion, though the current phase focused on univariate outlier detection. The analysis distinguished between global run indices (`Unnamed: 0`) and local fraction counters (`Fraction_number`) to prevent aggregation errors, ensuring that trends were assessed at the run level rather than the individual fraction level.

#Results and Discussion 

The visualization presented in Figure 1 provides a critical view of the distribution of peak widths (`fraction_volume`) across different experimental runs (`run_no`). The boxplot confirms that the initial data filtering step was successful, as the data points represent only fractions where precise volume metrics were available, accounting for approximately 88% of the total records. The plot reveals significant variability in peak widths across the analyzed runs. Specifically, several runs exhibit long right tails and a high density of highlighted outliers. These outliers, defined by the IQR-based Z-score method, represent extreme values in peak width that deviate significantly from the median. The presence of these outliers suggests potential process instability, column degradation, or sample heterogeneity within those specific runs. While the methodological approach of using Z-scores is appropriate for identifying extreme values, the visualization does not explicitly display the Tailing Factor calculations or the relationship between peak width and UV absorbance. Furthermore, the absence of error bars limits the assessment of the precision of the central tendency. Despite these limitations, the identification of runs with anomalous peak widths is a crucial first step in diagnosing process deviations. The data indicates that while the majority of runs may exhibit stable peak shapes, a subset requires immediate investigation to determine if the outliers are due to technical artifacts or genuine sample quality issues.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis successfully identified significant deviations in peak width distribution across specific experimental runs. The exclusion of flat baseline fractions, while introducing selection bias, provided a robust dataset for assessing peak-specific metrics like width and symmetry. The visualization of outliers highlights runs that likely suffer from process instability or column degradation. Future analyses should expand to include multivariate peak quality scoring, incorporating height and symmetry metrics alongside width, to provide a more comprehensive assessment of peak integrity. Additionally, investigating the correlation between outlier frequency and UV absorbance levels could clarify whether these width anomalies are associated with impurity profiles. Until then, the runs exhibiting long right tails and high outlier counts should be flagged for further technical review to ensure product safety and quality consistency.
