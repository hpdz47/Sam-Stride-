#Abstract 

This report details a rigorous data integrity assessment of High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) chromatographic data generated during biopharmaceutical manufacturing. The analysis addresses critical challenges including detector saturation, data corruption, and significant run-to-run variability within a dataset of 1.08 million fraction-level observations. Primary objectives involved filtering physically impossible negative values, reconciling discrepancies between primary volume metrics and precise measurements, and distinguishing legitimate high-concentration peaks from saturation artifacts exceeding 1500 mAU. Statistical assessments were stratified by 11 unique run identifiers to ensure batch-specific accuracy. Results confirm that a subset of runs suffers from severe data corruption and detector saturation, necessitating the application of Interquartile Range (IQR) and Z-score filtering strategies. The study concludes that baseline instability is a primary driver of low signal-to-noise ratios, and without the proposed exclusion criteria and imputation protocols, downstream manufacturing data validity would be compromised.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the integrity of chromatographic data is paramount for ensuring product quality and regulatory compliance. This dataset comprises 1.08 million fraction-level observations derived from HPLC and MS analyses, presenting significant challenges regarding data completeness and accuracy. Approximately 76% of the rows lack critical metadata such as `Sample_Code`, and the presence of negative values in `volume_ml` and absorbance columns indicates baseline errors or data corruption. Furthermore, the dataset exhibits a discrepancy between 32 unique `run_no` identifiers and only 11 unique `run` identifiers, which poses a risk of skewing statistical results if not properly managed. The primary motivation for this analysis was to establish a robust pre-processing framework that distinguishes legitimate high-concentration peaks from detector saturation artifacts and experimental failures. By applying mandatory filtering for negative values and saturation thresholds (>1500 mAU), and by reconciling volume discrepancies, the analysis aims to isolate corrupted records and flag deviations that could compromise product quality. The study specifically targets the detection of unusual spikes, missing peaks, and statistical outliers to enable timely corrective actions before downstream processing.

#Methodology 

The analytical workflow was executed in three distinct phases to ensure data quality and statistical validity. First, a data integrity and pre-processing phase was conducted on the `chromatography_combined.csv` file. This involved removing rows containing negative `volume_ml` or UV absorbance values exceeding 1500 mAU. A reconciliation logic was applied to flag rows where `volume_ml` differed from `fraction_volume_ml_min_precise` by more than 5% or was negative while the precise value was valid. Metadata, specifically `Sample_Code` and `chromatography_stage`, was imputed using a 'forward fill' strategy within `run`/`column` groups, with unmatchable rows flagged as 'Unknown' and excluded from main analysis. Second, a stratified outlier and peak detection phase was performed. Baseline noise was estimated as the mean absorbance of the first 10 valid fractions per run. Outliers were identified using both IQR and Z-score methods (flagging values >3*sigma from the mean), and peaks were detected as local maxima exceeding the baseline noise plus 3*sigma within a 5-fraction smoothing window. Third, a run-to-run variability assessment was conducted. Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) were calculated by dividing integrated peak area by baseline noise. Spearman correlations were performed to assess the relationship between `column` and SNR/outlier counts, and ANOVA tests were used to evaluate differences in mean peak height and area across run groups. All statistical assessments were strictly stratified by the 11 unique `run` identifiers to prevent false positives associated with labeling inconsistencies.

#Results and Discussion 

The diagnostic assessment presented in Figure 1 provides a comprehensive view of the data quality issues identified during the analysis. Panel A of Figure 1 displays boxplots of UV absorbance at 280nm and 260nm stratified by the 11 unique `run` identifiers. These visualizations confirm significant inter-run variability in peak heights and distributions, with several runs exhibiting extreme outliers indicative of potential detector saturation or experimental failure. Panel B illustrates representative chromatograms for three selected runs, highlighting the presence of sharp, high-amplitude spikes that exceed the 1500 mAU threshold, consistent with the plan's saturation filtering criteria. Panel C provides a bar chart comparing mean peak area (AUC) against baseline noise, revealing a strong inverse relationship where high-noise runs yield lower signal-to-noise ratios (SNR), suggesting that baseline instability is a primary driver of data quality issues. Finally, Panel D tabulates outlier counts per run, identifying specific batches with elevated anomaly rates. Collectively, these visualizations support the conclusion that a subset of runs suffers from data corruption (negative volumes implied by noise) and detector saturation, necessitating the exclusion of saturated peaks and the application of the proposed IQR/Z-score filtering strategies to ensure downstream manufacturing data validity.

#Conclusion 

This analysis demonstrates that the current dataset contains substantial data integrity issues, primarily driven by detector saturation and baseline instability. The rigorous application of negative value filtering, saturation thresholding (>1500 mAU), and volume reconciliation has successfully isolated corrupted records. The stratification by unique `run` identifiers proved essential in preventing false positives and accurately characterizing batch-specific variability. The strong inverse correlation between baseline noise and signal-to-noise ratio indicates that baseline instability is a critical factor affecting data quality. Consequently, the implementation of mandatory exclusion criteria for saturated peaks and the adoption of the proposed statistical filtering strategies are imperative for maintaining data validity. Future work should focus on investigating the root causes of the identified saturation events and baseline instabilities to prevent recurrence and ensure the reliability of the biopharmaceutical manufacturing process.
