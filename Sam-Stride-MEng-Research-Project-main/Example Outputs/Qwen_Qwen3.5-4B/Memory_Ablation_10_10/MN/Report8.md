#Abstract 

This report details the systematic extraction and characterization of chromatographic signals from high-resolution HPLC fraction-level datasets. The primary objective was to identify and quantify analyte peaks within UV absorbance signals (`UV_1_280_mAU`, `UV_2_260_mAU`) across a 10.8 million row dataset comprising 32 HPLC runs. Due to prevalent gaps in concentration metadata and sample codes, the analysis relied exclusively on UV signals and volume proxies to ensure product quality and safety. The workflow involved rigorous data cleaning using statistical filtering (Mean ± 3σ) to remove artifacts, followed by peak detection algorithms based on derivative thresholds and Signal-to-Noise Ratios. The final output provides a comprehensive inventory of peaks per run, serving as a foundation for downstream integration and purity assessment.

#Introduction 

The analysis of high-resolution HPLC data requires robust methodologies to distinguish significant analyte peaks from noise and data artifacts. This study focuses on a large-scale dataset containing 10.8 million rows representing individual fractions from 32 HPLC runs. While the dataset exhibits high completeness in UV absorbance measurements, critical metadata fields such as `Conc_B_ml` and `Sample_Code` are largely missing, necessitating a reliance on spectral and temporal features for peak identification. The primary challenge addressed in this workflow is the systematic filtering of noise-induced artifacts, specifically negative volume and absorbance values, which are indicative of data errors. The motivation for this analysis is to generate a reliable inventory of chromatographic peaks, mapping fraction indices to cumulative volumes to derive precise retention times, areas, and shapes. This inventory is essential for ensuring product quality and safety, despite the limitations imposed by incomplete concentration data.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three primary phases: data cleaning, peak detection, and run-level aggregation. First, a robust statistical filtering approach was applied to the raw `chromatography_data.csv` file. Rows containing negative values or deviations greater than three standard deviations from the mean (calculated for `volume_ml` and UV columns) were removed to eliminate noise artifacts. Second, peak detection and integration were performed on the cleaned dataset. Negative values in UV columns were masked, and baselines were estimated using linear interpolation between local maxima. Peaks were identified using a dual-criteria approach: a derivative threshold (slope change > 0.5 mAU/unit volume) combined with a minimum Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) of 3. A minimum height threshold was also applied to filter insignificant peaks. Third, run-level aggregation was conducted to summarize peak data by `run_no`. Summary statistics including peak count, total area, average peak height, and retention time ranges were calculated. Runs with zero peaks or total area below 10% of the median were flagged as anomalies. Concentration data was explicitly noted as unavailable due to missing metadata.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully isolated significant peaks from the high-resolution fraction data, as evidenced by the multi-panel visualization presented in Figure 1. Panel 1 of Figure 1 displays a line plot of `UV_1_280_mAU` versus volume for the first five runs, featuring a shaded region representing the estimated baseline and vertical markers indicating detected peaks. The visual confirmation supports the application of a peak detection algorithm that identifies local maxima, with the baseline appearing to be estimated via linear interpolation between peaks as planned. However, the figure lacks explicit labels for the statistical parameters used, making it difficult to verify if the filtering criteria (slope > 0.5 mAU/unit, SNR > 3) were strictly adhered to without referencing the underlying code. Panel 2 of Figure 1 shows a histogram of filtered peak areas, which follows a right-skewed distribution typical of chromatographic data. This distribution demonstrates a clear separation between low-area noise artifacts and high-area significant peaks, suggesting that the area thresholding was effective in isolating valid signals. Panel 3 of Figure 1 presents a scatter plot of `UV_1_280_mAU` versus `UV_2_260_mAU` peak heights, revealing a strong positive correlation. This correlation is expected for co-eluting analytes or consistent protein samples, validating the consistency of the UV signals across wavelengths. A primary methodological limitation observed is the absence of error bars or confidence intervals in the plots, which prevents an assessment of the variability in peak detection across different runs. Additionally, the figure does not explicitly show the handling of negative values in the raw data, though the clean appearance of the plotted signals suggests successful artifact filtering. Overall, the results indicate that significant peaks were successfully isolated, but the lack of detailed statistical annotations limits the ability to fully validate the robustness of the peak detection parameters without further investigation.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis workflow successfully extracted and characterized chromatographic signals from a large-scale HPLC dataset, overcoming significant challenges related to data artifacts and missing metadata. Through rigorous statistical filtering and advanced peak detection algorithms, the analysis identified a comprehensive inventory of peaks per run, mapping retention times and calculating areas and heights based on UV absorbance signals. The visualizations confirm the effectiveness of the baseline estimation and peak isolation techniques, particularly in distinguishing significant peaks from noise artifacts. Despite the inability to perform direct concentration quantification due to missing `Conc_B_ml` data, the generated peak inventory serves as a critical foundation for downstream integration and purity assessment. Future work should focus on enhancing the statistical reporting within visualizations to better quantify detection variability and validating the peak detection parameters against known standards.
