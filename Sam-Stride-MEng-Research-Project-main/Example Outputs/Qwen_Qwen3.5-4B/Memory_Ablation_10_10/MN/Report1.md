#Abstract 

This report presents the findings of a rigorous Data Integrity and Completeness Audit conducted on a high-resolution chromatographic dataset intended for downstream machine learning. The primary objective was to evaluate whether the data met strict biopharmaceutical standards regarding signal quality, completeness, and unit consistency. The analysis revealed critical feasibility gaps, including a 100% missingness rate for key process metadata variables (`chromatography_stage`, `Sample_Code`), which renders sample-specific modeling impossible without aggressive imputation. Furthermore, approximately 65.6% of concentration and pH variables (`Conc_B_ml`, `pH_ml`) were missing, and significant negative values were detected in physical quantities such as `UV_1_280_mAU` and `volume_ml`, indicating severe unit mislabeling or data corruption. Based on these findings, the dataset is deemed unfit for general sample-specific modeling. The report concludes that only a restricted subset of variables—specifically those with >95% completeness and valid, non-negative units—should be utilized for feature engineering, explicitly prohibiting the use of corrupted variables to avoid introducing bias into predictive models.

#Introduction 

The development of predictive models for biopharmaceutical manufacturing relies heavily on the integrity of the underlying chromatographic data. This study focuses on a dataset containing fraction-level time-series records from High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) and Mass Spectrometry (MS) systems. While the data offers high-resolution insights, it suffers from significant data quality issues that threaten the validity of any downstream analysis. The primary motivation for this analysis was to quantify the 'Modeling Feasibility Gap' and determine if the dataset is 'Fit for Use' for machine learning applications. Specifically, the analysis aimed to identify and quantify failures such as excessive missingness in critical process metadata, unit mislabeling leading to physically impossible values (e.g., negative absorbance), and the potential for bias introduced by aggressive imputation strategies. The scope of this report covers the execution of a three-step analytical workflow designed to audit data integrity, detect signal artifacts, and derive a final 'Fit for Use' feature set suitable for robust modeling.

#Methodology 

The analysis was executed through a structured three-step plan designed to isolate data integrity failures without attempting to correct them. Step 1 involved a comprehensive Data Integrity and Completeness Audit of the `chromatography_combined.csv` file. This step quantified missingness percentages for all variables, specifically flagging those exceeding 65% or 95% thresholds. It also calculated the 'Modeling Feasibility Gap' by determining the percentage of rows containing both `chromatography_stage` and `Sample_Code`. Step 2 focused on Signal Integrity and Artifact Detection. This involved visualizing the distribution of UV absorbance signals (`UV_1_280_mAU`, `UV_2_260_mAU`) to identify negative outliers and correlating `UV_1_280_mAU` with `volume_ml` to verify unit mislabeling errors. Step 3 derived the final 'Fit for Use' feature set. This phase filtered variables based on the criteria established in Steps 1 and 2, retaining only those with >95% completeness and valid, non-negative units. The final output included a summary of retained rows (1,084,069) and a detailed exclusion list for variables failing these criteria.

#Results and Discussion 

The audit results indicate severe data quality deficiencies that preclude standard modeling approaches. A critical finding is the complete absence of process metadata; 100% of rows are missing either `chromatography_stage` or `Sample_Code`, making sample-specific analysis infeasible. Additionally, approximately 65.6% of concentration (`Conc_B_ml`) and pH (`pH_ml`) variables are missing, rendering these features unusable for the majority of the dataset. Visual analysis of signal integrity, as depicted in Figure 1, confirms the presence of physical noise and data entry artifacts. The histogram of `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` displays a distinct cluster of negative values below the zero baseline, which is physically impossible for absorbance measurements. Furthermore, the scatter plot in Figure 1 demonstrates a strong positive linear correlation between `UV_1_280_mAU` and `volume_ml`, strongly suggesting that the variable labeled `UV_1_280_mAU` is actually cumulative volume mislabeled with absorbance units. These findings validate the exclusion of variables with negative values and mislabeled units. Consequently, the final 'Fit for Use' dataset consists of 1,084,069 rows, supporting only 24.32% of the total rows for sample-specific modeling. The analysis strictly adhered to exclusion criteria, prohibiting aggressive imputation for high-missingness variables to prevent bias, and retained only variables such as `run_no`, `run`, `Cond_mS_cm`, `Conc_B_%`, and `pH_pH` that met the >95% completeness and non-negative value thresholds.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the chromatographic dataset is not currently fit for use for comprehensive sample-specific machine learning due to critical data integrity failures. The combination of 100% missingness in process metadata, high missingness in concentration variables, and severe unit mislabeling resulting in negative physical values creates a fundamental modeling gap. The analysis confirms that relying on the full dataset or attempting to impute high-missingness variables would introduce severe bias. Therefore, the recommended approach is to restrict feature engineering to a specific subset of variables that satisfy strict completeness (>95%) and validity (non-negative) criteria. Future work should focus on resolving the data corruption issues identified in the `_ml` columns and obtaining complete process metadata to enable robust, sample-specific predictive modeling.
