#Abstract 

This report details a systematic data analysis workflow designed to ensure data integrity within a biopharmaceutical High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) dataset comprising 1.08 million rows. The primary objective was to distinguish between valid biological signals and instrumental artifacts, specifically detector saturation and flow rate clipping. Given the severe metadata gaps (>75% missing Sample_Code), the analysis employed a dual-mode strategy: run-level aggregation for the bulk dataset and sample-level deep dives for complete records. Structural corrections, including LOESS-based baseline alignment and temporal gap analysis, were applied first to resolve negative cumulative volumes and ensure continuity. Subsequently, anomaly detection protocols identified UV absorbance spikes exceeding 1500 mAU and flow rates capped at 4.0 ml/min. These anomalies were validated using Gaussian peak shape fitting (R² < 0.85) to classify events as detector artifacts. The results confirm the presence of hard caps in flow rates and a right-skewed distribution of UV absorbance with significant outliers, necessitating stratified run-level aggregation to prevent false positives in downstream quality control.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the integrity of chromatographic data is paramount for ensuring product quality and regulatory compliance. However, large-scale HPLC datasets often suffer from instrumental limitations and data acquisition errors, such as detector saturation, flow rate clipping, and baseline drifts. These artifacts can obscure genuine biological signals, leading to erroneous conclusions regarding peak purity and concentration. The current dataset presents a unique challenge with 1.08 million rows and significant metadata sparsity, where over 75% of records lack critical identifiers like Sample_Code. This report outlines a comprehensive analysis plan aimed at mitigating these risks. The motivation for this study is to establish a robust framework for identifying and validating experimental anomalies. By systematically applying structural corrections and statistical validation techniques, we aim to isolate sensor clipping from valid biological spikes, thereby enhancing the reliability of the dataset for downstream processing and quality assurance.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a three-step stratified workflow designed to handle data sparsity and validate signal integrity. First, structural corrections were applied to address baseline drifts and negative cumulative volumes. Median values for volume and conductivity were calculated per run and smoothed using LOESS regression (span=0.5) to establish a corrected baseline. Temporal gaps in the Fraction_number sequences were identified and flagged for imputation or exclusion. Second, anomaly detection and peak shape validation were performed on corrected data. Rows with UV absorbance >1500 mAU or flow rates >4.0 ml/min were flagged. For these flagged points, Gaussian fitting was applied to a window of ±20 fractions; a low R² value (<0.85) indicated a 'Detector Artifact' rather than a biological spike. Third, run-level aggregation was conducted to handle the metadata gaps. Runs with complete metadata were analyzed individually, while the majority of runs were aggregated by chromatography stage. Process parameters like Concentration B and pH were treated as sparse features, excluded from continuous correlations, and used only for descriptive statistics. This approach ensured that the analysis prioritized correcting structural inconsistencies before statistical outlier detection.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully identified and characterized specific instrumental anomalies within the dataset. Figure 1 presents a box plot of the flow rate variables, System_flow_ml_min and Sample_flow_ml_min. The visualization reveals a distinct horizontal line marking the upper threshold of 4.0 ml/min. The maximum observed data points align precisely with this threshold, providing strong evidence of a hard cap, likely due to sensor saturation or software capping. While this confirms the presence of flow rate clipping, the plot alone does not distinguish between hardware limits and software artifacts, nor does it correlate these capped flows with UV absorbance values, which is essential for the full 'Detector Artifact' classification logic. Figure 2 displays a histogram of UV absorbance at 280 nm, overlaid with a vertical dashed line at 1500 mAU. The distribution is strongly right-skewed, with the majority of data clustered below 1000 mAU. However, a significant tail of outliers extends beyond the 1500 mAU threshold, reaching a maximum of approximately 1922 mAU. This visual evidence confirms the existence of detector saturation events, justifying the plan's logic of flagging runs where the anomaly percentage exceeds 1%. Figure 3 provides a scatter plot of Fraction_number versus UV absorbance for flagged anomalies. The plot shows a baseline of low absorbance interrupted by sharp, vertical spikes reaching up to 1900 mAU. These spikes are clustered at specific fraction intervals, suggesting discrete events. The dashed line represents the Gaussian fit threshold (R² < 0.85), which appears to intersect the base of these spikes, highlighting their deviation from the smooth Gaussian curve expected for normal chromatographic peaks. Together, these figures validate the workflow's ability to detect flow rate clipping and UV saturation, confirming the need for stratified run-level aggregation to manage the non-negligible fraction of anomalous data points.

#Conclusion 

This analysis demonstrates a rigorous approach to identifying and validating anomalies in a large-scale, metadata-sparse HPLC dataset. By combining structural corrections with statistical peak shape validation, the workflow effectively distinguished between genuine biological signals and instrumental artifacts such as flow rate clipping and detector saturation. The results confirm that the dataset contains significant instances of sensor saturation, with flow rates capped at 4.0 ml/min and UV absorbance values exceeding 1900 mAU. The stratified aggregation strategy proved necessary to handle the high volume of incomplete records, ensuring that the analysis remained robust despite the >75% missing metadata. Future work should focus on correlating the capped flow rates with UV absorbance values to definitively classify the cause of clipping and further refine the Gaussian fit thresholds to reduce false positives in downstream quality control processes.
