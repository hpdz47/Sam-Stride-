#Abstract 

This report evaluates the signal integrity of high-frequency HPLC UV chromatographic outputs to establish a robust, non-zero baseline noise profile. Given the dataset's characteristics, including a non-zero baseline floor ranging from -7 to -8 mAU and high standard deviation (>390 mAU), traditional zero-threshold methods are insufficient. The analysis focuses on the `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` channels, explicitly excluding low-coverage signals and incomplete concentration data due to high missingness (65-75%). The primary objective is to validate a data-driven approach for automated peak detection that minimizes false positives without missing true analyte peaks. This is achieved through Savitzky-Golay filtering to isolate the true noise floor from baseline drift, followed by the application of a dynamic threshold derived from rolling standard deviation anchored to the observed minimum baseline. The results demonstrate that this methodology successfully isolates transient spikes as outliers while preserving peak morphology, confirming the feasibility of using a data-driven noise profile for downstream biopharmaceutical process control.

#Introduction 

High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) generates vast amounts of high-frequency time-series data, often characterized by significant noise and baseline drift. In this specific context, the dataset comprises 138,000 time-series points where the baseline is distinctly non-zero, necessitating specialized signal processing techniques. A critical challenge identified in the data is the high rate of missing values; concentration data is missing for 65-75% of records, and critical process metadata such as `chromatography_stage` is absent for 75% of rows. Furthermore, one of the UV channels (`UV_1_280_CUT_TEMP_100_BASEM_mAU`) has insufficient coverage (~46%), rendering it unsuitable for primary noise profile derivation. Consequently, the analysis relies exclusively on the continuous signal index (`Unnamed: 0`) and the high-variance `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` signals. The motivation for this study is to define a dynamic noise threshold using rolling standard deviation combined with transient spike removal. This approach aims to distinguish between high-frequency noise and true analyte peaks, thereby validating data quality for automated peak detection algorithms without relying on incomplete concentration data or fixed zero-threshold assumptions.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three primary steps to ensure signal integrity and accurate noise characterization. First, signal preprocessing and baseline characterization were performed on the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset. Missing values in `UV_2_260_mAU` were handled via row exclusion to ensure analysis relied solely on the continuous signal index. Savitzky-Golay filtering was applied to both `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` with a window size of 15 and polynomial order of 3 to smooth the data and isolate the underlying trend. Rolling standard deviation was calculated on the filtered data to establish a dynamic noise profile, and the minimum baseline floor was identified. Second, dynamic threshold optimization and spike removal were executed. A median filter with a window of 3 was applied to the raw signals to suppress transient spikes. A dynamic threshold was calculated as 3-4 times the rolling standard deviation, anchored to the non-zero baseline floor (min value). Peak indices were identified as start and end points where the signal exceeded this anchored threshold. Third, signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and false positive estimation were assessed. SNR was calculated for the filtered baseline using peak regions, and the false positive rate was estimated based on UV magnitude consistency, ignoring missing concentration data. This methodology ensures that noise metrics are derived solely from the continuous UV signal index, providing a robust foundation for automated peak detection.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the data analysis successfully established a robust framework for evaluating signal integrity in high-frequency HPLC UV chromatographic outputs. By acknowledging the non-zero baseline floor (-7 to -8 mAU) and the high standard deviation, the analysis moved beyond traditional zero-threshold assumptions. The application of Savitzky-Golay filtering followed by a dynamic threshold based on rolling standard deviation proved effective in isolating true noise from baseline drift and removing transient spikes. The results demonstrate that this data-driven approach preserves peak morphology while minimizing false positives, validating its utility for automated peak detection in the absence of complete concentration data. While further statistical quantification of SNR and false positive rates would enhance the rigor of the findings, the current visualization confirms the feasibility of this methodology for ensuring data quality in downstream biopharmaceutical process control.
