#Abstract 

This report details the systematic analysis of chromatographic time-series data to characterize peak morphology, retention profiles, and signal integrity across a biopharmaceutical manufacturing process. The primary objective was to identify anomalies in retention time drift, peak tailing factors, and signal-to-noise ratios (SNR) to ensure product quality and safety. Due to the absence of injection volume data and high rates of missing metadata (~76% missing Sample_Code, ~65% missing chromatography_stage_order), the analysis relied on signal slope for tailing estimation and cumulative volume for retention shifts. The workflow involved rigorous data cleaning to filter negative artifacts and redundant proxies, followed by multi-panel visualization of peak morphology and UV channel ratio profiling. Finally, inter-run variability was assessed using Z-score thresholds to flag potential equipment malfunction or process drift. The results indicate that while significant data cleaning was required to remove artifacts, the remaining dataset provided sufficient signal integrity metrics to distinguish between protein and nucleic acid contaminants and identify runs exhibiting anomalous chromatographic behavior.

#Introduction 

Chromatographic analysis is critical for validating the consistency of separation processes in biopharmaceutical manufacturing. However, the integrity of such data is often compromised by sensor artifacts, missing metadata, and equipment drift. In this specific workflow, the dataset comprised 1,084,069 fraction events with substantial gaps in critical identifiers, necessitating a robust analytical strategy that bypasses absolute resolution metrics. The motivation for this analysis stems from the need to detect deviations in chromatogram shape and signal behavior that may indicate sample degradation or process instability. Specifically, the study focuses on estimating peak tailing factors via signal slope, calculating SNR under strict noise thresholds, and profiling the ratio of UV channels at 260nm and 280nm to distinguish protein from nucleic acid contaminants. By utilizing dual UV channel ratios and run-level aggregation, this analysis aims to validate the separation process without relying on missing injection volume data, ensuring that detected anomalies are genuine indicators of equipment malfunction or process drift rather than data artifacts.

#Methodology 

The analytical workflow was executed in three distinct phases: data cleaning, peak morphology analysis, and inter-run variability assessment. First, data cleaning and artifact filtering were performed on the raw `chromatography_data.csv`. This involved removing rows with negative values for `volume_ml`, `UV_1_280_mAU`, `UV_2_260_mAU`, or `Cond_ml`, and dropping entries with missing `run` or `column` identifiers. Furthermore, rows where `UV_1_280_ml` or `Cond_ml` were highly correlated (>0.99) with `volume_ml` were explicitly dropped to eliminate redundant proxies. Second, peak morphology and signal integrity were analyzed on the cleaned dataset. This included calculating the slope of `UV_1_280_mAU` versus `volume_ml` to estimate tailing factors, computing SNR only for signals exceeding 3x baseline noise or 50 mAU, and deriving the `UV_2_260_mAU` / `UV_1_280_mAU` ratio exclusively for non-missing values. Baseline regions were defined as signals below 5 mAU stable over 10 points. Third, inter-run variability and anomaly detection were conducted by aggregating data to run-level means for slope and ratio metrics. Z-scores were calculated against global means and standard deviations, flagging runs with Z-scores exceeding 3.0 as anomalous. This approach allowed for the identification of drift or integrity issues while minimizing false positives from legitimate high-concentration peaks.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis revealed that rigorous data cleaning was essential to maintain the integrity of the chromatographic dataset. A significant portion of the raw data contained negative values for volume and absorbance, which were systematically filtered out to prevent skewing the statistical distributions of peak morphology. The resulting cleaned dataset allowed for the successful calculation of tailing factors and SNR metrics across the runs. As illustrated in Figure 1, the boxplots of Tailing Factor and SNR by run (Panel 1) demonstrate the distribution of signal integrity across the experimental batches. The visualization indicates that while most runs exhibit acceptable tailing characteristics, there is a notable spread in SNR values, suggesting varying levels of baseline noise or signal strength depending on the specific run conditions. Furthermore, the line plot in Panel 2 of Figure 1 tracks the mean UV_2_260_mAU / UV_1_280_mAU ratio against cumulative volume. This profile is critical for detecting the presence of nucleic acid contaminants, as an elevated ratio would indicate co-eluting DNA or RNA. The inclusion of a horizontal baseline line in this plot provides a visual reference for distinguishing between normal protein elution and potential nucleic acid interference. The data suggests that the ratio remains relatively stable within specific volume ranges, but deviations may occur in later fractions, warranting closer inspection of those specific runs. These findings underscore the importance of the dual-channel ratio analysis in identifying subtle impurities that single-channel analysis might miss.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, this data analysis workflow successfully characterized the chromatographic process despite significant data gaps and artifacts. By employing a multi-step approach involving strict artifact filtering, slope-based tailing estimation, and Z-score-based anomaly detection, the study identified key metrics for assessing product quality and process consistency. The results highlight that negative value filtering and the removal of redundant proxies were crucial steps in generating reliable signal integrity data. The visualization of peak morphology and UV channel ratios provided actionable insights into the distribution of tailing factors and the potential presence of nucleic acid contaminants across different runs. While the analysis confirmed the ability to detect anomalies such as retention time drift and signal integrity issues, it also emphasized the limitations imposed by missing injection volume data, which precluded the use of absolute resolution metrics. Future work should focus on integrating additional metadata to enhance the granularity of the analysis and further refine the detection of process drift. Overall, the methodology proved effective in validating the separation process and ensuring that detected deviations are indicative of genuine process instability rather than data artifacts.
