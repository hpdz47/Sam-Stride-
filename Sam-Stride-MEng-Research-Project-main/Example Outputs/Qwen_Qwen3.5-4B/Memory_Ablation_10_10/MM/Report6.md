#Abstract 

This report details the analysis of chromatographic data from the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset to assess the reproducibility of retention times (RT) across 11 experimental runs. Due to the absence of injection metadata for 99.9% of records, the analysis treats `run_no` as the primary unit of replication. Data integrity was ensured by filtering out 11.5% of rows with missing `Fraction_unique_ID` and clamping invalid volume values. Retention times were calculated by mapping the peak maximum of the `UV_2_260_mAU` signal to the sequential index, utilizing Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) and Savitzky-Golay smoothing to establish a reliable baseline. The primary objective was to determine if observed RT variations stem from random noise, systematic drift, or methodological instability. Results indicate a general upward trend in median RT with increasing run numbers and significant inter-quartile range variability in later runs, suggesting potential systematic drift. While the data supports the conclusion of increasing variability, the current visualization lacks explicit statistical significance testing to definitively distinguish between random noise and process drift.

#Introduction 

In biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the robustness of chromatographic processes is critical for ensuring product consistency. This study focuses on evaluating the reproducibility of retention times across multiple High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) runs. The dataset under investigation, `chromatography_combined.csv`, contains 1.08 million rows of time-series chromatographic data. A significant challenge in this dataset is the sparsity of critical injection metadata; specifically, the `Injection_ml` variable is missing for 99.9% of records, and approximately 11.5% of rows lack the `Fraction_unique_ID`. Consequently, traditional methods relying on individual injection volumes cannot be applied, necessitating a shift in analytical strategy where `run_no` serves as the primary unit of replication. The primary objective of this analysis is to determine whether variations in retention time are attributable to random experimental noise, systematic process drift, or genuine methodological inconsistencies. To achieve this, the data underwent rigorous preprocessing, including the removal of invalid volume values and the application of advanced signal smoothing techniques to accurately identify peak maxima and establish stable baselines for RT calculation.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a three-step workflow designed to address data sparsity and ensure signal quality. First, data integrity and signal preprocessing were performed on the raw `chromatography_combined.csv` file. Rows with missing `Fraction_unique_ID` were filtered out, and `volume_ml` values less than -10 ml were clamped to zero to mitigate baseline noise artifacts. Retention Time (RT) was calculated as the `volume_ml` plus an offset derived from the `UV_2_260_mAU` baseline. To enhance peak detection, the UV signal was subjected to Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) smoothing with parameters lambda=1e5 and trend=10, followed by Savitzky-Golay filtering (window=11, polyorder=2). Second, RT calculation and run-to-run variability were assessed. Mean, standard deviation (SD), and coefficient of variation (CV) were computed for RT across each `run_no`. The run exhibiting the lowest CV was selected as the representative signal for visualization. Third, reproducibility and drift assessment were conducted. Linear regression was performed on RT versus `run_no` to quantify temporal drift, with a slope magnitude greater than 0.01 indicating significant drift. Additionally, RT statistics were aggregated by `chromatography_stage` to evaluate stage-specific performance. All analyses strictly adhered to the dataset structure, utilizing `run_no` as the replication unit due to the lack of injection metadata.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis reveals distinct patterns in retention time stability across the experimental series. As illustrated in Figure 1, the left panel presents a box plot of RT values stratified by `run_no`, encompassing data from 11 distinct experimental runs. The visualization demonstrates a clear upward trend in the median RT as the run number increases. Furthermore, the inter-quartile range (IQR) exhibits significant variability, particularly in the later runs, which suggests potential systematic drift or methodological instability over the course of the experiment. The right panel of Figure 1 displays a representative smoothed UV signal (likely `UV_2_260_mAU`) for the run exhibiting the lowest coefficient of variation (CV), overlaid with the detected peaks. The signal preprocessing appears effective, characterized by a relatively stable baseline and the clear identification of distinct peaks. However, the plot lacks explicit error bars on the box plot to quantify the spread of individual fraction RTs within each run, and the specific smoothing parameters used for the signal are not annotated. While the visual evidence strongly supports the conclusion that RT variability exists and may increase with run number, the absence of statistical significance testing (e.g., ANOVA) or explicit drift slope quantification in the figure prevents a definitive conclusion on whether the observed variations are due to random noise or systematic process drift.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully assessed the reproducibility of retention times in a chromatographic process characterized by significant data sparsity. By treating `run_no` as the primary unit of replication and employing robust signal preprocessing techniques, the analysis identified a general upward trend in median RT and increased variability in later experimental runs. These findings suggest the presence of systematic drift or methodological instability that warrants further investigation. While the visual data indicates a degradation in signal quality or process stability over time, the lack of formal statistical testing limits the definitive attribution of these changes to specific causes. Future analyses should incorporate statistical significance testing and more granular drift quantification to distinguish between random experimental noise and systematic process failures, thereby providing a more robust validation of the manufacturing process.
