#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic separation performance derived from UV absorbance signals and associated fraction volumes. The primary objective was to determine if neighboring peaks were adequately resolved to ensure product purity, specifically identifying instances where resolution ($R_s$) fell below the acceptable threshold of 1.5. Due to significant data gaps, a rigorous preprocessing pipeline was implemented, filtering out records with negative volume anomalies (synchronization errors) and imputing missing precise volume data using median imputation to preserve local data structure. The analysis focused on calculating peak resolution and separation efficiency, converting volume-based metrics to time-based units using instantaneous flow rates. A critical constraint of this study was the stratification of results by chromatography stage, restricted to the 25% of records containing complete stage data to mitigate bias from the 75% missing stage information. The results indicate a general upward trend in resolution across all stages with increasing run numbers, though significant column-to-column variance was observed. Notably, specific data points were identified where $R_s < 1.5$, signaling potential co-elution risks that require process optimization.

#Introduction 

Chromatographic separation is a critical unit operation in biopharmaceutical manufacturing, where the resolution of neighboring peaks directly impacts product purity and safety. In this workflow, the quantitative evaluation of separation performance relies on metrics such as peak resolution ($R_s$), separation efficiency ($N$), and selectivity ($\alpha$). These metrics are derived from UV absorbance signals (`UV_1_280_mAU`, `UV_2_260_mAU`) and associated fraction volumes (`fraction_volume_ml_min_precise`, `fraction_volume_ml_max_precise`). The dataset presented in this analysis suffers from two primary data quality issues: 11.5% of records lack precise volume boundaries, and negative values in volume columns indicate synchronization errors that must be excluded prior to analysis. Furthermore, there is a substantial missingness in the `chromatography_stage` variable, affecting 75% of the records. This report addresses these challenges by implementing a robust preprocessing strategy, including median imputation for volume gaps and filtering for negative anomalies. The analysis aims to identify runs or process stages where peak overlap exceeds acceptable thresholds, thereby ensuring the quality and safety of the biopharmaceutical product. Due to the high variance in UV signals, rigorous baseline noise assessment using fixed-point rolling windows was employed to distinguish true peaks from fluctuations. Additionally, peak neighbors were identified via clustering signal maxima to handle complex co-elution scenarios, ensuring that the calculated resolution metrics are accurate.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three distinct phases: Data Preprocessing, Peak Detection and Resolution Calculation, and Stratified Efficiency Trending. 

In the Data Preprocessing phase, records were filtered to remove synchronization errors by excluding any entries where `volume_ml` or `fraction_volume_ml_min_precise` were negative. Missing precise volume data, present in 11.5% of records, was addressed using median imputation within `run_no` groups to preserve the local data structure. The impact of this imputation was quantified by calculating the mean and standard deviation of imputed volumes per run.

The Peak Detection and Resolution Calculation phase involved identifying peak maxima using signal clustering (Asymmetric Least Squares) on the `UV_1_280_mAU` signal. Records with missing precise volumes were excluded from peak width calculations, utilizing a fallback method of averaging adjacent complete records for instantaneous flow if necessary. Neighboring peaks were defined as peaks separated by a distance greater than 2x the median peak width. Resolution ($R_s$) and Separation Efficiency ($N$) were calculated for these neighboring peaks. To ensure standardization, volume-based metrics were converted into time-based units using the instantaneous `System_flow_ml_min` at the specific peak time point. Peak neighbors were identified via clustering signal maxima with baseline correction rather than simple proximity.

Finally, the Stratified Efficiency Trending phase stratified the analysis by `chromatography_stage`. To avoid bias introduced by the missing stage data, this analysis was restricted strictly to the 25% of records with complete `chromatography_stage` data. A minimum sample size threshold of 50 records per stage was enforced. Metrics were aggregated by `run_no` and `column` before plotting to ensure clarity and prevent overplotting.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of chromatographic performance reveals a consistent upward trend in resolution ($R_s$) across all four chromatography stages as the run number increases. As illustrated in Figure 1, Stage 4 consistently exhibits the highest resolution values, peaking near 2.5, while Stage 1 demonstrates the lowest resolution trajectory. This stratified view highlights the varying efficiency profiles across different process stages, suggesting that later stages may benefit from more optimized conditions or column performance. However, the shaded regions surrounding the trend lines indicate significant column-to-column variance in efficiency within each stage, implying that while the overall process trend is positive, individual column performance can fluctuate substantially. 

A critical finding from the analysis is the presence of specific data points annotated as 'Rs < 1.5'. These instances indicate poor peak resolution where separation efficiency is insufficient, potentially leading to co-elution of impurities with the target product. The identification of these low-resolution events is vital for process control, as they represent conditions where the separation is not adequate to ensure product purity. Despite the high variance observed, the data quality within the selected subset (the 25% of records with complete stage data) appears robust, with no obvious outliers or noise disrupting the general upward trend. This suggests that the data preprocessing steps, including the filtering of negative volume anomalies and the application of median imputation, successfully yielded a stable and interpretable dataset for trend analysis. The absence of the secondary Separation Efficiency ($N$) axis in the current visualization limits a full assessment of efficiency, but the primary resolution trend provides a strong indicator of overall separation performance improvement over time.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, this study successfully evaluated chromatographic separation performance despite significant data limitations, including missing stage data and volume anomalies. By enforcing a strict preprocessing pipeline and restricting the analysis to the 25% of records with complete stage data, we were able to generate a reliable trend analysis of resolution ($R_s$). The results demonstrate a positive correlation between run number and resolution across all stages, with Stage 4 showing superior performance. However, the identification of specific runs where $R_s < 1.5$ underscores the necessity for continuous monitoring and potential process adjustments to prevent co-elution. The significant column-to-column variance observed warrants further investigation into column selection and maintenance protocols. Overall, the methodology employed provides a robust framework for assessing separation efficiency, ensuring that the data used to guide biopharmaceutical production is both accurate and representative of the process capabilities.
