#Abstract 

This report details the evaluation of high-frequency chromatographic data integrity, focusing on the `UV_1_280_mAU` signal to ensure product safety and process reliability. The primary objective was to quantify Signal-to-Noise (SNR) ratios and assess baseline stability across experimental batches. Due to significant data missingness in `UV_2_260_mAU` (~70%) and critical process parameters like `Injection_ml` (~99.9% missing), the analysis prioritized `UV_1_280_mAU` (completeness ~94%) while excluding it from general quality metrics. A rigorous preprocessing pipeline was implemented, including the removal of rows with missing `Fraction_unique_ID` values, the application of baseline correction (e.g., Asymmetric Least Squares), and the filtering of negative `volume_ml` outliers (< -5 mL). The methodology involved calculating intra-run noise variance and detecting temporal drift by analyzing the first five runs chronologically. Results indicate that while data quality is sufficient for specific assessments, distinct variations in noise levels and SNR exist across runs, suggesting potential batch effects or sensor degradation that requires further investigation.

#Introduction 

In high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC), the integrity of time-series data is paramount for accurate fraction collection and downstream quality control. This study focuses on the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset, which contains high-frequency UV absorbance data. The primary challenge in this dataset is the disparity in data completeness between variables; `UV_1_280_mAU` exhibits high completeness (~94%), whereas `UV_2_260_mAU` suffers from significant missingness (~70%). Furthermore, critical process parameters such as `Injection_ml` are missing in nearly all rows (99.9%), necessitating a reliance on internal standards or Total Ion Current (TIC) scaling rather than injection volume normalization. To mitigate data integrity issues, the analysis protocol mandated the exclusion of rows where the `Fraction_unique_ID` is missing to prevent artificial skewing of noise metrics, and the application of baseline correction to stabilize the signal prior to outlier detection. The central motivation for this analysis is to identify excessive noise, sensor drift, and data artifacts—specifically negative baseline values or severe missingness—that could compromise the accuracy of the chromatographic process.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three primary phases: Data Integrity & Preprocessing, Noise Variance Analysis & Drift Detection, and Outlier Run Identification. In the preprocessing phase, the dataset was loaded and the 'Unnamed: 0' column was renamed to 'Fraction_number'. Rows with missing `Fraction_unique_ID` values were filtered out, and negative `volume_ml` values were flagged if less than -5 mL. Baseline correction was applied to the `UV_1_280_mAU` variable, while `UV_2_260_mAU` was explicitly excluded from general quality metrics. The second phase involved calculating intra-run noise variance, defined as the standard deviation of baseline segments for each 'run'. Temporal drift was assessed by plotting noise variance against 'Fraction_number' for the first five runs sorted chronologically. The Signal-to-Noise (SNR) ratio was calculated using the median peak height versus baseline noise, visualized as a boxplot grouped by 'run'. Finally, outlier runs were identified by flagging those with intra-run noise variance exceeding the mean plus two standard deviations. These flagged runs were then cross-referenced with counts of negative `volume_ml` outliers. All analyses were grouped by the categorical 'run' variable to accurately capture batch effects in the absence of injection volume data.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the first five runs, sorted chronologically, revealed critical insights into data integrity and signal stability. As illustrated in Figure 1, the top subplot visualizes the relationship between noise variance and the fraction number, providing a direct assessment of baseline stability over time. The plot indicates that noise levels vary significantly across the runs, with certain fractions exhibiting higher standard deviations in the baseline segments. This variation is critical for determining if specific batches exhibit excessive drift or sensor degradation that could compromise downstream quality control. The bottom subplot of Figure 1 presents a boxplot of the Signal-to-Noise (SNR) ratio, grouped by the categorical 'run' variable. This visualization serves as a comparative metric for signal quality, revealing that while the data quality appears sufficient for this specific assessment, distinct differences in SNR exist between the experimental batches. The presence of distinct boxplots suggests that the filtering of missing `Fraction_unique_ID` values and the application of baseline correction were successful in generating usable signal metrics. However, the figure alone cannot confirm the absolute magnitude of the noise or the specific threshold used to flag outliers, as these values are not annotated. Furthermore, the analysis is limited to a subset of the first five runs; therefore, conclusions regarding the broader dataset's drift characteristics are extrapolated from this subset. The observed variation in noise levels and SNR across these initial runs suggests that while the data is usable, specific batches may require closer scrutiny to ensure they do not exhibit excessive drift or sensor degradation that could invalidate downstream quality control decisions.

#Conclusion 

This report has successfully evaluated the integrity of high-frequency chromatographic data, focusing on the `UV_1_280_mAU` signal to ensure process reliability. The methodology effectively addressed data integrity challenges by filtering missing IDs, correcting baselines, and excluding high-missingness variables. The results, particularly those depicted in Figure 1, confirm that while the dataset is generally suitable for analysis, significant variations in intra-run noise variance and SNR ratios exist across the first five runs. These variations suggest potential batch effects or sensor drift that warrants further investigation beyond the initial subset. Future work should expand the analysis to the full dataset to establish robust thresholds for noise and drift, ensuring that all experimental batches meet the stringent requirements for product safety and process reliability. The current findings support the continuation of the workflow but highlight the need for targeted quality control measures for runs exhibiting elevated noise or low SNR.
