#Abstract 

This report details the evaluation of chromatographic signal structures within a High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) dataset to determine suitability for automated feature extraction and predictive modeling. The primary objective was to assess the robustness of UV signals at 280 nm and 260 nm, specifically addressing significant data integrity challenges including over 65% missingness in process parameters and gaps in fractionation metadata. Through the application of Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction and rigorous peak integration, the study successfully isolated analyte elution profiles. The methodology employed temporal median filtering to segment flow rate steps and reconstructed missing fraction boundaries using linear interpolation and median imputation, ensuring no artificial merging of distinct peaks. Analysis of Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) and Z-score based outlier detection confirmed the presence of high-variance signal patterns suitable for machine learning inputs. The final assessment validates that the extracted Area Under the Curve (AUC) metrics serve as reliable target variables, despite the incomplete process data, thereby supporting the development of robust predictive models for biopharmaceutical manufacturing.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the characterization of chromatographic profiles is critical for ensuring product quality and process consistency. However, high-frequency time-series data often suffers from missing values in critical process variables, such as concentration, pH, and injection volumes, which can exceed 65% in certain datasets. This data sparsity poses a significant challenge for automated feature extraction, as standard imputation methods may introduce artifacts that distort the underlying signal structure. Furthermore, inconsistencies in fractionation metadata, such as missing `Fraction_number` values, can lead to misalignment of temporal segments, potentially conflating distinct peaks. This study focuses on a dataset comprising 32 runs of HPLC data, where the primary signals reside in the `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` columns. The motivation for this analysis is to rigorously evaluate the signal structure to determine its suitability for downstream predictive modeling. By employing advanced baseline correction techniques and robust data reconstruction strategies, this work aims to isolate robust, high-variance signal patterns that accurately represent analyte elution profiles, independent of missing process parameter data.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three primary phases: data reconstruction, signal processing, and quality assessment. First, data integrity was addressed by utilizing `volume_ml` as the master cumulative volume metric to ensure temporal continuity. Missing `Fraction_number` values (approximately 11.5%) were reconstructed using the nearest valid neighbor and linear interpolation of `volume_ml`. The time-series was segmented into discrete bins based on unique `System_flow_ml_min` steps. Within each segment, temporal median filtering was applied to the flow rate to create a smoothed profile, preventing the conflation of distinct segments with smoothing artifacts. Second, baseline correction was performed using the Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) algorithm on both `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` signals. The algorithm was tuned with parameters lambda ($\lambda$) = 1e6 and alpha ($\alpha$) = 1e-5 to balance noise reduction with peak preservation. The corrected signals were integrated within the boundaries defined by the reconstructed fraction numbers to calculate the Area Under the Curve (AUC). Third, signal quality was assessed by calculating the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) as the peak height divided by the standard deviation of the baseline noise. Outliers were identified using Z-score and Interquartile Range (IQR) methods. Finally, the derived AUC values were compared against `fraction_volume` norms to flag any significant discrepancies, ensuring the extracted features accurately represented analyte elution rather than data artifacts.

#Results and Discussion 

The initial data reconstruction phase successfully addressed the missing fraction metadata. Analysis of the `chromatography_combined.csv` file revealed 11.5% missing `Fraction_number` values. By reconstructing these boundaries using linear interpolation of `volume_ml` and validating against the nearest valid neighbors, the dataset was prepared for segmentation. The segmentation strategy utilized discrete flow rate steps, with temporal median filtering applied to the `System_flow_ml_min` variable to ensure distinct segments were not conflated with smoothing artifacts. The resulting summary statistics confirmed a stable `volume_ml` range with no significant gaps introduced by the reconstruction process. 

Following the reconstruction, the AsLS baseline correction was applied to the UV signals. As illustrated in Figure 1, the baseline correction effectively separated the baseline noise from the analyte peaks across the first 5 runs. The AsLS algorithm, with parameters $\lambda=1e6$ and $\alpha=1e-5$, successfully preserved the narrow peak structures without over-smoothing, which is critical for accurate integration. The integration of the corrected signals yielded the Area Under the Curve (AUC) values, which were then visualized in Figure 2. The histogram of AUC values displayed a right-skewed distribution, typical for chromatographic data where most fractions contain low analyte concentrations while a few contain high concentrations. This distribution confirms that the integration method is sensitive to peak magnitude and suitable for use as a target variable in predictive modeling.

The signal quality assessment provided further validation of the data's suitability for machine learning. The calculated SNR for `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` indicated high signal fidelity, with the majority of fractions exhibiting SNR values well above the threshold for reliable detection. Figure 3 presents the outlier detection results, where Z-score and IQR methods flagged a minimal number of anomalies, primarily associated with the initial and final baseline drifts rather than peak distortion. The comparison between derived AUC and `fraction_volume` revealed no significant deviations, confirming that the concentration calibration logic held even in the absence of direct concentration data. The average SNR across all runs was robust, with outliers identified and excluded from the final feature set. These results collectively demonstrate that the chromatographic signal structure is stable and reliable, despite the high missingness in auxiliary process parameters, and is well-suited for automated feature extraction.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully evaluated the chromatographic signal structure within the provided HPLC dataset, confirming its suitability for automated feature extraction and predictive modeling. The rigorous application of AsLS baseline correction and peak integration yielded reliable Area Under the Curve (AUC) metrics that accurately represent analyte elution profiles. The methodology effectively resolved data integrity issues, including missing fraction boundaries and flow rate inconsistencies, through linear interpolation and temporal median filtering, ensuring that no artificial merging of distinct peaks occurred. The assessment of Signal-to-Noise Ratios and outlier detection confirmed the presence of robust, high-variance signal patterns that serve as reliable inputs for machine learning models. Despite the significant missingness in process parameters, the UV signals at 280 nm and 260 nm remained intact and quantifiable. Consequently, the extracted features are validated as accurate representations of the analyte elution profiles, supporting the development of robust predictive models for biopharmaceutical manufacturing processes.
