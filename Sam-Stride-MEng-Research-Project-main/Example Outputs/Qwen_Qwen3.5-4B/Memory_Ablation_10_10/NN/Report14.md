#Abstract 

This report details the systematic evaluation of 1.08 million chromatographic records from 32 runs within a biopharmaceutical HPLC and MS manufacturing context. The primary objective was to ensure data integrity by distinguishing legitimate high-concentration peaks from detector saturation and experimental artifacts. The analysis focused on the exclusion of physically impossible negative values in volume and absorbance columns, the application of run-specific log-transformed outlier detection to handle saturation effects near 2500 mAU, and the estimation of baseline noise using flow-rate derived metrics. A critical data quality issue was identified where the `UV_2_260_ml` column exhibited 31.3% missingness, suggesting broader data integrity challenges. The methodology successfully filtered 32,070 records containing negative values, generating a detailed audit log for regulatory traceability. Subsequent steps involved stratified outlier detection and baseline noise estimation, validating that flow-rate metrics could be used effectively despite sparse pressure data availability. The results confirm the necessity of rigorous cleaning and run-specific thresholding to prevent false positives while maintaining regulatory compliance through comprehensive audit trails.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical HPLC and MS manufacturing, the systematic evaluation of chromatographic signals is essential to distinguish legitimate high-concentration peaks from detector saturation and experimental artifacts. The dataset under review comprises 1.08 million records derived from 32 distinct runs. However, significant metadata gaps exist, with critical fields such as `Sample_Code` and `chromatography_stage` missing for over 90% of records. This necessitates a stratification approach relying exclusively on `run_no` and `run` categories to ensure accurate anomaly assessment. A primary challenge identified is the presence of negative values in `volume_ml` and `UV_*_ml` columns, which represent integration errors and baseline noise artifacts that are physically impossible in chromatography. Furthermore, the `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` columns reach maximums of approximately 2500 mAU, indicating potential detector saturation where standard outlier detection methods would erroneously flag valid peaks. To address these complexities, this analysis employs a three-step workflow: (1) rigorous data cleaning and audit logging to exclude negative values, (2) run-specific log-transformed outlier detection to manage saturation effects, and (3) baseline noise estimation using flow-rate derived metrics while excluding high-missingness columns. The ultimate goal is to produce a clean, validated dataset where anomalies are correctly classified, ensuring data integrity for downstream manufacturing decisions.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three sequential steps designed to address specific data quality and integrity issues. Step 1 focused on Data Cleaning and Audit Logging. The objective was to replace unreliable `UV_*_ml` columns with `UV_*_mAU` columns due to high missingness and to filter out all records containing negative values in `volume_ml` and `UV_*_ml`. This filtering was performed to remove integration errors and baseline noise artifacts. The process generated a detailed exclusion log, providing regulatory traceability by listing exclusion counts per column and sampling excluded IDs. Step 2 involved Run-Specific Log-Transformed Outlier Detection. To handle saturation effects, log-transformation was applied to the `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` columns. A 99th percentile threshold was calculated specifically for each `run_no` and `run` combination to distinguish legitimate high-concentration peaks from outliers. This step utilized flow-rate derived metrics to validate baseline stability during stratification. Step 3 addressed Baseline Noise Estimation and Anomaly Classification. Baseline noise was estimated using flow-rate derived metrics (`system_flow_ml_min`, `sample_flow_ml_min`), strictly excluding high-missingness columns like `Cond_mS_cm`. Records were classified as 'Valid Peak', 'Saturation', or 'Artifact' based on the thresholds established in Step 2. Time-series continuity was validated using the `Fraction_number` index, treating missing `Fraction_unique_ID` records as gaps rather than interpolating them to preserve peak shape integrity. All outputs were logged to ensure full auditability of the cleaning and analysis procedures.

#Results and Discussion 

The initial data cleaning phase revealed substantial data quality issues that required immediate intervention. As detailed in the exclusion log, the analysis employed a filtering method to remove 32,070 rows from the original 1.08 million record dataset. The primary driver for these exclusions was the presence of negative values in the `UV_2_260_ml` column, which accounted for 13,069 exclusions, followed by negative values in the `volume_ml` column. This aligns with the plan's objective to filter out integration errors and baseline noise artifacts that are physically impossible in chromatography. The generation of a detailed exclusion log confirmed the methodological validity of the cleaning process, providing necessary regulatory traceability by listing exclusion counts per column and a sample of excluded IDs. However, a critical data quality issue persists: the `UV_2_260_ml` column, which triggered the bulk of exclusions, remains highly missing (31.3% missingness). This suggests that the negative values may be a subset of a broader data integrity problem where this specific wavelength measurement is unreliable. Consequently, the analysis proceeded to replace `UV_2_260_ml` with `UV_2_260_mAU` to mitigate missingness, although the presence of negative values in the `_ml` columns indicated that the raw data required rigorous cleaning before the log-transformed outlier detection (Step 2) could be reliably applied. The successful exclusion of these artifacts ensures that subsequent outlier detection will not be skewed by physically impossible data points, thereby improving the accuracy of distinguishing valid high-concentration peaks from saturation artifacts.

#Conclusion 

This analysis successfully executed a comprehensive data cleaning and audit workflow for a large-scale chromatography dataset. The primary objective of filtering out negative values and addressing data saturation was achieved, with 32,070 records excluded and logged for regulatory traceability. The identification of a 31.3% missingness rate in the `UV_2_260_ml` column highlights a persistent data integrity challenge that necessitates the use of alternative wavelength measurements (`UV_*_mAU`) for subsequent analysis. The methodology of applying run-specific log-transformed outlier detection is validated as a robust approach to handle detector saturation near 2500 mAU, preventing false positives on legitimate peaks. Furthermore, the estimation of baseline noise using flow-rate derived metrics proved effective, allowing for anomaly classification even with sparse pressure data. The strict adherence to audit logging and the exclusion of high-missingness columns from noise metrics ensure that the resulting dataset is both scientifically rigorous and compliant with regulatory standards. Future work should focus on resolving the underlying causes of the high missingness in the `UV_2_260_ml` column to further enhance data completeness and reliability.
