#Abstract 

This report details the data analysis workflow designed to evaluate temporal trends in HPLC detector signals and system stability across 11 distinct chromatographic runs. The primary objective was to identify gradual shifts indicative of detector instability, lamp degradation, or column fouling by analyzing UV absorbance (280nm and 260nm) and conductivity metrics against the chromatography stage order. While the initial aggregation of 1.08 million records into 19 stages per run successfully calculated mean signal levels, a critical methodological flaw was identified during the statistical processing phase. The standard deviation calculations for all variables returned 'nan' values, rendering the dataset unsuitable for the planned Shewhart control charts, regression slope analysis, and variance component analysis. Consequently, the current output confirms the presence of non-linear signal peaks but fails to quantify drift rates or assess data quality regarding noise. The analysis concludes that the workflow requires re-execution of the aggregation step to generate valid standard deviations before trend detection and stability assessment can proceed.

#Introduction 

In the context of High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC), maintaining detector stability and system integrity is paramount for ensuring product quality and data integrity. This study focuses on the systematic evaluation of temporal trends in HPLC detector signals, specifically UV absorbance at 280nm and 260nm, alongside system stability metrics such as conductivity and pressure. The dataset comprises 1.08 million records representing fraction collections from 11 unique runs. A critical challenge in this analysis is the dataset structure, where the temporal axis must be defined by `chromatography_stage_order` (ranging from 2 to 20) rather than the `run_no`, as the latter represents discrete experimental batches. Furthermore, significant metadata gaps exist; critical control variables such as injection volume, system flow rate, and sample concentration are missing in approximately 66% to 99.9% of rows. This absence prevents the validation of whether observed drifts are caused by flow rate instability or sample preparation errors, necessitating a reliance on internal detector metrics and fully populated physical parameters. Additionally, the presence of negative values in continuous variables suggests potential data entry errors or baseline artifacts that must be handled carefully. The motivation for this analysis is to isolate drift patterns from inherent process variability to determine if corrective maintenance actions are required.

#Methodology 

The data analysis plan was executed in three distinct steps to address the objectives of trend detection and stability assessment. Step 1 involved Data Preprocessing and Temporal Aggregation. Rows were filtered to include only `chromatography_stage_order` values between 2 and 20. Data was grouped by `run_no` (11 unique runs) to prevent conflating run variability with stage drift. Within each run, data was aggregated by `chromatography_stage_order`, calculating mean and standard deviation. Rows with missing `chromatography_stage_order` were dropped, and negative values were flagged as outliers rather than clipped to zero to preserve baseline artifact information. Step 2 focused on Trend Detection and Control Charting. Linear regression was performed on the aggregated data to calculate slopes against stage order. Rolling means and standard deviations were calculated using a window size of 5 to capture short-term instabilities. Shewhart control charts (3-sigma limits) were generated for both UV channels, and `Cond_mS_cm` was used to check for pressure stability. Step 3 involved Correlation and Residual Analysis. A correlation matrix was calculated between UV channels and conductivity for each run. Detrended residual analysis was performed by subtracting the linear trend from original signals, and the variance of these residuals was calculated for each stage. The final output was intended to include summary statistics, control charts, regression slopes, correlation coefficients, and a conclusion on the linearity of drift.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the preprocessed data revealed distinct mean signal levels per chromatographic stage. For instance, the UV_1_280_mAU signal peaked at stages 12 and 18, suggesting potential non-linear drift or process peaks rather than simple linear degradation. However, a critical limitation emerged immediately following the aggregation step. The standard deviation columns for all variables (UV_1_280_mAU, UV_2_260_mAU, and Cond_mS_cm) were populated with 'nan', indicating that the aggregation step failed to calculate variance. This failure is likely due to insufficient data points per stage (only 19 stages for 5 distinct runs within the analyzed subset) or a software error in the aggregation function. As a direct consequence of this methodological flaw, the planned downstream analyses could not be executed. Specifically, it is impossible to apply Shewhart control charts with 3-sigma limits or calculate regression slopes to quantify drift rates without valid standard deviation inputs. While the absence of negative values in the outlier log was noted, the fundamental lack of variance data renders the current output insufficient for the stated objective of identifying detector instability or lamp degradation. The results confirm that the dataset contains non-linear signal characteristics but cannot currently support statistical modeling of drift rates or noise assessment. The analysis requires re-execution of the aggregation step to generate valid standard deviations before trend detection and control charting can proceed.
