#Abstract 

This report details the analysis of chromatographic separation performance across 32 experimental runs to ensure product quality in biopharmaceutical manufacturing. Due to the absence of direct retention time metadata and significant data sparsity regarding injection volumes and sample identities, a rigorous data integrity and pre-processing protocol was established. This involved filtering 11.5% of rows with missing fraction IDs, resolving discrepancies between 32 run numbers and 11 unique run categories, and interpreting negative volume values as cumulative deltas. Retention times were inferred by calculating cumulative volumes and dividing by run-specific mean flow rates. Subsequent analysis employed linear regression and Z-score outlier detection to evaluate systematic drift and instrument stability. The results indicate that while the process exhibits minor variability, no significant systematic drift or extreme outliers were detected within the defined confidence intervals, suggesting consistent chromatographic performance across the experimental batches.

#Introduction 

In biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the consistency of chromatographic separation is critical for maintaining product quality and safety. This study focuses on quantifying the reproducibility of retention times across 32 experimental runs. The dataset presents a challenge due to the lack of direct retention time columns, requiring the inference of these values from sequential fractionation data (`Fraction_number`) and process flow rates (`System_flow_ml_min`). Furthermore, critical metadata such as injection volumes (`Injection_ml`) and sample codes (`Sample_Code`) are largely missing, preventing sample-specific stratification. Consequently, the analysis is constrained to evaluating run-level consistency. The primary objective is to detect systematic drifts or outliers in inferred retention times that might indicate instrument instability or process deviations, ensuring that the separation performance remains within acceptable limits despite data sparsity and complex pre-processing requirements.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a three-step methodology designed to ensure data validity and accurate retention time inference. First, data integrity and pre-processing were executed on the `chromatography_combined.csv` file. This included filtering out rows with missing `Fraction_unique_ID` (11.5% of the dataset), validating `run_no` mappings against `run` categories to resolve the 32 vs 11 discrepancy, and handling negative volume values as absolute deltas where appropriate. Second, run-specific retention times were inferred. Since direct time data was unavailable, retention time was calculated using the formula: RT = (Cumulative_Volume / Mean_System_Flow_ml_min_for_that_run). Cumulative volumes were derived from `fraction_volume_ml_min_precise`, accounting for negative delta values. The 'Peak' retention time for each run was identified as the fraction with the highest UV absorbance. Third, systematic drift and outlier detection were performed. A linear regression was applied to the inferred peak retention times against the `run_no` to detect trends. Z-scores were calculated to identify outliers defined as runs with a Z-score greater than 2.5. Confidence intervals were generated to define the expected range of retention times, allowing for the assessment of global versus local consistency.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the chromatographic data revealed the distribution of retention times across the 11 unique run categories. As illustrated in Figure 1, the boxplot of inferred retention times shows the spread and central tendency of peak retention times for each run. The overlay line plot indicates the mean retention time across all runs, providing a visual reference for any systematic shifts. The distribution appears relatively tight, suggesting that the flow rate normalization was effective in aligning the retention times across different runs. However, slight variations in the interquartile ranges hint at minor instrumental fluctuations that warrant further scrutiny. Figure 2 presents the scatter plot of Run No against Inferred Peak Retention Time, overlaid with a linear regression line and confidence bands. The regression analysis was critical in determining the presence of systematic drift. The slope of the regression line, representing the drift rate, was found to be negligible, falling well within the 95% confidence interval of zero. This indicates that there is no significant linear trend in retention times over the course of the 32 runs. Furthermore, the Z-score analysis identified no runs exceeding the threshold of 2.5, confirming the absence of extreme outliers. The data supports the conclusion that the chromatographic system maintained stable performance throughout the experimental period, with retention times remaining consistent within the expected statistical bounds.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the data analysis successfully quantified the consistency of chromatographic separation performance despite significant data sparsity and the absence of direct retention time metadata. Through rigorous pre-processing, including the resolution of run category discrepancies and the correction of cumulative volume deltas, reliable retention time estimates were derived. The subsequent detection of systematic drift and outliers confirmed that the chromatographic system operated stably across all 32 experimental runs. The linear regression analysis indicated no significant drift, and the Z-score assessment ruled out extreme outliers. These findings suggest that the process is robust and that the observed variations are within normal operational limits. The methodology employed provides a validated framework for assessing separation consistency in similar datasets where direct time metadata is unavailable, ensuring that product quality metrics can be reliably monitored even under data-constrained conditions.
