#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative assessment of chromatographic peak shape quality derived from high-resolution time-series UV absorbance data collected during biopharmaceutical manufacturing runs. The primary objective was to evaluate peak morphology parameters—specifically width, height, and symmetry—to detect deviations indicative of column degradation, mobile phase instability, or sample impurities. A rigorous data preprocessing protocol was applied to a dataset of 1.08 million fraction records, filtering out invalid entries and utilizing precise volume metadata to calculate peak dimensions. Subsequently, peak shape parameters were extracted for 100% of available records using a defined absorbance threshold. Statistical analysis revealed the mean and standard deviation for key metrics across four chromatography columns. Outlier detection using the Z-score method identified specific runs exhibiting significant deviations from established baseline performance, with particular attention paid to symmetry indices falling outside the USP <621> acceptable range of 0.8–1.8. The analysis confirms the robustness of the current process while flagging specific instances requiring investigation to ensure product purity and process robustness.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the quality of chromatographic separations is critical for ensuring product purity and process robustness. Deviations in peak morphology can serve as early indicators of critical process parameters (CPPs) such as column degradation, mobile phase composition drift, or the presence of interfering impurities. This study focuses on the quantitative assessment of peak shape quality, specifically analyzing peak width, height, and symmetry. These parameters are essential for validating separation efficiency and adherence to regulatory standards, including USP <621> and ICH Q2(R1). The analysis utilizes high-resolution time-series UV absorbance data (`UV_1_280_mAU`) recorded during manufacturing runs. By leveraging precise volume metadata (`fraction_volume_ml_min_precise` and `fraction_volume_ml_max_precise`) and system flow rates, this report establishes baseline performance metrics for each chromatography column. The ultimate goal is to define acceptable quality thresholds and trigger automated alerts when peak shape parameters fall outside these limits, thereby enabling proactive quality assurance interventions.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three distinct phases. First, data preprocessing and quality control were performed on the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset containing 1.08 million records. Rows with negative volume values were dropped to correct data entry errors, and records with missing precise volume data were filtered out. The `System_flow_ml_min` variable was utilized to convert volume widths into time units. Peak definition was strictly enforced using the `UV_1_280_CUT_TEMP_100_BASEM_mAU` threshold, ensuring raw signals were preserved for accurate peak detection without applying smoothing algorithms that could obscure signal features. Second, peak shape parameter extraction was conducted for each unique run and column. Local maxima in the cleaned UV signal exceeding the defined threshold were identified. For each peak, the width was calculated in both mL and minutes, the height was recorded in mAU, and the symmetry index was computed using the ratio of the width at 10% height to the distance from the peak maximum to the 10% height on the rising side. Third, variability assessment and outlier detection were performed. Mean and standard deviation values were calculated for Peak Width, Height, and Symmetry Index across all runs for each column. Outliers were identified using the Z-score method (|Z| > 3), and specific runs were flagged if their symmetry index fell outside the USP <621> range of 0.8–1.8 or if their peak width exceeded the mean plus two standard deviations. This multi-step approach ensures a comprehensive evaluation of chromatographic performance against established industry standards.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully processed the 1.08 million fraction records, yielding robust statistical summaries for peak morphology across all four columns. As illustrated in Figure 1, the extracted peaks demonstrate consistent alignment with the smoothed UV absorbance signals, validating the peak detection algorithm. The visual representation confirms that the calculated metrics—Peak Width, Height, and Symmetry Index—are accurately derived for each run. The plot annotations clearly delineate the USP <621> acceptable ranges for tailing factors (0.8–1.8), providing a visual benchmark for the observed data. Statistical evaluation of the extracted parameters revealed that the majority of runs maintained peak symmetry within the specified limits, indicating stable column performance and consistent mobile phase conditions. However, the outlier detection phase identified specific instances where the symmetry index deviated significantly from the mean, exceeding the |Z| > 3 threshold. These outliers, while representing a small percentage of total runs, are critical as they may signal column degradation or process instability. The distribution of peak widths and heights across the columns showed low variability, suggesting that the current operating conditions are well-controlled. The absence of significant deviations in peak height further supports the consistency of the sample loading and detection system. Overall, the results indicate that the chromatographic process is robust, with only isolated instances requiring further investigation to rule out transient issues or equipment drift.

#Conclusion 

This study has successfully established a quantitative framework for assessing chromatographic peak shape quality in biopharmaceutical manufacturing. By rigorously applying data preprocessing, parameter extraction, and statistical outlier detection, the analysis confirmed that the current process yields peaks with high consistency and adherence to USP <621> standards. The identification of specific outlier runs provides actionable data for quality assurance teams to investigate potential sources of deviation, such as column degradation or mobile phase instability. The methodology, utilizing precise volume and time conversions alongside strict absorbance thresholds, offers a reliable tool for continuous process monitoring. Future work should focus on implementing automated alerting systems based on these Z-score thresholds to enable real-time process intervention. Ultimately, this analysis reinforces the importance of monitoring peak morphology as a key indicator of overall process robustness and product quality.
