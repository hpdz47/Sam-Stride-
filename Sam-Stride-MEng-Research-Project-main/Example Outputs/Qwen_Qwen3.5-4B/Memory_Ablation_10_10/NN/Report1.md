#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative analysis of High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) data aimed at establishing a correlation between integrated peak areas and concentration estimates. The workflow involved rigorous signal preprocessing, including Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) smoothing and dynamic baseline correction to address negative signal artifacts. However, the analysis encountered critical data quality constraints, specifically >66% missingness in the target variable (`Conc_B_%`) and significant gaps in critical process parameters (`pH`, `pressure`). While a preliminary Pearson correlation coefficient of R = -0.5766 was calculated between integrated peak areas and imputed concentrations, the validity of this result is severely compromised by a complete failure in batch stratification (0 batches processed). This failure is attributed to the inability to group data due to a cardinality mismatch between `run_no` (32) and `run` (11) and the prevalence of missing values preventing the application of USP/IUPAC quality filters. Consequently, the observed moderate negative correlation cannot be validated for batch-specific consistency, and the stability of the relationship remains unsupported.

#Introduction 

The primary objective of this study was to validate the relationship between chromatographic peak integration and concentration estimates for compound B. This validation is essential for ensuring the accuracy of quantitative assays in process development. The dataset, derived from a long-form time-series of HPLC runs, presented significant challenges regarding data integrity. Specifically, the target variable, `Conc_B_%`, exhibited severe missingness (>66%), and critical process parameters such as pH and pressure were largely absent. To mitigate these issues, a multi-step analytical strategy was employed, encompassing signal preprocessing, peak integration, and sensitivity analysis. The analysis sought to determine if the correlation between integrated peak areas and concentration estimates remained stable (within ±5%) despite the exclusion of process parameters, thereby confirming the robustness of the quantitative model under varying conditions.

#Methodology 

The analytical workflow consisted of three distinct phases. First, signal preprocessing and quality control were performed on the raw HPLC data (`chromatography_combined.csv`). AsLS smoothing (smoothness=1e7, asymmetry=2e-4) was applied to UV signals at 280 nm and 260 nm. Given the high cardinality of missing values in `UV_1_280_CUT_TEMP_100_BASEM_mAU`, local baselines were calculated to filter negative values using a 3-sigma threshold or a -5 mAU cutoff. Second, peak integration and abundance calculation were executed. Peaks were identified via local maxima above the corrected baseline, and integrated areas were computed using single-fraction volumes (`fraction_volume`). Due to the extensive missingness in `Conc_B_%`, KNN imputation (k=5) was applied, restricted to runs with sufficient coverage of `system_flow_ml_min` and `cond_mS_cm`. Third, correlation validation and sensitivity analysis were conducted. A Pearson correlation coefficient was calculated between integrated peak areas and imputed concentrations. A sensitivity analysis was performed to assess the impact of excluding pH and pressure, aiming to verify stability within ±5%. Furthermore, USP/IUPAC quality filters ($R_s \geq 1.5$, $0.8 \leq T \leq 1.8$, $N > 2000$) were applied to filter low-quality peaks. Batch stratification was attempted using `Run_Log_Logbook` as the primary stratifier and `run` as a fallback, though this step ultimately failed due to data inconsistencies.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis yielded a Pearson correlation coefficient of R = -0.5766 between integrated peak areas and concentration estimates, indicating a moderate negative relationship. A sensitivity analysis revealed a negligible shift (-0.00%) when pH and pressure were excluded, suggesting the correlation is theoretically stable and not driven by these specific process parameters. However, the batch stratification step reported a failure (0 batches processed), which critically undermines the validity of the correlation. This failure likely stems from the dataset's severe data quality issues: >66% missingness in the target variable (`Conc_B_%`) and critical process parameters (`pH`, `pressure`), combined with a high cardinality mismatch between `run_no` (32) and `run` (11) that prevented successful grouping logic. Furthermore, the presence of negative values in UV signals and volume metrics indicates potential baseline correction or data alignment errors that were not visibly resolved in the output. Without successful batch stratification, the correlation cannot be validated for batch-specific consistency, and the stability claim is unsupported by the actual stratification results. Consequently, while the mathematical correlation exists, its scientific utility for batch-to-batch comparison is currently limited.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, while the data analysis successfully identified a moderate negative correlation (R = -0.5766) between integrated peak areas and concentration estimates, the results are not yet scientifically robust for validation purposes. The complete failure of the batch stratification process, driven by >66% missingness in the target variable and critical process parameters, prevents the verification of the correlation's stability across batches. The observed stability in the sensitivity analysis (-0.00% shift) is theoretical and lacks empirical support from stratified data. Future work must prioritize data cleaning to address the missingness in `Conc_B_%`, resolve the cardinality mismatch between `run_no` and `run`, and correct baseline artifacts to enable successful batch stratification. Only after these data quality issues are resolved can the correlation be validated for its intended purpose in quantitative assay development.
