#Abstract 

This report details the automated detection and characterization of abnormal chromatographic peak shapes within a biopharmaceutical manufacturing dataset. Due to the absence of critical metadata such as Sample Codes and concentration data, the analysis relied exclusively on time-series UV absorbance signals to assess peak symmetry. The primary objective was to identify deviations from ideal Gaussian elution profiles, specifically excessive fronting and tailing, which serve as critical indicators of column health and process stability. Using Adaptive Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction and Z-score outlier detection, the workflow evaluated 11 unique chromatographic runs. The results indicate that while the majority of the process batches maintained acceptable peak symmetry, three specific runs (Run 3, Run 7, and Run 10) exhibited significant deviations, flagged by Tailing Factors exceeding the 2.0 threshold. Conversely, specific runs also demonstrated excessive fronting. These findings enable immediate quality assurance intervention to prevent the release of compromised product batches.

#Introduction 

In the biopharmaceutical manufacturing process, the quality of the final product is heavily dependent on the integrity of the chromatographic purification steps. Abnormal peak shapes, characterized by excessive fronting or tailing, are often indicative of column degradation, sample degradation, or process instability. The United States Pharmacopeia (USP) <621> guidelines establish a Tailing Factor (TF) threshold of 2.0 as a standard metric for acceptable peak symmetry. However, the dataset under review presented significant challenges: it contained 1.08 million rows representing individual fraction collections across 16 purification stages, yet critical identifiers such as `Sample_Code` and `Fraction_unique_ID` were missing for over 99% of records. Furthermore, the signal variable `UV_1_280_mAU` contained negative values indicative of baseline noise or drift. Consequently, this study focuses on the automated detection of abnormal peak shapes using only the available time-series absorbance signals and volume data. The analysis aims to flag specific fraction runs exhibiting non-symmetric peak characteristics, thereby enabling immediate intervention to ensure product quality despite the lack of comprehensive metadata.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three sequential steps to ensure robust peak shape characterization. First, data preprocessing and adaptive baseline correction were performed on the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset. Rows where volume or fraction volume was greater than zero were filtered, and negative absorbance values were excluded. An Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) algorithm was applied to correct baseline drift, utilizing an adaptive parameter selection process where the smoothing parameter ($\lambda$) ranged from $10^6$ to $10^7$ and the probability parameter ($p$) ranged from $2 \times 10^{-5}$ to $2 \times 10^{-4}$. Second, peak detection and shape metric calculation were conducted for each unique `run_no`. Using the corrected `UV_1_280_mAU` signal, peak regions were identified, and Tailing Factor (TF) and Fronting Factor (FF) were calculated. Runs were flagged if TF > 2.0 or FF > 2.0, adhering to USP <621> guidelines. Third, outlier detection and reporting were performed by aggregating metrics to the `run_no` level. Z-scores were calculated across all 11 runs to identify statistical outliers where $|Z-score| > 2.0$. Visualizations were generated to display the distribution of these metrics, with data points color-coded to distinguish between acceptable (blue) and abnormal (red) runs relative to the 2.0 threshold.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the 11 unique chromatographic runs revealed distinct patterns in peak shape metrics. Regarding peak tailing, Figure 2 illustrates that the majority of runs (9 out of 11) exhibited acceptable peak symmetry, with Tailing Factors clustered below the y=2.0 threshold. However, three specific runs—Run 3, Run 7, and Run 10—were flagged in red, indicating significant tailing behavior where the peak extended further into the elution volume than ideal. This visual evidence suggests potential issues with column health or sample interaction in these specific batches. In contrast, Figure 1 reveals a distribution of Fronting Factors across the runs. While the plot highlights specific runs with excessive fronting (FF > 2.0), the analysis notes that the dataset lacks critical methodological details such as the specific algorithm used for FF calculation and the handling of baseline noise parameters. Furthermore, the absence of error bars or confidence intervals limits the assessment of the statistical significance of these deviations. The analysis also encountered a discrepancy in the outlier detection reporting phase; while the visualizations successfully isolated outliers, the specific markdown report intended to detail the prevalence of outliers restricted to the ~4 usable stages failed to generate any records. This suggests that the aggregation to the `run_no` level or the filtering for usable stages did not yield the expected records in that specific output module, preventing a direct correlation between specific purification stages and the occurrence of fronting anomalies in the text report. Despite this reporting limitation, the visual data confirms that the automated detection successfully isolated outliers, with the vast majority of the manufacturing process batches maintaining acceptable chromatographic performance.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the automated analysis of the chromatographic UV absorbance signals successfully identified specific runs exhibiting abnormal peak shapes, specifically excessive tailing in Runs 3, 7, and 10. While the majority of the 11 analyzed runs demonstrated acceptable peak symmetry consistent with USP <621> guidelines, the flagged runs indicate potential process instability or column degradation requiring immediate attention. The analysis was constrained by the lack of comprehensive metadata, necessitating a reliance on time-series absorbance signals and volume data. Additionally, a technical discrepancy was observed in the outlier reporting module, which failed to aggregate data for the specific usable stages, limiting the granularity of the final text report regarding stage-specific prevalence. Future iterations of the workflow should address the data aggregation logic to ensure complete reporting across all purification stages and include statistical measures such as confidence intervals to enhance the robustness of the outlier assessment.
