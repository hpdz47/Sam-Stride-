#Abstract 

This report details the systematic extraction and validation of chromatographic peaks from high-resolution High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) fraction-level data. The primary objective was to ensure product quality and safety by detecting significant signal maxima within UV absorbance profiles at 260 nm and 280 nm, while rigorously filtering data artifacts. The analysis addressed critical data quality issues, including negative cumulative volumes and baseline noise, through a multi-step workflow involving data cleaning, baseline subtraction, and signal-to-noise ratio (S/N) calculations. The methodology successfully generated a comprehensive inventory of detected peaks with precise retention times and fraction identifiers, distinguishing valid chromatographic events from baseline noise using S/N ratios and trapezoidal integration. The final output provides a robust dataset essential for downstream quality control assessments.

#Introduction 

The analysis of high-resolution HPLC data is critical for identifying and characterizing chemical components within complex mixtures. In this study, the focus was on extracting peak information from time-series data representing individual fractions collected during HPLC runs. The dataset contained UV absorbance signals at 260 nm and 280 nm, which are standard wavelengths for monitoring nucleic acids and proteins, respectively. However, the data presented significant challenges, including negative cumulative volumes indicating data cleaning errors and negative UV absorbance readings indicative of baseline noise or detector offsets. Furthermore, critical operational metadata such as injection volume and sample codes were largely missing, necessitating a methodology that prioritizes signal characteristics over injection conditions. The motivation for this analysis was to generate a comprehensive inventory of peaks with precise retention times and fraction identifiers, ensuring that the analysis accounts for data quality issues before proceeding to quantitative integration. The end goal was to produce a robust dataset of peaks that distinguishes valid chromatographic events from baseline noise using S/N ratios and baseline subtraction, thereby facilitating accurate quality control assessments.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three primary phases: data cleaning and preprocessing, signal processing and peak detection, and quality control assessment. First, the raw data file 'chromatography_combined_data.csv' underwent rigorous cleaning. Negative volume values were filtered out to prevent calculation errors. For each run, the median UV absorbance was calculated for valid data points only; if no valid data existed, the run was skipped or a baseline of zero was set. This median value was then subtracted from the UV signals to correct for baseline offsets. Second, the cleaned data was processed to calculate the Signal-to-Noise (S/N) ratio using a 5-fraction rolling window. Peaks were identified by locating local maxima, with boundaries defined relative to the peak height for trapezoidal integration. An S/N threshold of 3.0 was used to filter out noise, while peaks with an S/N ratio below 5.0 were flagged as 'low confidence'. Third, a multi-wavelength correlation and quality control assessment was performed. The 260/280 ratio was calculated for each peak, handling zero denominators to avoid NaN errors. Data was grouped by chromatography stage to visualize S/N distributions and area correlations, generating a text-based QC report summarizing average S/N per stage and identifying stages with excessive low-confidence peaks. The entire workflow was executed using Python packages including pandas, numpy, scipy, and seaborn.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully processed the chromatography dataset, addressing the identified data quality artifacts. The initial data cleaning phase effectively removed negative volume entries and normalized UV signals by subtracting run-specific medians, ensuring that baseline noise did not interfere with peak detection. The subsequent peak detection phase identified local maxima using a robust S/N calculation method. As visualized in Figure 1, the distribution of S/N ratios varied significantly across different chromatography stages, with some stages exhibiting higher variability in signal quality. This variation highlights the importance of stage-specific parameter tuning in maintaining detection accuracy. Figure 2 reveals a strong positive correlation between the peak areas at 260 nm and 280 nm across all stages, confirming that the detected peaks represent consistent chemical entities rather than random noise. The color coding by stage further indicates that while the overall correlation holds, the magnitude of the signal varies by processing stage. The final peak inventory, summarized in Figure 3, demonstrates that the methodology successfully distinguished valid peaks from baseline noise. The aggregate peak counts per run provide a quantitative measure of the sample complexity, while the confidence levels indicate the reliability of the detected maxima. The exclusion of peaks with S/N ratios below 3.0 effectively filtered out data artifacts, resulting in a clean dataset suitable for quality control reporting. The absence of injection volume metadata was mitigated by focusing on signal characteristics, allowing for a robust analysis of peak areas and retention times without needing to correlate with specific injection conditions. Overall, the workflow achieved its primary objective of generating a comprehensive and reliable peak inventory.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the systematic data analysis workflow successfully extracted and validated chromatographic peaks from high-resolution HPLC data, overcoming significant data quality challenges such as negative volumes and baseline noise. By employing baseline subtraction, rigorous S/N ratio calculations, and multi-wavelength correlation analysis, the study produced a robust dataset of peaks with precise retention times and fraction identifiers. The results, supported by visualizations of S/N distributions and area correlations, confirm the effectiveness of the methodology in distinguishing valid chromatographic events from artifacts. The generated peak inventory serves as a critical foundation for product quality and safety assessments, ensuring that all significant signal maxima are detected and characterized. Future work may involve integrating missing metadata to further refine the analysis, but the current results provide a solid basis for quality control decisions.
