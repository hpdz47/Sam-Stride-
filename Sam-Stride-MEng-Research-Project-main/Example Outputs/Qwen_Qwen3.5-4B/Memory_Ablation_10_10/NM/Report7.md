#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic separation efficiency based on a dataset comprising 1,084,069 fractionated elution events. The primary objective was to assess the resolution ($R_s$) between neighboring peaks to ensure product purity and safety. The analysis workflow involved rigorous data cleaning to address alignment artifacts, specifically capping negative values in volume and UV absorbance columns at zero. Subsequently, peak detection and resolution metrics were calculated, stratified by experimental batch (`run_no`) and chromatography column (`column`). The results indicate that while the dataset is robust, significant data artifacts were present requiring correction. The methodology successfully mitigated the 'binning effect' inherent in fractionated data to provide a more accurate assessment of peak widths ($W_{1/2}$). Ultimately, the analysis confirms that separation efficiency varies systematically across batches and columns, necessitating specific quality control measures for downstream biopharmaceutical processes.

#Introduction 

In the biopharmaceutical industry, High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) is critical for separating target compounds from impurities. The efficacy of this separation is quantified by the resolution ($R_s$) between adjacent peaks. However, analyzing chromatographic data presents unique challenges, particularly when data is aggregated into discrete fractions. This aggregation can obscure true peak widths due to the 'binning effect,' potentially leading to an underestimation of resolution. Furthermore, raw datasets often contain artifacts, such as negative values resulting from alignment errors or noise, which must be corrected before meaningful statistical analysis can proceed. This study focuses on a large-scale dataset of 1,084,069 rows to evaluate separation efficiency. The primary motivation for this analysis is to determine if the chromatographic system effectively separates compounds across different experimental batches (`run_no`) and column types (`column`). Given that approximately 75% of the data lacks `chromatography_stage` metadata, the analysis is constrained to stratification by `run_no` and `column` only. The goal is to identify systematic variations in separation efficiency that could compromise product quality, utilizing robust statistical methods to handle data artifacts and fractionation biases.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three distinct phases: Data Cleaning, Peak Detection and Metric Calculation, and Variance Analysis. First, the raw dataset (`chromatography_combined.csv`) underwent signal preprocessing. Negative values in the `volume_ml` and UV absorbance columns (ranging down to -33.8 ml and -7.9 mAU) were identified and capped at zero to correct for alignment errors. Rows with missing `run_no` or `column` identifiers were excluded, while `chromatography_stage` was explicitly ignored due to high missingness. Second, peak detection was performed on the continuous `volume_ml` axis using the `UV_1_280_mAU` signal. To mitigate the binning effect, peak widths ($W_{1/2}$) were calculated using interpolation between data points. Resolution ($R_s$) was subsequently computed between adjacent peaks. These metrics were stratified by `run_no` (1–32) and `column` (4 unique types). Finally, batch variance was assessed by calculating the mean and standard deviation of $R_s$ and $W_{1/2}$ for each combination. Outliers were identified using the Interquartile Range (IQR) method, and groups with zero detected peaks were excluded from the final efficiency summary. This structured approach ensures that the derived metrics accurately reflect the true separation performance despite data aggregation and noise.

#Results and Discussion 

The initial data cleaning phase confirmed the dataset integrity, retaining all 1,084,069 rows after capping negative artifacts. However, the presence of significant negative values in volume and absorbance columns highlighted the necessity of the preprocessing step. As illustrated in the visualizations, the peak detection methodology successfully identified local maxima on the continuous volume axis. Figure 1 displays the chromatographic profile for Run 1, where the interpolation technique effectively estimated the peak width at half-height ($W_{1/2}$), mitigating the distortion caused by fraction binning. Similarly, Figure 2 shows the profile for Run 16, confirming that the detection algorithm performs consistently across different experimental batches. The calculated resolution metrics ($R_s$) reveal distinct patterns when stratified by column type. Figure 3 presents a box plot of $R_s$ values across the four columns, indicating that separation efficiency is not uniform. While the median resolution appears acceptable for most columns, the interquartile ranges suggest variability in peak sharpness. Furthermore, the variance analysis identified specific outliers in $R_s$ and $W_{1/2}$ values for certain `run_no` and `column` combinations. These outliers, flagged by the IQR method, suggest potential systematic issues in specific batches or column conditions that could lead to inconsistent product purity. The absence of `chromatography_stage` data limits the ability to correlate these variations with specific manufacturing stages, but the stratification by `run_no` and `column` successfully isolates the batch and hardware effects. Overall, the results demonstrate that while the system is generally effective, the binning effect and batch-specific variances must be accounted for in quality control protocols.

#Conclusion 

This analysis successfully evaluated the chromatographic separation efficiency of a large-scale dataset, addressing critical data artifacts and fractionation biases. The methodology of capping negative values and utilizing interpolation for peak width calculation provided a robust framework for calculating resolution ($R_s$). The results confirm that separation efficiency varies significantly between experimental batches and chromatography columns, as evidenced by the stratified variance and outlier detection. While the system generally meets the criteria for separating target compounds from impurities, the identified outliers and column-specific variations pose risks to downstream biopharmaceutical quality. Future analyses should focus on investigating the root causes of the identified outliers and exploring the impact of fraction volume on resolution to further mitigate the binning effect. Until then, the stratified monitoring of $R_s$ by `run_no` and `column` serves as a critical indicator for maintaining product safety and purity.
