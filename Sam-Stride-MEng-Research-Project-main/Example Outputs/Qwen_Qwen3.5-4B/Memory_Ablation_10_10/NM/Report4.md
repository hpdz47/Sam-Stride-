#Abstract 

This report details the evaluation of chromatographic separation quality for biopharmaceutical samples based on UV_2_260_mAU time-series data. Due to significant missingness in linking variables such as Sample_Code and chromatography_stage, the analysis was restricted to run-level and column-level assessments. The primary objective was to identify instances of peak co-elution that compromise product purity. The methodology involved rigorous baseline noise estimation using 3-sigma limits and zero-value filtering to eliminate artifacts, followed by peak detection, integration, and resolution calculation (Rs). A critical focus was placed on filtering out baseline noise and artifacts defined as UV_2_260_mAU values below zero or outside the 3-sigma baseline limit to prevent false peak detections. The analysis successfully identified a general decline in resolution over time across all four chromatography columns, particularly in runs 20–32, suggesting potential column degradation or systematic process drift. While early runs exhibited stable separation, later runs suffered from co-elution issues, necessitating further process optimization.

#Introduction 

Chromatographic separation is a critical step in biopharmaceutical manufacturing, ensuring the purity of the final product by resolving distinct analytes. However, the integrity of this process can be compromised by baseline noise, sensor drift, and column degradation, leading to co-elution where distinct peaks overlap. In this study, we analyzed a complete UV_2_260_mAU time-series dataset to evaluate separation quality. A significant challenge in this dataset was the severe missingness in critical linking variables, including Sample_Code and chromatography_stage, which accounted for over 75% of the missing data. This limitation prevented sample-specific resolution assessment and forced a shift in focus to run-level and column-level patterns. Furthermore, the presence of negative values in the UV signal indicated significant baseline noise or sensor drift. Therefore, the primary motivation for this analysis was to establish robust noise filtering criteria (specifically 3-sigma limits and zero thresholds) and to detect instances of co-elution at the run and column level to guide actionable process optimizations without relying on incomplete metadata.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three primary steps designed to ensure data integrity and accurate resolution metrics. First, baseline noise estimation and signal cleaning were performed on the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset. This involved replacing volume_ml filtering with direct filtering of `UV_2_260_mAU` values below zero or outside 3-sigma limits to align with noise criteria. Missing `Fraction_number` values (11.5%) were imputed using the median of the group before mapping to retention time. Crucially, values masked as noise were not interpolated; interpolation was restricted to valid data points to prevent propagating noise. Second, peak detection and resolution analysis were conducted. Peaks were detected using the cleaned signal, and Resolution (Rs) was calculated between adjacent peaks. Pairs with Rs < 1.5 were flagged as co-eluting, and an overlap ratio (area of intersection / area of union) was calculated for these flagged pairs to quantify the extent of overlap. Third, run-to-run variability and trending were analyzed. To ensure statistical stability, columns were required to have a minimum run count of five before calculating mean Rs. Outlier runs were explicitly linked to their specific column identifiers, and a line chart visualizing the mean resolution trend over time was generated to identify systematic drift.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the chromatographic resolution trend reveals a clear degradation in separation quality over the course of the experimental runs. As illustrated in Figure 1, the mean Resolution (Rs) between adjacent peaks generally declined over time for all four chromatography columns. The visualization, which plots mean Rs against the chronological sequence of runs (1–32) with error bars representing standard deviation, confirms that the analysis was conducted on columns meeting the minimum run count threshold of five runs. The primary observed pattern indicates a general decline in resolution, particularly noticeable in the later runs (20–32). This trend suggests potential column degradation or systematic process drift, leading to co-elution issues in the latter half of the experiment. While early runs exhibited stable separation, the increasing variability and decreasing resolution in later runs indicate that the separation quality is not consistent throughout the production batch. This finding is critical as co-elution can compromise product purity, necessitating immediate attention to process optimization or column replacement strategies to ensure the quality of the final biopharmaceutical product.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, this study successfully evaluated the chromatographic separation quality of biopharmaceutical samples despite limitations in sample-level metadata. By implementing rigorous baseline noise estimation using 3-sigma limits and zero-value filtering, the analysis effectively removed artifacts that could lead to false peak detections. The results demonstrate a consistent decline in resolution over time across all tested columns, with significant co-elution occurring in runs 20–32. This trend points to column degradation or systematic process drift as the primary drivers of reduced separation quality. Future work should focus on optimizing chromatographic conditions or implementing preventive maintenance schedules to mitigate this drift. Additionally, efforts should be made to recover missing linking variables to enable sample-specific resolution assessments, which would provide a more granular understanding of process variability.
