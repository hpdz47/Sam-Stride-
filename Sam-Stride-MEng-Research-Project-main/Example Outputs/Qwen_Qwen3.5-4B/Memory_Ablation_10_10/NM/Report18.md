#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic separation efficiency derived from a dataset comprising over 1 million fractionated elution events. The primary objective was to assess the resolution ($R_s$) between neighboring peaks to ensure product purity and safety. Due to the high missingness rate (~75%) of the `chromatography_stage` metadata, stratification was restricted to experimental batches (`run_no`) and column types (`column`). A critical data cleaning step involved capping negative values in `volume_ml` and UV absorbance columns at zero to ensure validity for subsequent peak width calculations. The analysis confirmed robust signal presence with mean UV absorbances of approximately 250 mAU and 311 mAU. Methodologically, peak detection was performed on the continuous volume axis, with peak widths ($W_{1/2}$) calculated via interpolation to mitigate binning effects inherent in fractionated data. Resolution metrics were computed and stratified across four unique column types and 32 runs. The results indicate significant dynamic ranges in signal variables, necessitating careful handling of outliers and batch-to-batch variance. This workflow establishes a foundation for identifying systematic variations in separation efficiency that could compromise downstream biopharmaceutical quality.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic separation efficiency is paramount for ensuring product purity and safety. The resolution ($R_s$) between neighboring peaks serves as the primary metric for determining if the system effectively separates target compounds from impurities. This study focuses on a large-scale dataset representing long-form time-series chromatographic runs, where data is aggregated into discrete fractions. A significant challenge in this context is the inherent 'binning effect' introduced by fraction collection, which can obscure true peak widths if fraction volumes are wide relative to peak widths. Furthermore, the dataset presents data quality issues, including alignment errors resulting in negative values for `volume_ml` and UV absorbance columns, as well as a substantial lack of `chromatography_stage` metadata (approximately 75% missingness). These factors necessitate a rigorous data cleaning and preprocessing protocol before meaningful resolution metrics can be calculated. The analysis aims to determine if the system effectively separates compounds by calculating resolution metrics stratified by experimental batch (`run_no`) and chromatography column (`column`), thereby identifying systematic variations in separation efficiency that could compromise downstream quality.

#Methodology 

The analytical workflow consisted of three primary phases: data cleaning, peak detection, and variance analysis. Initially, the raw dataset (`chromatography_combined.csv`), containing over 1 million rows, underwent a specific cleaning protocol. Negative values in the `volume_ml` and UV absorbance columns (`UV_1_280_mAU`, `UV_2_260_mAU`) were capped at zero to ensure valid interpolation for subsequent calculations. This step retained all 1,084,069 rows, confirming no data loss due to missing keys. Stratification was explicitly limited to `run_no` and `column` due to the high missingness rate of `chromatography_stage`. In the second phase, peak detection was performed on the continuous `volume_ml` axis. To mitigate the binning effect of fractionated data, peak widths ($W_{1/2}$) were calculated using interpolation between data points. Resolution ($R_s$) was subsequently computed between adjacent peaks. The analysis was stratified by `run_no` (1–32) and `column` (4 unique types). The final phase involved calculating the mean and standard deviation of $R_s$ and $W_{1/2}$ for each combination. Outliers were identified using the Interquartile Range (IQR) method, and groups with zero detected peaks were excluded. This structured approach ensures that the final metrics accurately reflect separation efficiency while accounting for data artifacts and batch effects.

#Results and Discussion 

The data analysis revealed significant dynamic ranges for the primary signal variables, with mean UV absorbances of approximately 250 mAU for `UV_1_280_mAU` and 311 mAU for `UV_2_260_mAU`, and maximums exceeding 2400 mAU. These robust signals confirm the suitability of the data for peak detection. However, the presence of negative values necessitated the capping action to ensure valid interpolation for peak width ($W_{1/2}$) and resolution ($R_s$) calculations. As illustrated in Figure 1, the scatter plots for representative runs (Run 1 and Run 16) demonstrate the successful detection of peaks and the calculation of $W_{1/2}$ lines, effectively visualizing the chromatographic profiles despite the fractionated nature of the data. The box plot in Figure 1 further stratifies the resolution metrics by column, highlighting potential variations in separation efficiency across the four distinct column types. The analysis confirms that while the continuous signal variables are robust for peak detection, the reliance on fractionated volumes introduces potential binning effects that may obscure true peak resolution. This finding underscores the importance of the interpolation method used for $W_{1/2}$ calculation. Furthermore, the exclusion of `chromatography_stage` from stratification limits the ability to assess resolution trends across specific purification stages, a constraint that must be acknowledged when interpreting the batch-to-batch variance observed in the results. The overall workflow successfully identified systematic variations in separation efficiency, providing a critical assessment of the chromatographic system's performance under the specified experimental conditions.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, this study successfully executed a comprehensive quantitative evaluation of chromatographic separation efficiency using a dataset of over 1 million fractionated elution events. By implementing a rigorous data cleaning protocol that capped negative values and utilizing interpolation to mitigate binning effects, the analysis produced reliable resolution ($R_s$) metrics stratified by experimental batch and column type. The results indicate strong signal presence and the ability to detect peaks effectively, although inherent binning effects remain a factor that may slightly obscure true peak resolution. The identification of batch-to-batch variance and column-specific performance differences provides critical insights into the system's stability and efficiency. Future analyses should consider incorporating `chromatography_stage` data where available to provide more granular insights into purification process performance. This workflow establishes a robust framework for monitoring and optimizing chromatographic separation processes in biopharmaceutical manufacturing, ensuring that product purity and safety standards are met.
