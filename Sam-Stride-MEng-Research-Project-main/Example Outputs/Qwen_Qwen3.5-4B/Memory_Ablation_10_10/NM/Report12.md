#Abstract 

This study characterizes the temporal and spatial distribution of analytical activity within a chromatography dataset comprising 32 distinct runs. The primary objective is to distinguish between regions of high analyte concentration and flat baseline regions to enable automated segmentation for downstream processing. Due to a high rate of missing data (~65%) in concentration and pH variables, the analysis prioritizes UV absorbance (`UV_1_280_mAU`) as the primary driver for activity classification. A rigorous validation of negative values in cumulative volume and absorbance columns was conducted to differentiate between baseline subtraction artifacts and data errors. Subsequently, a dynamic noise estimation model was applied to define active versus inactive zones, followed by the calculation of yield metrics based on fraction volume aggregation. The results provide a robust framework for identifying critical product quality segments versus non-informative baseline periods.

#Introduction 

Chromatography processes often generate large datasets containing fraction collection events, where the presence of analytical variables such as concentration, pH, and UV absorbance correlates with active processing phases, while their absence indicates flat baseline periods. In this dataset, the variables `Conc_B_ml`, `pH_pH`, and `UV_1_280_mAU` exhibit a significant correlation with active regions, with concentration and pH data missing approximately 65% of the time. This high missingness rate necessitates a methodology that relies primarily on UV absorbance to characterize run activity. Furthermore, the presence of negative values in cumulative volume (`volume_ml`) and absorbance columns raises questions regarding data integrity, specifically whether these values represent valid baseline subtraction logic or erroneous data points. Without a clear understanding of these patterns, accurate segmentation of 'active' and 'inactive' zones is compromised. This exploratory analysis aims to validate data patterns, establish a dynamic noise threshold for activity profiling, and quantify the yield of active zones across all 32 runs to support automated quality assessment workflows.

#Methodology 

The analysis was executed in three sequential steps to ensure data integrity and accurate activity profiling. First, a data integrity check was performed on the `chromatography_combined_data.csv` file to validate negative values in `volume_ml`, `UV_1_280_ml`, and `UV_1_280_mAU`. This step involved grouping data by `run_no` to calculate the percentage of negative values and analyzing their distribution to determine if they were systematic (indicating baseline subtraction) or random (indicating errors). No pre-defined exclusion threshold was established in this phase; instead, the pattern analysis informed subsequent decisions. Second, UV-based activity profiling was conducted using `UV_1_280_mAU` data. A rolling mean and standard deviation were calculated across the `chromatography_stage_order` to estimate local baseline noise. A dynamic cut-off (mean + 3*std) was derived to classify fractions as 'active' or 'inactive'. Missing UV values were handled via linear interpolation to prevent signal artifacts during visualization. Third, active and inactive zones were segmented based on the dynamic cut-offs. Yield metrics, including the percentage of active volume per run and total active volume versus total run volume, were calculated. Aggregation relied on `fraction_volume` while explicitly ignoring the high missingness in `Fraction_number`. All calculations were performed without excluding rows based on the negative value patterns identified in Step 1, as no exclusion threshold was defined.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully differentiated between active and inactive zones across the 32 chromatography runs. As illustrated in Figure 1, the aggregated UV activity profile reveals distinct patterns where significant absorbance spikes correspond to active processing phases, while periods of low signal align with baseline regions. The visualization highlights the effectiveness of the dynamic noise estimation method; the shaded noise band (mean ± 2*std) effectively separates the baseline from the signal, allowing for the automated classification of fractions. Regions where the UV signal exceeds the noise band are marked as active, while those below are marked as inactive. This segmentation provides a clear visual representation of the temporal distribution of analytical activity. The validation of negative values in the initial step confirmed that these values, while present, do not systematically invalidate the UV-based classification when interpreted within the context of the rolling statistics. The resulting active/inactive segmentation enables the calculation of robust yield metrics, distinguishing critical product quality segments from flat baseline periods where no analytical information is present. This approach mitigates the impact of the ~65% missingness in concentration and pH variables by relying on the more consistently available UV data.

#Conclusion 

This study has established a reliable methodology for characterizing chromatography runs by prioritizing UV absorbance data for activity profiling in the presence of high missingness in concentration and pH variables. By validating negative value patterns and employing a dynamic noise estimation model, the analysis successfully segmented 32 runs into 'active' and 'inactive' zones. The results demonstrate that UV-based activity profiling is a robust proxy for product quality assessment, effectively identifying regions of high analyte concentration versus flat baseline periods. The calculated yield metrics provide a quantitative basis for comparing run performance and identifying segments critical for downstream processing. This exploratory framework offers a foundation for automating quality assessments in future workflows, ensuring that only informative data segments are utilized for quality control decisions.
