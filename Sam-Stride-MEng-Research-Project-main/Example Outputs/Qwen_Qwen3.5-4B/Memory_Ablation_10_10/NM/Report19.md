#Abstract 

This report details a data integrity and peak shape analysis conducted on chromatographic fractionation data to ensure product quality in biopharmaceutical manufacturing. Due to significant data missingness in process parameters, the analysis focused on intra-stage consistency checks using UV absorbance signals (`UV_1_280_mAU`, `UV_2_260_mAU`) against `fraction_volume`. The methodology involved defining granular stages, detecting outliers in peak width metrics, applying Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction to handle negative absorbance artifacts, and calculating Tailing and Fronting factors according to USP <621> criteria. The primary objective was to identify deviations from ideal Gaussian profiles, specifically peaks with Tailing Factors > 1.8 or Fronting Factors > 1.8. Results indicate the presence of distinct peak structures with varying degrees of tailing. Visualizations confirm that automated analysis successfully identified regions exceeding the tailing threshold, with red overlays marking specific bins in the 280 nm channel. The 260 nm channel also displays a primary peak structure consistent with protein elution. These findings suggest potential column degradation or process parameter failures requiring further investigation.

#Introduction 

Chromatographic purity and peak symmetry are critical indicators of product quality and safety in biopharmaceutical manufacturing. Abnormal peak shapes, characterized by excessive tailing or fronting, often signal underlying issues such as column degradation, sample impurities, or failures in process parameters like pH or injection volume. This analysis was initiated to characterize these abnormal peak shapes within the available `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset. The dataset presents challenges, including over 60% missingness in critical process parameters (`Injection_ml`, `pH_ml`) and sparse stage identifiers, which limits the ability to correlate peak abnormalities with specific process conditions. Consequently, the analysis strategy prioritized data integrity checks, robust peak definition using `fraction_volume`, and the calculation of symmetry metrics independent of missing process data. The focus was narrowed to intra-stage consistency checks to mitigate the impact of missing categorical data. By transforming univariate statistical summaries into visual and statistical assessments, this study aims to detect deviations from Gaussian profiles and provide actionable insights into the chromatographic performance.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a three-step workflow designed to ensure data integrity and accurate peak shape characterization. First, data integrity and granular stage definition were performed. Rows with missing `Fraction_number` were excluded from peak integration. Peak start and end points were defined based on the first and last non-missing `fraction_volume` values, with x-axis gaps handled without interpolation. Second, outlier detection in peak width metrics was executed. Initial width estimates (e.g., distance between 5% and 95% height points) were calculated for all identified peaks. Peaks where the width deviated significantly (>3 standard deviations) from the median were flagged and excluded from subsequent calculations to prevent skewing results. Third, peak shape metrics and visualization were generated. Tailing Factor (Tf) and Fronting Factor (Ff) were calculated using the formula Tf = (tR + w0.05) / (2 * tR) and Ff = (tR + w0.95) / (2 * tR) on the non-outlier filtered data. To manage high data density and prevent performance crashes, raw data was binned into 1000 bins along the x-axis (`fraction_volume`), plotting mean UV intensity per bin. Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline estimation was applied to handle negative absorbance values, with noise estimated as the standard deviation of the baseline. Peaks were further validated by ensuring they met a minimum height/area threshold and had a Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) exceeding 3.0. Visualizations were generated for both `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` channels, with red overlays indicating bins containing abnormal peaks (Tf > 1.8 or Ff > 1.8).

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully identified peak morphology and detected regions of abnormality. The `UV_1_280_mAU` channel (Figure 1) reveals a primary chromatographic peak characterized by a sharp rise and a gradual, extended tail extending toward higher volumes, which is consistent with a tailing profile. A secondary, smaller peak is visible at lower volumes. Notably, the plot includes a red overlay highlighting specific bins containing abnormal peaks, indicating that the automated analysis successfully identified regions where the Tailing Factor likely exceeds the USP <621> threshold of 1.8. The presence of negative absorbance values near the baseline suggests the necessity of the applied baseline correction. The `UV_2_260_mAU` channel (Figure 2) displays a distinct, high-intensity peak structure, likely representing a protein elution profile, characterized by a sharp rise and a gradual decline. While the distribution appears relatively smooth, suggesting that the binning strategy successfully smoothed noise, the absence of visible red overlays in this specific figure interpretation implies that the specific thresholding or outlier filtering steps may have yielded different results or the visualization did not explicitly render the flagged bins in the same manner as the 280 nm plot, or the abnormality was less pronounced in this channel. The distribution in Figure 2 suggests that the binning successfully mitigated performance issues and 'hairline' artifacts. Overall, the visualizations confirm the existence of tailing peaks and the application of the binning methodology to manage data volume, effectively transforming raw time-series data into a morphological assessment. The analysis isolates potential process failures related to column degradation or impurity elution within the specified chromatographic stages, particularly in the 280 nm channel where clear red-flagged regions were observed.

#Conclusion 

This analysis has successfully characterized the peak shapes within the provided chromatographic dataset, overcoming significant data integrity challenges such as missing process parameters and sparse stage identifiers. By employing a rigorous methodology involving outlier detection, ALS baseline correction, and USP <621> compliant factor calculations, the study identified specific regions of abnormal peak morphology. The results indicate the presence of tailing peaks, particularly evident in the `UV_1_280_mAU` channel where red overlays highlight bins exceeding the tailing threshold of 1.8. These findings suggest that the observed deviations from ideal Gaussian profiles may be indicative of column degradation, sample impurities, or process parameter failures. While the 260 nm channel also displays a clear peak structure, the explicit identification of abnormal regions was more pronounced in the 280 nm data. Future work should focus on investigating the root causes of the identified tailing, potentially by analyzing the specific chromatography stages associated with the flagged peaks, once more complete process data becomes available. The current results provide a critical baseline for quality assurance and highlight areas requiring immediate process optimization or equipment maintenance.
