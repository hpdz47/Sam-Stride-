#Abstract 

This report details a rigorous quality control assessment of high-frequency High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) time-series data. The primary objective was to distinguish genuine chromatographic anomalies from systematic artifacts, specifically addressing negative UV baselines and physically impossible negative volume offsets. The analysis employed Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) for baseline correction and validated a global volume offset calibration strategy. Furthermore, temporal correlation analysis between pressure surges and UV spikes was utilized to isolate mechanical artifacts, while dynamic change-point detection inferred missing process states. While the methodology successfully validated the global nature of the volume offset and identified mechanical noise sources, the final peak symmetry analysis revealed a critical bifurcation in data quality. Approximately half of the experimental runs failed to yield valid peak width metrics, suggesting that the volume offset correction was not uniformly applied or that peak detection thresholds were insufficient for the remaining corrupted data. These findings highlight the necessity of robust preprocessing validation before downstream symmetry analysis.

#Introduction 

High-frequency HPLC data often suffers from structural artifacts that can obscure genuine chromatographic signals. In this dataset, two primary data quality issues were identified: negative UV baseline values and a systematic offset in volume measurements resulting in physically impossible negative values (minimum -33.8 ml). Additionally, approximately 75% of the data lacked `chromatography_stage` metadata, and 11.5% of rows were missing `Fraction_unique_ID`, complicating the distinction between intentional wash steps and missing peaks. The motivation for this analysis was to establish a robust preprocessing pipeline to correct these artifacts, thereby validating detector integrity and experimental consistency. Specifically, the workflow aimed to determine if the volume offset was a global constant or run-specific, correlate pressure surges with UV spikes to filter mechanical noise, and infer process states using dynamic change-point detection. The ultimate goal was to perform a reliable injection consistency and peak symmetry analysis to assess column performance and gradient-dependent distortion.

#Methodology 

The data analysis followed a three-step plan. First, Baseline Correction and Volume Offset Calibration were performed. Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) with hyperparameters lambda=10^5 and p=0.005 was applied to correct negative UV baselines. To address the volume offset, the minimum `volume_ml` value for each of the 11 runs was analyzed to determine if the offset was global or run-specific. Second, Temporal Correlation and Process State Inference were conducted. A lagged correlation matrix (window +/- 5 rows) was calculated between `DeltaC_pressure_MPa` and `UV_1_280_mAU` to identify mechanical artifacts (correlation > 0.7). For rows with missing metadata, dynamic change-point detection on the `Conc_B_%` profile was used to infer `chromatography_stage`, while wash steps were validated by checking for stable gradient slopes at concentration extremes. Third, Injection Consistency and Peak Symmetry Analysis were executed. The consistency of sparse `Injection_ml` values was checked via variance analysis. Peaks were identified in baseline-corrected UV data, and Peak Width at Half-Height (W½) and asymmetry factors were calculated in volume units. A sampling uniformity check flagged peaks with >5% variation in `volume_ml` differences as low confidence.

#Results and Discussion 

The validation of the volume offset strategy, as visualized in Figure 1, revealed a consistent clustering of minimum volume values around -33.8 ml across all 11 runs. This uniformity confirms that the negative offset is a global constant rather than a run-specific artifact, validating the application of a single global correction constant to restore physical validity to the `volume_ml` variable. Subsequent analysis of temporal correlations, shown in Figure 2, demonstrated a strong association between pressure surges (`DeltaC_pressure_MPa`) and UV spikes within the defined lag window. This supports the hypothesis that mechanical artifacts are a primary driver of certain UV anomalies, allowing for their effective isolation. Additionally, the change-point detection on `Conc_B_%` profiles successfully inferred process states for the 75% of rows lacking metadata, effectively segmenting runs into equilibration, wash, and elution phases.

However, the results of the peak symmetry analysis presented a critical challenge. As depicted in Figure 3, the data exhibits a stark bifurcation in quality. Runs 1, 2, 4, 16, and 17, despite having high injection counts, yielded an average peak width of 0.000000 ml and extremely high injection variance. This indicates a failure in the peak detection or width calculation logic for these runs, likely because the volume offset correction was not successfully applied or the peak detection algorithm was overly sensitive to residual artifacts. Conversely, Runs 18, 19, 20, and 21 produced physically plausible peak widths (0.03–0.07 ml) and low low-confidence peak counts, suggesting successful preprocessing for this subset. The zero-width results for the majority of runs render the symmetry analysis invalid for the bulk of the dataset, highlighting that while the methodological framework is sound, its execution was inconsistent. The scatter plot in Figure 3, therefore, only represents the valid subset of data, limiting the ability to draw broad conclusions regarding gradient-dependent distortion across the entire experimental set.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis successfully validated the global nature of the volume offset error and established a robust method for isolating mechanical artifacts through temporal correlation. The inference of missing process states via change-point detection also proved effective in reconstructing the experimental timeline. However, the study concluded that the peak symmetry analysis was compromised for the majority of the dataset. The failure to calculate valid peak widths for five out of eleven runs suggests that the volume offset correction was not uniformly applied or that the preprocessing pipeline requires further refinement to handle the specific artifacts present in those runs. Future work must focus on re-applying the global offset correction to the affected runs and re-evaluating peak detection thresholds to ensure consistent, physically valid symmetry metrics across the entire dataset. Until these issues are resolved, the assessment of column performance and gradient-dependent distortion remains incomplete.
