#Abstract 

This report details a comprehensive data analysis workflow designed to quantify baseline stability and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) in chromatography data across four distinct columns (CM, DEAE, Q, SP) and multiple runs. The primary objective was to distinguish genuine analyte peaks from baseline artifacts by establishing unit-specific noise thresholds and correcting for baseline offsets. The methodology involved a three-step process: calculating baseline offsets and noise floors using pre-injection data, performing column-stratified SNR assessments while excluding gradient transitions, and exploring the correlation between buffer conductivity and baseline drift. Results indicate significant variability in baseline stability across columns, with the Q column exhibiting the highest noise levels and the 260 nm UV channel demonstrating consistently higher variability than the 280 nm channel. The analysis successfully identified specific run-column combinations with high variance, providing a robust diagnostic foundation for quality control and peak detection validation.

#Introduction 

In high-resolution chromatography, the accurate detection of analyte peaks relies heavily on the stability of the baseline and the precise quantification of the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). Variability in baseline drift, often induced by buffer conductivity changes or column degradation, can lead to false positives or the masking of genuine peaks. This study focuses on analyzing high-frequency time-series data from four chromatography columns (CM, DEAE, Q, SP) across multiple runs, utilizing two UV detection channels: 280 nm (`UV_1_280_mAU`) and 260 nm (`UV_2_260_mAU`). The 260 nm channel is known to exhibit higher inherent variability, necessitating independent noise profiling. The primary motivation for this analysis is to establish a rigorous, unit-specific framework for peak detection that accounts for column-specific degradation and run-to-run variability. By correcting baseline offsets derived from stable pre-injection regions and excluding gradient transition zones, this workflow aims to define reliable 3σ and 5σ detection thresholds. Furthermore, the study explores the relationship between buffer conductivity and baseline drift to identify potential sources of systematic error, ensuring that subsequent quantitative analyses are based on robust, noise-corrected data.

#Methodology 

The data analysis followed a structured three-step plan. Step 1 involved Baseline Stability & Noise Threshold Calculation. Data was filtered to include only pre-injection regions (`volume_ml` < 0) where the buffer concentration (`Conc_B_%`) was stable (±0.5% of 0.0 or 100.0). A rolling standard deviation (window=25 scans) was applied to the offset-corrected signal to calculate global noise thresholds (3σ and 5σ) for each column and channel combination. Groups with fewer than 25 valid scans were excluded. Step 2 focused on Column-Stratified SNR & Peak Detection. Using the offsets from Step 1, the analysis identified valid baseline regions post-injection, explicitly excluding gradient transitions and potential peak regions (signal > 3σ). Local noise was recalculated on these clean regions, and SNR was determined as the ratio of peak height to local noise standard deviation. Results were aggregated by column and run, with a specific focus on visualizing the top three runs with the highest variance. Step 3 conducted a Conductivity Correlation & Drift Analysis. Constant gradient regions were isolated based on strict linearity (R² > 0.99) and stability criteria. Linear regression was used to calculate drift rates (mAU/mL), which were then correlated with buffer conductivity (`Cond_mS_cm`) using Pearson correlation, stratified by column, to assess the impact of buffer properties on baseline stability.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of baseline stability revealed significant heterogeneity across the four chromatography columns. As detailed in the baseline analysis, the Q column exhibited the highest noise levels, with 3σ thresholds reaching up to 0.36 mAU at 260 nm, whereas the CM and SP columns demonstrated superior stability with thresholds generally below 0.06 mAU. This confirms that unit-specific factors, potentially related to column fouling or degradation, significantly impact the noise floor. Furthermore, the 260 nm channel consistently displayed higher noise thresholds than the 280 nm channel across all columns, aligning with the dataset's inherent variability characteristics. The offset correction step proved critical, with observed baseline drifts ranging from -0.28 mAU to +1.3 mAU, validating the necessity of pre-injection mean subtraction. Figure 1 visualizes the resulting SNR performance, stratified by column and distinguishing between the two UV channels. The plot highlights the top three runs with the highest variance, serving as a diagnostic tool for identifying unstable experimental conditions. High SNR values indicate robust peak detection capabilities, while the variability observed in specific column-run combinations suggests inconsistent baseline stability or detector sensitivity issues. The exclusion of gradient transitions and the application of the 3σ threshold ensured that the SNR values reflect true signal quality rather than process dynamics. Additionally, the exploratory conductivity correlation analysis provided insights into buffer-induced drift, although the absence of temperature data limits the ability to fully decouple conductivity effects from other environmental factors. Overall, the results provide a robust, unit-specific foundation for quality control, enabling the identification of columns requiring maintenance and ensuring that peak calls are not artifacts of baseline instability.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully established a rigorous framework for assessing baseline stability and SNR in multi-column chromatography workflows. The analysis confirmed that noise profiles are highly dependent on both the specific column unit and the UV detection channel, with the Q column and 260 nm channel exhibiting the highest variability. The implementation of offset correction and strict gradient exclusion logic was essential for deriving accurate noise thresholds and SNR values. The visualization of high-variance runs (Figure 1) provides a clear diagnostic metric for identifying potential equipment issues or experimental inconsistencies. While the correlation between conductivity and drift offers valuable insights into buffer-induced baseline shifts, future work should incorporate temperature data to fully elucidate the drivers of baseline instability. The methodology presented herein ensures that peak detection is based on statistically sound, unit-specific thresholds, thereby enhancing the reliability of downstream quantitative analyses.
