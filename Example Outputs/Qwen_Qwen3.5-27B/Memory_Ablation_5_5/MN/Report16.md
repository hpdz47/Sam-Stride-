#Abstract 

This report evaluates the data integrity of 11 high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) runs, focusing on the prevalence of non-physical negative absorbance artifacts and the efficacy of baseline correction algorithms. An audit of raw UV signals revealed significant baseline drift and negative excursions reaching -7.95 mAU, necessitating volume axis normalization and advanced baseline correction. Comparative analysis between the existing vendor algorithm (BASEM) and simulated adaptive methods (Polynomial and Valley-to-Valley) demonstrates that BASEM fails to meet the required noise floor (<0.5 mAU) and zero-negative-value thresholds. The adaptive Polynomial algorithm, utilizing dynamic windowing based on empirical peak widths, significantly outperformed the vendor settings in reducing residual noise and eliminating negative artifacts. Furthermore, a moderate negative correlation between residual noise and pH suggests process conditions influence baseline stability. The study recommends abandoning the current vendor configuration in favor of the adaptive Polynomial method to ensure signal integrity for downstream quantitative analysis.

#Introduction 

In biopharmaceutical quality control, the accuracy of High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) data is paramount for reliable quantitative integration. However, raw UV sensor data often suffers from baseline drift and pre-injection artifacts, leading to physically impossible negative absorbance values that compromise data validity. The dataset under analysis, comprising 11 chromatography runs, exhibited severe baseline deviations, with raw signal minima reaching -7.95 mAU. Additionally, the volume axis displayed negative offsets, indicating pre-injection artifacts that required normalization. The primary objective of this analysis was to validate the necessity of baseline correction, assess the performance of the existing vendor algorithm (`UV_1_280_CUT_TEMP_100_BASEM_mAU`), and determine if re-parameterization or a novel adaptive approach was required. The analysis aimed to achieve a residual noise floor below 0.5 mAU and eliminate all negative values while maintaining a relative noise threshold of less than 1% of the peak height. By simulating adaptive baseline algorithms constrained by empirically derived peak widths, this study seeks to establish a robust methodology for preserving signal integrity in high-frequency datasets.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three sequential steps. First, a Baseline Artifact Audit and Volume Normalization was performed. Raw data was grouped by run number, and the volume axis was shifted to ensure the minimum volume was 0.0 mL, effectively excluding pre-injection artifacts. Statistics were calculated for raw UV signals and the vendor-corrected signals to quantify negative value counts and magnitudes. Second, an Adaptive Baseline Simulation and Noise Floor Validation was executed. Raw signals were smoothed using a Savitzky-Golay filter (window=11, order=2). Empirical peak widths were derived via first-derivative analysis. Two adaptive algorithms were implemented: a Polynomial fit (degree 2-3) with dynamic windowing (2.5x peak width) and a Valley-to-Valley linear interpolation. Corrected signals were generated by subtracting the fitted baselines from the smoothed raw signals. Residual noise was estimated in flat regions and compared against the 0.5 mAU threshold. Third, Algorithm Efficacy and Metadata Correlation were assessed. Residual noise metrics were aggregated across runs to compare the vendor algorithm against the simulated methods. Pearson correlation analysis was conducted between residual noise and process metadata (Conc_B_% and pH) to identify environmental drivers of baseline instability.

#Results and Discussion 

The audit of the 11 chromatography runs confirmed a critical data quality issue: all runs exhibited physically impossible negative absorbance values in the raw UV signals, with minima reaching -7.95 mAU. Consequently, volume normalization was required for every run, with shifts ranging from 5.01 mL to 33.83 mL to correct pre-injection artifacts. As illustrated in Figure 1, the visual comparison of the baseline correction simulations demonstrates the efficacy of the adaptive algorithms. The figure displays a 3x4 grid of subplots, each overlaying the smoothed raw signal, the polynomial baseline fit, the valley-to-valley baseline fit, and the corrected signal. Visually, both simulated algorithms effectively track the low-signal regions, successfully lifting the signal floor and eliminating the negative artifacts observed in the raw data. The corrected signals generally reside above the zero line, confirming the removal of non-physical excursions.

Quantitative analysis revealed that the existing vendor algorithm (BASEM) is insufficient for the required data quality standards. Despite processing, BASEM failed to eliminate negative values entirely, with minima remaining between -0.05 and -1.98 mAU, and exceeded the 0.5 mAU noise threshold in every run. In contrast, the simulated adaptive methods demonstrated superior performance. The Polynomial algorithm generated significantly lower residual noise (195,622 units) and fewer negative counts (123,420) compared to the vendor algorithm (434,951 noise units; 210,162 negatives). The Valley-to-Valley method also outperformed the vendor settings but showed slightly higher noise metrics than the Polynomial approach. Furthermore, correlation analysis indicated a moderate negative correlation (-0.60) between residual noise and pH, suggesting that process conditions significantly influence baseline stability. These findings strongly support the recommendation to abandon the current vendor settings and adopt the adaptive Polynomial baseline algorithm to achieve the necessary noise floor and signal integrity for downstream quantitative analysis.

#Conclusion 

The analysis conclusively demonstrates that the raw HPLC data requires rigorous baseline correction to remove non-physical negative artifacts and normalize volume axes. The existing vendor algorithm (BASEM) fails to meet the stringent criteria of a <0.5 mAU noise floor and zero negative values, rendering it unsuitable for high-precision quantitative analysis. The adaptive Polynomial baseline algorithm, utilizing dynamic windowing based on empirical peak widths, emerged as the superior method, significantly reducing residual noise and ensuring signal integrity. The observed correlation between noise and pH highlights the importance of considering process conditions in baseline modeling. It is recommended to implement the adaptive Polynomial algorithm as the standard correction method for this dataset to ensure the reliability of downstream biopharmaceutical quality assessments.
