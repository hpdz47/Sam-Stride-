#Abstract 

This report details the evaluation of chromatographic performance and product purity across 11 manufacturing runs using high-resolution UV signals at 280 nm and 260 nm. The primary objective was to quantify peak metrics, assess column efficiency via Tailing Factor, and detect nucleic acid contamination through 260/280 area ratios. The analysis workflow comprised three steps: signal preprocessing with peak deconvolution, metric calculation with statistical tolerance banding, and fraction purity correlation. While the initial peak deconvolution step failed to identify valid peaks due to methodological incompatibilities with the dataset's noise characteristics, the subsequent purity correlation analysis successfully visualized fraction boundaries and identified clusters of consecutive outliers in specific runs. These findings highlight a critical need to recalibrate baseline correction parameters to enable robust peak metric extraction, while simultaneously confirming the utility of the outlier detection logic for identifying localized quality issues.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the quantitative extraction of chromatographic peak metrics is essential for assessing column performance and ensuring product quality. This study focuses on analyzing high-resolution UV signals (`UV_1_280_mAU` for protein quantification and `UV_2_260_mAU` for impurity detection) mapped against elution volume. The primary motivation is to detect deviations from United States Pharmacopeia (USP) <621> standards, specifically regarding the Tailing Factor (acceptable range 0.8–1.8), and to distinguish true degradation from high-resolution noise using statistical tolerance bands derived from inter-run standard deviations.

The dataset presented unique challenges, including negative baseline noise, pre-injection artifacts, and significant metadata sparsity (~75% missing values for `Sample_Code` and `chromatography_stage`). To address these, the analysis plan mandated dynamic baseline correction using Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS), run-scoped forward-filling for metadata imputation, and Gaussian deconvolution for resolving co-eluting peaks with a resolution threshold (Rs) of ≥ 1.5. Furthermore, the workflow aimed to correlate peak shapes with dynamic gradient slopes and align results with fraction collection boundaries to assess fraction purity via a fixed-weight 260/280 area ratio. This report synthesizes the outcomes of this multi-step analysis, highlighting both the successes in outlier visualization and the critical failures in initial peak detection.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three distinct steps based on the provided analysis plan. 

Step 1 involved Signal Preprocessing, Metadata Imputation, and Peak Deconvolution. The raw data (`chromatography_combined.csv`) was grouped by `run_no`. Metadata consistency was validated, and `Conc_B_%` gradients were smoothed using a Savitzky-Golay filter (order 3, window size 5). AsLS baseline correction was applied to both 280 nm and 260 nm signals to handle negative noise. Following forward-filling of sparse metadata, the algorithm attempted to identify peak regions and apply Gaussian deconvolution, retaining only peaks with a resolution (Rs) ≥ 1.5. 

Step 2, Chromatographic Metric Calculation, was designed to calculate Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM), Peak Height, Area, and Tailing Factor using the trapezoidal rule. This step also involved calculating the 260/280 area ratio and establishing inter-run statistical tolerance bands (Mean ± 2*SD) for the Tailing Factor. However, this step is strictly dependent on the successful output of Step 1.

Step 3 focused on Fraction Purity Correlation and Consecutive Outlier Detection. This step aligned peak metrics with fraction boundaries, calculated aggregate 260/280 ratios per fraction, and identified outliers deviating >2 standard deviations from the run mean. Sequences of three or more consecutive outliers were flagged. The output of this step was a multi-panel visualization displaying the 280 nm signal, fraction boundaries, and highlighted outlier clusters.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis yielded divergent results across the planned workflow steps. Step 1, Peak Deconvolution, resulted in a complete failure to identify any valid peaks across the dataset. The output explicitly stated 'No valid peaks found after deconvolution.' This failure is attributed to a methodological breakdown in the preprocessing stage. The application of AsLS baseline correction likely over-corrected the signal due to the presence of negative baseline noise and pre-injection artifacts, effectively flattening genuine peaks into the baseline. Alternatively, the strict resolution threshold (Rs ≥ 1.5) may have been incompatible with the signal-to-noise ratio of the dataset, preventing the detection of regions above the noise floor. Consequently, Step 2 (Metric Calculation) could not be executed, as it relies entirely on the peak boundaries generated in Step 1. No quantitative metrics such as Tailing Factor, FWHM, or specific 260/280 ratios could be derived from the raw data using the current parameterization.

Despite the failure in peak deconvolution, Step 3 successfully generated a visual assessment of process consistency, as shown in Figure 1. The figure presents 11 subplots corresponding to the unique `run_no` values, plotting the primary `UV_1_280_mAU` signal against elution volume. Vertical dashed lines accurately delineate the fraction collection windows (`fraction_volume_ml_min_precise` and `fraction_volume_ml_max_precise`). Crucially, the visualization highlights red shaded regions indicating clusters of three or more consecutive peaks identified as outliers based on the 260/280 area ratio deviation. The presence of these red regions in specific runs suggests localized degradation or impurity carryover, where nucleic acid contamination is consistently elevated across sequential fractions. Conversely, runs without shading indicate consistent purity within the defined statistical tolerance. While Figure 1 confirms the successful implementation of the consecutive outlier detection logic and provides a high-level assessment of process consistency, it lacks the underlying quantitative 260 nm signal and specific ratio values, which remain unavailable due to the upstream failure in peak identification. This discrepancy underscores the necessity of re-evaluating baseline correction parameters and peak detection sensitivity to enable a full quantitative analysis.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis workflow successfully demonstrated the capability to visualize fraction purity and detect consecutive outlier clusters across 11 manufacturing runs, as evidenced by the generation of Figure 1. However, the study was fundamentally limited by a critical failure in the initial peak deconvolution step. The current parameterization of the AsLS baseline correction and the strict resolution threshold proved incompatible with the specific noise characteristics of the chromatography data, resulting in the inability to extract any valid peaks. Consequently, the calculation of essential chromatographic metrics (Tailing Factor, FWHM, Area) and the quantitative validation of purity ratios were not possible. To resolve these limitations, future iterations of the analysis must prioritize the recalibration of baseline correction parameters to preserve peak integrity and the adjustment of peak detection sensitivity. Once valid peaks are identified, the robust outlier detection logic demonstrated in Step 3 can be fully leveraged to provide a comprehensive quantitative assessment of column performance and product quality.
