#Abstract 

This report evaluates the signal integrity and process segmentation of 11 high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) runs to determine their viability for downstream modeling. The analysis focused on reconstructing missing chromatography stage metadata, segmenting Cleaning-in-Place (CIP) cycles, and calculating Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) and retention volume variability. While visual inspection of sensor streams confirmed high signal quality and successful algorithmic reconstruction of process boundaries (Load, Wash, Elute) and CIP events, the quantitative analysis failed to detect valid product peaks. Consequently, critical metrics such as SNR and Relative Standard Deviation (RSD) of retention volumes could not be calculated, resulting in a total of zero runs meeting the criteria for USP compliance assessment. This discrepancy suggests a misalignment between the reconstructed stage boundaries and the actual peak detection logic, necessitating a review of the peak height thresholds and stage definition parameters.

#Introduction 

The rigorous assessment of continuous HPLC sensor streams is essential for validating data quality prior to downstream modeling. The primary objective of this analysis was to verify the structural integrity of high-frequency time-series data indexed by volume, specifically focusing on UV absorbance (280 nm and 260 nm), conductivity, buffer concentration, and pH. A significant challenge in this dataset was the 75.7% missingness in the `chromatography_stage` metadata, which required the inference of stage boundaries (Load, Wash, Elute) from continuous conductivity and gradient profiles. Furthermore, the dataset contained mixed process and cleaning data, necessitating the segmentation of CIP cycles based on extreme pH thresholds. The analysis aimed to quantify signal viability by calculating SNR, assessing peak purity via UV ratios, and evaluating run-to-run retention volume variability against United States Pharmacopeia (USP) standards (RSD < 2.0%). This report details the methodology employed to reconstruct process stages and discusses the unexpected failure in peak detection despite robust raw signal quality.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three primary steps applied to a combined dataset of 11 chromatography runs. First, signal pre-processing involved applying a rolling mean smoothing filter (window size 5-10) to conductivity (`Cond_mS_cm`) and Buffer B concentration (`Conc_B_%`) to reduce noise. Chromatography stages were reconstructed by detecting inflection points in these smoothed signals using a second-derivative zero-crossing method. Simultaneously, CIP cycles were segmented by identifying regions where pH exceeded 10 or dropped below 2. Second, peak purity and gradient correlation were assessed by filtering for valid UV signals and calculating the UV260/UV280 ratio, alongside cross-correlation analysis between buffer concentration and UV280 absorbance. Third, retention volume variability was evaluated by identifying the primary product peak within the reconstructed 'Elute' stage. A fallback rule was applied: if no peak was detected in the 'Elute' stage or if the peak height was less than 10% of the maximum run signal, the run was flagged as 'No Peak Detected' and excluded from RSD calculations. SNR was calculated by comparing the standard deviation of the baseline (pre-run, volume < 0) against the peak standard deviation.

#Results and Discussion 

The visual analysis presented in Figure 1 confirms the high fidelity of the raw sensor data and the effectiveness of the stage reconstruction algorithm. The top subplot displays distinct, high-amplitude UV280 peaks with clear baseline separation from the pre-run phase, indicating a high signal-to-noise ratio. The middle subplot demonstrates that the second-derivative zero-crossing method successfully delineated chromatography stage boundaries (Load, Wash, Elute) by aligning inflection points in the smoothed conductivity and gradient profiles. The bottom subplot effectively segregates CIP cycles, highlighting regions where pH deviated significantly from the stable process range. Despite these visual successes, the quantitative results derived from the markdown analysis reports indicate a critical failure in the downstream peak detection logic. The summary statistics report 'Total Runs Analyzed: 0', with all 11 runs flagged as 'No Peak Detected'. Consequently, the SNR columns for both UV280 and UV260 are populated with 'NaN', and the calculated RSD for retention volume is 0.00. This suggests that while the algorithm successfully identified the 'Elute' stage boundaries, the specific peak detection criteria—likely the requirement for a peak height exceeding 10% of the maximum signal or a strict alignment with the reconstructed 'Elute' window—were not met. The presence of valid, continuous UV signals with 0% missingness contradicts the 'No Peak Detected' status, pointing to a potential misalignment between the reconstructed stage metadata and the actual peak apex coordinates, or an overly stringent threshold that excluded valid peaks. Therefore, while the data is robust for visual inspection and stage reconstruction, it currently fails the automated quality control checks required for USP compliance validation.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the data analysis successfully validated the structural integrity of the HPLC sensor streams and demonstrated the efficacy of the algorithmic approach for reconstructing missing chromatography stage metadata and segmenting CIP cycles. Visual evidence confirms that the dataset contains distinct, high-quality protein elution peaks. However, the quantitative assessment of signal viability failed due to a complete inability to detect valid peaks within the reconstructed 'Elute' stages, resulting in zero runs passing the USP compliance check. This outcome highlights a discrepancy between the visual quality of the data and the automated peak detection logic, likely stemming from misaligned stage boundaries or excessive stringency in peak height thresholds. Future work must focus on refining the peak detection parameters and verifying the alignment between reconstructed stage boundaries and actual peak apexes to enable accurate SNR and retention variability calculations.
