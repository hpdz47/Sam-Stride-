#Abstract 

This report evaluates the performance and product quality of 11 chromatography runs through the quantitative analysis of UV signal metrics. The primary objectives were to assess column efficiency via Tailing Factor calculations against USP <621> standards and to detect nucleic acid contamination using 260/280 area ratios. The analysis employed Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction, Gaussian deconvolution for co-eluting peaks, and statistical tolerance banding to distinguish systematic failures from noise. Results indicate a critical methodological inconsistency where specific runs (notably Runs 1 and 4) failed to generate valid peak metrics, likely due to baseline correction errors misidentifying noise as peaks. Conversely, valid runs demonstrated acceptable peak symmetry but revealed localized purity failures in specific fraction boundaries, characterized by clusters of consecutive outliers in the 260/280 ratio. These findings highlight the necessity of robust preprocessing validation before quality assessment and identify specific fractionation zones requiring further investigation into gradient slopes and column performance.

#Introduction 

Chromatographic separation is a critical step in biopharmaceutical manufacturing, where column performance and product purity are paramount. The quality of the eluted protein is typically assessed using metrics such as the Tailing Factor, which indicates peak symmetry and column health, and the 260/280 absorbance ratio, which serves as a proxy for nucleic acid contamination. Regulatory standards, such as USP <621>, define acceptable ranges for these metrics (e.g., Tailing Factor between 0.8 and 1.8). However, high-resolution data often contains baseline noise and co-eluting peaks that complicate automated analysis.

This study aims to automate the extraction of these critical metrics from high-frequency UV signals across 11 manufacturing runs. The workflow addresses common data quality issues, including negative baseline noise and sparse metadata, through dynamic baseline correction and run-scoped forward-filling. By applying Gaussian deconvolution to resolve co-eluting peaks and establishing statistical tolerance bands based on inter-run variability, this analysis seeks to provide a robust assessment of column performance and fraction purity. The ultimate goal is to identify systematic deviations that may indicate column degradation, gradient inconsistencies, or fractionation errors, thereby ensuring the integrity of the final product.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three primary steps designed to preprocess signals, calculate metrics, and correlate purity with fraction boundaries.

First, signal preprocessing involved loading the combined chromatography data and sorting by run number and elution volume. Negative UV signals were clipped to zero, and Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction was applied to remove baseline drift and noise. Metadata fields, specifically `Sample_Code` and `chromatography_stage`, which were approximately 75% missing, were imputed using a forward-filling logic that strictly reset at the start of each `run_no` to prevent data leakage between runs. Peak detection was performed using first-derivative zero-crossing with a 5% maximum threshold. For co-eluting regions where peak distance was less than 1.5 times the Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM), Gaussian deconvolution was applied to resolve peaks with a resolution (Rs) of at least 1.5.

Second, chromatographic metrics were calculated for the detected peaks. This included FWHM, peak height, area integration at 280nm and 260nm, and the Tailing Factor. The 260/280 area ratio was computed via trapezoidal integration within peak boundaries. To establish quality control limits, a statistical tolerance band was derived by calculating the standard deviation of the mean Tailing Factor across all runs (Mean ± 2*SD). Peaks were flagged as outliers if their Tailing Factor fell outside the USP <621> range (0.8–1.8) and exceeded the calculated tolerance band.

Third, fraction purity was assessed by aligning peak metrics with fraction collection boundaries. The aggregate 260/280 ratio was calculated for each fraction. Outliers were identified as ratios deviating more than two standard deviations from the run mean. A specific algorithm detected clusters of three or more consecutive peaks flagged as outliers, which were then visualized to highlight potential systematic impurity carryover or fractionation errors. The dynamic gradient slope at the peak apex was also derived using a Savitzky-Golay filter (order 3) to contextualize peak shape relative to the mobile phase composition.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the 11 chromatography runs revealed significant disparities in data quality and product purity. A critical finding from the raw detection phase was a systematic failure in metric calculation for specific runs, most notably Runs 1 and 4. In these runs, thousands of peaks were detected with a Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM) and Tailing Factor of 0.0000. This anomaly suggests that the AsLS baseline correction or thresholding logic failed for these datasets, causing the algorithm to misidentify baseline noise as distinct peaks. Consequently, the data for these runs is currently invalid for assessing column performance or product quality and requires re-processing. In contrast, Runs 2, 16, 17, 18, 19, 20, 21, 30, and 32 exhibited valid metrics, with FWHM values ranging from approximately 4.6 to 220 and Tailing Factors generally within or near the USP <621> acceptable range (0.8–1.8).

Regarding product purity, the visualization in Figure 1 provides a comprehensive overview of the 260/280 area ratio deviations across the valid runs. The figure displays the primary protein signal (UV_1_280_mAU) against elution volume, with vertical dashed lines marking fraction collection boundaries. Red shaded regions highlight clusters of three or more consecutive peaks where the 260/280 ratio exceeded two standard deviations from the run mean, indicating significant nucleic acid contamination. The spatial distribution of these outliers is non-random; they are predominantly localized to specific elution volumes, often coinciding with the trailing edges of major protein peaks or specific fraction boundaries. This pattern suggests potential co-elution of impurities or errors in the fractionation logic where impurities are being collected alongside the target protein.

The consistency of the baseline across the valid runs confirms the effectiveness of the AsLS correction for the majority of the dataset. However, the presence of red shaded regions in specific runs indicates that while most batches maintain acceptable purity profiles, certain batches or columns exhibit systematic impurity carryover. The inability of the current visualization to explicitly show the 260nm signal trace or the magnitude of the ratio deviation limits the immediate diagnosis of the specific impurity source, but the clustering of outliers strongly points to issues with gradient slope optimization or column performance in those specific regions. Further investigation into the dynamic gradient slope at the apex of these outlier peaks is recommended to determine if the separation conditions were insufficient to resolve the nucleic acid contaminants.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis successfully identified both methodological inconsistencies and specific product quality issues within the chromatography dataset. The primary conclusion is that the current preprocessing pipeline is robust for the majority of runs but fails catastrophically for specific datasets (Runs 1 and 4), necessitating a review of the baseline correction parameters and noise thresholding logic. For the valid runs, the analysis confirms that while column performance generally adheres to USP <621> standards, there are distinct, localized purity failures characterized by clusters of high 260/280 ratios. These failures are consistently associated with the trailing edges of protein peaks, suggesting that the current fractionation boundaries may be capturing co-eluting nucleic acid impurities. Future work should focus on re-processing the failed runs to recover valid metrics and optimizing the gradient slope or fraction collection windows in the identified outlier regions to improve product purity.
