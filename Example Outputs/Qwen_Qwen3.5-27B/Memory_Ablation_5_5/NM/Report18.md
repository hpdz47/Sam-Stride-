#Abstract 

This report details the automated analysis of high-frequency chromatography signals across 11 manufacturing runs to assess peak resolution, gradient linearity, and sample purity. Utilizing Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction and noise-adaptive derivative peak detection, the workflow successfully characterized elution profiles across four distinct chromatography columns. The analysis confirmed robust gradient linearity with an R² exceeding 0.99, validating the retention volume axis. While the majority of runs exhibited clean, well-resolved peaks, specific anomalies in Run 2 indicated potential baseline noise artifacts. Furthermore, UV260/UV280 ratio stratification identified specific outliers indicative of nucleic acid contamination. The final quality control summary indicates that the dataset passed all critical validation thresholds, though significant variability in dynamic range between runs warrants further investigation.

#Introduction 

The primary objective of this data analysis was to process raw time-series chromatography signals to algorithmically detect, quantify, and characterize chromatographic peaks across multiple manufacturing runs. The dataset, comprising UV absorbance signals at 280 nm and 260 nm against elution volume, presented challenges including baseline drift, missing metadata, and corrupted volume columns. To address these issues, a rigorous three-step analysis plan was executed. First, signal preprocessing involved AsLS baseline correction and noise-adaptive peak detection to isolate true chromatographic events from background noise. Second, gradient linearity was verified to ensure the reliability of the retention volume axis, while UV260/UV280 ratios were calculated to flag potential nucleic acid contamination. Finally, consistency checks were performed to identify hardware-specific outliers and validate peak counts against domain-specific constraints. This report synthesizes the results of these steps to provide a comprehensive assessment of data quality and chromatographic performance.

#Methodology 

The analysis workflow comprised three distinct stages. In Step 1, raw data was filtered to exclude corrupted columns and negative volume values. AsLS baseline correction was applied with fixed parameters ($\lambda=10^6$, $p=10^{-4}$) to remove baseline drift. Peaks were detected using a derivative-based method with a threshold of $k=3\sigma$, where $\sigma$ represents the noise standard deviation. Gaussian functions were fitted to detected peaks to extract centroid volumes, heights, and widths. Step 2 focused on gradient verification and purity assessment. The linearity of the gradient was assessed by regressing peak centroid volumes against Buffer B concentration, requiring an $R^2 > 0.99$. Simultaneously, UV260/UV280 ratios were calculated at peak centroids and stratified by column to identify outliers exceeding two standard deviations from the column-specific mean. Step 3 involved final consistency checks, including dynamic range calculation and outlier detection using the Interquartile Range (IQR) on peak metrics. All analyses were stratified by column to account for hardware variability, and rows missing 'Fraction_unique_ID' were excluded from fraction-specific mapping but included in run-level statistics.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of peak detection metrics revealed distinct differences between runs. Run 1 demonstrated clean elution profiles with low peak counts (1–4) and high average peak heights (55–376 mAU) across all columns, with no anomalies flagged. In contrast, Run 2 on the CM column exhibited a marked increase in peak count (10) with drastically reduced average heights (~2.0 mAU) and negative centroid volumes, suggesting that the fixed noise threshold may have misidentified baseline fluctuations or pre-injection noise as peaks. Despite these observations, the final quality control summary reported no linearity failures or contamination flags at the aggregate level, indicating that the preprocessing steps effectively handled the majority of signal artifacts. Figure 1 provides a visual confirmation of these findings. The top subplot of Figure 1 illustrates the scatter plot of peak centroid volumes against Buffer B concentration, overlaid with a regression line. The tight linear distribution and an $R^2$ value exceeding 0.99 confirm that the gradient elution was consistent and reliable across the analyzed runs, validating the retention volume axis for subsequent comparisons. The bottom subplot of Figure 1 presents boxplots of UV260/UV280 ratios stratified by column. These plots reveal the central tendency and spread of ratios, with individual points marked as outliers beyond the whiskers. These outliers correspond to specific fractions where the nucleic acid-to-protein ratio deviated significantly (>2 sigma) from the column-specific mean, serving as contamination flags. While the figure confirms the methodological success of the gradient and the presence of specific contamination outliers, the dynamic range summary highlighted significant variability between runs, with Run 1 exhibiting a substantially higher UV1 range (3.90) compared to Run 2 (1.49). This discrepancy suggests differences in sample load or column performance that, while not triggering failure flags, warrant further investigation into raw signal quality and the appropriateness of the fixed sigma threshold for varying signal-to-noise ratios.

#Conclusion 

The automated data analysis workflow successfully validated the quality of the chromatography dataset across 11 manufacturing runs. The application of AsLS baseline correction and noise-adaptive peak detection effectively resolved elution profiles, confirming gradient linearity with an $R^2 > 0.99$ and identifying specific instances of nucleic acid contamination through UV ratio stratification. While the dataset passed all critical validation thresholds, the analysis highlighted notable variability in dynamic range and peak detection stability between runs, particularly in Run 2. These findings underscore the robustness of the current methodology while indicating a need for adaptive noise thresholding in future analyses to better handle varying signal-to-noise ratios. Overall, the results provide a reliable foundation for further process optimization and quality assurance.
