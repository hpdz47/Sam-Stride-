#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative analysis of High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) data to assess system stability, gradient alignment, and product purity. The workflow involved preprocessing raw UV signals to correct for baseline drift, integrating peak areas to calculate abundance and purity ratios (260/280 nm), and monitoring system pressure. While the analysis successfully identified significant data quality issues, including 63.7% of fractions flagged for high-purity risk and 1253 instances of gradient misalignment, a critical methodological deviation was observed. The planned Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction was substituted with simple linear interpolation, potentially compromising the accuracy of integrated area calculations. Visualizations confirm effective baseline correction in the summary plots, yet the reliance on temporary preprocessing methods suggests that current quantitative estimates should be treated as preliminary pending full implementation of the robust AsLS algorithm.

#Introduction 

The primary objective of this data analysis was to process high-frequency HPLC signals to generate robust estimates of product abundance and purity. The workflow focused on the quantitative integration of UV signals at 280 nm (abundance) and 260 nm (purity) against elution volume. A critical challenge in this dataset was the presence of physically impossible negative volume values representing pre-run intervals and a documented negative baseline drift of approximately -7.95 mAU. The analysis plan mandated the exclusion of pre-run data and the application of Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction to ensure accurate peak integration. Furthermore, the dataset exhibited significant metadata gaps, with 75.7% of rows lacking `Sample_Code`, necessitating an aggregation strategy based on `run_no` and `column`. The ultimate goal was to flag fractions with purity ratios exceeding 0.6, assess gradient elution alignment, and monitor system stability via pressure differentials during both collection and waste phases.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a three-step protocol. First, signal preprocessing involved loading the combined chromatography dataset and filtering out all rows with negative `volume_ml` to exclude pre-run intervals. The plan specified the application of AsLS baseline correction with parameters $\	ext{lambda} = 10^6$ and $p = 10^{-4}$ to correct the negative drift. However, the executed analysis utilized 'Simple Linear Interpolation' as a temporary measure, a deviation that may impact the precision of subsequent integration. Rows with missing `Fraction_unique_ID` were segregated for system stability monitoring while being excluded from abundance calculations. Second, peak integration was performed using the trapezoidal method on the corrected signals. Purity ratios were calculated as the ratio of integrated areas at 260 nm to 280 nm, with fractions exceeding a ratio of 0.6 flagged as high-risk. Abundance was normalized by total run area and fraction volume. Gradient alignment was verified by checking if peak apexes occurred within the 10-90% buffer concentration range. Third, system stability and gradient alignment were visualized. A multi-panel plot was generated for a subset of runs, overlaying corrected UV signals with the gradient profile and plotting pressure differentials. Retention time outliers were identified using the Interquartile Range (IQR) method.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis revealed substantial data quality concerns despite the successful generation of visual summaries. As illustrated in Figure 1, the multi-panel visualization confirms the application of baseline correction and the identification of retention time outliers via red 'x' markers. Panel A of Figure 1 demonstrates that the baseline correction effectively returned signals to near-zero levels in non-elution regions, and distinct peaks align with the gradient ramp. Panel B indicates relatively stable system pressure with minor fluctuations, validating system performance during collection and waste phases. However, the quantitative results highlight critical issues. The substitution of the planned AsLS correction with linear interpolation is a significant limitation, as it may fail to adequately address the documented negative drift, potentially skewing the integrated areas and purity ratios. The integration results indicate that 1797 fractions (63.7% of analyzed fractions) are flagged as high-purity risk with a 260/280 ratio greater than 0.6. Furthermore, 1253 fractions exhibit severe gradient misalignment, with peak apexes occurring at near-zero buffer concentration (Mean Apex_Conc_B: 0.90%), suggesting a discrepancy between the expected gradient profile and actual elution behavior. Additionally, 75.7% of the data lacks `Sample_Code`, restricting sample-level attribution. While the visualization in Figure 1 supports the validity of the preprocessing steps applied, the high volume of misaligned gradients and the methodological deviation in baseline correction suggest that the current quantitative purity and abundance estimates are preliminary and require re-evaluation with the full AsLS implementation.

#Conclusion 

This analysis successfully processed HPLC data to identify system stability and gradient alignment characteristics, as visualized in Figure 1. The workflow confirmed that the system operated with stable pressure and that baseline correction was visually effective. However, the quantitative assessment of purity and abundance is currently compromised by two main factors: the use of simple linear interpolation instead of the planned AsLS baseline correction, and a high prevalence of gradient misalignment and purity risks. The finding that 63.7% of fractions exceed the purity threshold and 1253 fractions show gradient misalignment indicates significant process variability or data acquisition errors. Consequently, the current quantitative metrics should be treated as preliminary. Future work must prioritize the implementation of the robust AsLS algorithm to ensure accurate integration and a detailed investigation into the root causes of the observed gradient misalignments to validate the product quality assessments.
