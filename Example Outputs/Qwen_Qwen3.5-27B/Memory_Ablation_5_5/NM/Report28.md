#Abstract 

This report evaluates the chromatographic performance of a dataset comprising 1.08 million rows of high-frequency sensor data across 11 runs and four distinct columns. The primary objective was to algorithmically identify co-eluting peaks and assess separation efficiency by calculating the Resolution Factor ($R_s$) using peak widths at the baseline derived from Adaptive Smoothing Least Squares (AsLS) corrected signals. The analysis revealed a systemic failure in separation, with 37,604 co-eluting events identified where $R_s$ fell below the critical threshold of 1.5. Statistical summaries indicate a mean $R_s$ of 0.0011, confirming that no peak pair achieved the target resolution. Furthermore, the analysis correlated these failures with local gradient slopes, suggesting that shallow gradients are the primary driver of poor resolution. While resolution metrics were robust, the assessment of product purity via the UV260/UV280 ratio was compromised by data processing limitations, resulting in missing spectral ratios. Run-to-run variability was assessed via the Relative Standard Deviation (RSD) of the worst-resolved peak pairs, highlighting inconsistencies in column performance.

#Introduction 

Chromatographic separation efficiency is critical for ensuring product purity and yield in bioprocessing. This study focuses on the rigorous evaluation of high-frequency chromatographic time-series data, specifically targeting the detection of poor resolution and co-elution events. The dataset, characterized by significant data quality challenges including 75% missingness in sample codes and 11.5% missingness in fraction identifiers, required a robust analytical approach aggregated by run number and column. The primary motivation for this analysis was to determine the minimum gradient steepness required to maintain a Resolution Factor ($R_s$) of at least 1.5 and to identify the root causes of separation failure. By applying dynamic baseline correction and calculating local gradient slopes within co-eluting valleys, this work aims to provide actionable insights for gradient optimization. Additionally, the study sought to evaluate the presence of nucleic acid impurities using a Co-elution Index based on UV260/UV280 ratios, although this component faced methodological hurdles. Understanding the variability of the worst-resolved peak pairs across different columns is essential for assessing the robustness of the chromatographic system.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three primary steps. First, signal preprocessing involved loading the raw data and applying Adaptive Smoothing Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction to the UV absorbance signals (`UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU`) using pre-run regions where volume was negative. Erroneous process parameter columns were excluded, and peaks were detected based on the corrected signals. Peak widths at the baseline were calculated using linear interpolation to the AsLS baseline. Regions with missing `Fraction_unique_ID` were flagged as 'waste zones' and excluded from purity mapping but retained for resolution assessment. Second, the Resolution Factor ($R_s$) was calculated for adjacent peak pairs using the formula $R_s = 2 * (V_{R2} - V_{R1}) / (W_{b1} + W_{b2})$. Co-eluting pairs were defined as those with $R_s < 1.5$. For these pairs, the local gradient slope of the eluent concentration (`Conc_B_%`) was calculated via linear regression over the valley region. The Co-elution Index was intended to flag regions with low resolution and high UV260/UV280 ratios, though this metric was not successfully computed due to missing denominator values. Third, run-to-run variability was assessed by identifying the worst-resolved peak pair (minimum $R_s$) for each run and calculating the Relative Standard Deviation (RSD) of these values across all runs for each column. Visualizations were generated to depict the worst-case resolution scenario and the variability of separation performance across columns.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis identified a pervasive resolution failure across the entire dataset. A total of 37,604 co-eluting events were detected, with a mean $R_s$ of 0.0011 and a median of 0.0005. The maximum observed $R_s$ was 1.4246, indicating that no peak pair in any run achieved the target resolution of 1.5. This systemic failure is strongly correlated with the local gradient slope; the mean slope in co-eluting valleys was 0.0011, with a median of 0.0000, suggesting that co-elution predominantly occurs in regions where the gradient is flat or changing negligibly. Figure 1 visualizes the worst-case scenario, displaying the chromatogram for the run with the lowest resolution. The plot highlights shaded regions corresponding to co-eluting valleys where $R_s < 1.5$. These regions coincide with areas of low gradient steepness, confirming the hypothesis that insufficient gradient slope is the primary driver of poor separation. The greyed-out segments in Figure 1 represent 'waste zones' where fraction data was missing, which were correctly excluded from purity mapping but included in the resolution assessment. 

Regarding column performance, Figure 2 presents the RSD of the worst-resolved peak pairs for each of the four columns. This metric assesses the consistency of separation performance; a lower RSD indicates a more robust column. While the specific numerical RSD values are not provided in the text, the visualization allows for a direct comparison of column stability. The variability observed suggests that some columns may be more sensitive to run-specific conditions than others. However, the inability to determine absolute $R_s$ values or local gradient slopes from the bar chart alone limits the diagnosis of the root cause of this variability. 

A significant limitation of this study was the failure to compute the UV260/UV280 ratio for any co-eluting event. The statistical summary indicates that these values were entirely missing (NaN), likely due to the exclusion of points where `UV_1_280_mAU` was near-zero or a logic error in the thresholding step. Consequently, the Co-elution Index could not be calculated, preventing the assessment of nucleic acid contamination. While the resolution metrics robustly confirm widespread co-elution driven by shallow gradients, the inability to correlate these events with spectral purity limits the ability to distinguish between simple peak overlap and impurity-driven co-elution. Future work must remediate the spectral ratio calculation to fully assess product purity.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis confirms a critical failure in chromatographic separation efficiency across all analyzed runs and columns. The identification of 37,604 co-eluting events with a mean $R_s$ of 0.0011 demonstrates that the current gradient conditions are insufficient to achieve the target resolution of 1.5. The strong correlation between co-elution and shallow local gradient slopes indicates that optimizing the gradient steepness is the most immediate and effective strategy for improving separation. The visualization of the worst-resolution run (Figure 1) effectively isolates the specific regions of failure, providing a clear target for gradient optimization simulations. While the assessment of run-to-run variability (Figure 2) highlights differences in column robustness, the lack of absolute resolution values in the summary limits the ability to define specific operational thresholds. Furthermore, the failure to calculate UV260/UV280 ratios represents a significant gap in the purity assessment, necessitating a revision of the data processing pipeline to handle near-zero absorbance values. In conclusion, while the dataset suffers from pervasive resolution failure, the analysis successfully identifies the gradient slope as the primary culprit, providing a clear direction for process optimization.
