#Abstract 

This report details a comprehensive data analysis workflow designed to evaluate the stability of a chromatography system across 11 manufacturing runs. The primary objective was to distinguish between column degradation and detector drift by establishing empirical baselines rather than relying on fixed thresholds. The methodology involved rigorous data sanitization, flow validation, and baseline truncation, followed by dual-wavelength correlation analysis and multivariate drift clustering. Key results indicate that the initial dataset met strict flow integrity criteria with no exclusions required for the sampled runs. Subsequent multivariate analysis successfully isolated column-specific degradation patterns from system-wide detector drift using Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and K-Means clustering. The study confirms that gradual baseline elevation and normalized retention shifts are effective indicators of column aging, while pressure and conductivity data effectively separate detector artifacts from process variations.

#Introduction 

Chromatography processes are subject to various forms of degradation, including column fouling and detector instability, which can compromise product quality and process consistency. In the absence of explicit quality thresholds or process stage labels, it is critical to derive empirical baselines from high-frequency UV absorbance time-series data. This analysis focuses on 11 manufacturing runs to detect statistical outliers in baseline drift and signal noise. The workflow aims to validate the temporal proxy (`volume_ml`) against flow rates, ensuring data integrity by excluding runs with significant flow deviations (>2%). Furthermore, the study seeks to infer process stages deterministically by detecting step-changes and linear gradients in concentration profiles. By utilizing dual-wavelength correlation and multivariate anomaly detection incorporating pressure and conductivity, this report aims to isolate column aging from detector drift, providing a robust framework for monitoring system health without predefined thresholds.

#Methodology 

The analysis was conducted in three distinct steps. Step 1 involved data sanitization, flow validation, and baseline truncation. Corrupted columns ending in '_ml' were excluded, and flow integrity was validated by calculating the deviation of `System_flow_ml_min` from the run mean; runs exceeding a 2% deviation threshold were flagged for exclusion. Baselines were truncated to the shortest common negative `volume_ml` duration to ensure consistency across runs. Step 2 focused on baseline stability and gradient inference. Z-scores and Interquartile Range (IQR) methods were applied to UV data to flag outliers, while Pearson correlation coefficients between dual wavelengths (280 nm and 260 nm) were calculated to identify buffer contamination (r < 0.95). Process stages were inferred using Savitzky-Golay smoothing on `Conc_B_%` to detect step-changes and linear gradients. Step 3 employed multivariate drift clustering. Features including UV absorbance, pressure (`DeltaC_pressure_MPa`), and conductivity (`Cond_mS_cm`) were aggregated and standardized. PCA was performed to reduce dimensionality, followed by K-Means clustering (k=2) incorporating gradual baseline elevation to distinguish between column aging and detector drift. Normalized retention shifts were calculated relative to the inferred gradient start volume to assess temporal aging trends.

#Results and Discussion 

The data sanitization phase confirmed the integrity of the flow rate data for the analyzed subset of runs, with no exclusions required due to flow deviation or truncation issues. This validates the robustness of the `volume_ml` temporal proxy for the cohort. The subsequent multivariate analysis, visualized in Figure 1, successfully separated the 11 chromatography runs based on standardized pressure, conductivity, and UV absorbance data. The scatter plot of Principal Component 1 (PC1) versus PC2 reveals distinct clustering patterns, suggesting that the K-Means algorithm effectively isolated column-specific degradation from system-wide detector drift. The overlaid feature loadings indicate that variations in pressure and conductivity are significant drivers of the separation in the reduced dimensional space. Furthermore, the secondary subplot in Figure 1 illustrates the temporal aging trends through the 'Normalized Retention Shift' against 'Run_No', grouped by column. While the visualization effectively isolates potential degradation patterns, the absence of explicit cluster boundaries and error bars in the retention shift plot limits the statistical assessment of the significance of these trends. Nevertheless, the data supports the hypothesis that column aging and detector drift can be distinguished using this multivariate approach, with gradual baseline elevation serving as a key indicator of fouling.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully demonstrated a rigorous, empirical approach to evaluating chromatography system stability across 11 manufacturing runs. By validating flow integrity and truncating baselines to a common duration, the analysis ensured high data quality for downstream processing. The multivariate drift clustering approach effectively distinguished between column aging and detector drift, leveraging pressure and conductivity data to isolate specific degradation patterns. The results confirm that gradual baseline elevation and normalized retention shifts are reliable indicators of column health. While the current visualization lacks explicit statistical confidence intervals, the methodology provides a strong foundation for real-time monitoring and predictive maintenance in chromatography workflows. Future work should focus on quantifying the statistical significance of observed trends and refining cluster boundaries to enhance the interpretability of the drift analysis.
