#Abstract 

This report details the algorithmic analysis of high-frequency chromatographic data from 11 biopharmaceutical manufacturing runs. The primary objective was to quantify elution events, validate process consistency across runs, and detect critical deviations indicative of nucleic acid contamination. Through a three-step workflow involving signal preprocessing, non-linear alignment, and anomaly detection, the analysis successfully identified a systematic process failure in Run 16.0. The results highlight pervasive 'High Nucleic Acid' contamination, evidenced by 260/280 absorbance ratios significantly exceeding the critical threshold of 0.8. These findings suggest a specific failure in nuclease digestion or column performance during this run, necessitating immediate investigation to ensure product purity and process robustness.

#Introduction 

In biopharmaceutical manufacturing, maintaining the purity of protein products is paramount. Chromatographic separation is a critical unit operation where the co-elution of impurities, particularly nucleic acids, can compromise product safety and efficacy. The dataset under analysis consists of time-series UV absorbance signals (`UV_1_280_mAU` for protein and `UV_2_260_mAU` for nucleic acids) recorded across 11 distinct runs. A significant challenge in this dataset is the absence of metadata for approximately 76% of process stages and missing fraction collection data for 11.5% of detected events. Furthermore, sensor drift and pre-run artifacts (negative volumes) complicate direct signal interpretation. To address these challenges, a rigorous data analysis workflow was designed to algorithmically infer process stages, quantify peaks, and identify deviations. The motivation for this analysis is to establish a signal-driven inventory of elution events, validate run-to-run consistency using non-linear alignment, and flag specific anomalies that indicate potential process failures, thereby ensuring the integrity of the manufacturing process.

#Methodology 

The data analysis followed a structured three-step plan. First, **Signal Preprocessing and Peak Detection** was performed on the raw `chromatography_combined.csv` file. Pre-run artifacts were removed by filtering rows where `volume_ml` was less than or equal to zero. Dynamic baseline correction was applied using the Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) method to mitigate sensor drift in both 280 nm and 260 nm channels. Peaks were detected using a derivative-based method with a Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) threshold greater than 3.0. For each detected peak, the retention volume (apex) and area were calculated. The 260/280 absorbance ratio was computed at the peak apex using linear interpolation of the nearest five data points to ensure robustness against noise. Process stages (Load, Wash, Elution) were inferred by calculating the slope of the buffer concentration (`Conc_B_%`) using a centered rolling window of 10 points. Peaks lacking a unique fraction ID were flagged as 'Uncollected'.

Second, **Cross-Run Alignment and Consistency Validation** was conducted to assess process reproducibility. Retention volumes from Step 1 were aligned across all 11 runs using Dynamic Time Warping (DTW), utilizing a weighted cost matrix (70% UV signal, 30% buffer concentration) to resolve ambiguities. The Relative Standard Deviation (RSD) of aligned retention volumes was calculated for corresponding peaks, with an RSD threshold of 2% used to identify outliers.

Third, **Anomaly Detection and Process Deviation Reporting** synthesized the findings. Peaks with a 260/280 ratio exceeding 0.8 were flagged as 'High Nucleic Acid' contamination. These deviations were cross-referenced with alignment outliers and 'Uncollected' status to generate a comprehensive deviation report.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the 11 manufacturing runs revealed a critical systematic deviation specifically within Run 16.0. The anomaly detection algorithm flagged multiple instances of 'High Nucleic Acid' contamination in this run. The evidence indicates that the 260/280 absorbance ratio, a key metric for purity, consistently exceeded the critical threshold of 0.8. Observed ratios ranged from 0.846 to a maximum of 2.180. In chromatographic analysis, a 260/280 ratio near 0.6 is characteristic of pure protein, whereas ratios approaching 1.8–2.0 are indicative of pure nucleic acids. The presence of ratios exceeding 2.0 strongly suggests significant co-elution of nucleic acids with the protein of interest. The pervasiveness of these high ratios across numerous peaks in Run 16.0, rather than as isolated incidents, implies a fundamental process failure specific to this run. Potential causes include incomplete nuclease digestion prior to chromatography or column fouling that altered selectivity. While the analysis successfully identified the presence and magnitude of this contamination, the truncated nature of the output report limits the ability to pinpoint the exact retention volumes or peak areas of the compromised fractions without referencing the full data export. Additionally, the current output focuses primarily on the purity metric and does not explicitly cross-reference these deviations with 'Uncollected' status or gradient slope anomalies in the provided summary, suggesting a need for further granular investigation into the elution conditions of Run 16.0.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis workflow successfully processed complex, high-frequency chromatographic data to identify a significant quality deviation in Run 16.0. By employing dynamic baseline correction, SNR-based peak detection, and multi-wavelength ratioing, the study confirmed a systematic failure characterized by high nucleic acid contamination. The observed 260/280 ratios, reaching as high as 2.180, provide compelling evidence of compromised product purity. These findings underscore the utility of algorithmic anomaly detection in identifying process failures that may be obscured by missing metadata or sensor drift. Immediate investigation into the specific elution conditions, nuclease digestion efficiency, and column status for Run 16.0 is recommended to prevent recurrence and ensure the safety and efficacy of the biopharmaceutical product.
