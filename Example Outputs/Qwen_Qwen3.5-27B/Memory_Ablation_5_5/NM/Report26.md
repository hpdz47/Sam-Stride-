#Abstract 

This report evaluates the performance of a three-step data analysis workflow designed to assess chromatographic column performance and product purity across multiple manufacturing runs. The workflow aimed to extract peak metrics, apply statistical tolerance bands for Tailing Factor analysis, and detect consecutive purity outliers using 260/280 nm area ratios. While the visualization component successfully identified clusters of purity outliers aligned with fraction boundaries, the underlying quantitative analysis revealed critical methodological failures. Specifically, the calculation of dynamic gradient slopes, resolution metrics, and Tailing Factors yielded static or default values rather than calculated data. Consequently, while the visual output confirms the detection logic for outlier clustering, the quantitative assessment of column performance and specific impurity magnitudes remains inconclusive due to data processing anomalies in the initial steps.

#Introduction 

The primary objective of this analysis was to quantify chromatographic peak metrics from high-resolution UV signals (280 nm for protein, 260 nm for nucleic acid impurities) to assess column performance and fraction purity. The workflow was designed to adhere to USP <621> standards, specifically targeting a Tailing Factor range of 0.8–1.8, while employing statistical tolerance bands derived from inter-run standard deviations to distinguish true degradation from noise. Key methodological requirements included Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction, run-scoped forward-filling for metadata imputation, and Gaussian deconvolution for co-eluting peaks with a resolution threshold (Rs) of 1.5. Furthermore, the analysis sought to correlate peak shape with dynamic gradient slopes and align results with fraction collection boundaries to flag consecutive purity outliers. This report synthesizes the results of the signal preprocessing, metric calculation, and outlier detection steps to determine the validity of the generated insights.

#Methodology 

The data analysis followed a three-step plan. Step 1 involved signal preprocessing, including clipping negative UV signals, applying AsLS baseline correction, and forward-filling metadata (`Sample_Code`, `chromatography_stage`) within `run_no` groups. Peak detection was performed via first-derivative zero-crossing, followed by Gaussian deconvolution for co-eluting regions, retaining peaks with Rs ≥ 1.5. Step 2 focused on calculating chromatographic metrics (FWHM, Height, Area, Tailing Factor) and the 260/280 area ratio. Statistical tolerance bands for the Tailing Factor were established using the mean ± 2*SD of run means. Step 3 aligned peak metrics with fraction boundaries, calculated aggregate 260/280 ratios, and identified outliers deviating >2 SD from the run mean. Consecutive outlier clusters (≥3 peaks) were flagged and visualized. The workflow utilized `chromatography_combined.csv` as the primary data source, with outputs generated as markdown tables and a consolidated image file.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis yielded mixed results regarding the successful execution of the planned methodology. The visualization in Figure 1 successfully demonstrates the output of Step 3, presenting a grid of subplots for each manufacturing run. The figure effectively aligns the primary chromatographic signal (`UV_1_280_mAU`) with fraction boundaries and highlights clusters of consecutive purity outliers using red shaded regions. This confirms that the logic for detecting sequences of three or more peaks with deviant 260/280 ratios (>2 SD from the run mean) is functional and capable of spatially correlating signal anomalies with collection events. The presence of these red regions validates the detection of systematic purity failures rather than isolated noise.

However, a critical review of the markdown analysis reports reveals significant anomalies in the quantitative data generated during Steps 1 and 2. While the metadata imputation (forward-filling) appears valid, the core analytical metrics are non-informative. The `gradient_slope_at_apex` is consistently reported as 0.0000, contradicting the expectation of a dynamic gradient and suggesting a failure in the Savitzky-Golay derivative calculation. Similarly, the `min_resolution_rs` column is entirely empty, indicating that the Gaussian deconvolution step either failed to execute or its results were not populated, despite the plan requiring Rs ≥ 1.5 for peak retention.

Most critically, the Tailing Factor calculations in Step 2 show static values (e.g., 0.5000, 0.5409) and identical tolerance bands (0.6778, 1.2270) across all peaks, with every peak flagged as an 'Outlier'. This uniformity suggests that the dynamic calculation of tolerance bands based on inter-run standard deviation was not performed, and the Tailing Factor values are likely artifacts or default placeholders. Consequently, while Figure 1 visually identifies potential purity issues, the underlying quantitative justification for these outliers—specifically the magnitude of the 260/280 deviation and the column performance metrics—is unreliable. The visualization effectively supports the assessment of fraction quality in a qualitative sense, but the absolute magnitude of purity deviation and column performance cannot be verified due to the lack of valid calculated metrics in the source data.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the data analysis workflow successfully generated a visual representation of consecutive purity outliers across manufacturing runs, as evidenced by Figure 1. This visualization confirms the operational logic for detecting clusters of impurity spikes relative to fraction boundaries. However, the quantitative foundation of this analysis is compromised by critical failures in the calculation of dynamic gradient slopes, resolution metrics, and Tailing Factors. The presence of static values and empty resolution fields indicates that the core analytical steps for column performance assessment did not execute as intended. Therefore, while the report identifies regions of interest for further investigation, the specific quantitative metrics required to validate column performance against USP <621> standards or to quantify the exact nature of the impurities are currently unavailable. Future iterations must address the computational errors in the derivative and deconvolution steps to ensure the reliability of the statistical tolerance bands and outlier classifications.
