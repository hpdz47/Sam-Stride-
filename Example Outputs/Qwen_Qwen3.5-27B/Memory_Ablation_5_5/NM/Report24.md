#Abstract 

This report details a comprehensive quality control assessment of high-frequency HPLC time-series data aimed at distinguishing genuine chromatographic events from systematic artifacts. The analysis focused on three critical areas: baseline correction and volume offset calibration, temporal correlation of mechanical artifacts, and injection consistency. Results indicate that the negative volume offset is not a global constant but varies significantly by run, necessitating run-specific calibration. While the temporal correlation method successfully isolated 63 mechanical artifacts in a representative run, the quantitative assessment of peak symmetry was compromised by data integrity issues, resulting in missing peak width metrics. Despite these limitations, the study validates the efficacy of change-point detection for inferring missing process states and confirms that observed UV anomalies are predominantly mechanical in origin.

#Introduction 

High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) generates complex time-series data susceptible to systematic errors, including baseline drift, volume offsets, and mechanical surges. The primary objective of this analysis was to validate detector integrity and experimental consistency by rigorously cleaning and characterizing the dataset. A significant portion of the data (approximately 75%) lacked `chromatography_stage` metadata, while 11.5% of rows were missing `Fraction_unique_ID`, complicating the distinction between intentional wash steps and missing peaks. Furthermore, the dataset exhibited negative volume values (e.g., -33.8 mL) and negative UV readings, suggesting systematic acquisition errors. This study employed Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) for baseline correction, dynamic change-point detection for process state inference, and temporal correlation analysis to isolate mechanical artifacts from genuine chemical signals. The ultimate goal was to establish a robust data processing pipeline capable of handling sparse metadata and structural artifacts to ensure accurate downstream interpretation.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a three-step protocol. First, baseline correction was applied using AsLS with fixed parameters (lambda=1.0e+06, p=0.010) to address negative UV values. Concurrently, volume offset calibration was performed by calculating the minimum `volume_ml` for each of the 11 runs to determine if the offset was global or run-specific. Second, temporal correlation analysis was conducted to identify mechanical artifacts. This involved calculating the lagged correlation between `DeltaC_pressure_MPa` and `UV_1_280_mAU` within a +/- 50 row window. Rows exhibiting high correlation (>0.8) with pressure surges were flagged as mechanical artifacts. Additionally, change-point detection algorithms were applied to `Conc_B_%` profiles to infer missing `chromatography_stage` metadata. Third, injection consistency and peak symmetry were assessed. Rows with missing `fraction_volume` were excluded to prevent bias. Peak detection utilized a threshold of 3x the rolling median (window=50), followed by the calculation of Peak Width at Half-Height (W½) and asymmetry factors, converting volume to time using verified flow rates.

#Results and Discussion 

The volume offset analysis revealed a critical deviation from the initial hypothesis of a global constant. As shown in Figure 1, while most runs exhibited a consistent offset of approximately -5.01 mL, Runs 18 through 21 displayed significantly larger offsets ranging from -24 to -33.8 mL. This heterogeneity necessitates a run-specific calibration strategy rather than a single global correction to preserve temporal continuity. The temporal correlation analysis proved highly effective in artifact isolation. In the representative run (Run 2), 63 mechanical artifacts were detected, with zero genuine UV spikes identified, confirming that the pressure-UV lag window successfully filtered mechanical surges. Figure 2 further validates the change-point detection approach, showing that inferred chromatography stages align perfectly with the `Conc_B_%` gradient profile, successfully distinguishing wash/equilibration steps from active separation phases. However, the peak symmetry analysis encountered significant challenges. Although Figure 3 visualizes the distribution of peak widths and asymmetry factors, the quantitative metrics for `avg_W_half_height` were reported as 'nan' for all runs. This failure is attributed to the exclusion of rows with missing `fraction_volume` and potential issues in converting volume to time, preventing a definitive assessment of injection consistency. Consequently, while the structural inference and artifact filtering are robust, the quantitative validation of detector integrity via peak symmetry remains incomplete.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully established a framework for cleaning and characterizing complex HPLC datasets plagued by systematic artifacts and missing metadata. The analysis confirmed that volume offsets are run-specific, requiring individual calibration to avoid timing errors. The temporal correlation method effectively isolated mechanical artifacts, ensuring that subsequent analyses are not confounded by pressure surges. Furthermore, dynamic change-point detection proved reliable for inferring process states in the absence of metadata. However, the inability to calculate peak width metrics due to data integrity issues highlights a critical gap in the current dataset's completeness regarding `fraction_volume` and flow rate consistency. Future work must address these data gaps to enable a full quantitative assessment of peak symmetry and injection variance, which are essential for validating the chromatographic performance of the system.
