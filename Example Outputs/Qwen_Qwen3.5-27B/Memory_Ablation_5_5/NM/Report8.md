#Abstract 

This report details the algorithmic evaluation of chromatographic resolution across 11 runs and four distinct columns (CM, DEAE, Q, SP) using high-frequency time-series data. The primary objective was to identify co-eluting peaks and assess run-to-run variability to inform gradient optimization. Utilizing Adaptive Smoothing Least Squares (AsLS) for dynamic baseline correction, the analysis calculated Resolution Factors ($R_s$) based on peak widths at the baseline. The study identified 112 co-eluting events ($R_s < 1.5$) with a mean $R_s$ of 0.586, indicating widespread separation challenges. Furthermore, significant heterogeneity in performance stability was observed across columns, with Relative Standard Deviation (RSD) of the worst-resolved peak pairs varying substantially. These findings highlight specific regions where gradient slopes were insufficient to drive separation and identify columns requiring immediate investigation for process consistency.

#Introduction 

Chromatographic separation efficiency is critical for ensuring product purity and process robustness in biopharmaceutical manufacturing. This analysis focuses on the rigorous evaluation of UV absorbance signals ($UV_{280}$ and $UV_{260}$) against elution volume and gradient concentration to detect poor resolution. The dataset, comprising 1.08 million rows of high-frequency sensor data, presented significant challenges, including 75% missingness in sample codes and 11.5% missingness in fraction identifiers, necessitating aggregation by run and column. The primary motivation for this workflow was to algorithmically identify co-eluting peaks where the Resolution Factor ($R_s$) falls below 1.5, a threshold indicating incomplete baseline separation. Additionally, the study aimed to correlate these resolution failures with local gradient slopes and assess the consistency of column performance through run-to-run variability analysis. By distinguishing 'waste zones' (regions with missing fraction IDs) from active separation regions, the analysis sought to provide a robust assessment of column performance independent of fraction collection logic, ultimately guiding gradient optimization strategies.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three primary stages. First, signal preprocessing involved loading the raw chromatography data and applying Adaptive Smoothing Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction to the $UV_{280}$ and $UV_{260}$ signals. This correction utilized the pre-run region (volume < 0) to estimate and subtract baseline drift, ensuring accurate peak detection. Erroneous process parameter columns misaligned with the volume axis were excluded. Peak detection was performed on the corrected signals, identifying thousands of peaks per run, with peak widths calculated via linear interpolation to the baseline. Second, resolution metrics were computed by iterating through adjacent peak pairs to calculate the Resolution Factor ($R_s$) using the formula $R_s = 2 * (V_{R2} - V_{R1}) / (W_{b1} + W_{b2})$. Co-eluting events were flagged where $R_s < 1.5$. For these events, the local gradient slope of the elution buffer concentration ($Conc_B$) was derived, and a Co-elution Index was computed by comparing the $UV_{260}/UV_{280}$ ratio against a statistically derived purity threshold. Third, run-to-run variability was assessed by identifying the worst-resolved peak pair (minimum $R_s$) for each run and calculating the Relative Standard Deviation (RSD) of these values across all runs for each column. Visualizations were generated to highlight the worst-case resolution scenarios and column performance heterogeneity.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis revealed significant challenges in peak resolution across the dataset. A total of 112 co-eluting events were identified, with a mean Resolution Factor ($R_s$) of 0.586 and a median of 0.485, confirming that the majority of adjacent peaks were not baseline resolved. As illustrated in Figure 1, the chromatogram representing the worst-case scenario exhibits multiple distinct peaks where specific regions between adjacent peaks fail to return to the baseline. These co-eluting valleys, highlighted in the visualization, coincide with regions where the elution gradient slope is relatively shallow, suggesting that the current gradient profile lacks the necessary driving force to separate these components effectively. The overlay of the $Conc_B$ gradient confirms that insufficient slope steepness is a primary contributor to these resolution failures. Furthermore, the Co-elution Index flagged only two events where the $UV_{260}/UV_{280}$ ratio exceeded the purity threshold, indicating that while resolution is poor, nucleic acid contamination in these specific co-eluting valleys may be low or the purity threshold was conservative. However, the extremely high number of detected peaks (ranging from ~1,800 to ~3,900 per run) warrants caution, as this may indicate over-segmentation of noise rather than distinct chemical species. Regarding process consistency, Figure 2 demonstrates significant heterogeneity in column performance. The bar chart of the RSD for the worst-resolved peak pairs reveals that while some columns exhibit low variability, indicating consistent separation of difficult peak pairs, others display high RSD. This high variability suggests that specific columns are prone to inconsistent gradient delivery, column degradation, or packing heterogeneity, leading to unpredictable co-elution events. These findings underscore the need for targeted gradient optimization in regions of shallow slope and further investigation into the physical state of columns exhibiting high run-to-run variability.

#Conclusion 

The comprehensive analysis of the chromatographic dataset has successfully identified critical areas of poor resolution and process inconsistency. The widespread occurrence of co-eluting events ($R_s < 1.5$) and the correlation of these failures with shallow gradient slopes indicate a clear need for gradient optimization to enhance separation efficiency. The visualization of the worst-case run (Figure 1) provides a direct reference for the specific volume ranges requiring steeper gradient profiles. Additionally, the assessment of run-to-run variability (Figure 2) highlights significant differences in column stability, with certain columns showing high RSD in their worst-resolved peak pairs, necessitating immediate maintenance or replacement. While the Co-elution Index suggests that nucleic acid contamination is not the primary driver of the observed co-elution, the sheer volume of detected peaks suggests that peak detection parameters may require refinement to distinguish true chemical entities from instrumental noise. Future work should focus on simulating optimized gradient profiles to achieve $R_s \geq 1.5$ in the identified failure zones and conducting physical inspections of the columns exhibiting high variability to ensure long-term process robustness.
