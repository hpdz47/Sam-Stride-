#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative analysis of high-resolution High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) data across 11 runs utilizing four distinct column types (CM, SP, DEAE, Q). The primary objective was to characterize peak morphology, assess resolution between adjacent peaks, and correlate these metrics with local gradient conditions. The methodology employed Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) for baseline correction and Savitzky-Golay differentiation for gradient slope estimation. Results indicate exceptional baseline separation between adjacent peaks, with Resolution ($R_s$) values ranging from 15.92 to 661.04 and valley depths consistently exceeding 88%. However, a critical anomaly was observed in the gradient analysis, where local gradient slopes were uniformly zero for the majority of peak pairs, suggesting either isocratic elution conditions or limitations in the differentiation algorithm's adaptive windowing. Despite this, the chromatographic performance in terms of peak separation was robust.

#Introduction 

The optimization of chromatographic separations requires precise quantification of peak shape, resolution, and the influence of elution gradients. In this study, we analyzed raw HPLC signals to evaluate the performance of four column types (CM, SP, DEAE, Q) across multiple experimental runs. The motivation for this analysis stems from the need to establish a robust quality control framework capable of detecting aberrant peak shapes and ensuring high-resolution separations. Key metrics of interest include peak width at half maximum (FWHM), USP <621> Tailing Factors, and Resolution ($R_s$). Furthermore, understanding the relationship between peak shape and the local gradient slope ($d(Conc_B)/d(volume)$) is essential for method development. This report synthesizes the results of a three-step data analysis workflow involving signal preprocessing, gradient correlation, and anomaly detection to provide a comprehensive assessment of the chromatographic data quality.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three sequential steps. First, raw signals from `chromatography_combined.csv` were preprocessed. Data was downsampled to a uniform 0.1 ml grid, and Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction was applied independently for each `run_no` and `column` combination using lambda values between $10^6$ and $10^7$ and p values between $10^{-4}$ and $10^{-5}$. This step incorporated negative volume pre-injection windows and inter-fraction baseline rows to ensure accurate drift modeling. Peak detection was performed to calculate height, area, FWHM, and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR). Tailing factors were calculated using $W_{0.05}$ for SNR $
less$ 10 and $W_{0.10}$ for SNR < 10.

Second, Gradient Correlation and Resolution Analysis was conducted. The local gradient of the mobile phase concentration (`Conc_B_%`) was computed using Savitzky-Golay differentiation with an adaptive window (15 or 30 points based on data density). Resolution ($R_s$) and Valley Depth were calculated for adjacent peak pairs, with $R_s$ reported only if the valley depth exceeded 10% of the smaller peak's height. Finally, Anomaly Detection and Quality Flagging were performed. Metrics were aggregated per run and column, and aberrant shapes were flagged based on strict criteria: Tailing Factor > 1.8, Resolution < 1.5 (with valley depth > 10%), or statistical outliers exceeding 3 Median Absolute Deviations (MAD) from the median. Runs with zero detected peaks were explicitly flagged as 'No Peaks Detected'.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the chromatographic runs revealed highly effective separation performance across the tested conditions. The calculated Resolution ($R_s$) values for adjacent peak pairs were exceptionally high, ranging from 15.92 to 661.04. These values significantly exceed the standard threshold for baseline separation ($R_s > 1.5$), indicating that the peaks were well-resolved. Furthermore, the Valley Depth percentages were consistently high, falling between 88% and 99.9%, which confirms that the signal between adjacent peaks dropped significantly, validating the distinct nature of the detected peaks.

A notable finding pertains to the local gradient slope analysis. The `Local_Gradient_Slope` was calculated to be 0.0000 for the vast majority of peak pairs across all runs and column types. Only two specific peak pairs in Runs 19, 20, and 21 exhibited non-zero slopes of approximately 1.04. This uniformity suggests that the Savitzky-Golay differentiation either failed to detect a gradient due to the specific windowing logic or that the elution conditions for the majority of the analyzed peaks were effectively isocratic (flat gradient) at the apex. While high-resolution separations in gradient chromatography typically correlate with non-zero local slopes, the robustness of the peak detection and the high valley depths suggest that the separation quality itself was not compromised. The zero gradient values warrant further investigation into the `Conc_B_%` profile or the sensitivity of the differentiation parameters, but the primary conclusion remains that the chromatographic conditions yielded excellent peak resolution. No aberrant shapes were flagged based on the defined criteria, and the data quality regarding peak identification appears robust.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the data analysis workflow successfully quantified peak morphology and resolution for 11 chromatography runs across four column types. The results demonstrate near-perfect baseline separation with exceptionally high resolution ($R_s$) and valley depth metrics. While the analysis identified a potential discrepancy in the local gradient slope calculations—showing uniform zero values for most runs—the integrity of the peak separation data remains high. The methodology effectively flagged peak metrics and validated the robustness of the chromatographic conditions. Future work should focus on refining the gradient differentiation algorithm to better capture local slope variations, ensuring a more complete correlation between gradient dynamics and peak shape.
