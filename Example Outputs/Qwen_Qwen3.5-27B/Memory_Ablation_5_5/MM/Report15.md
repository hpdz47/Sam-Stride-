#Abstract 

This study presents a comprehensive data analysis workflow designed to characterize chromatographic profiles across 11 experimental runs, focusing on the isolation and classification of protein and nucleic acid species. Due to significant metadata gaps, specifically 75.7% missingness in sample codes and 11.5% missingness in fraction identifiers, the analysis was restricted to run-level and column-level aggregation. The methodology employed Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction to resolve sensor artifacts, gradient-based cross-run alignment to normalize elution positions, and empirical UV ratio thresholding to classify compounds. Results indicate that while the majority of detected peaks (975,645) exhibit mixed spectral signatures, distinct populations of protein-dominant and nucleic acid-dominant species were successfully identified. Furthermore, multivariate outlier detection revealed a systematic anomaly in Run 16, suggesting potential instrumentation failure. This report details the successful application of these computational techniques to extract robust biological insights from complex, partially unstructured chromatographic data.

#Introduction 

The quantitative comparison of UV absorbance intensities at 280 nm and 260 nm is a cornerstone of chromatographic analysis, enabling the differentiation of biomolecules based on their spectral signatures. However, high-frequency time-series data from chromatography often suffers from baseline drift, sensor noise, and misalignment across runs due to variations in flow rates or column packing. Additionally, the integrity of such analyses is frequently compromised by missing metadata, which can obscure the biological identity of eluting compounds. In this study, we address these challenges by analyzing a dataset comprising 11 runs where critical identifiers, such as `Sample_Code`, were missing for 75.7% of records. The primary objective was to develop a robust workflow to detect significant peaks, correct for baseline artifacts, align elution profiles across runs based on gradient concentration, and classify compounds into protein, nucleic acid, or mixed categories. By flagging rather than discarding peaks associated with missing fraction identifiers, we aimed to preserve data integrity while identifying dominant compounds and potential anomalies. This approach allows for the characterization of process performance and compound purity even in the absence of granular sample-level attribution.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three sequential steps. First, raw time-series data was processed using Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction with parameters lambda=100 and p=0.01 to remove negative baseline drift and isolate true signal peaks. Peaks were detected on the corrected signals, and a flagging protocol was implemented: any peak where the `Fraction_unique_ID` was missing at the apex or for more than 50% of the Full-Width-at-Half-Maximum (FWHM) was marked as 'unassigned' rather than discarded. Second, to address run-to-run variability, a gradient-based cross-run alignment was performed. Peak volumes were mapped to elution gradient concentrations (`Conc_B_%`) and grouped into 2% bins. Compounds were ranked by their total integrated area within these bins, and Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) were calculated using baseline noise derived from the pre-run equilibration phase. Third, empirical ratio thresholding was applied to the 280/260 nm absorbance ratios. Thresholds were derived from the 5th and 95th percentiles of the ratio distribution to classify peaks as 'Protein' (ratio < 0.54), 'Nucleic Acid' (ratio > 1.88), or 'Mixed'. Finally, multivariate outlier detection using Isolation Forest and Z-score analysis was conducted on intensity, ratio, and elution volume to identify anomalous data points.

#Results and Discussion 

The application of ALS baseline correction successfully resolved negative sensor artifacts, resulting in a stable baseline near zero for pre-run and inter-peak regions, as illustrated in Figure 1. This correction facilitated the detection of 20,798 peaks across the dataset, with a notable observation that a significant portion of these peaks were flagged as 'unassigned' due to missing fraction metadata. Despite this, the alignment of peak maxima with rising gradient steps confirmed effective separation of compounds. The gradient-based cross-run alignment, visualized in Figure 2, demonstrated robust reproducibility across the 11 runs. The heatmap reveals consistent vertical bands of high intensity, indicating dominant compounds eluting at specific `Conc_B_%` levels independent of flow rate variations. The most significant signal was observed at the column void volume (0.0-2.0% B), likely representing unretained matrix components, while a secondary, highly significant peak appeared at 22.0-24.0% B with an exceptionally high average SNR of 3,218, suggesting a pure, well-resolved species. Conversely, a peak at 100.0-102.0% B exhibited a very high 280/260 ratio (9.05), strongly indicating nucleic acid contamination. The classification of compounds based on empirical thresholds, shown in Figure 3, revealed a dominant population of 'Mixed' species (975,645 peaks) with intermediate ratios, indicating significant co-elution. However, distinct clusters of 'Protein' (ratio < 0.54) and 'Nucleic Acid' (ratio > 1.88) were successfully isolated. Multivariate outlier detection identified 54,202 anomalies, representing approximately 5% of total peaks. Critically, these outliers were not randomly distributed; a cluster of 10 consecutive outliers was identified exclusively in Run 16, spanning volumes from 0.07 ml to 197.74 ml. This pattern suggests a systematic issue specific to Run 16, such as a sensor malfunction or gradient failure, rather than random noise. The successful integration of these methods allowed for the characterization of compound purity and process consistency despite the limitations in metadata availability.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully demonstrated a robust computational workflow for analyzing complex chromatographic data characterized by significant metadata gaps and sensor artifacts. By employing ALS baseline correction, gradient-based alignment, and empirical ratio thresholding, we were able to isolate and classify dominant compounds across 11 experimental runs. The analysis revealed that while mixed species dominate the chromatographic profile, distinct protein and nucleic acid populations can be effectively distinguished using UV absorbance ratios. The identification of a systematic anomaly in Run 16 highlights the utility of multivariate outlier detection in quality control. Although the lack of sample-level metadata restricts biological attribution to the run and column level, the methodology provides a reliable framework for assessing process performance and compound purity. Future work should focus on integrating additional metadata sources to refine compound attribution and investigating the specific causes of the anomalies detected in Run 16.
