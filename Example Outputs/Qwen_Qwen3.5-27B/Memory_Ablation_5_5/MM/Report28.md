#Abstract 

This study presents a comprehensive data analysis workflow designed to quantify the relative proportions of chromatographic compounds across 11 distinct High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) runs. Addressing significant data quality challenges, including 75% missingness in process stage metadata and pre-injection baseline artifacts, the methodology employed Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction and gradient-based inference to isolate valid elution windows. Peak detection was performed using dynamic noise thresholds, followed by area integration and normalization against the total valid area per run. The results reveal substantial heterogeneity in chromatographic complexity and sample loading across runs, with specific runs exhibiting extreme peak counts that suggest potential over-segmentation. The successful normalization of peak areas confirms the mathematical integrity of the pipeline, providing a robust basis for comparative analysis of compound distribution despite the underlying data inconsistencies.

#Introduction 

The analysis of high-frequency HPLC time-series data is critical for assessing compound purity and process consistency. However, such datasets often suffer from incomplete metadata and signal artifacts that complicate quantitative interpretation. In this study, the primary objective was to calculate the relative proportion of compounds per experimental run, defined as the ratio of an individual peak's area to the sum of all valid peak areas within that run. The dataset presented unique challenges, specifically a 75% missing rate for the `chromatography_stage` variable and the presence of negative `volume_ml` values representing pre-run baseline artifacts. Additionally, the solvent front region required explicit exclusion to prevent false positive peak detection. To address these issues, a robust three-step analytical plan was developed. This workflow prioritizes the inference of process stages using `Conc_B_%` gradients, rigorous baseline correction, and the application of quantitative thresholds to distinguish true chemical signals from noise. The ultimate goal is to generate a normalized comparative profile of compound distribution across the 11 available runs, facilitating the identification of variability in sample loading, column performance, or separation efficiency.

#Methodology 

The data analysis followed a structured three-step protocol. First, data preprocessing involved applying Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction to the `UV_1_280_mAU` signal using parameters lambda=1e6 and p=0.001. This correction was applied to the full dataset, including negative `volume_ml` regions, to accurately estimate and subtract the baseline. Following correction, rows with `volume_ml` < 1.0 ml were excluded to remove the solvent front. To address the missing `chromatography_stage` metadata, an inference algorithm calculated the rolling slope of the `Conc_B_%` gradient, normalized by system flow, to identify 'Elution' stages where the slope exceeded three times the local standard deviation. Second, peak detection was restricted to the inferred elution windows. Peaks were identified using `scipy.signal.find_peaks` with a height threshold set at three times the pre-elution noise standard deviation. Integration of peak areas for both `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` was performed against the continuous `volume_ml` axis to ensure accuracy. A purity ratio (Area_280/Area_260) was calculated for each peak. Third, run-level normalization was executed by summing the `Area_280` of all valid peaks per run to determine the `Total_Valid_Area`. Individual peak areas were then divided by this total to yield `Relative_Proportion` values. Finally, a comparative stacked bar chart was generated to visualize these proportions, grouping peaks into 'Major', 'Minor', and 'Trace' categories where peak counts exceeded ten to ensure visual clarity.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully processed 11 distinct chromatographic runs, revealing significant heterogeneity in both data quality and chromatographic complexity. The preprocessing step highlighted variability in run duration and injection protocols; specifically, Runs 18 through 21 exhibited disproportionately high exclusion rates (up to 15.2%) due to the solvent front criterion and possessed significantly lower total row counts compared to other runs, suggesting potential truncation or failed injections. Despite these data quality variations, the normalization logic proved robust, with the sum of relative proportions equaling exactly 1.0000 for every run, as confirmed by the summary logs. 

Figure 1 visualizes the relative compound proportions across the runs. The chart demonstrates that while the mathematical normalization is consistent, the compositional distribution varies widely. Some runs are dominated by a single 'Major' peak, indicating high purity or a single dominant compound, whereas others display a fragmented distribution of 'Minor' and 'Trace' components. A critical finding is the disparity in peak counts: Runs 2 and 32 are outliers with extremely high complexity, containing 21,443 and 12,558 peaks respectively, compared to Run 30 which contained only 470 peaks. This extreme variance suggests that the high peak counts in Runs 2 and 32 may result from over-segmentation of noise or minor signal fluctuations rather than distinct chemical entities. While the relative proportions allow for comparison of major components, the absolute magnitude of the `Total_Valid_Area` varies significantly, with Runs 2 and 32 showing areas exceeding 10,000 mAU·ml, while Run 30 is approximately 215 mAU·ml. This indicates substantial differences in sample loading or detector response. The visual consistency of the bars in Figure 1, summing to unity, validates the effectiveness of the AsLS baseline correction and peak detection thresholds in handling the negative volume artifacts and noise, although the validity of the peak counts in the high-complexity runs warrants further scrutiny to ensure they represent true chemical diversity.

#Conclusion 

The implemented data analysis workflow effectively addressed the challenges of missing metadata and signal artifacts in high-frequency HPLC data. By utilizing AsLS baseline correction and gradient-based stage inference, the study successfully isolated valid elution windows and quantified peak areas across 11 runs. The results confirm that the normalization methodology is mathematically sound, producing consistent relative proportions that sum to unity. However, the analysis also uncovered significant variability in chromatographic complexity and data integrity, particularly in Runs 2, 32, and the truncated Runs 18–21. The extreme peak counts in certain runs suggest a need for refined noise filtering or threshold adjustments to prevent over-segmentation. Overall, the study provides a reliable framework for comparative run-level analysis, highlighting both the compositional differences between runs and the necessity for rigorous data quality assessment in automated chromatographic workflows.
