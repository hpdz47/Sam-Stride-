#Abstract 

This report evaluates the structural integrity and analytical viability of high-frequency HPLC sensor data for downstream modeling and USP compliance assessment. The analysis focused on reconstructing missing chromatography stage boundaries, segmenting Cleaning-in-Place (CIP) cycles, and validating peak purity and gradient correlation mechanisms. While signal integrity was confirmed with high Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) and successful CIP segmentation, a critical failure was observed in the mechanistic correlation between the buffer gradient and UV280 elution peaks. Furthermore, the retention volume variability analysis yielded no valid results due to a disconnect between reconstructed stages and detected peaks. These findings indicate that while the raw sensor data is of high quality, the current data processing pipeline requires significant refinement in peak detection logic and stage alignment to support regulatory compliance assessments.

#Introduction 

The primary objective of this data analysis workflow was to rigorously assess the quality of continuous HPLC sensor streams to determine their suitability for modeling chromatographic mechanisms and ensuring product consistency. The dataset, comprising over one million high-frequency rows indexed by volume, presented significant challenges, including 76% missingness in critical `chromatography_stage` metadata and a complex mixture of process runs and Cleaning-in-Place (CIP) cycles indicated by extreme pH excursions. To address these challenges, a three-step analysis plan was executed: (1) Signal Integrity and Stage Reconstruction, (2) Peak Purity and Gradient Correlation, and (3) Retention Volume Variability. The motivation for this work stems from the necessity to infer chromatographic mechanisms via gradient-peak correlation and to quantify run-to-run retention variability against United States Pharmacopeia (USP) standards. This report details the methodology employed and discusses the divergent outcomes between successful signal validation and the failure of mechanistic and retention analyses.

#Methodology 

The analysis proceeded in three distinct steps. Step 1 involved pre-processing the `chromatography_combined.csv` file by grouping data by `run_no` and forward-filling missing stage labels. Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) were calculated for UV280 and UV260 signals by comparing baseline standard deviations to peak regions defined as contiguous segments where UV280 exceeded the baseline mean plus two standard deviations. Stage boundaries were reconstructed by detecting inflection points in conductivity and buffer concentration profiles using a normalized second derivative threshold with a minimum distance constraint of 50 points. CIP cycles were segmented based on pH thresholds (>10 or <2) with a minimum duration of 50 points. Step 2 focused on peak purity by calculating the UV260/UV280 ratio for regions where UV280 exceeded 5.0 mAU. A cross-correlation analysis was performed between the buffer concentration (`Conc_B_%`) and UV280 signal to determine lag times and correlation strength, restricted to 'Elute' and 'Wash' stages. Step 3 aimed to identify the primary retention volume as the peak with the maximum Area Under Curve (AUC) following the gradient start, calculating the Relative Standard Deviation (RSD) across valid runs to assess USP compliance.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of signal integrity (Step 1) demonstrated robust data quality. As visualized in Figure 1, the multi-panel grid confirms the presence of distinct, high-amplitude UV280 peaks and clear step-changes in conductivity and buffer concentration. The reconstruction of missing `chromatography_stage` labels was largely successful, with vertical markers aligning effectively with gradient inflection points. Furthermore, the pH profile successfully distinguished process runs from CIP cycles; 10 of 11 runs showed 0% CIP content, while Runs 19, 20, and 21 correctly identified CIP segments (5.5–9.0%) corresponding to extreme pH excursions. Signal-to-Noise Ratios were generally high, with Run 17 exhibiting a UV280 SNR exceeding 1300, confirming the viability of the sensor streams for downstream modeling.

However, Step 2 revealed a critical discrepancy between visual expectations and quantitative results. While Figure 2 visually suggests a temporal relationship between the rising `Conc_B_%` gradient and the emergence of UV280 peaks, the quantitative cross-correlation analysis yielded a coefficient of effectively zero (0.0000). This contradicts the fundamental mechanism of gradient elution, where UV peaks should align with buffer concentration changes. This failure suggests a misalignment in the reconstructed stage boundaries or a flaw in the peak detection logic. Additionally, 19% of the 841 analyzed peaks were flagged as outliers in the UV260/UV280 ratio, indicating potential sample impurities or detector noise that warrants further investigation.

Step 3, the Retention Volume Variability analysis, failed to produce any valid results, reporting zero valid runs and N/A metrics. This failure is likely a direct consequence of the issues identified in Step 2; the primary peak detection algorithm could not identify a maximum AUC peak post-gradient start. This implies a disconnect between the 'Elute' stages reconstructed in Step 1 and the actual signal peaks, or an incorrect definition of the gradient start volume. Consequently, the dataset, while possessing high raw signal integrity, currently lacks the processed structural alignment necessary to quantify retention variability or assess USP compliance.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the data analysis workflow successfully validated the raw signal integrity and structural segmentation of the HPLC dataset. The high SNR values and effective CIP segmentation confirm that the sensor streams are of sufficient quality for modeling. However, the analysis pipeline failed to establish the critical mechanistic link between the gradient profile and elution peaks, resulting in a zero correlation coefficient and a complete failure to quantify retention volume variability. The visual evidence in Figure 2 suggests that the relationship exists, but the quantitative algorithms failed to capture it, likely due to misaligned stage boundaries or peak detection thresholds. To proceed with USP compliance assessment, the methodology must be revised to correct the peak detection logic and ensure precise alignment between reconstructed chromatography stages and actual elution events. Without these corrections, the current data cannot support regulatory decisions regarding column performance or product consistency.
