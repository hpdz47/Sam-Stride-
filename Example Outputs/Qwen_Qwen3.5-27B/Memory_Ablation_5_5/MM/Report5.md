#Abstract 

This report details the initial phase of a rigorous quality assessment workflow designed to validate High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) time-series data for downstream modeling. The primary objective was to address severe data ingestion artifacts, reconstruct missing process metadata, and apply dynamic baseline corrections to ensure signal integrity. The analysis successfully removed 11 redundant columns, including erroneous `_ml` suffixed duplicates, and reconstructed the `chromatography_stage` metadata for 147,203 rows. While the majority of the dataset was recovered, approximately 3.2% of rows (4,725) were flagged as 'Unverified' due to mismatches between forward-filled stages and observed concentration step-changes. Dynamic baseline correction was applied using linear trend fits on negative volume regions, revealing significant variability in sensor warm-up behaviors across different runs and columns. These foundational steps establish the data quality necessary for subsequent USP <621> compliance validation and outlier classification.

#Introduction 

The reliability of downstream predictive modeling in chromatography is contingent upon the integrity of the input time-series data. The dataset under analysis presented significant challenges, including severe ingestion errors where columns such as `Conc_B_ml` and `pH_ml` were identified as erroneous duplicates of `volume_ml`. Furthermore, approximately 76% of the critical event-based metadata, specifically the `chromatography_stage`, was missing due to logging failures. To address these issues, a three-stage data preparation pipeline was executed. The first stage focused on data sanitization to remove redundant variables. The second stage involved the reconstruction of `chromatography_stage` metadata using forward-filling techniques validated against `Conc_B_%` step-changes. The third stage implemented dynamic baseline correction on `UV_1_280_mAU` and `Cond_mS_cm` signals, utilizing negative `volume_ml` regions to model sensor warm-up drift via linear trend fitting. This report summarizes the execution of Step 1 of the analysis plan, evaluating the efficacy of these sanitization and correction protocols in preparing the data for regulatory-grade signal integrity assessment.

#Methodology 

The methodology followed a structured three-stage pipeline as defined in the data analysis plan. First, data sanitization was performed by dropping all columns ending in `_ml` with the exception of `volume_ml` and `Conc_B_ml`, explicitly excluding `pH_ml` as a duplicate. Second, metadata reconstruction was executed by forward-filling the `chromatography_stage` within `run` groups. Missing initial rows were imputed based on `Conc_B_%` thresholds (values <5% assigned to 'Equilibration'). Crucially, transitions were validated against `Conc_B_%` step-changes; any mismatch exceeding a threshold of >5% within a 1 mL window was flagged as 'Unverified' rather than forced. Third, dynamic baseline correction was applied to `UV_1_280_mAU` and `Cond_mS_cm`. This involved counting negative `volume_ml` rows per `run`/`column` combination. For groups with a count greater than 5, a linear trend was fitted to the negative region and subtracted from the signal. Groups with fewer than 5 negative points utilized a fallback mean or zero baseline. The results were logged to track the logic applied and the resulting slopes.

#Results and Discussion 

The execution of the data sanitization and baseline correction pipeline yielded significant improvements in data structure and signal quality. The removal of 11 redundant columns successfully resolved ingestion errors associated with duplicate volume proxies. Metadata reconstruction was largely effective, recovering 147,203 rows of process stage data. However, 4,725 rows (approximately 3.2% of the total) were flagged as 'Unverified'. These flags indicate potential mismatches between the forward-filled `chromatography_stage` and the observed `Conc_B_%` step-changes, suggesting that while the general trend was captured, specific transition points may require manual review or refined thresholding in future iterations. 

The dynamic baseline correction revealed substantial heterogeneity in sensor behavior across the dataset. The linear trend fitting logic was successfully applied to all displayed entries, as confirmed by 'NegCount' values ranging from 65 to 588, which exceeded the minimum threshold of 5 required for linear fitting. The resulting baseline slopes exhibited significant variability; for instance, Run R1 on Column CM displayed a steep conductivity drift with a slope of -1.9818, whereas Run R16 on the same column showed a much shallower slope of -0.0689. This variance suggests distinct sensor warm-up behaviors or varying pre-run conditions that the linear correction effectively addressed. The successful application of the linear trend subtraction method, rather than the fallback mean/zero baseline, validates the robustness of the negative volume data in modeling pre-run drift. While the sanitization and correction steps appear methodologically sound, the presence of 'Unverified' rows and the wide variance in baseline slopes warrant careful monitoring in downstream steps to ensure these factors do not propagate into signal integrity assessments or peak detection errors.

#Conclusion 

The initial data preparation phase has successfully established a clean and structurally sound foundation for the HPLC dataset. By removing redundant columns and reconstructing the majority of missing metadata, the dataset is now ready for advanced signal integrity assessment. The dynamic baseline correction effectively accounted for sensor warm-up drift, although the observed variability in baseline slopes highlights the importance of run-specific calibration. The 3.2% of 'Unverified' rows represent a manageable risk that should be tracked during the subsequent USP <621> compliance validation and outlier classification steps. Overall, the methodology has proven effective in transforming raw, error-prone ingestion data into a format suitable for rigorous regulatory-grade analysis.
