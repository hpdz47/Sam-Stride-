#Abstract 

This report evaluates the efficacy of a three-step data analysis workflow designed to characterize high-frequency HPLC sensor data from 11 manufacturing runs. The primary objectives were to correct baseline drift, detect and integrate chromatographic peaks, identify the target product via clustering, and assess purity through multi-channel correlation of protein (280 nm) and nucleic acid (260 nm) signals. While the workflow successfully generated a global correlation visualization, critical methodological failures were identified in the initial peak detection and integration stages. Specifically, the baseline correction algorithm produced physically impossible negative peak areas and Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM) values, and the peak detection logic resulted in significant peak splitting artifacts. Consequently, while the correlation plot suggests a strong relationship between protein and nucleic acid signals, the quantitative metrics required for precise target identification and impurity categorization are currently invalid. Immediate algorithmic corrections regarding baseline subtraction and peak merging are required before the data can support robust quality control decisions.

#Introduction 

The analysis of High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) data is critical for ensuring the purity and consistency of biopharmaceutical manufacturing processes. This study focuses on processing high-frequency sensor data from 11 distinct manufacturing runs to generate a comprehensive peak catalog and assess product purity. The workflow was designed to address specific challenges inherent in the dataset, including negative baseline drift, variable system flow rates necessitating volume-based retention time analysis, and the presence of non-fractionated regions. The primary motivation was to algorithmically distinguish the target product from potential impurities and critical anomalies by establishing a statistical elution window and correlating UV absorbance at 280 nm (protein) and 260 nm (nucleic acid). By applying Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction, derivative-based peak detection, and Gaussian fitting, the aim was to create a robust framework for automated quality assessment. However, the integrity of such an analysis relies heavily on the accuracy of the initial signal processing steps. This report details the methodology employed, highlights the discrepancies found between the planned analysis and the actual outputs, and discusses the implications of these findings on the final purity assessment.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three sequential steps. Step 1 involved Baseline Correction and Peak Detection. Raw data from `chromatography_combined.csv` was grouped by `run_no` and sorted by `volume_ml`. An Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) algorithm was applied to the `UV_1_280_mAU` signal with parameters lambda=10^5 and p=0.001 to correct for negative drift. Following baseline subtraction, derivative-based peak detection was performed using a Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) threshold of >3. Detected peaks were fitted with Gaussian curves to calculate Area Under the Curve (AUC), retention volume, and Full Width at Half Maximum (FWHM). Step 2, Target Identification, intended to cluster high-intensity peaks across runs using normalized volume to define a statistical elution window and categorize anomalies. Step 3 focused on Multi-Channel Correlation and Purity Assessment. This step aligned the 260 nm and 280 nm signals using cross-correlation or fixed offsets, extracted signal intensities within a tolerance window based on FWHM, and calculated the 260/280 area ratio for each peak. The results were visualized in a scatter plot to identify sub-populations indicative of nucleic acid contamination. The analysis utilized 11 runs, with a specific focus on handling missing fraction IDs and variable flow rates.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the generated outputs reveals a dichotomy between the visual correlation results and the underlying quantitative data integrity. Figure 1 displays the multi-channel correlation analysis, plotting `UV_1_280_mAU` against `UV_2_260_mAU`. The plot demonstrates a strong positive correlation, indicating that the majority of detected peaks contain both protein and nucleic acid components, which is consistent with expectations for crude biological samples. Distinct sub-populations are visible: points with high 280 nm signal and low 260 nm signal likely represent the purified target product, while clusters with a steep slope suggest peaks with high nucleic acid content, potentially identifying impurities. The density overlay effectively manages the high cardinality of the dataset, and the alignment of the two channels appears successful, suggesting the cross-correlation strategy compensated for detector drift.

However, the validity of Figure 1 is compromised by critical failures identified in the Step 1 markdown analysis reports. The peak detection and integration logic produced physically impossible results. The `peak_area_mAU_ml` column contains negative values (e.g., -188.25, -429.67), and `fwhm_ml` values are also negative (e.g., -3.34). Since peak area and FWHM are inherently positive metrics derived from Gaussian fitting, these results indicate a fundamental error in the baseline subtraction algorithm, likely subtracting the baseline incorrectly or failing to handle signal polarity. Furthermore, the analysis revealed severe 'peak splitting' artifacts, where single chromatographic events were fragmented into dozens of sequential peaks with identical retention volumes but varying heights. This suggests the derivative-based detection parameters were overly sensitive, failing to merge adjacent data points.

Consequently, while Figure 1 provides a qualitative overview of the signal relationship, the specific data points plotted are derived from flawed integration metrics. The calculated 260/280 ratios and the identification of 'Critical Anomalies' or 'Potential Impurities' based on these ratios are unreliable. The presence of negative areas and split peaks means the statistical distribution used to define purity thresholds is distorted. The workflow successfully visualized the data but failed to generate the accurate quantitative catalog required for the subsequent steps of target identification and impurity categorization. The negative values and splitting artifacts must be resolved by correcting the AsLS baseline parameters and adjusting the peak merging thresholds before the analysis can be considered valid for scientific or regulatory purposes.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the attempted data analysis workflow successfully processed the raw HPLC data to generate a visual correlation between protein and nucleic acid signals, as evidenced by Figure 1. The visualization confirms a strong positive correlation and suggests the presence of distinct sub-populations that could theoretically differentiate target products from impurities. However, the quantitative foundation of this analysis is fundamentally flawed. The baseline correction and peak detection algorithms produced physically impossible negative peak areas and FWHM values, alongside significant peak splitting artifacts. These errors render the derived metrics invalid for the intended purposes of target identification, statistical elution window definition, and precise purity assessment. While the correlation plot offers a high-level qualitative insight, it cannot currently support rigorous scientific conclusions or quality control decisions. Future work must prioritize the correction of the baseline subtraction logic and the refinement of peak detection parameters to eliminate negative values and merging errors. Only after these methodological issues are resolved can the workflow reliably categorize anomalies and assess the purity of the manufacturing runs.
