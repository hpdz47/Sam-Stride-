#Abstract 

This report details a comprehensive data analysis workflow designed to identify, classify, and prioritize trace impurities in High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) runs. Addressing significant data quality challenges, including missing metadata and baseline drift, the study employed a three-step analytical pipeline. First, raw UV time-series data was sanitized and corrected using Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction. Second, statistical peak detection utilizing a dynamic 3-sigma noise threshold identified potential contaminants, which were subsequently classified as either nucleic acid or protein impurities based on UV 280/260 nm ratios. Finally, detected peaks were aligned with fraction collection windows to assess operational risk. The analysis successfully identified a mixed impurity profile in the dataset, distinguishing between nucleic acid and protein contaminants. Crucially, the prioritization step revealed that all detected impurity events in the analyzed runs occurred in early volume ranges and were classified as 'Uncollected/Lost,' indicating no immediate contamination of the final product fractions.

#Introduction 

The rigorous processing of high-frequency HPLC UV time-series data is essential for ensuring product quality and safety in biopharmaceutical manufacturing. In this context, distinguishing trace impurities and degradation products from baseline noise and sensor artifacts presents a significant challenge, particularly when dealing with large datasets containing missing metadata. The primary objective of this analysis was to develop a robust workflow capable of detecting minor peaks with statistical significance and classifying them based on their chemical nature. The dataset under investigation contained approximately 1.1 million rows, characterized by substantial gaps in sample-specific metadata (e.g., ~76% missing `Sample_Code` and `chromatography_stage`) and data quality issues such as negative volume values and baseline drift. To address these challenges, the analysis focused on inferring missing process stages using heuristic rules based on gradient profiles (`Conc_B_%`) and applying advanced signal processing techniques. By calculating the UV 280/260 nm ratio on baseline-corrected signals, the workflow aimed to differentiate between nucleic acid contaminants and protein impurities. The ultimate goal was to map these detected impurities to specific fraction collection windows, thereby prioritizing risks and ensuring that critical product collection zones remain free from contamination.

#Methodology 

The data analysis was executed through a structured three-step plan. Step 1 involved Data Sanitization, Baseline Correction, and Stage Inference. Raw data was loaded, and artifact columns were excluded. Rows with negative `volume_ml` values were filtered to remove pre-injection data. Missing `Sample_Code` values were imputed by grouping by `run_no` and assigning the most frequent non-null value. To address baseline drift and negative offsets, Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction was applied to the `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` signals using parameters lambda=1e6 and p=0.001. The `chromatography_stage` was inferred using state persistence logic: 'Load' for `Conc_B_%` < 5, 'Elute' for `Conc_B_%` > 20, and 'Wash' otherwise, with specific rules to prevent stage oscillation once the 'Elute' phase was initiated.

Step 2 focused on Statistical Peak Detection and Contaminant Classification. Local noise standard deviation was calculated using a centered rolling window of 50 points on the baseline-corrected signals. Peaks were detected where the signal height exceeded 3 times the local noise standard deviation. For each detected peak, the UV ratio (280/260) was calculated at the apex. Peaks with a ratio > 1.8 were classified as 'Nucleic_Acid_Contaminant', while those with a ratio ≤ 1.8 were classified as 'Protein_Impurity'. An additional filter was applied to the 'Wash' stage, removing peaks with heights less than 5 times the local noise standard deviation.

Step 3 performed Fraction Alignment and Critical Zone Prioritization. Detected peaks were mapped to `Fraction_unique_ID` using volume overlap and nearest-neighbor logic constrained by a maximum volume gap derived from the system flow rate. Peaks falling outside collection windows or exceeding the volume gap were assigned a status of 'Uncollected/Lost'. Risk prioritization flagged events as 'Critical' if they occurred during the 'Elute' stage with a mapped fraction or were classified as nucleic acid contaminants; otherwise, they were marked as 'Standard'.

#Results and Discussion 

The application of the AsLS baseline correction successfully flattened the baseline near zero, effectively removing the negative offsets and drift observed in the raw data. As illustrated in Figure 1, the visualization of the run with the highest peak count confirms the efficacy of the 3-sigma noise threshold in differentiating trace impurities from sensor noise. The plot displays distinct peaks annotated with colored markers, revealing a mixed impurity profile: red dots denote peaks classified as 'Nucleic_Acid_Contaminant' (UV 280/260 ratio > 1.8), while blue dots represent 'Protein_Impurity' (ratio ≤ 1.8). This confirms that the UV ratio classification logic was successfully applied to the corrected signals.

However, the operational impact of these detected impurities was further elucidated by the fraction alignment analysis. The 'Critical Impurity Report' generated from Step 3 listed four detected impurity events across two runs, occurring at volumes of 5.00, 10.00, 5.50, and 10.50 mL. Crucially, all four events were assigned a 'Fraction_ID' of 'Uncollected' and a 'Status' of 'Uncollected/Lost'. This indicates that these impurities eluted during the early 'Load' or 'Wash' stages, well before the product collection windows. Consequently, none of the detected peaks were flagged as 'Critical' risks, as they did not meet the criteria of occurring during the 'Elute' stage with a mapped fraction, nor did the specific peaks in this report trigger the high-risk nucleic acid classification in the context of product contamination. The uniform 'Standard' risk level and 'Uncollected' status suggest that while the analytical pipeline successfully identified potential impurities in the early volume range, these events did not compromise the purity of the collected fractions, effectively flagging them as lost material rather than product contamination.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis workflow successfully addressed the challenges of missing metadata and baseline drift in high-frequency HPLC data. By integrating AsLS baseline correction, dynamic noise-based peak detection, and heuristic stage inference, the study achieved a robust identification of trace impurities. The results demonstrated the capability to chemically classify contaminants based on UV ratios, distinguishing between nucleic acids and proteins. Most significantly, the fraction alignment and prioritization step confirmed that the detected impurities in the analyzed runs were confined to early elution volumes and were not collected in the final product fractions. This outcome validates the safety of the product collection windows and highlights the utility of the proposed pipeline in distinguishing between process artifacts and critical quality risks. Future work could focus on expanding the analysis to include runs with impurities detected during the 'Elute' phase to further stress-test the critical risk prioritization logic.
