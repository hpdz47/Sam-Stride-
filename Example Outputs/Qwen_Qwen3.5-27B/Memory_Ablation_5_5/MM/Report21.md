#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative analysis of high-frequency HPLC chromatography data to assess system stability, gradient alignment, and product purity. The workflow addressed significant data quality challenges, including negative baseline drift and the exclusion of non-physical pre-run intervals. By applying Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction and rigorous filtering, we successfully processed the dataset to calculate integrated peak areas for abundance and purity metrics. Key findings indicate a mean purity ratio (260/280) of 1.0266 with no fractions flagged for high-purity risk, suggesting the absence of significant nucleic acid contamination. System stability analysis confirmed consistent retention times across columns and stable pressure profiles, despite minor fluctuations. The study demonstrates the efficacy of the proposed preprocessing and integration pipeline in generating robust quality metrics for chromatographic runs.

#Introduction 

High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) is a critical technique for the separation and quantification of biomolecules, where data integrity relies heavily on accurate signal processing. This analysis focuses on a dataset characterized by high-frequency UV signals, specifically targeting `UV_1_280_mAU` for protein abundance and `UV_2_260_mAU` for purity assessment. The primary motivation for this study was to develop a robust pipeline capable of handling raw data artifacts, such as negative baseline drift (approx. -7.95 mAU) and physically impossible negative volume readings representing pre-run intervals. Furthermore, the dataset presented a significant metadata gap, with approximately 75% of rows lacking specific `Sample_Code` identifiers, necessitating an aggregation strategy based on `run_no` and `column` rather than individual samples. The objectives were to correct baseline drift using Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS), exclude non-physical data, calculate integrated peak areas for purity ratios (260/280), and visualize system stability through pressure monitoring and gradient alignment. Achieving these goals ensures reliable assessment of product quality and process consistency.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three sequential steps designed to preprocess signals, integrate peaks, and visualize system performance. 

Step 1 involved Signal Preprocessing and Baseline Correction. The raw dataset (`chromatography_combined.csv`) was loaded, and redundant columns were removed. Rows with `volume_ml` < 0.0 were strictly excluded to eliminate pre-run intervals. The remaining data was grouped by `run_no` and `column`, and Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction was applied to both `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` using parameters λ=1e6 and p=1e-4 to address negative drift. This generated corrected signals (`UV_1_280_corrected`, `UV_2_260_corrected`). Rows with missing `Fraction_unique_ID` were retained in a separate subset for system stability monitoring but excluded from integration.

Step 2 focused on Peak Integration and Purity Ratio Calculation. Trapezoidal integration was performed on the corrected signals against `volume_ml`. Rows with missing `Sample_Code` were excluded from sample-specific aggregation, resulting in the exclusion of 820,392 rows. Purity ratios (`Ratio_260_280`) were calculated as the ratio of integrated areas (Area_260 / Area_280), with a safety check to prevent division by zero. Fractions with a ratio > 0.6 were flagged as high-purity-risk. Abundance was normalized by total run area and fraction volume. Gradient alignment was verified by detecting step changes in the `Conc_B_%` profile.

Step 3 entailed System Stability and Gradient Alignment Visualization. A multi-panel grid was generated to visualize corrected UV signals, gradient profiles, and system pressure (`DeltaC_pressure_MPa`). Retention time outliers were identified using peak apex detection and Interquartile Range (IQR) analysis, while pressure anomalies were flagged as points exceeding mean ± 3 standard deviations.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis yielded robust quantitative metrics and visual confirmation of system integrity. In terms of data processing, the AsLS baseline correction effectively flattened pre-run and post-run baselines to near zero, successfully removing the negative drift observed in raw data. The exclusion of negative volume data ensured that only physical flow intervals were analyzed. 

Quantitative results from the integration step revealed a mean purity ratio (260/280) of 1.0266 across the dataset, with a maximum observed ratio of 3.1143. Crucially, no fractions were flagged as high-purity-risk (Ratio > 0.6), indicating that the dataset does not exhibit significant nucleic acid contamination or protein degradation based on this metric. The gradient analysis successfully identified 9,868 step instances across 11 runs, confirming the detection of elution events and alignment with the expected gradient profile. 

As illustrated in Figure 1, the system stability overview confirms the efficacy of the preprocessing steps. The UV peaks align well with the gradient profile (`Conc_B_%`), suggesting successful gradient alignment. The pressure traces (`DeltaC_pressure_MPa`) are largely stable, with no large-scale spikes that would compromise run integrity. While the algorithm highlighted specific minor pressure fluctuations (points exceeding mean ± 3 std), these anomalies do not appear to affect the overall chromatographic performance. Furthermore, the retention times of peak apexes appear consistent across different columns for the same run, with no major outliers deviating significantly from the median. This consistency supports the conclusion of good system reproducibility. The visualization also confirms that the preprocessing logic correctly distinguished between collection phases and waste phases, providing a comprehensive view of the entire run cycle.

#Conclusion 

The implemented data analysis workflow successfully addressed the challenges posed by raw HPLC data, including baseline drift, non-physical volume readings, and incomplete metadata. The application of AsLS baseline correction and rigorous filtering resulted in high-quality integrated signals suitable for quantitative analysis. The study confirms that the analyzed dataset exhibits acceptable purity levels, with no high-risk fractions identified, and demonstrates excellent system stability and gradient alignment. The consistency of retention times across columns further validates the reproducibility of the chromatographic process. These findings provide a solid foundation for future quality control assessments and process optimization, ensuring that abundance and purity metrics are derived from accurate, artifact-free data.
