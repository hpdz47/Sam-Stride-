#Abstract 

This report details a rigorous quality control assessment of high-frequency High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) time-series data. The primary objective was to distinguish genuine chromatographic anomalies from systematic artifacts through non-linear baseline correction and volume offset calibration. Analysis revealed that the negative volume offset error is not a global constant but varies significantly between experimental runs, necessitating a run-specific calibration strategy. Furthermore, injection consistency analysis identified critical procedural deviations in specific runs (18–21), characterized by erratic injection counts and inflated variance. These findings highlight the necessity of granular, run-level diagnostics to ensure data integrity before proceeding to downstream peak symmetry and process state inference analyses.

#Introduction 

High-frequency HPLC data often contains structural artifacts that can obscure genuine chromatographic signals. In this dataset, significant challenges included negative volume values indicating systematic offset errors, missing metadata for chromatography stages, and potential mechanical artifacts manifesting as UV spikes correlated with pressure surges. The motivation for this analysis was to validate detector integrity and experimental consistency by first correcting baseline drift using Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) and determining the nature of the volume offset error. A critical hypothesis was whether this offset was a global constant applicable to the entire dataset or a run-specific parameter requiring individual calibration. Additionally, the study aimed to assess injection consistency and peak symmetry to identify runs with mechanical or procedural failures. Establishing these baselines is essential for accurate temporal correlation analysis and the inference of missing process states via change-point detection.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow comprised three primary steps. First, baseline correction was applied to UV signals (280 nm and 260 nm) using AsLS with optimized parameters (lambda=1e5, p=0.1) to remove drift. Concurrently, the volume offset was investigated by calculating the minimum `volume_ml` for each `run_no` to determine if the offset was global or run-specific. Second, temporal correlation analysis was planned to isolate mechanical artifacts by correlating pressure surges (`DeltaC_pressure_MPa`) with UV spikes, though the current report focuses on the calibration and consistency results. Third, injection consistency and peak symmetry were evaluated. Rows with missing `fraction_volume` were excluded to prevent bias. Peak Width at Half-Height (W½) was calculated by converting volume to time using verified flow rates, and the asymmetry factor was derived. Statistical metrics, including mean injection volume and variance, were computed per run to flag inconsistencies. Visualizations were generated to validate the volume offset distribution and peak symmetry metrics across the gradient.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the volume offset calibration revealed a critical heterogeneity in the data. As illustrated in Figure 1, the distribution of minimum volume values across the 11 runs is not uniform. While the majority of runs (1, 2, 4, 16, 17, 30) exhibited a consistent offset of approximately -5.01 mL, a distinct subset of runs (18, 19, 20, 21) displayed significantly larger negative offsets ranging from -24.11 to -33.83 mL. This finding refutes the hypothesis of a global constant offset and validates the necessity of applying run-specific calibration constants to prevent systematic errors in time-series alignment. The tight clustering within the outlier group suggests a consistent procedural deviation or mechanical fault specific to that experimental batch.

Injection consistency analysis further corroborated the presence of anomalies in runs 18 through 21. These runs displayed erratic behavior with drastically reduced injection counts (ranging from 2 to 6) compared to the stable count of 12 observed in the other runs. Furthermore, the injection variance in these outlier runs was inflated up to 12,169, compared to approximately 761 in stable runs. This inconsistency likely contributed to the failure of peak detection algorithms in these specific runs, evidenced by `nan` values for the average W½. 

Figure 2 presents the aggregate peak symmetry analysis. The top panel shows the distribution of W½ values, while the bottom panel plots the asymmetry factor against the mobile phase concentration (`Conc_B_%`). While the figure provides a high-level assessment of chromatographic performance, the presence of `nan` values and the exclusion of data from the inconsistent runs (18–21) limit the interpretability of the symmetry trends for the full dataset. The scatter plot suggests that for the valid runs, asymmetry remains relatively stable across the gradient, although the lack of granular run-specific data in the visualization prevents the immediate identification of specific concentration ranges where symmetry might degrade. The consistent exclusion of 11.5% of data rows (those missing `Fraction_unique_ID`) across all runs confirms the successful filtering of wash steps, yet the high flow variance flag remains active for all runs, indicating potential underlying flow instability that warrants further investigation.

#Conclusion 

The quality control assessment successfully identified significant data quality issues that would have compromised downstream analysis if unaddressed. The primary conclusion is that the volume offset error is run-specific, necessitating a tailored calibration approach rather than a global correction. Furthermore, runs 18 through 21 exhibit severe injection inconsistencies and procedural deviations, rendering their peak symmetry data unreliable. These findings underscore the importance of granular, run-level diagnostics in HPLC data processing. Future steps should focus on investigating the mechanical causes of the anomalies in runs 18–21 and re-evaluating the peak symmetry metrics using only the validated, stable runs to ensure accurate process state inference and artifact isolation.
