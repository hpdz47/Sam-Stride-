#Abstract 

This report evaluates the reproducibility of chromatographic retention times across four distinct column types (CM, DEAE, Q, and SP) using data from 11 experimental runs. The analysis employed a rigorous workflow involving Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction, signal-to-noise thresholding, and flow-rate normalization to derive retention times. Following stratified alignment and outlier filtering, the Coefficient of Variation (CV) was calculated and benchmarked against the USP <621> standard of ≤2.0%. The results indicate a critical failure in system reproducibility, with all four column types exhibiting CVs ranging from 58.19% to 68.56%. These exceptionally high values suggest that the observed variability is driven by systematic issues, such as inconsistent peak identification or the inclusion of non-product peaks, rather than random noise. Consequently, the current methodology fails to reliably distinguish the main product peak across runs, rendering the system unsuitable for precise chromatographic analysis under the tested conditions.

#Introduction 

Chromatographic system performance is fundamentally dependent on the reproducibility of retention times, a key metric for ensuring consistent separation and identification of analytes. In this study, the primary objective was to quantify the reproducibility of retention values across four different chromatography columns (CM, DEAE, Q, and SP) using a dataset comprising over 1.08 million data points from 11 experimental runs. The analysis was motivated by the need to validate system stability against the United States Pharmacopeia (USP) <621> benchmark, which stipulates a maximum Coefficient of Variation (CV) of 2.0% for retention time reproducibility.

The dataset presented specific challenges, including the absence of an explicit time column, necessitating the derivation of retention times from volume and flow rate data. Furthermore, the lack of explicit `Sample_Code` metadata (75% missing) and the presence of multiple column types required a stratified approach to prevent the conflation of different stationary phases, which would artificially inflate variance. The study aimed to determine whether the current data processing pipeline—incorporating baseline correction, peak detection, and alignment—could successfully isolate the main product peak and demonstrate acceptable system reproducibility.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three primary steps designed to process raw chromatographic signals and quantify reproducibility. 

First, data preprocessing involved loading the raw signal data and filtering out rows with non-positive volume values. Retention times were calculated by dividing `volume_ml` by `System_flow_ml_min`, with safeguards against zero-flow errors. Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction was applied to the `UV_1_280_mAU` signal, with parameters (lambda and p) optimized via grid search to minimize residual error while preserving peak sharpness. Peak detection was performed using a signal-to-noise (S/N) threshold of >3, where noise was defined as the standard deviation of the baseline in a rolling window. In cases where `chromatography_stage` metadata was missing, the peak with the maximum area was selected as the main product peak.

Second, stratified alignment and outlier filtering were conducted. Data were grouped by column type to account for differences in stationary phases. A linear regression was fitted to detect systematic drift across sequential runs; if significant drift was detected (p < 0.05), Loess alignment was applied to correct for it. Outliers were subsequently removed using the Interquartile Range (IQR) method (Q1 - 1.5*IQR to Q3 + 1.5*IQR) to ensure robustness given the small sample size (n=11). Columns with fewer than five remaining runs were flagged as having insufficient data.

Third, reproducibility was quantified by calculating the mean and standard deviation of the aligned retention values for each column. The CV was computed as (StdDev / Mean) * 100 and compared against the 2.0% USP <621> benchmark. Visualizations were generated to display the distribution of retention values relative to the dynamic ±2.0% threshold bands around the column means.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the 11 experimental runs revealed a catastrophic failure in chromatographic reproducibility across all four column types. As illustrated in Figure 1, the distributions of aligned retention values for the CM, DEAE, Q, and SP columns exhibit extreme variability, with the majority of data points falling well outside the acceptable ±2.0% CV threshold bands. The calculated Coefficients of Variation (CV) were exceptionally high, ranging from 58.19% for the Q column to 68.56% for the CM column. These values are drastically higher than the 2.0% benchmark required by USP <621>, indicating that the system is not performing with the necessary precision.

The standard deviations for the retention times (approximately 80–83 minutes) were nearly equal to the mean retention times (approximately 120–140 minutes). This magnitude of variation suggests that the observed spread is not attributable to random analytical noise but rather to systematic errors. Potential sources of this failure include inconsistent peak identification across runs, a failure of the alignment algorithm to correct for systematic drift, or the inadvertent inclusion of non-product peaks due to the missing `chromatography_stage` metadata. The high number of data points aggregated per column (165k–435k) further suggests that the analysis may have treated individual signal points as distinct retention events rather than aggregating them into single run-level values, or that the outlier filtering was insufficient to remove significant systematic shifts. Consequently, the current methodology cannot reliably distinguish the main product peak, and the calculated metrics are statistically invalid for assessing true chromatographic reproducibility.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the data analysis workflow failed to demonstrate acceptable reproducibility for the chromatographic system under investigation. All four column types significantly exceeded the USP <621> benchmark of 2.0% CV, with observed values ranging between 58% and 69%. The extreme variability in retention times points to fundamental issues in the data processing pipeline, likely stemming from systematic errors in peak identification or alignment rather than random noise. The absence of critical metadata, such as `chromatography_stage`, appears to have compromised the ability to isolate the main product peak, leading to the inclusion of irrelevant signal data in the reproducibility calculations. To achieve valid results, the methodology requires refinement, specifically in the peak selection logic to ensure consistent identification of the target analyte and a re-evaluation of the data aggregation strategy to ensure run-level metrics are accurately derived. Until these systematic issues are resolved, the system cannot be considered reliable for quantitative chromatographic analysis.
