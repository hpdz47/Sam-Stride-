#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative analysis of High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) data to assess product purity, abundance, and system stability. The workflow addressed significant data quality challenges, including negative baseline drift and pre-run artifacts, through the application of Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction and rigorous volume filtering. Key findings indicate that while system pressure remained stable and gradient alignment was generally successful for the sampled runs, there are critical concerns regarding product purity. Specifically, 63.7% of collected fractions exhibited high 260/280 ratios (>0.6), suggesting widespread nucleic acid contamination or protein degradation. Furthermore, 1,253 instances of gradient misalignment were detected. A major limitation in the analysis was the absence of `Sample_Code` metadata for 75.7% of the dataset, restricting detailed sample-level aggregation to a small subset. Despite these limitations, the preprocessing pipeline successfully normalized signals and identified process inconsistencies requiring further investigation.

#Introduction 

The objective of this data analysis was to process high-frequency HPLC signals to generate accurate relative abundance estimates and assess product purity. The primary focus involved the quantitative integration of UV signals at 280 nm (`UV_1_280_mAU`) for abundance and 260 nm (`UV_2_260_mAU`) for purity ratio calculations. The dataset presented specific challenges, including physically impossible negative volume values representing pre-run intervals and a consistent negative baseline drift of approximately -7.95 mAU. Additionally, the dataset suffered from incomplete metadata, with 75.7% of rows lacking `Sample_Code`, necessitating a primary analysis strategy based on `run_no` and `column` aggregation. The analysis aimed to resolve these issues through robust preprocessing, calculate purity flags based on integrated peak areas (flagging ratios > 0.6), and verify gradient elution alignment. Furthermore, the study sought to monitor system stability by retaining waste-phase data for pressure analysis, ensuring a comprehensive quality control assessment of the chromatographic process.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a three-step workflow. First, signal preprocessing was performed to remove non-physical data and correct baseline drift. All rows with negative `volume_ml` were excluded. Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction was applied to both UV channels using parameters λ=1e6 and p=1e-4 to eliminate the observed negative drift. Data was stratified by `run_no` and `column`, with rows containing missing `Fraction_unique_ID` retained for system monitoring but excluded from abundance calculations. Second, peak integration and purity calculation were executed. Trapezoidal integration was used to calculate total areas for 280 nm and 260 nm signals. The purity ratio (`Ratio_260_280`) was computed as the ratio of integrated areas. Fractions with ratios exceeding 0.6 were flagged as high-risk. Abundance was normalized by total run area and fraction volume. Gradient alignment was verified by checking if peak apex volumes fell within the 10–90% `Conc_B_%` window. Third, system stability and gradient alignment were visualized using a representative subset of five runs. This involved overlaying corrected UV signals against the gradient profile and monitoring pressure stability (`DeltaC_pressure_MPa`) across the full volume range, including waste phases. Retention time outliers were identified using the Interquartile Range (IQR) method.

#Results and Discussion 

The preprocessing steps successfully addressed the initial data quality issues. As illustrated in Figure 1, the AsLS baseline correction effectively removed the negative drift, with UV signals in pre-run and post-run regions hovering near zero. The gradient profile displayed a consistent step-wise increase, and UV peaks aligned temporally with the expected elution windows (10–90% B concentration), validating the gradient alignment logic for the sampled runs. System pressure, monitored in Panel B of Figure 1, remained relatively stable with only minor fluctuations, indicating no major blockages or leaks during the monitored periods. The visualization also successfully distinguished between collection phases and waste phases (missing `Fraction_unique_ID`), confirming correct data segregation. However, the figure lacks explicit markers for retention time outliers, limiting the visual verification of the IQR-based detection results.

Despite the successful preprocessing, the quantitative analysis revealed significant purity risks. A total of 1,797 fractions, representing 63.7% of the total collected fractions, were flagged for high 260/280 ratios (>0.6). This high prevalence suggests potential nucleic acid contamination or protein degradation across the majority of the dataset. Additionally, 1,253 instances of gradient misalignment were detected, indicating discrepancies between peak apex volumes and the expected gradient window. These findings point to underlying process inconsistencies that require further investigation. A critical limitation affecting the depth of this analysis is the missing `Sample_Code` data for 75.7% of the records. Consequently, detailed sample-level aggregation and correlation of purity issues with specific samples were restricted to only 24% of the dataset. While the generalizability of the stability metrics to the full dataset of 11 runs is supported by the representative visualization, the inability to link specific quality flags to sample identities for the majority of the data hinders a comprehensive root cause analysis.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis workflow successfully implemented robust preprocessing techniques to handle baseline drift and pre-run artifacts, enabling accurate integration of HPLC signals. The visualization in Figure 1 confirms that the system operated with stable pressure and correct gradient alignment for the sampled runs. However, the results highlight critical quality concerns, with nearly two-thirds of fractions showing signs of contamination or degradation based on elevated 260/280 ratios, alongside a significant number of gradient misalignments. The analysis was substantially constrained by the lack of `Sample_Code` metadata for the majority of the dataset, preventing a granular assessment of sample-specific quality. Future efforts should prioritize the recovery or imputation of sample metadata to enable comprehensive correlation between process parameters and product quality. Immediate attention is required to investigate the sources of the high purity flags and gradient misalignments to ensure process consistency.
