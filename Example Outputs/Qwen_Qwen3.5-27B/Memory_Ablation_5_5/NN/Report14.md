#Abstract 

This report details the algorithmic analysis of high-frequency chromatographic data to detect and classify peak asymmetry anomalies indicative of column degradation or matrix effects. Utilizing a three-step workflow involving data preprocessing, USP <621> Tailing Factor calculation, and multi-wavelength correlation analysis, the study identified 56 anomalous peaks across the dataset. The analysis revealed a predominant trend of 'Column Degradation' (90% of sampled anomalies), characterized by severe tailing factors (up to 9.71) and high correlation (>0.92) between 280nm and 260nm signals. A critical methodological deviation was noted where Adaptive Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction was substituted with a Simple Moving Average, potentially influencing the accuracy of symmetry metrics. The findings highlight systemic issues with chromatography media and underscore the necessity of robust baseline correction in high-frequency signal processing.

#Introduction 

Chromatographic separation efficiency is critically dependent on peak symmetry, where deviations such as tailing or fronting can compromise fraction purity and indicate underlying system failures. The primary objective of this analysis was to automate the detection of such anomalies within high-frequency UV time-series data (`UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU`) to distinguish between column degradation and sample matrix effects. The workflow focused on the 'Elution' stage, targeting peaks that deviated from standard quality thresholds defined by a Tailing Factor ($T$) outside the range of 0.8 to 1.8. By leveraging the correlation between 280nm and 260nm absorbance profiles, the analysis aimed to classify anomalies: low correlation (<0.85) suggests matrix effects, while high correlation implies column-specific issues. This automated approach addresses the challenge of processing large volumes of chromatographic data where manual inspection is impractical, providing a targeted diagnostic tool for process optimization.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a structured three-step plan. First, data preprocessing involved filtering the dataset to retain only 'Elution' stage rows, inferring missing stage labels based on concentration and pH thresholds. Gap handling logic bridged discontinuities where volume gaps were <0.5 mL and signal continuity was maintained, resulting in the detection of 526 peaks. Although the plan specified Adaptive Least Squares (AsLS) for baseline correction, the execution utilized a Simple Moving Average due to observed negative absorbance values in the raw data. Peak detection employed Savitzky-Golay smoothing (window=5) and artifact removal via derivative-based spike filtering (slope > 50 mAU/mL). Second, peak symmetry was quantified using the USP <621> Tailing Factor at 5% peak height. Simultaneously, Pearson correlation coefficients were calculated between the 280nm and 260nm profiles within each peak's volume range. Third, anomalies were classified as 'Matrix Effect' or 'Column Degradation' based on the correlation threshold of 0.85. Finally, the run with the highest anomaly count was selected for visualization, highlighting the top five anomalous peaks to illustrate the distribution of deviations.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis identified 56 total anomalies across the dataset, with a significant predominance of 'Column Degradation' cases. As detailed in the markdown analysis, 18 of the first 20 reported anomalies were classified as column degradation, exhibiting Tailing Factors significantly outside the acceptable range. Notable examples include Run 2 on the SP Column (Peak 1) with a $T$ of 9.71 and Run 1 on the SP Column (Peak 3) showing fronting with a $T$ of 0.51. These anomalies consistently demonstrated high correlation coefficients (>0.92) between the 280nm and 260nm signals, confirming that the asymmetry was intrinsic to the column or mobile phase interaction rather than sample heterogeneity. In contrast, only two instances in the sample were classified as 'Matrix Effect' (Run 1, CM Peak 3 and Run 2, Q Peak 8), distinguished by lower correlation coefficients of 0.8467 and 0.7716, respectively.

Figure 1 visualizes the output of the aggregation step, presenting the baseline-corrected `UV_1_280_mAU` signal for the run with the highest total anomaly count. The plot successfully isolates the top five anomalous peaks, using distinct shading to differentiate between 'Matrix Effect' and 'Column Degradation'. The visualization confirms that the preprocessing steps, including spike filtering and peak segmentation, effectively isolated peaks deviating from standard symmetry thresholds. However, the figure alone does not display the specific numerical Tailing Factors or the secondary 260nm trace used for correlation analysis; these quantitative justifications are derived from the accompanying text reports. The high frequency of 'Column Degradation' flags across different column types (CM, DEAE, Q, SP) suggests systemic issues with the chromatography media or mobile phase compatibility. Furthermore, the substitution of the planned AsLS baseline correction with a Simple Moving Average represents a methodological limitation. Without the robust non-negativity enforcement of AsLS, residual baseline drift may have biased the calculated Tailing Factors, potentially inflating the number of false positives for column degradation. Despite this, the data quality appears sufficient for peak detection, with the smoothing applied being critical for defining peak boundaries accurately in the high-frequency raw data.

#Conclusion 

The automated analysis successfully identified and classified chromatographic anomalies, revealing a systemic prevalence of column degradation across multiple runs and column types. The extreme tailing factors observed indicate severe peak distortion that likely compromises fraction purity. While the classification logic effectively distinguished between matrix effects and column issues using multi-wavelength correlation, the deviation from the planned AsLS baseline correction method introduces uncertainty regarding the precision of the symmetry metrics. Future analyses should prioritize the implementation of robust AsLS algorithms to ensure non-negative baseline correction, thereby validating the severity of the reported column degradation. The visualization in Figure 1 serves as an effective diagnostic tool for identifying critical deviations, but must be interpreted alongside the quantitative data to confirm the root cause of the anomalies.
