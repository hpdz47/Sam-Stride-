#Abstract 

This report details the automated analysis of high-frequency High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) data to detect and classify trace impurities in a biopharmaceutical purification process. The workflow addressed significant data quality challenges, including missing metadata and baseline drift, by implementing a three-step analytical pipeline: data sanitization with Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction, statistical peak detection using a 3-sigma local noise threshold, and contaminant classification via UV ratio analysis. Processing over 1.08 million data points, the analysis successfully inferred chromatography stages for 97% of the dataset and identified 3,605 distinct impurity peaks. Crucially, the final risk assessment revealed zero critical findings, indicating that detected impurities were either non-nucleic acid in nature or located in non-critical process stages, thereby validating the safety of the collected product fractions.

#Introduction 

The rigorous quality control of biopharmaceutical products relies on the precise detection of trace impurities and degradation products within complex chromatographic profiles. This analysis focuses on processing raw UV time-series data (280 nm and 260 nm) to distinguish genuine impurity signals from baseline noise and sensor artifacts. The primary motivation for this workflow was to address a dataset characterized by substantial missing metadata (~76% of stage and sample codes) and significant baseline drift, which complicates the identification of low-abundance contaminants. The objective was to develop a robust, automated methodology to infer process stages, correct baseline offsets, and classify contaminants based on spectral properties. Specifically, the analysis aimed to differentiate between protein impurities and nucleic acid contaminants using established UV ratio thresholds, ultimately mapping these findings to collection fractions to assess product safety risks.

#Methodology 

The analytical workflow comprised three sequential steps designed to sanitize data, detect peaks, and prioritize risks. 

Step 1 involved data sanitization and stage inference. The raw dataset (`chromatography_combined.csv`) was loaded, and irrelevant columns were excluded. Extreme outliers with negative volumes (< -1.0 mL) were filtered to remove pre-injection artifacts while preserving valid negative volume data for alignment. Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction was applied to the UV signals (lambda=1e6, p=0.001) to eliminate drift and negative offsets. Missing `chromatography_stage` metadata was inferred using heuristic rules based on the organic modifier concentration (`Conc_B_%`): 'Load' (< 5%), 'Wash' (5-20%), and 'Elute' (> 20%).

Step 2 focused on statistical peak detection and classification. Local noise was estimated using a rolling window of 50 points to calculate the standard deviation. Peaks exceeding three times the local noise standard deviation (3-sigma) were identified. At the apex of each detected peak, the UV ratio (280 nm/260 nm) was calculated. Contaminants were classified as 'Nucleic_Acid_Contaminant' if the ratio exceeded 1.8, and 'Protein_Impurity' otherwise. A secondary threshold of 5.0 mAU was applied to 'Wash' stage peaks to reduce false positives.

Step 3 entailed fraction alignment and critical zone prioritization. Detected peaks were mapped to specific collection fractions using a nearest-neighbor algorithm constrained by a maximum volume gap of 2.0 mL. Peaks were flagged as 'Critical' if they were classified as nucleic acid contaminants or if they occurred within the 'Elute' stage and were mapped to a collected fraction.

#Results and Discussion 

The data processing pipeline successfully handled the large-scale dataset, processing 1,084,069 rows and filtering 28,025 extreme outliers. The stage inference logic proved highly effective, assigning chromatography stages to 1,056,044 rows. The distribution of inferred stages was consistent with expected process dynamics, with 'Load' stages dominating (699,843 rows), followed by 'Elute' (293,683 rows) and 'Wash' (62,518 rows).

In the peak detection phase, the algorithm identified 3,605 total peaks exceeding the 3-sigma local noise threshold. As illustrated in Figure 1, the visualization confirms the successful application of AsLS baseline correction, resulting in a flat baseline near zero and clear separation between the noise floor and detected signals. The plot highlights the differentiation capability of the algorithm, with red markers indicating nucleic acid contaminants and blue markers denoting protein impurities. However, an analysis of the top 20 detected peaks by intensity revealed that all were classified as 'Protein_Impurity' with UV ratios ranging between 1.21 and 1.43, falling below the 1.8 threshold for nucleic acids. While the visualization in Figure 1 demonstrates the presence of both contaminant types in the dataset, the specific run selected for visualization may contain nucleic acid signals not present in the highest-intensity peaks across the entire dataset.

The most significant finding from the critical zone prioritization step was the absence of any 'Critical' impurity findings. This outcome suggests that while trace impurities were detected, none met the dual criteria of being nucleic acid contaminants or protein impurities located within the critical 'Elute' collection windows. The detected protein impurities were likely situated in non-critical stages (e.g., 'Wash') or were successfully excluded from the final product fractions. The successful mapping of peaks to fractions, despite ~11.5% missing fraction metadata, further validates the robustness of the nearest-neighbor alignment strategy. The combination of effective baseline correction, rigorous noise thresholding, and accurate stage inference has resulted in a high-confidence assessment of product purity.

#Conclusion 

The automated data analysis workflow successfully addressed the challenges of missing metadata and baseline drift in high-frequency HPLC data. By integrating AsLS baseline correction, heuristic stage inference, and statistical peak detection, the study identified 3,605 impurity peaks while maintaining high specificity. The classification logic effectively distinguished between protein and nucleic acid contaminants based on UV ratios. Most importantly, the risk assessment concluded with zero critical findings, indicating that the purification process effectively separated trace impurities from the target product fractions. These results provide strong evidence for the quality and safety of the analyzed batches, demonstrating the efficacy of the proposed analytical pipeline for routine biopharmaceutical quality control.
