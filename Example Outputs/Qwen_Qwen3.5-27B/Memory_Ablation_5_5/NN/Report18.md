#Abstract 

This report details the comprehensive analysis of high-frequency chromatography signals across 11 manufacturing runs and four distinct column types. The primary objective was to algorithmically detect, quantify, and characterize chromatographic peaks while assessing data quality through gradient linearity verification and nucleic acid contamination screening. Utilizing Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) for baseline correction and derivative-based detection with noise-adaptive thresholds, the workflow successfully processed raw time-series data. Key findings indicate that all assessed gradient profiles met the strict linearity requirement ($R^2 > 0.99$). However, the analysis identified a significant volume of contamination flags, exceeding 575,000 instances, predominantly concentrated in Run 16 on the CM column. Additionally, hardware outliers were detected across multiple runs and columns, suggesting potential instrument variability. These results provide a critical foundation for quality control, highlighting specific runs requiring further investigation regarding nucleic acid contamination and hardware performance.

#Introduction 

Chromatography is a cornerstone technique in biomanufacturing for the purification of biomolecules, relying on the precise separation of components based on their interaction with a stationary phase. The integrity of this process depends heavily on the accurate interpretation of UV absorbance signals, specifically at 280 nm (protein) and 260 nm (nucleic acids). In this study, we analyzed raw sensor streams from 11 distinct manufacturing runs, where the elution volume served as the time proxy. The dataset presented several challenges, including missing metadata for 75.7% of rows, corrupted volume columns, and baseline drift indicated by negative UV values. The primary motivation for this analysis was to establish a robust, automated workflow to detect peaks, verify gradient linearity, and flag potential contamination events without manual intervention. By stratifying data by column type, we aimed to isolate hardware variability and ensure that the detected peaks and calculated ratios reflected true process deviations rather than instrumental artifacts. This report outlines the methodology employed to address these challenges and discusses the critical findings regarding gradient performance and data quality.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three distinct steps. First, signal preprocessing and peak detection were performed on the raw time-series data. Corrupted columns ending in '_ml' were explicitly excluded, and negative volume values were trimmed to prevent baseline skew. Baseline drift was corrected using Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) with fixed parameters ($\lambda=10^6$, $p=10^{-4}$). Following correction, peaks were detected using a derivative-based method with a noise-adaptive threshold set at three times the standard deviation ($3\sigma$) of the baseline-corrected signal. Gaussian functions were fitted to these peaks to extract centroids, heights, and widths. An anomaly flag was triggered if the peak count exceeded 50 per run. Second, gradient linearity and UV ratio stratification were assessed. The relationship between peak centroid volume and Buffer B concentration was evaluated via linear regression, classifying profiles as 'linear' only if $R^2 > 0.99$. Simultaneously, UV260/UV280 ratios were calculated at peak centroids. Ratios were stratified by column type, and outliers deviating more than two standard deviations from the column-specific mean were flagged as potential nucleic acid contamination. Finally, consistency checks were conducted to identify hardware outliers using the Interquartile Range (IQR) on peak metrics and to summarize the dynamic range of UV signals across runs. All fraction mapping excluded rows missing a 'Fraction_unique_ID', while full chromatogram integration included all valid data points.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the chromatography data yielded significant insights into both the physical performance of the gradient system and the chemical purity of the eluted fractions. As illustrated in Figure 1, the gradient linearity verification confirmed that the relationship between elution volume and buffer concentration was highly consistent across the dataset. The scatter plot in the top subplot of Figure 1 demonstrates that data points are tightly clustered along the regression line, visually confirming an $R^2$ value that meets the stringent threshold of $> 0.99$. This indicates that the gradient pumps and mixing systems operated within expected parameters, with zero linearity failures recorded in the final consistency checks. Consequently, the retention times of the detected peaks can be reliably correlated with the buffer composition. However, the assessment of product purity revealed critical issues. The bottom subplot of Figure 1 presents boxplots of the UV260/UV280 ratios for each of the four columns. While the majority of data points fall within the expected interquartile ranges, the plot clearly highlights numerous outliers extending beyond the whiskers. These outliers correspond to the >575,000 contamination flags identified in the text-based analysis. The concentration of these anomalies in Run 16 on the CM column, with specific ratios deviating significantly (e.g., 1.314 for waste fractions), strongly suggests nucleic acid contamination or signal anomalies specific to that run. Furthermore, the analysis identified hardware outliers in Runs 1, 4, and 17 across DEAE, Q, CM, and SP columns, characterized by specific UV signal ranges that deviate from the norm. These findings suggest that while the gradient delivery system is robust, specific runs and columns exhibit variability that may compromise product quality or indicate instrument drift. The integration of peak detection metrics with these ratio analyses provides a comprehensive view of run quality, isolating specific instances where process deviations occurred.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the automated data analysis workflow successfully processed complex chromatography datasets to quantify peak characteristics and assess data quality. The methodology, combining AsLS baseline correction, noise-adaptive peak detection, and rigorous statistical stratification, proved effective in handling data quality issues such as baseline drift and missing metadata. The results confirm that the gradient delivery system performed with high linearity across all analyzed runs, satisfying the $R^2 > 0.99$ criterion. However, the analysis uncovered a substantial number of contamination events, particularly in Run 16, and identified hardware outliers that warrant further investigation. These findings underscore the importance of continuous monitoring of UV ratios and hardware performance to ensure product purity. Future work should focus on a deeper investigation of the flagged runs to determine the root cause of the contamination and to refine the outlier detection thresholds for hardware variability. The structured approach presented here offers a scalable solution for quality control in high-throughput chromatography manufacturing.
