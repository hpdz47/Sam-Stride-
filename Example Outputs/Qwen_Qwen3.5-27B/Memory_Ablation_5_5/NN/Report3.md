#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative assessment of baseline stability, noise thresholds, and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) across four chromatography columns (CM, DEAE, Q, SP) and two UV detection channels (280 nm and 260 nm). The analysis aimed to distinguish genuine analyte peaks from baseline artifacts by implementing a rigorous offset correction strategy using pre-injection data and calculating noise thresholds via rolling standard deviations. Results indicate significant unit-specific variability, with the Q column exhibiting the highest noise floor and the SP column showing unique positive baseline offsets. While the 260 nm channel generally demonstrated higher SNR values, suggesting robust peak detection, the correlation analysis between baseline drift and conductivity revealed methodological anomalies for the DEAE and Q columns, necessitating further review. These findings provide a critical foundation for optimizing detection limits and identifying potential column degradation.

#Introduction 

In high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC), the accurate quantification of analytes relies heavily on the stability of the baseline and the precision of noise threshold calculations. Variability in baseline drift, often induced by buffer conductivity changes or column degradation, can lead to false positives or the masking of low-concentration analytes. This study focuses on evaluating the performance of four distinct chromatography units (CM, DEAE, Q, SP) across 11 experimental runs. The primary objective was to establish robust noise floors and offset corrections to differentiate true signal peaks from background noise, specifically targeting the 3σ (Limit of Detection) and 5σ (Limit of Quantification) thresholds. Furthermore, the analysis sought to investigate the relationship between baseline drift and buffer conductivity, a secondary factor known to influence detector stability. By stratifying data by column and run, this work aims to identify unit-specific performance metrics and ensure the reliability of downstream peak detection algorithms.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow comprised three sequential steps. First, baseline stability and noise thresholds were calculated by isolating pre-injection data (volume < 0 mL, excluding the first 5 scans) and constant gradient regions (Conc_B% within ±0.5% of 0.0 or 100.0). Global offsets were determined per column and signal channel, followed by the computation of rolling standard deviations (window size = 25) to derive 3σ and 5σ noise thresholds. Second, a column-stratified SNR analysis was performed. Local offsets were recalculated for each run, and valid post-injection regions were defined. Peaks were identified where the signal exceeded 3σ and 5σ thresholds relative to local noise. The SNR was calculated as the ratio of peak height to local noise standard deviation, aggregated by column across all 11 runs. Finally, a conductivity correlation analysis was conducted to assess baseline drift. Linear regression was applied to constant gradient regions to determine drift rates (mAU/mL), which were then correlated with log-transformed conductivity values using Pearson correlation. This multi-step approach ensured that gradient transitions were excluded and that noise estimation was temporally consistent.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of baseline parameters revealed significant heterogeneity across the four chromatography columns. The Q column exhibited the highest noise levels, with 3σ thresholds approximately three to four times higher than those observed for the CM and SP columns, suggesting a compromised Limit of Detection for this unit. Conversely, the SP column displayed unique positive global offsets (~1.4 mAU), contrasting with the negative offsets observed in the other columns, which may indicate distinct calibration drift or baseline instability. 

As illustrated in Figure 1, the SNR analysis demonstrates that the UV_2_260_mAU channel consistently yields higher mean SNR values compared to the UV_1_280_mAU channel across most columns and runs. This aligns with the dataset's higher variability in the 260 nm channel, indicating that signal peaks in this channel are proportionally larger relative to the calculated local noise. The error bars in Figure 1 highlight run-to-run consistency; wider bars in specific columns suggest greater variability in peak detection or baseline stability, potentially indicative of column fouling or degradation. 

However, the conductivity correlation analysis presented critical methodological concerns. For the DEAE and Q columns, the average p-values for the correlation between drift and conductivity were exactly 1.0, and correlation coefficients were exactly 1.0 or -1.0. These statistically impossible values for real-world data suggest a data processing error, likely stemming from the exclusion of variable data points during the constant gradient filtering step. In contrast, the CM and SP columns showed realistic, albeit non-significant, correlations, indicating that baseline drift in these units is only weakly associated with conductivity changes. Consequently, while the SNR results provide a clear view of detection performance, the drift analysis for half the dataset requires immediate methodological review before definitive conclusions regarding buffer-induced offsets can be drawn.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully quantified baseline stability and SNR across four chromatography columns, revealing distinct unit-specific performance characteristics. The Q column's elevated noise floor and the SP column's positive offsets highlight the necessity of individualized calibration and monitoring for each unit. The UV_2_260_mAU channel proved superior for peak detection, offering higher SNR values despite higher absolute variability. While the primary SNR analysis provides robust metrics for peak identification, the conductivity correlation results for the DEAE and Q columns are compromised by statistical anomalies, likely due to data filtering artifacts. Future work must address these methodological inconsistencies to accurately assess the impact of buffer conductivity on baseline drift. Overall, the established noise thresholds and SNR metrics offer a validated framework for optimizing detection limits and identifying early signs of column degradation in this chromatography workflow.
