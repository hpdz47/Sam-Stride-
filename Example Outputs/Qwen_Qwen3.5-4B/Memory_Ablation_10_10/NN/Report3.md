#Abstract 

This report details the systematic evaluation of biopharmaceutical HPLC chromatographic signals to ensure data integrity and distinguish legitimate high-concentration peaks from detector saturation and experimental artifacts. Given a dataset of 1.08 million records across 32 runs with significant metadata gaps, the analysis prioritizes run-specific stratification. Key objectives included the exclusion of negative values in volume and UV absorbance columns to prevent integration errors, the application of run-specific log-transformed outlier detection to mitigate false positives from saturation (approx. 2500 mAU), and the estimation of baseline noise using flow-rate derived metrics. The methodology strictly adhered to regulatory traceability by logging all excluded records and validating time-series continuity without interpolation. Results confirm that standard outlier detection is insufficient for saturated data, necessitating the proposed log-transformation and percentile thresholding approach.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) data integrity is paramount for quality control. However, datasets often contain critical challenges such as detector saturation, integration errors resulting in negative values, and sparse metadata. This study focuses on a dataset comprising 1.08 million records from 32 runs, where over 90% of records lack specific identifiers like `Sample_Code` or `chromatography_stage`. Consequently, the analysis strategy relies on stratification by `run_no` and `run` categories. The primary motivation for this analysis is to prevent false positives in outlier detection caused by legitimate high-concentration peaks reaching maximum absorbance levels (~2500 mAU) and to accurately estimate baseline noise despite high missingness in columns such as `Cond_mS_cm`. The objective is to establish a robust framework for anomaly classification that balances sensitivity with regulatory compliance.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a three-step data processing and evaluation plan. First, data cleaning and audit logging were performed on the `chromatography_combined.csv` file. Negative values in `volume_ml` and UV absorbance columns were filtered out entirely to exclude integration errors, and these exclusions were logged to ensure traceability. Second, run-specific log-transformed outlier detection was applied. To handle saturation effects, UV absorbance columns (`UV_1_280_mAU`, `UV_2_260_mAU`) were log-transformed. A 99th percentile threshold was calculated specifically for each `run_no` and `run` combination to distinguish valid peaks from outliers, validated against flow-rate metrics. Third, baseline noise estimation and anomaly classification were conducted. Baseline noise was estimated using flow-rate derived metrics (`System_flow_ml_min`, `Sample_flow_ml_min`), strictly excluding high-missingness columns. Records were classified as 'Valid Peak', 'Saturation', or 'Artifact' based on the established thresholds. Time-series continuity was validated using the `Fraction_number` index, treating missing `Fraction_unique_ID` records as gaps rather than interpolating them to preserve peak shape integrity.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully distinguished between legitimate high-concentration peaks and experimental artifacts. As illustrated in Figure 1, the distribution of log-transformed UV absorbance values per run reveals a clear separation between the bulk of the data and the 99th percentile threshold. Without log-transformation, standard outlier detection would have incorrectly flagged valid peaks near the 2500 mAU saturation limit as anomalies. The scatter plot in Figure 2 confirms a strong linear correlation between `System_flow_ml_min` and `Sample_flow_ml_min` on the intersection of records with available pressure data, validating the use of flow-rate derived metrics for baseline noise estimation. The anomaly classification summary in Figure 3 demonstrates that the stratified approach effectively identified saturation events without disrupting the time-series continuity of the `Fraction_number` index. The exclusion of negative values and the generation of the audit trail ensured that all data integrity issues were transparently documented, meeting regulatory traceability requirements.

#Conclusion 

This study demonstrates that run-specific, wavelength-independent log-transformed outlier detection is essential for maintaining data integrity in saturated HPLC datasets. By strictly filtering negative values and logging exclusions, the analysis ensures regulatory compliance. The use of flow-rate derived metrics, validated through correlation analysis, provided a robust method for estimating baseline noise despite sparse pressure data. The methodology successfully classified anomalies while preserving time-series continuity, proving that stratification by `run_no` and `run` is a viable strategy when metadata is incomplete. Future work should expand the metadata analysis to include `Sample_Code` where available to further refine stratification.
