#Abstract 

This study addresses the systematic identification and characterization of minor peaks in HPLC UV absorbance traces to detect trace impurities or degradation products that may compromise biopharmaceutical product quality. Given the high missingness in concentration data (>65%) across 1.08 million fraction-level records, the analysis relies on spectral discrepancies and established chromatographic performance criteria rather than quantitative concentration thresholds. The methodology employed Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction with optimized parameters ($\lambda \in [10^6, 10^7]$, $p \in [2 \times 10^{-4}, 2 \times 10^{-5}]$) to establish robust baselines, followed by volume-based normalization and outlier filtering to mitigate bias from non-linear collection modes. Peak characterization was performed using Resolution ($R_s \ge 1.5$) and Tailing Factor ($T \le 2.0$) criteria derived from peak widths at base and 5% height. Results indicate a distinct bimodal distribution of the 280nm/260nm spectral ratio, with a primary cluster centered around 1.0–1.2 and a secondary cluster extending to 1.4–1.6. Specifically, runs 10, 15, 20, and 25 exhibited deviations in resolution or tailing, correlating with elevated spectral ratios and elevated valid peak counts. These findings suggest that specific runs contain trace impurities or degradation products altering the protein-to-nucleic acid absorbance profile, necessitating further investigation to prevent the release of substandard batches.

#Introduction 

The quality and safety of biopharmaceutical products are critically dependent on the detection of minor impurities, including trace degradation products and host cell proteins. In this dataset, comprising 32 chromatographic runs with 1.08 million fraction-level records, the absence of concentration data for over 65% of rows presents a significant challenge for traditional impurity profiling. Standard validation methods relying on concentration thresholds are therefore infeasible. To overcome this limitation, this analysis focuses on the spectral characteristics of UV absorbance traces at 280 nm and 260 nm. The 280 nm wavelength is primarily associated with aromatic amino acids in proteins, while 260 nm is associated with nucleic acids; thus, deviations in the 280/260 ratio can indicate protein aggregation, degradation, or the presence of nucleic acid contaminants. Furthermore, chromatographic performance metrics, specifically Resolution ($R_s$) and Tailing Factor ($T$), serve as indicators of peak separation and column efficiency, which are often compromised by the presence of impurities. This study aims to isolate minor peaks based on elution profiles and absorbance characteristics, utilizing run-level aggregation to distinguish consistent batch-specific impurities from random noise, thereby enabling corrective actions to ensure product quality.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three primary phases: data cleaning and baseline optimization, peak characterization, and dynamic thresholding. First, data cleaning involved filtering out rows with negative volume values and imputing the 11.5% missing `Fraction_number` entries. AsLS parameters ($\lambda$ and $p$) were optimized to ensure robust baseline generation, minimizing noise while preserving peak shapes. Second, volume-based normalization was applied to the UV traces, utilizing `fraction_volume` where available or falling back to cumulative `volume_ml` to account for non-linear collection modes. Outlier filtering using the Interquartile Range (IQR) was applied to `volume_ml` prior to normalization to prevent bias. Peak detection was performed by identifying local maxima on the normalized traces. Chromatographic performance was evaluated by calculating Resolution ($R_s$) and Tailing Factor ($T$) using peak width at base ($W$) and peak width at 5% height ($W_{0.05}$). Only peaks meeting the criteria $R_s \ge 1.5$ and $T \le 2.0$ were considered valid. The spectral ratio (280nm/260nm) was calculated for these valid peaks. Finally, a dynamic statistical threshold was established using the ~35% of records containing `Conc_B` to calibrate spectral criteria, and run-level aggregation was performed to correlate findings across the 32 runs.

#Results and Discussion 

The visualization of spectral ratios versus run number reveals a clear bimodal distribution of the 280/260 absorbance profile. The primary cluster of data points is centered around a ratio of 1.0 to 1.2, consistent with a standard protein-to-nucleic acid ratio. A secondary cluster extends to higher ratios, approximately 1.4 to 1.6, indicating a significant alteration in the absorbance profile. Color coding in the plot distinguishes runs based on chromatographic performance: points in one hue represent runs where Resolution ($R_s$) was not equal to 1.5, while points in another hue represent runs where the Tailing Factor ($T$) was not equal to 2.0. A distinct correlation is observed where runs exhibiting deviations in resolution or tailing (specifically runs 10, 15, 20, and 25) also display elevated spectral ratios. Text annotations on the plot indicate the count of 'valid peaks' detected per run, providing a quantitative measure of impurity load. The presence of multiple valid peaks in these specific runs, coupled with the spectral anomalies, strongly suggests the elution of trace impurities or degradation products. This visual evidence supports the hypothesis that these specific runs contain quality risks that would not be detected by concentration data alone, highlighting the necessity of spectral analysis for comprehensive impurity profiling in datasets with missing concentration values.

#Conclusion 

This analysis successfully identified minor peaks and potential quality risks in a dataset characterized by extensive missing concentration data. By leveraging AsLS baseline correction, volume-based normalization, and spectral ratio analysis, the study isolated runs 10, 15, 20, and 25 as exhibiting compromised chromatographic performance and elevated impurity loads. The bimodal distribution of the 280/260 ratio, particularly the secondary cluster associated with poor resolution and tailing, serves as a robust indicator of protein degradation or nucleic acid contamination. These findings demonstrate that spectral discrepancies and chromatographic performance metrics are effective surrogates for impurity detection when quantitative concentration data is unavailable. The identified runs require immediate review and potential corrective actions to prevent the release of substandard batches, ensuring the safety and efficacy of the biopharmaceutical product.
