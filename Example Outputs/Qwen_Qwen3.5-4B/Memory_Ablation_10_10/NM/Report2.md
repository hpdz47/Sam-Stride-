#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic peak characteristics derived from a dataset of 1.08 million records to assess product quality and process stability in biopharmaceutical manufacturing. The primary objective was to identify deviations in peak shape quality that may indicate process instability or column degradation, while rigorously accounting for significant data missingness. Analysis revealed that peak shape metrics, specifically volume boundaries, were missing for approximately 12% of records, restricting the quality profile analysis to fractions with detectable UV absorbance. Furthermore, process parameters such as conductivity and pH were missing for up to 65% of records, necessitating a restricted subset for correlation analysis to avoid selection bias. The methodology involved normalizing cumulative volume offsets to establish a zero baseline, calculating peak width and height, and deriving Tailing Factors and Theoretical Plates against USP <621> criteria (Tailing Factor: 0.8–1.2; Theoretical Plates: ≥2000). The results indicate that the analyzed subset exhibits high plate counts but variable tailing behavior, with a concentration of data points in the upper-left quadrant of the quality profile. Outlier detection identified 12% of the analyzed volume records as missing or non-compliant, suggesting potential process variability or data acquisition issues. The study concludes that while the current dataset demonstrates robust peak capacity, the high rate of missing process parameters limits the ability to fully correlate peak shape deviations with specific operational conditions, highlighting a need for improved data capture during manufacturing runs.

#Introduction 

In biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the integrity of chromatographic peaks is a critical indicator of product purity and process consistency. This study focuses on the quantitative assessment of peak shape characteristics, including width, height, and symmetry, extracted from the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset. The analysis aims to evaluate the performance of 32 experimental runs against established quality standards, specifically the United States Pharmacopeia (USP) <621> criteria. These criteria define an acceptable Tailing Factor range of 0.8 to 1.2 and a minimum Theoretical Plate count of 2000. The significance of this analysis lies in its ability to detect subtle process instabilities, such as column degradation or sample impurities, that might otherwise go unnoticed. However, the dataset presents substantial challenges regarding data completeness. Approximately 125,000 records (12%) lack precise volume boundary data, and process parameters like conductivity and pH are missing for a significant portion of the dataset (up to 65%). This missingness introduces a risk of selection bias, particularly when attempting to correlate peak metrics with process conditions. Therefore, this report outlines a rigorous methodology that normalizes volume data, calculates peak metrics for non-missing records, and restricts parameter correlations to valid subsets to ensure the reliability of the findings. The ultimate goal is to generate a comprehensive quality profile that accurately reflects the manufacturing process while transparently addressing the limitations imposed by data gaps.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three primary phases: data filtering and metric extraction, peak shape quality validation, and process parameter correlation. First, the raw data from `chromatography_combined.csv` underwent rigorous filtering. Records were retained only where `fraction_volume_ml_min_precise` was not null. To address baseline noise, the `volume_ml` variable was normalized by subtracting the minimum observed value (approximately -33.8 mL), effectively setting the baseline to zero. Peak width was calculated as the difference between `max_precise` and `min_precise`, and peak height was determined by the maximum `UV_1_280_mAU` value. Records with negative widths were excluded to ensure data integrity. Second, peak shape quality was validated against USP <621> standards. The Tailing Factor (T) was calculated using the formula T = Width / (2 * Height), and Theoretical Plates (N) were estimated using N = 16 * (Retention_Time / Width)^2, with retention time approximated by the fraction volume. Records were flagged as outliers if T > 2.0 or N < 2000. These metrics were aggregated using the local `Fraction_number` counter for each run to generate run-specific statistics. Third, process parameter correlations were quantified. To mitigate selection bias, correlations between process parameters (Conductivity, pH, flow rates) and peak metrics were calculated exclusively for records where these parameters were not null. Pearson correlation coefficients were computed, and the mean width and height were compared between 'Missing' and 'Present' subsets to quantify any inherent bias. All visualizations and statistical outputs were generated to provide a clear overview of the 32 experimental runs, ensuring that the analysis remained focused on the subset of data capable of supporting robust quality assessments.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the chromatographic data reveals a distinct pattern in peak shape quality across the 32 experimental runs. As illustrated in Figure 1, the scatter plot of Tailing Factor versus Theoretical Plates shows a heavy concentration of data points in the upper-left quadrant, characterized by high Theoretical Plate counts (N ≥ 2000) and variable Tailing Factors. This distribution indicates that the fractions with detectable peaks generally exhibit excellent column efficiency, a hallmark of a well-maintained chromatographic system. However, the variable tailing suggests that while peak capacity is robust, symmetry is subject to some degree of run-to-run fluctuation. The shaded polygon in Figure 1 delineates the acceptable quality region defined by USP <621> criteria (Tailing Factor: 0.8–1.2; N ≥ 2000). The majority of the analyzed points fall within or near this region, confirming that the process generally meets quality standards for peak shape. However, the presence of outliers and the specific distribution of points outside the ideal T range (0.8–1.2) warrant further investigation. The annotation on the figure indicates that approximately 12% of the records are outliers or missing critical data. This missingness is significant; it implies that nearly one in eight analyzed fractions lacks the precise volume boundary data required for accurate metric calculation. This limitation arises from the initial data filtering step, where records with null `fraction_volume_ml_min_precise` were excluded. Consequently, the quality profile presented in Figure 1 is not representative of the entire 1.08 million record dataset but rather a subset of fractions with significant UV absorbance. This selection bias is further compounded by the high missingness in process parameters (up to 65% for conductivity and pH). The inability to correlate peak shape deviations with specific operational conditions in these cases limits the diagnostic power of the current analysis. The concentration of points in the upper-left quadrant also suggests that the process may be operating at the edge of efficiency, where minor variations in flow rate or sample load could lead to significant changes in tailing behavior. The absence of points in the lower-right quadrant (low N, low T) likely indicates that the most degraded peaks were filtered out during the initial cleaning step or simply do not occur in this specific manufacturing context. Overall, while the data confirms that the process maintains high plate counts, the variability in tailing and the substantial data gaps highlight the need for improved data capture protocols to fully link peak shape anomalies to root cause process parameters.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully evaluated the chromatographic peak characteristics of 32 experimental runs, providing a comprehensive quality profile against USP <621> standards. The analysis confirmed that the manufacturing process generally maintains high column efficiency, with Theoretical Plate counts exceeding the 2000 threshold for the vast majority of fractions. However, the results also highlight significant challenges regarding data completeness, with approximately 12% of volume boundary records missing and up to 65% of process parameters unavailable. These data gaps restrict the scope of the quality analysis to fractions with detectable peaks and limit the ability to correlate peak shape deviations with specific process conditions. The scatter plot in Figure 1 demonstrates that while peak capacity is robust, tailing behavior exhibits variability, suggesting potential sensitivity to process parameters that were not fully captured in the dataset. The methodology employed, including volume normalization and restricted correlation analysis, ensured that the findings were not skewed by missing data, although the inherent selection bias remains a limitation. Future work should focus on improving data capture during manufacturing to reduce missingness in volume boundaries and process parameters. This will enable a more robust correlation analysis, allowing for the precise identification of root causes for peak shape deviations. Until then, the current quality profile serves as a baseline indicator of process stability, confirming high efficiency while flagging the need for enhanced data integrity in future runs.
