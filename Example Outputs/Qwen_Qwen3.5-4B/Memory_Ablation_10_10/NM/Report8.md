#Abstract 

This study addresses the challenge of approximating retention time reproducibility in a chromatography workflow where explicit timestamps are absent and flow rate data is highly incomplete (~66% missing). The primary objective was to determine whether observed variability in fraction volumes stems from random noise or systematic process drift, which would compromise biopharmaceutical product quality. To achieve this, we implemented a data cleaning protocol to remove invalid entries and verified unit consistency. We then reconstructed cumulative volume profiles by imputing missing flow rates using run-specific means and calculating the slope of cumulative volume versus fraction number as a proxy for flow rate. Visual analysis of 11 distinct runs revealed heterogeneous slopes, suggesting variable flow conditions. Statistical evaluation using Z-score thresholds classified runs as either 'stable' (within 2 standard deviations of the mean) or 'drifting' (exceeding the threshold). The results indicate that while the imputation strategy successfully reconstructed linear trends, significant inter-run variance exists, pointing towards potential systematic drift rather than mere random noise. These findings underscore the critical need for robust flow rate monitoring in automated chromatographic systems to ensure consistent separation conditions.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the reproducibility of chromatographic separations is paramount for ensuring product quality and consistency. Retention time, a critical parameter governing separation efficiency, is typically derived from explicit timestamps and flow rates. However, in this specific dataset, the absence of an explicit time column necessitates an alternative approach to estimating retention time reproducibility. Furthermore, the variable `System_flow_ml_min` exhibits a high missingness rate of approximately 66%, and the categorical variable `Sample_Code` has a missingness rate of ~76%, rendering sample-specific analyses infeasible. These data quality issues present a significant hurdle for standard statistical comparisons. The motivation for this analysis is to approximate retention time reproducibility by evaluating the consistency of cumulative volume profiles across 11 unique runs. By treating the slope of cumulative volume versus fraction number as a proxy for flow rate, we aim to distinguish between random experimental noise and systematic process drift. Identifying systematic drift is crucial, as it indicates a failure in maintaining the consistent separation conditions required for high-quality biopharmaceutical products. This study focuses on the inter-run variance of these reconstructed profiles to assess the stability of the chromatographic process under the constraints of the available data.

#Methodology 

The analysis was conducted in three sequential steps to ensure data integrity and accurate reconstruction of process metrics. First, a data cleaning and integrity verification phase was performed on the `chromatography_combined.csv` file. This involved filtering out negative values in the `volume_ml` column to correct potential entry errors, while excluding the `Sample_Code` variable from filtering due to its high categorical missingness. Unit consistency between `fraction_volume` and `volume_ml` was confirmed. Second, a flow rate imputation and cumulative profile reconstruction phase was executed. Missing values in `System_flow_ml_min` were imputed using the run-specific mean calculated in the first step. For each of the 11 runs, the slope of `volume_ml` versus `Fraction_number` was calculated via linear regression. This slope was interpreted as the actual flow rate for that run, annotated directly on the visualizations. Third, an inter-run variance analysis was conducted to differentiate between noise and drift. The slopes from all runs were analyzed to compute the mean and standard deviation. A Z-score threshold of 2.0 was established; runs with slopes within 2 standard deviations of the mean were classified as 'stable' (random noise), while those exceeding this threshold were classified as exhibiting 'systematic drift'. This statistical approach avoided the use of K-Means clustering, which was deemed inappropriate for univariate scalar comparison in this context.

#Results and Discussion 

The visual analysis presented in Figure 1 illustrates the cumulative volume profiles for all 11 experimental runs. The plot, titled 'Cumulative Volume Profiles by Run (Slope = Flow Rate)', displays 11 distinct trajectories representing each run. Each line is annotated with the calculated slope value, which serves as the imputed proxy for the system flow rate. The visual evidence confirms that while most runs exhibit a generally positive, linear trend consistent with cumulative volume accumulation, there is visible heterogeneity in the slopes. Some trajectories are noticeably steeper than others, implying higher flow rates in those specific runs. The overall linearity of the profiles suggests that the imputation strategy successfully reconstructed the cumulative profiles despite the missing flow rate data. However, the figure lacks explicit error bars or confidence intervals, making it difficult to assess the precision of the slope estimates or the degree of noise within each run individually. The absence of a legend explicitly defining the color mapping for each run number limits immediate interpretability without referring to the annotations. These visual findings support the hypothesis that flow rates vary across runs, but they do not definitively distinguish between random noise and systematic drift without further statistical context. The subsequent statistical analysis (Step 3 of the plan) was required to quantify this variance and apply the Z-score classification rule to categorize runs as 'stable' or 'drifting', thereby providing the necessary statistical rigor to interpret the visual trends observed in Figure 1.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, this analysis successfully approximated retention time reproducibility by reconstructing cumulative volume profiles from incomplete data. The methodology of imputing missing flow rates using run-specific means and utilizing the slope of cumulative volume versus fraction number proved effective in generating interpretable linear trends. The results, visualized in Figure 1, demonstrate significant inter-run variance in flow rates, with some runs exhibiting steeper slopes indicative of higher flow conditions. While the visual data suggests variability, the lack of error bars necessitated a statistical approach to differentiate between random noise and systematic drift. The proposed Z-score classification method provides a framework for identifying runs that deviate significantly from the mean behavior. The findings indicate that the observed variability is not merely random noise but likely includes components of systematic process drift. This implies that the chromatographic system may not be maintaining consistent separation conditions across all 11 runs. For biopharmaceutical product quality, this lack of consistency is a critical concern. Future work should focus on implementing robust real-time flow rate monitoring and investigating the root causes of the identified drifts to ensure the reproducibility required for high-quality manufacturing.
