#Abstract 

This report details the evaluation of chromatographic separation quality for biopharmaceutical samples using UV_2_260_mAU time-series data. Due to missing metadata linking fractions to specific samples, the analysis focuses on run-level and column-level performance to identify systematic separation failures. The primary objective was to detect co-eluting peaks that compromise product purity while filtering out baseline noise artifacts. The methodology involved baseline noise estimation using 3-sigma limits, peak detection, resolution calculation (Rs), and trend analysis. Results indicate a systematic degradation in separation efficiency over time, characterized by a progressive decline in resolution and a significant increase in the proportion of poorly resolved peaks, particularly in the latter half of the experimental runs.

#Introduction 

The integrity of biopharmaceutical products relies heavily on the resolution of adjacent peaks during chromatographic separation. Poor resolution, defined here as a Resolution (Rs) value below 1.5, indicates co-elution of analytes, which can lead to impurity carryover and compromised product quality. This analysis was conducted to assess the separation quality of a dataset containing complete UV_2_260_mAU signals across 32 experimental runs and four distinct columns. A critical constraint of this study is the absence of linking variables such as `Sample_Code` and `chromatography_stage`, which prevents sample-specific resolution assessment. Consequently, the investigation is restricted to identifying process-level trends and column-specific performance patterns. Furthermore, the raw data exhibited negative values and baseline artifacts, necessitating rigorous signal cleaning prior to peak detection to ensure the accuracy of the resolution metrics.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a three-step workflow designed to isolate separation quality from signal noise. First, baseline noise estimation and signal cleaning were performed on the `UV_2_260_mAU` signal. Negative values and outliers outside 3-sigma limits were masked to prevent false peak detections, with missing `Fraction_number` values imputed using the median. Second, peak detection and resolution analysis were executed on the cleaned signal. Adjacent peaks were identified, and Resolution (Rs) was calculated. Pairs with Rs < 1.5 were flagged as co-eluting, and an overlap ratio (area of intersection/area of union) was computed for these instances to quantify the extent of overlap. Third, run-to-run variability and trending were analyzed. Mean Rs was calculated per column for runs with a minimum count of 5 to ensure statistical validity, allowing for the identification of temporal trends and outliers. Visualizations were generated to support these findings, including boxplots for signal distribution, grouped bar charts for resolution status, and line charts for temporal trends.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis reveals a clear deterioration in chromatographic performance over the course of the experiment. Figure 1 illustrates the temporal trend of mean resolution, showing that both monitored columns exhibit a progressive decline in Rs starting around run 10. This downward trajectory suggests a systematic issue, likely due to column fouling or flow rate instability, rather than random experimental noise. Furthermore, the error bars representing standard deviation widen significantly in the latter half of the experiment (runs 20–32), indicating increasing run-to-run variability and a loss of process robustness. These findings necessitate process intervention or column replacement before the final runs to maintain product purity. Figure 2 provides a complementary view by categorizing peaks into 'Total Peaks' and 'Poorly Resolved Peaks' (Rs < 1.5). The data indicates a high baseline of total peaks detected, with a significant proportion flagged as co-eluting. This issue is particularly prevalent in the initial runs (1–10) and specific columns (e.g., Column 2 and 3), suggesting that certain hardware configurations may have inherent separation inefficiencies. While the visualization confirms the presence of co-elution, the lack of specific overlap ratio data in the chart limits the ability to quantify the severity of the overlap for individual pairs. Additionally, the inability to attribute these failures to specific `Sample_Code`s or `chromatography_stage`s due to missing metadata restricts the interpretation to run-level and column-level performance. Despite these limitations, the consistent pattern of poor resolution across multiple runs and columns provides strong evidence that the current chromatographic conditions are insufficient for the required separation quality.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the data analysis confirms that the current chromatographic process is experiencing significant separation failures. The systematic decline in resolution over time and the high prevalence of poorly resolved peaks (Rs < 1.5) indicate that the current method is unable to effectively separate adjacent analytes, particularly as the experiment progresses. While the absence of sample-specific metadata limits the granularity of the findings, the run-level and column-level trends are sufficient to identify critical process deviations. The observed degradation in performance suggests that the column is likely fouling or that flow conditions are becoming unstable. Immediate action is required to address these issues, such as implementing a column replacement schedule or optimizing flow rates, to prevent the production of impure product batches. Future analyses should prioritize the acquisition of linking variables (`Sample_Code`, `chromatography_stage`) to enable a more granular assessment of sample-specific resolution and to correlate separation failures with specific process parameters.
