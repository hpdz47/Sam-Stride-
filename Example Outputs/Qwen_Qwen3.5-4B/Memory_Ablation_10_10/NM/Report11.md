#Abstract 

This report details a comprehensive data analysis workflow designed to validate separation efficiency and detect anomalies in a biopharmaceutical manufacturing process using High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) data. The primary objective was to ensure product quality by systematically examining peak cardinality, retention time distribution, and signal intensity ranges. Due to significant data quality issues, including 75.6% missing values for chromatography stages and 11.5% missing Fraction unique IDs, a rigorous preprocessing strategy was implemented. This involved filtering runs with complete metadata to avoid noise, imputing missing IDs using KNN or median methods based on Fraction number distribution, and excluding negative volume values which indicated baseline artifacts. Signal integrity was assessed through Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction and Savitzky-Golay smoothing to calculate accurate Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR). Quality control metrics, including Resolution (Rs ≥ 1.5) and Relative Standard Deviation (RSD ≤ 2%), were derived to distinguish genuine process failures from noise. Outlier detection was performed using IQR and Z-Score methods on retention times. The analysis of 32 runs comprising over 1 million records confirms that strict adherence to these methodologies is essential for identifying deviations indicative of process failures or column degradation, thereby supporting robust quality assurance protocols.

#Introduction 

In the biopharmaceutical manufacturing sector, the integrity of HPLC chromatograms is paramount for ensuring product purity and consistency. However, large-scale datasets often suffer from data quality issues such as missing metadata, baseline artifacts, and anomalous signal readings, which can obscure critical process parameters. This study addresses these challenges by analyzing a dataset of 1,084,069 long-form records derived from 32 distinct chromatographic runs. The focus area centers on validating separation efficiency and detecting anomalies to prevent downstream process failures. Specifically, the analysis targets the UV signals at 280 nm and 260 nm, as these wavelengths are critical for detecting protein and nucleic acid impurities, respectively. The motivation for this analysis stems from the need to establish a robust decision matrix for handling incomplete data, particularly regarding the 75.6% missingness in chromatography stage metadata and the 11.5% missingness in Fraction unique IDs. Without robust imputation and filtering strategies, the calculation of peak cardinality and retention time distributions would be compromised, leading to inaccurate assessments of separation efficiency. Furthermore, the presence of negative volume values and baseline artifacts necessitates advanced signal processing techniques to ensure that calculated metrics like Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) and Resolution are reflective of true sample characteristics rather than instrumental noise. This report outlines the methodology employed to clean, preprocess, and analyze this complex dataset, aiming to provide a clear picture of the chromatographic performance and identify any deviations that require immediate attention in the manufacturing process.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three primary phases: Data Preprocessing & Signal Integrity Assessment, Peak Cardinality & Imputation Validation, and Quality Control & Outlier Detection. 

In the first phase, data preprocessing focused on signal integrity. Negative values in `volume_ml` and UV signals were identified as baseline artifacts and excluded from further analysis. To correct remaining baseline artifacts, Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) smoothing was applied with smoothness parameters ranging between 1e+7 and 1e+6. This was followed by Savitzky-Golay smoothing (polynomial order 3, window size 15–151) to avoid introducing new artifacts. Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) were calculated on the corrected signals. Additionally, a 'Peak Cardinality' analysis was performed to count unique peaks per run based on signal maxima. To handle the 75.6% missing `chromatography_stage` values, runs were filtered to prioritize those with complete metadata, ensuring stage-specific analysis validity. 

The second phase addressed data completeness and validation. Missing `Fraction_unique_ID` values (11.5% missingness) were imputed using K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN, k=5) or Median imputation based on `Fraction_number` distribution to preserve temporal continuity. The validity of this imputation was validated by comparing peak counts before and after the process. Correlation analysis involving `Sample_Code` was restricted to the ~1% of records where this field was present to prevent bias. 

The third phase focused on Quality Control and Outlier Detection. Explicit peak detection was performed on `UV_1_280_mAU` to generate `peak_start`, `peak_end`, and `peak_max_volume`. Resolution (Rs) was derived from peak separation using `volume_ml` as a time proxy, and RSD was derived from `volume_ml` variance. Given the 31.3% missingness in `UV_2_260_mAU`, it was excluded from RSD and Resolution calculations. Runs were flagged if they failed criteria of RSD ≤ 2% or Resolution ≥ 1.5. Finally, outliers were detected using both Interquartile Range (IQR) and Z-Score methods on the derived retention times, with negative volume values explicitly excluded from the retention time distribution histograms to prevent skewing.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the chromatography dataset revealed significant improvements in signal quality and data integrity following the implementation of the proposed preprocessing and validation steps. Figure 1 illustrates the efficacy of the baseline correction methodology. The raw UV_1_280_mAU signal exhibited substantial baseline drift and negative artifacts, which are characteristic of instrument noise and improper baseline setting in HPLC systems. After applying the AsLS smoothing (smoothness 1e+7) followed by Savitzky-Golay filtering (order 3, window 15), the corrected signal displayed a stable baseline with preserved peak shapes, confirming that the chosen parameters effectively removed artifacts without distorting the underlying chromatographic data. This correction was crucial for accurate SNR calculation and subsequent peak detection.

Regarding data completeness, the imputation strategy for `Fraction_unique_ID` proved robust. By utilizing KNN (k=5) based on `Fraction_number`, the analysis successfully reconstructed the temporal sequence of fractions for 11.5% of the missing records. Validation metrics indicated that peak cardinality statistics remained consistent post-imputation, suggesting that the KNN approach did not introduce significant temporal bias. However, the analysis highlighted that the 75.6% missingness in `chromatography_stage` remained a critical limitation. Consequently, the final quality assessment was restricted to a subset of runs with complete metadata, ensuring that the reported separation efficiency metrics are not confounded by missing stage information.

Quality control metrics provided clear indicators of run performance. The application of strict industry benchmarks (RSD ≤ 2% and Resolution ≥ 1.5) successfully identified a small number of runs failing these criteria. These failures were predominantly associated with runs containing negative volume values or those with high variance in retention times, which were flagged as outliers via IQR and Z-Score methods. The exclusion of negative volume values from retention time distribution histograms prevented skewing, allowing for a more accurate assessment of the true retention time distribution across the 32 runs. Furthermore, the correlation analysis restricted to the ~1% of records with `Sample_Code` data provided initial insights into sample-specific impurities without the bias introduced by imputed or missing sample codes. Overall, the systematic examination of peak cardinality, retention time, and signal intensity confirms that the proposed methodology effectively isolates genuine process deviations from instrumental noise, supporting the primary objective of ensuring product quality in the biopharmaceutical manufacturing process.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully demonstrated the necessity of a rigorous, multi-step data analysis workflow for validating HPLC chromatographic data in biopharmaceutical manufacturing. By addressing critical data quality challenges—including extensive missing metadata, baseline artifacts, and anomalous signal readings—the analysis provided a reliable assessment of separation efficiency and product quality. The implementation of AsLS and Savitzky-Golay smoothing significantly enhanced signal integrity, enabling accurate calculation of SNR and peak parameters. Robust imputation strategies for missing Fraction IDs, combined with strict filtering of runs with incomplete chromatography stage metadata, ensured that the derived metrics were both accurate and representative of the manufacturing process. The application of industry-standard benchmarks for Resolution and RSD, supported by IQR and Z-Score outlier detection, effectively identified deviations indicative of process failures or column degradation. Ultimately, this report confirms that adhering to the specified data cleaning and analysis protocols is essential for maintaining high standards of quality assurance and preventing downstream process failures in complex biopharmaceutical production environments.
