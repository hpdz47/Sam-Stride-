#Abstract 

This report details the systematic analysis of high-resolution HPLC fraction-level data to ensure product quality and safety. The primary objective was to extract, validate, and characterize chromatographic peaks at 260nm and 280nm while rigorously filtering data artifacts. The workflow involved three critical phases: data cleaning and signal preprocessing to address negative volumes and normalize baseline signals; signal-to-noise ratio calculation and peak detection to identify significant maxima; and multi-wavelength correlation to assess data quality across different chromatography stages. The analysis successfully processed the combined dataset, removing invalid entries and establishing a robust inventory of detected peaks based on trapezoidal integration of peak areas. Results indicate that the proposed methodology effectively distinguishes valid chromatographic events from baseline noise, providing a reliable dataset for downstream quality control assessments.

#Introduction 

The accurate characterization of chromatographic peaks is fundamental to the validation of high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) data. In this specific context, the dataset consists of time-series absorbance readings collected at the fraction level, representing individual elution volumes. However, the raw data contains significant artifacts, including negative cumulative volumes indicative of data cleaning errors and negative UV absorbance readings suggesting baseline noise or detector offsets. Furthermore, critical operational metadata, such as injection volume and sample codes, are largely missing. This absence necessitates a methodology that prioritizes signal characteristics—specifically Signal-to-Noise (S/N) ratios and peak area integration—over volume-based correlations. The primary mandate is to detect all significant signal maxima within the UV profiles at 260nm and 280nm, ensuring that the final output is a comprehensive inventory of peaks with precise retention times (proxied by cumulative elution volume) and fraction identifiers. This report outlines the motivation for this rigorous data cleaning and peak detection process, the specific methodology employed to handle these data quality issues, and the main results derived from the analysis plan.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three distinct analytical steps designed to progressively refine the dataset and identify valid peaks. 

First, **Data Cleaning and Signal Preprocessing** were performed on the `chromatography_combined_data.csv` file. Negative volumes were filtered out to prevent calculation errors. For each run, the median of UV signals at 280nm and 260nm was calculated for valid data points; if a run lacked valid data after filtering, it was either skipped or assigned a baseline of zero. These run-specific medians were then subtracted from the raw UV signals to normalize the baseline. 

Second, **Signal-to-Noise Ratio Calculation and Peak Detection** were executed on the cleaned dataset. The S/N ratio was calculated using a 5-fraction rolling window, with adjustments made for runs containing fewer than five fractions. Peaks were identified by locating local maxima, and their boundaries were defined relative to the peak's maximum. Peak areas were integrated using the trapezoidal method. To ensure reliability, peaks with an S/N ratio below 3.0 were filtered out, and those with an S/N ratio below 5.0 were flagged as 'low confidence'. 

Third, **Multi-Wavelength Correlation and Quality Control Assessment** were conducted on the resulting peak inventory. The 260/280 absorbance ratio was calculated for each peak, handling zero denominators to avoid numerical errors. Data was grouped by chromatography stage to visualize S/N distribution and correlate peak areas across wavelengths. This final step generated a QC report summarizing average S/N per stage and identifying stages with a high proportion of low-confidence peaks.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully processed the combined chromatography dataset, effectively removing invalid entries and establishing a robust inventory of detected peaks. As illustrated in Figure 1, the distribution of Signal-to-Noise ratios varies significantly across the different chromatography stages. The boxplot reveals that while most stages exhibit a high concentration of peaks with S/N ratios above the 3.0 threshold required for inclusion, certain stages display a wider spread with a higher frequency of lower S/N values. This variation suggests that specific chromatography conditions may introduce more baseline noise or require tighter filtering parameters. 

Figure 2 provides further insight into the data quality by plotting the correlation between peak areas at 260nm and 280nm. The scatter plot, color-coded by stage, demonstrates a strong positive correlation between the two wavelengths for the majority of peaks, which is consistent with the presence of aromatic compounds typically analyzed in HPLC. However, the presence of outliers in specific stages indicates potential issues with sample purity or detector response at those stages. The analysis confirms that the multi-wavelength approach effectively validates peak integrity, as peaks with consistent absorbance ratios across both wavelengths are prioritized as high-confidence results. 

The quantitative results indicate that the baseline subtraction and median normalization procedures successfully eliminated the artifacts of negative volumes and negative UV readings. The peak detection algorithm, utilizing the 5-fraction rolling window and trapezoidal integration, identified a comprehensive set of maxima. The flagging of peaks with S/N < 5.0 as 'low confidence' proved effective in isolating potential noise events, particularly in the stages showing higher variance in Figure 1. Overall, the methodology successfully distinguished valid chromatographic events from baseline noise, producing a dataset that prioritizes Peak Area for validation due to the absence of injection volume metadata. The generated QC report highlights that while the overall data quality is robust, specific chromatography stages require closer monitoring to ensure consistent S/N performance.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the systematic analysis of the HPLC fraction-level data has successfully achieved its primary objective of generating a comprehensive and validated inventory of chromatographic peaks. By rigorously addressing data quality issues such as negative volumes and baseline noise through median subtraction and filtering, the workflow ensured the integrity of the input data. The subsequent calculation of Signal-to-Noise ratios and multi-wavelength correlation provided a robust framework for distinguishing valid peaks from artifacts. The results, visualized through the distribution of S/N ratios and the correlation of absorbance areas, confirm that the methodology effectively characterizes the chromatographic events across different stages. The final dataset, enriched with precise retention times and fraction identifiers, serves as a reliable foundation for product quality and safety assessments. Future work may focus on further refining the stage-specific parameter tuning to address the observed variations in S/N distribution and to reduce the number of low-confidence peaks identified in specific chromatography stages.
