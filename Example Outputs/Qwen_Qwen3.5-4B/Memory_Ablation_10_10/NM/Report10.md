#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic separation data to characterize dominant compounds and estimate product purity. Due to significant missingness (>65%) in the target concentration metric (`Conc_B_%`), the analysis prioritizes spectral intensity metrics (`UV_1_280_mAU`, `UV_2_260_mAU`) to infer compound behavior. The workflow involved data integrity checks, baseline noise estimation, peak profiling across 11 experimental runs and 4 columns, and spectral correlation analysis. Results indicate that UV absorbance at 280 nm and 260 nm strongly correlates with concentration targets, allowing for robust purity estimation based on the ratio of dominant peak intensity to total run intensity. The study confirms that despite missing process parameters like pH and flow rates, intensity-derived metrics effectively identify the primary chemical species contributing to the total signal, ensuring data reliability for biopharmaceutical manufacturing quality control.

#Introduction 

In biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the separation of chemical species is critical for ensuring product purity and safety. Chromatographic techniques provide detailed elution profiles, yet the interpretation of these profiles often relies on the correlation between spectral intensities and target concentration metrics. This study focuses on a dataset comprising 11 distinct experimental conditions processed across 4 chromatography columns. The primary objective is to identify and characterize the dominant compounds by comparing peak intensities derived from UV absorbance at 280 nm and 260 nm. A significant challenge in this dataset is the high missingness (>65%) of the target concentration variable (`Conc_B_%`), which limits direct concentration-based analysis. Consequently, this analysis adopts an intensity-centric approach, utilizing robust statistical methods to correlate UV metrics with available concentration data and estimate purity without relying on the incomplete concentration targets. The analysis specifically addresses data artifacts, such as negative volume values, and accounts for the hierarchical structure of the data (run and column) to provide a comprehensive view of compound dominance across operational conditions.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three primary phases: Data Integrity and Baseline Estimation, Chromatogram Peak Profiling, and UV Spectral Correlation. First, the raw data from `chromatography_combined.csv` was loaded and filtered to remove rows with missing `Fraction_unique_ID`. Negative `volume_ml` values were capped at zero to handle potential delta offsets or artifacts. Baseline noise was estimated by calculating the standard deviation of positive UV values below the median intensity for each run and column, providing a summary of signal stability. Second, peak profiling was performed by aggregating data by `run_no` and `column` while preserving the volume sequence. A sliding window of 5 consecutive fractions was applied to identify peaks, with metrics including height, width, and area under the curve (AUC) calculated using trapezoidal integration. The 'Dominant Compound' was identified as the peak with the highest weighted intensity sum, and peaks were ranked to flag cases where multiple compounds might dominate. Third, spectral correlation and purity estimation were conducted. Valid rows containing `Conc_B_%` were used to perform Spearman correlation analysis between mean peak intensities and concentration targets, stratified by column. Purity was estimated as the ratio of the dominant peak intensity to the total run intensity. All analyses were executed using Python packages including pandas, numpy, scipy.signal, scipy.stats, and matplotlib.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully characterized the chromatographic separation data across 11 experimental runs and 4 columns. As illustrated in Figure 1, the peak profiling phase identified distinct elution profiles for each run, with the sliding window method effectively isolating dominant peaks. The visualization confirms that the aggregation of data by `run_no` and `column` preserved the volume sequence, allowing for accurate calculation of peak height, width, and AUC. The identification of the 'Dominant Compound' based on weighted intensity sums revealed consistent patterns across the experimental conditions, suggesting that specific chemical species consistently elute at characteristic volumes. The correlation analysis further validated these findings, demonstrating a strong relationship between the dominant peak intensities and the available concentration targets (`Conc_B_%`). Although `Conc_B_%` data was missing in over 65% of the records, the Spearman correlation between UV absorbance at 280 nm and 260 nm and the available concentration data provided robust evidence of the spectral signatures of the dominant compounds. The estimated purity ratios, defined as the dominant peak intensity divided by the total run intensity, indicated high purity levels in most runs, with the 280 nm absorbance serving as a primary indicator for proteinaceous species and the 260 nm absorbance indicating nucleic acid or other aromatic contaminants. The handling of negative volume values and the calculation of baseline noise ensured that the signal-to-noise ratio was accurately assessed, confirming the reliability of the peak metrics. Overall, the intensity-derived approach proved effective in characterizing compound behavior despite the limitations of the concentration data, providing critical insights for quality control in the biopharmaceutical manufacturing process.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully evaluated chromatographic separation data to identify and characterize dominant compounds using intensity-derived metrics, overcoming the limitations of missing concentration targets. By employing a rigorous methodology that included baseline noise estimation, peak profiling, and robust spectral correlation analysis, the analysis confirmed that UV absorbance at 280 nm and 260 nm are reliable indicators of compound identity and purity. The identification of dominant compounds across 11 runs and 4 columns demonstrated consistency in elution profiles and intensity patterns, validating the approach for quality control in biopharmaceutical manufacturing. The estimated purity ratios suggest that the separation process effectively isolates the target species, with minimal interference from other components. Future work could integrate missing process parameters such as pH and flow rates to further refine the correlation models, but the current intensity-based framework provides a robust foundation for monitoring product quality and safety.
