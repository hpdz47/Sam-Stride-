#Abstract 

This report evaluates the reliability of automated peak detection in a high-variance chromatographic dataset characterized by severe flow instability and detector artifacts. The primary objective was to quantify baseline noise using inter-peak valleys and assess the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) of detected analytes. Initial data preprocessing revealed that 100% of the sampled runs (Run IDs 1, 2, 4, 16, 17, 18, 19, 20, 21, 30, 32) exhibited extreme flow instability, with Coefficient of Variation (CV) values for system and sample flow rates ranging from 69% to 203%, far exceeding the 5% stability threshold. Consequently, all runs were stratified as 'Unstable,' suggesting that pump-induced artifacts fundamentally compromise the data quality. Despite these critical failures in flow control, the peak detection visualization confirms the implementation of negative value clipping and Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) smoothing. However, the analysis indicates a complete failure in peak detection reliability, with zero peaks meeting the >3x noise criteria across all 32 runs. The reported Mean SNR is undefined ('nan'), and the false positive rate is 0.00%, indicating that the noise floor estimation likely failed or the signal intensity is genuinely below the detection threshold. These findings suggest that the current hardware conditions and measurement protocols are unsuitable for high-precision chromatographic analysis without significant intervention.

#Introduction 

Accurate quantification in chromatography relies heavily on the ability to distinguish true analyte peaks from baseline noise and artifacts. In this study, the focus area was defined as quantifying baseline noise characteristics within inter-peak valleys identified via peak detection algorithms tuned with agglomerative hierarchical clustering. The primary objective was to assess SNR and define a 'true analyte peak' as one meeting a signal intensity threshold ($> 3 	imes$ estimated noise) and a shape criterion (Resolution $R_s 
eq 1.5$). Crucially, the analysis plan mandated the clipping of negative UV values, the application of wavelength-specific AsLS smoothing, and the verification of flow stability using a CV < 5% threshold. The dataset presented significant challenges, including high variance (CV > 150%) in UV signals, the presence of negative values indicating detector artifacts, and a high percentage of missing metadata. The motivation for this analysis was to determine if the high baseline noise and flow instability significantly compromise automated peak detection reliability and to identify necessary adaptations to the analysis workflow.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three primary steps. First, data preprocessing and flow stability stratification were performed on the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset. This involved filtering rows with missing `Fraction_unique_ID`, treating the time index as continuous, and calculating flow statistics per run. Runs were flagged as 'Unstable' if the CV for `System_flow_ml_min` or `Sample_flow_ml_min` exceeded 5%. Negative UV values were clipped to zero, and AsLS smoothing (lambda=0.1, p=0.01) was applied. Second, peak detection and inter-peak valley clustering were executed on the smoothed data. Peaks were detected in both wavelengths, and inter-peak valleys were identified using Agglomerative Hierarchical Clustering (Euclidean distance, Ward linkage). Valleys were clustered into groups of 3+ points with a distance <2.0 mAU; clusters with a mean depth <1.0 mAU were excluded. The mean of valid clustered valley depths was used to establish a scalar 'estimated_noise_floor'. Third, SNR and false positive quantification were calculated. SNR was computed per peak using the estimated noise floor. True analyte peaks were filtered for intensity >3x noise and $R_s 
eq 1.5$. If fewer than two peaks were detected per run, resolution was reported as N/A. The methodology explicitly required stratification by run stability to exclude pump-induced artifacts before noise estimation.

#Results and Discussion 

The results indicate a critical failure in the data analysis pipeline due to unaddressed hardware instability and data quality issues. Flow stability stratification revealed that all 11 runs included in the stability assessment (Run IDs 1, 2, 4, 16, 17, 18, 19, 20, 21, 30, 32) were classified as 'Unstable.' The Coefficient of Variation (CV) for both system and sample flow rates ranged from approximately 69% to 203%, vastly exceeding the 5% threshold. This extreme variability suggests significant run-to-run fluctuations or sensor noise rather than a single catastrophic pump failure, rendering the data unsuitable for high-precision analysis. Concurrently, the peak detection analysis yielded zero valid peaks across all 32 runs. The reported statistics show a Mean SNR of 'nan', 0.00% of peaks meeting the >3x noise criteria, and a 0.00% false positive rate. This outcome implies that either the noise floor estimation failed (resulting in a divisor of zero), the preprocessing steps (clipping and smoothing) were not successfully executed, or the signal intensity is genuinely below the detection threshold. The visualization in Figure 1 displays the application of the peak detection algorithm, featuring red dots for detected peaks and a dashed line for the AsLS-smoothed baseline. While the visual confirms the implementation of negative value clipping and smoothing, the lack of explicit annotations for parameters (e.g., clustering distance threshold of 2.0 mAU, valley depth cutoff of 1.0 mAU) makes verification difficult. The histogram of clustered valley depths, intended to establish the noise floor, appears to lack valid data points given the zero peak detection. The conclusion that 'detection reliability appears stable' is misleading; in reality, the system is unable to detect any analyte. The high variance in the dataset and the structural heterogeneity of the data (mix of fractionated and raw data) further complicate the analysis, necessitating a complete overhaul of the flow control system or significant adaptation of the analysis plan before valid SNR calculations can be performed.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the current data analysis workflow has failed to produce reliable peak detection results due to severe flow instability and high baseline noise. The finding that 100% of sampled runs are 'Unstable' with flow CVs exceeding 150% indicates that the measurement conditions are fundamentally unsuitable for chromatographic analysis. The complete absence of detected peaks (0 peaks per run) and the undefined SNR ('nan') confirm that the noise floor estimation could not be reliably established, likely due to the failure of preprocessing steps or the overwhelming nature of the noise. The visualization in Figure 1, while showing the correct application of smoothing and valley clustering, highlights a lack of quantifiable success metrics. To proceed, the analysis plan must be adapted to handle the sparse metadata and high baseline noise, and the hardware flow control system requires immediate intervention to reduce variability below the 5% threshold. Until these fundamental issues are resolved, any downstream analysis relying on these runs for quantification is compromised.
