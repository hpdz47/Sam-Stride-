#Abstract 

This report details the analysis of chromatographic data to assess retention time reproducibility in the absence of explicit timestamps. Due to high missingness in flow rate variables (~66%) and the lack of time-stamping, the study approximates reproducibility by evaluating the consistency of cumulative volume profiles across 11 experimental runs. The primary objective was to determine whether observed variability in fraction volumes stems from random noise or systematic process drift, a distinction critical for biopharmaceutical product quality. Data integrity protocols were first applied to filter negative volume values and ensure unit consistency between concentration metrics. Subsequently, run-level linear regression slopes were calculated to estimate flow rate consistency, and the variance of fraction volumes was quantified. Statistical differentiation between noise and drift was performed using an F-test with a significance threshold of p < 0.05 and a variance ratio exceeding 1.5. The results indicate significant inter-run variability, with fraction volume distributions widening progressively from Run 1 to Run 11. While the visual data suggests a degradation in consistency over time, the specific statistical conclusion regarding systematic drift versus random noise requires the explicit F-statistic and p-value output from the final analytical step, which is not visually represented in the provided figures.

#Introduction 

In biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the reproducibility of chromatographic separations is paramount for ensuring product quality and consistency. Retention time is a critical parameter that dictates the purity and yield of the final product. However, in this specific dataset, precise retention time calculation is compromised by the absence of explicit timestamps and the high missingness of flow rate variables (approximately 66% missing for `System_flow_ml_min`). Furthermore, critical identifiers such as `Sample_Code` are missing for over 75% of the data, preventing sample-specific analysis. To address these data limitations, this study adopts a volume-based approach to approximate retention time reproducibility. The core hypothesis is that if the chromatographic process is stable, the slope of cumulative volume versus fraction number (representing flow rate) should remain consistent across runs, and the variance in individual fraction volumes should not exhibit a systematic trend. The motivation for this analysis is to identify whether the observed degradation in data quality and process consistency is attributable to random experimental noise or systematic process drift, which would indicate a failure in maintaining the necessary separation conditions.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three primary phases. First, a data integrity and unit consistency filtering protocol was executed on the raw `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset. This step explicitly excluded rows containing negative `volume_ml` values to ensure physical validity, removed entries with missing (`NaN`) or non-numeric `Conc_B_ml` values, and flagged inconsistencies where `Conc_B_ml` values exceeded 100% or fell below 0% relative to `Conc_B_%`. Second, a run-level slope and variance calculation was performed on the cleaned dataset. Data was grouped by `run`, and linear regression was applied to `volume_ml` against `Fraction_number` to estimate flow rate consistency. Additionally, the mean and standard deviation of `fraction_volume` were calculated for each run. Third, a statistical differentiation between noise and drift was conducted using an F-test. The variance of the calculated slopes (`slope_volume_fraction`) was compared against the variance of the fraction volumes (`std_fraction_volume`). The analysis utilized a dual-criteria decision rule: a p-value < 0.05 and a variance ratio > 1.5 were required to conclude 'Systematic Drift'; otherwise, the variability was attributed to 'Random Noise'. Visualizations were generated to support these statistical findings, plotting cumulative volume trends and fraction volume distributions across the 11 runs.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis reveals significant inter-run variability that warrants further statistical interpretation. As depicted in Figure 1, the line chart of cumulative volume versus fraction number shows distinct trajectories for each of the 11 runs. While some runs exhibit a consistent, positive linear trajectory indicative of steady flow, others display steeper inclines or deviations from linearity, suggesting variable flow rates or system pressure changes. This divergence in slopes visually supports the objective of approximating retention time reproducibility by comparing flow rate metrics, although the absence of explicit timestamps necessitates reliance on volume-based metrics. The chart highlights that the data effectively captures the variability between runs but does not visually confirm the presence of negative volume values, implying that the data integrity filtering step successfully removed erroneous rows or that such values fall outside the plotted range. Figure 2 provides a complementary view, presenting a scatter plot of fraction volume distribution by run. This visualization reveals a clear trend where Runs 2 through 11 display progressively wider distributions compared to Run 1, which exhibits a tight distribution centered near 0.01 ml. Run 11 shows the largest spread, extending up to approximately 0.08 ml. This widening variance suggests that as the run number increases, the consistency of fraction collection degrades, potentially indicating systematic drift in the chromatographic process or increasing noise in later runs. However, while the visual evidence strongly suggests a degradation in consistency, the figure alone cannot definitively attribute this variance to systematic drift versus random noise. The determination of whether the variance exceeds the 1.5x threshold required to declare systematic drift relies on the calculated F-statistic and p-value from the final statistical step, which are not explicitly displayed in the visualizations. Consequently, while the data indicates a need for investigation into process stability, the granular statistical output required to make a conclusive claim about the nature of the observed variance remains pending the full text report of the F-test results.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully approximated retention time reproducibility by evaluating volume-based metrics across 11 experimental runs, overcoming the limitations of missing timestamps and high flow rate data missingness. The data integrity protocols effectively filtered out physically invalid data, ensuring the robustness of subsequent statistical comparisons. The analysis identified a significant trend of increasing variance in fraction volumes from early to late runs, as evidenced by the widening distributions in Figure 2 and the divergent slopes in Figure 1. These visual indicators suggest a potential degradation in process consistency over time. However, the final conclusion regarding whether this variability represents systematic process drift or random noise is contingent upon the explicit F-statistic and p-value derived from the statistical differentiation step. Without these specific numerical outputs confirming the variance ratio exceeds 1.5 and the p-value is below 0.05, a definitive attribution cannot be made solely from the visual data. Future work should prioritize the inclusion of the statistical test results to finalize the assessment of process stability and determine if corrective actions are required to mitigate systematic drift.
