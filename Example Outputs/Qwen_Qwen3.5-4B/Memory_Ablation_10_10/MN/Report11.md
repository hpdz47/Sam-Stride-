#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic peak characteristics derived from the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset, focusing on the ~88% of records containing precise volume boundaries. The primary objective was to assess product quality and safety by identifying deviations in peak shape quality indicative of process instability or column degradation. The analysis employed a rigorous methodology involving volume offset normalization, outlier detection via Interquartile Range (IQR) and Z-scores, and the assessment of selection bias inherent in excluding flat baseline fractions. Key findings, illustrated in Figure 1, reveal significant variability in peak width across different runs, characterized by a wide interquartile range and numerous outliers. These statistical anomalies suggest potential process failures or data artifacts, particularly where negative volume values were not fully normalized prior to calculation. The report concludes that while robust statistical methods were applied to mitigate selection bias, the observed instability warrants further investigation into specific run conditions and column integrity.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the integrity of chromatographic peaks is a critical indicator of product quality and safety. This analysis focuses on the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset, which comprises 1.08 million long-form time-series records. However, the availability of precise peak shape metrics—specifically width and symmetry—is limited to approximately 88% of the total records, as the remaining 12% contain missing volume boundaries or represent flat baseline fractions. This exclusion introduces a selection bias, necessitating a methodology that strictly adheres to high-availability variables while acknowledging the limitations of assessing global run stability. Furthermore, the dataset contains negative values in the `volume_ml` variable, likely representing cumulative offsets or noise artifacts, which must be normalized to zero before calculating peak widths to prevent erroneous results. The motivation for this analysis is to generate actionable insights on peak integrity across different runs and columns, distinguishing between global run indices and local fraction counters to ensure accurate aggregation. By utilizing robust statistical methods such as IQR and Z-score thresholds (>3), this study aims to identify specific deviations in peak shape that may indicate process instability, column degradation, or sample impurities, ultimately supporting the optimization of manufacturing protocols.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three distinct phases to ensure data integrity and statistical rigor. The first phase, Data Filtering and Volume Normalization, involved loading the dataset and filtering rows where both `fraction_volume_ml_min_precise` and `fraction_volume_ml_max_precise` were non-null. A critical preprocessing step involved setting negative `volume_ml` values to zero to normalize cumulative offsets. Peak width was calculated as the difference between the maximum and minimum precise volumes, with rows resulting in widths less than or equal to 0.001 excluded to prevent division errors. The second phase, Peak Shape Quality and Outlier Detection, focused on calculating the Tailing Factor using the formula (R + 2*W)/2R. Simultaneously, Z-scores for `fraction_volume` and UV absorbance were computed using the IQR method (1.5*IQR). The third phase, Peak Shape Trending and Selection Bias Assessment, involved filtering for non-null `chromatography_stage_order` values to calculate symmetry and height metrics. A 'Peak Quality Score' was derived using min-max normalization of width, height, and symmetry to analyze trends across chromatography stages. All visualizations and statistical outputs were generated to support the identification of outliers and the assessment of run-to-run variability.

#Results and Discussion 

The primary result of the analysis is presented in Figure 1, which displays the distribution of `fraction_volume` (peak width in ml) stratified by `run_no`. The visualization utilizes a boxplot format to clearly delineate the median, quartiles, and individual outliers for each run. The data quality assessment reveals significant noise and potential bias within the dataset. Specifically, the boxplots exhibit a wide interquartile range and a high frequency of outliers across multiple `run_no` values. This high variability in peak widths is consistent with the dataset's high missingness in precise volume boundaries (~12%), as the analysis is restricted to the ~88% of records where peaks were detected, potentially skewing the distribution toward larger widths. The presence of numerous outliers suggests process instability or column degradation in specific runs. While the plan context mentions the normalization of negative `volume_ml` values, the visual evidence of extreme outliers implies that this normalization may not have been fully effective or that other sources of variability exist. The figure confirms a lack of consistency in peak width across different runs, validating the need for further investigation into the specific conditions of the affected runs. It is important to note that while the figure effectively highlights deviations, it does not provide context on the relationship between these widths and other peak shape metrics like height or symmetry, which are crucial for a complete quality assessment. Nevertheless, the identification of these outliers represents potential process failures requiring immediate attention.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, this analysis successfully evaluated the quantitative characteristics of chromatographic peaks from the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset, adhering to strict protocols regarding volume normalization and outlier detection. The results, particularly those depicted in Figure 1, indicate significant run-to-run variability in peak width, characterized by a wide distribution and frequent outliers. These findings suggest that while the methodology effectively isolated high-quality data points, the underlying process may be experiencing instability or column degradation, especially given the persistence of outliers despite normalization efforts. The exclusion of flat baseline fractions and records with missing volume boundaries, while necessary for accurate metric calculation, introduces a selection bias that limits the assessment of overall run stability to peak-specific metrics. Future work should focus on investigating the root causes of the identified outliers, potentially by correlating them with specific `run_no` conditions or column statuses, and by expanding the analysis to include symmetry and height metrics to provide a more holistic view of peak integrity. Until these issues are resolved, the current data suggests a need for closer monitoring of the affected runs to ensure product quality and safety.
