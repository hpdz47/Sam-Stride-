#Abstract 

This report evaluates the data integrity and baseline stability of UV absorbance data (`UV_2_260_mAU`) from 32 chromatographic runs to ensure compliance with USP <621> and ICH Q2(R1) standards. The primary objective was to identify non-physical measurements, specifically negative absorbance values and high relative standard deviations (RSD), and to assess systematic drift affecting quantitative accuracy. Analysis of the raw dataset revealed significant data integrity issues, including physically impossible negative values and a distinct upward drift in baseline levels over time, indicative of sensor instability. To address these artifacts, an Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction algorithm was applied. Results indicate that while AsLS effectively removed high-frequency noise and negative artifacts, residual analysis revealed a persistent systematic upward trend in the residuals, suggesting that the initial correction parameters were insufficient to fully stabilize the baseline. This report concludes that while AsLS is a necessary preprocessing step, a secondary correction strategy is required to meet strict regulatory baseline stability criteria before proceeding with concentration calculations.

#Introduction 

The validity of biopharmaceutical quality metrics depends heavily on the integrity of chromatographic sensor data. This study focuses on the evaluation of UV absorbance data (`UV_2_260_mAU`) generated across 32 chromatographic runs. The analysis is driven by the need to identify and mitigate signal artifacts that violate USP <621> standards for baseline stability and ICH Q2(R1) requirements for analytical method validation. Critical data gaps, including the absence of critical process parameters such as injection volume and chromatography stage, necessitate a focus on run-level data preprocessing rather than flow-rate-dependent normalization. The primary motivation for this analysis was to distinguish between isolated sensor glitches and systematic baseline instability. The dataset, comprising over one million records, exhibited a high prevalence of non-physical measurements, including negative absorbance values and elevated RSDs, which compromise the accuracy of downstream concentration calculations (`Conc_B_%`). Consequently, this report details the methodology employed to detect outliers, analyze baseline trends, and apply corrective algorithms to restore data integrity.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three sequential phases: Data Integrity Assessment, Baseline Stability Trend Analysis, and Baseline Correction. In the first phase, the dataset was sorted chronologically by run number and fraction ID to handle missing values. A rigorous outlier detection protocol was implemented using Z-score and Interquartile Range (IQR) methods to flag non-physical measurements, defined as absorbance values below -1.0 mAU or RSD exceeding 2%. The second phase focused on characterizing baseline behavior. Mean absorbance values were aggregated per run to visualize temporal drift, while boxplots were generated to assess inter-run variability and the distribution of outliers. In the final phase, Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction was applied to the `UV_2_260_mAU` data using parameters lambda=1e6 and alpha=2e-4. This algorithm was selected to suppress high-frequency noise and remove negative artifacts. Following correction, residuals (Raw - Corrected) were calculated and analyzed to determine the efficacy of the correction in eliminating systematic bias. The analysis prioritized run-level statistics, excluding records with missing `Fraction_unique_ID` to ensure data validity.

#Results and Discussion 

The initial assessment of data integrity revealed significant challenges in the raw chromatographic data. As illustrated in Figure 1, the analysis of mean absorbance per run number demonstrated a distinct upward drift in baseline levels, particularly pronounced in the later runs (25–32). This trend indicates systematic instrument instability rather than random noise. Furthermore, the boxplot distribution of absorbance values per run highlighted substantial inter-run variability, with several runs exhibiting outliers extending into negative values. These negative absorbance values are physically impossible and confirm the presence of sensor artifacts that violate USP <621> requirements. The presence of these non-physical measurements necessitated the application of baseline correction algorithms prior to any quantitative concentration calculations. Following the application of the Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) algorithm, Figure 2 provides a detailed view of the correction process. The plot displays the raw absorbance data, the AsLS-corrected baseline, and the resulting residuals. The corrected baseline successfully tracks the underlying signal trend and effectively suppresses high-frequency noise, removing the negative artifacts identified in the integrity phase. However, the residual line exhibits a non-random pattern with a clear upward trend and increasing variance in the latter half of the runs. This observation suggests that while AsLS is effective for noise reduction, it did not fully eliminate the systematic bias present in the data, likely due to the specific drift profile or the chosen correction parameters (lambda=1e6, alpha=2e-4). Consequently, the data requires a secondary correction step, such as a moving average or polynomial fitting, to address the remaining systematic drift and ensure full compliance with baseline stability criteria.

#Conclusion 

This analysis confirms that the UV absorbance data (`UV_2_260_mAU`) from the 32 chromatographic runs suffers from significant baseline instability and the presence of non-physical negative values. The systematic upward drift observed across the runs, particularly in the later stages, compromises the accuracy of downstream quality metrics. The application of Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction successfully mitigated high-frequency noise and removed negative artifacts, demonstrating its utility as a primary preprocessing step. However, the residual analysis indicates that AsLS alone is insufficient to stabilize the baseline completely, as a systematic upward trend persists in the residuals. To ensure regulatory compliance with USP <621> and the validity of biopharmaceutical quality metrics, a secondary baseline correction method must be implemented to address the remaining systematic drift. Future work should focus on optimizing correction parameters or integrating additional smoothing techniques to achieve a stable baseline before proceeding with quantitative analysis.
