#Abstract 

This report details a rigorous data integrity assessment of High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) chromatographic data comprising 1.08 million fraction-level observations. The primary objective was to distinguish legitimate high-concentration peaks from detector saturation artifacts and experimental failures to ensure data quality for biopharmaceutical manufacturing. The analysis employed a multi-step workflow including mandatory filtering of physically impossible negative values and detector saturation events (>1500 mAU), followed by a reconciliation of volume discrepancies between primary and precise measurement columns. To prevent false positives arising from labeling inconsistencies, all statistical assessments were stratified by the 11 unique 'run' identifiers rather than the 32 'run_no' codes. The methodology successfully identified significant inter-run variability, quantified outlier density using Interquartile Range (IQR) and Z-Score methods, and calculated Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) to evaluate peak prominence. Results indicate that while the pre-processing steps effectively isolated corrupted records, the dataset exhibits substantial heterogeneity in absorbance profiles and outlier frequencies across different runs, necessitating targeted corrective actions.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the integrity of chromatographic data is paramount for ensuring product quality and regulatory compliance. This dataset contains 1.08 million observations derived from HPLC runs, characterized by a high prevalence of missing metadata (approximately 76% lacking 'Sample_Code') and data corruption indicators such as negative volume values and absorbance levels exceeding the detector's linear range (>1500 mAU). Standard analysis approaches often fail when applying stratification based on inconsistent identifiers, such as 'run_no', which can skew results due to labeling mismatches between 32 unique codes and only 11 actual experimental runs. Consequently, a robust analytical framework was required to first cleanse the data by removing physically impossible values and reconciling conflicting volume metrics. Subsequently, the analysis must stratify by the true experimental unit ('run') to accurately assess batch-specific variability, detect statistical outliers indicative of experimental failure, and quantify the Signal-to-Noise Ratio to differentiate true analyte peaks from baseline noise or saturation artifacts.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three distinct phases. First, Data Integrity and Pre-Processing involved loading the 'chromatography_combined.csv' file and applying strict filters to exclude rows with negative 'volume_ml' or UV absorbance values exceeding 1500 mAU. A reconciliation step flagged and addressed discrepancies where 'volume_ml' differed from 'fraction_volume_ml_min_precise' by more than 5% or was negative while the precise volume was valid. Metadata gaps were addressed using a forward-fill strategy within 'run' and 'column' groups, with unmatchable rows excluded. Second, Stratified Outlier and Peak Detection utilized the cleaned dataset to calculate baseline noise as the mean absorbance of the first 10 valid fractions per run. Statistical outliers were identified using both IQR and Z-Score methods (flagging values >3 sigma from the mean), while peaks were defined as local maxima exceeding (baseline noise + 3 sigma) within a 5-fraction smoothing window. Third, Artifact and Failure Source Attribution involved calculating the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) for each run, performing Spearman correlation analysis between SNR and outlier counts against the 'column' variable, and conducting ANOVA to test for significant differences in peak metrics across run groups. All visualizations and statistical summaries were stratified by the 11 unique 'run' identifiers to ensure accurate variability assessment.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully identified significant inter-run variability in the chromatographic data, confirming the necessity of stratifying by the unique 'run' identifier rather than the inconsistent 'run_no' codes. As illustrated in Figure 1, the top-left panel reveals substantial differences in median UV absorbance (280nm and 260nm) across the 11 experimental runs, with the presence of high-frequency outliers that align with the pre-processing goals of filtering saturation artifacts. The representative chromatograms in the top-right panel of Figure 1 provide qualitative evidence of sharp, high-amplitude peaks alongside baseline noise, validating the quantitative outlier detection thresholds applied. The bottom-left panel of Figure 1 demonstrates the calculated Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR), showing a direct measure of peak prominence relative to background noise, which varies significantly between run groups. Finally, the outlier count summary supports the attribution of failure sources, quantifying the frequency of statistical deviations. Collectively, these results indicate that the dataset contains a mix of valid high-concentration peaks and corrupted records, primarily driven by detector saturation and volume measurement errors, which were effectively isolated through the rigorous pre-processing and stratification protocols described. The high variability observed suggests that specific runs require immediate attention to ensure product quality before downstream processing.

#Conclusion 

The comprehensive data integrity assessment confirms that the HPLC dataset contains significant experimental variability and data corruption that must be addressed to maintain manufacturing standards. The rigorous application of negative value filtering, saturation thresholding, and volume reconciliation successfully isolated corrupted records, while the run-level stratification prevented the skewing of results caused by identifier inconsistencies. The detection of outliers and the calculation of SNR have provided a clear picture of data quality, highlighting specific runs with high outlier density and low signal-to-noise ratios. These findings enable the formulation of targeted corrective actions, such as re-running specific batches or adjusting detector gain settings, thereby ensuring that only high-quality, reliable data proceeds to downstream biopharmaceutical processing. Future work should focus on investigating the root causes of the identified outliers and implementing automated checks to prevent similar data integrity issues in subsequent runs.
