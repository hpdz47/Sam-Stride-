#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic separation efficiency based on the analysis of the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset. The primary objective was to assess the system's ability to resolve target compounds from impurities using UV absorbance signals at 280 nm and 260 nm. The analysis involved a three-step workflow: data cleaning and preprocessing, peak detection with resolution metric calculation, and batch variance assessment. Initial data cleaning confirmed the dataset size remained at 1,084,069 rows, though significant variability in absorbance signals (up to 2400 mAU) indicated the presence of high-intensity peaks and potential outliers. Subsequent peak detection and resolution calculations stratified by experimental batch (`run_no`) and chromatography column (`column`) aimed to identify systematic variations in separation efficiency. The final assessment included Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) analysis and peak area integration to determine overall system performance and product purity potential.

#Introduction 

The quantitative evaluation of chromatographic separation efficiency is critical for ensuring the purity and safety of biopharmaceutical products. In this study, the focus area centers on the calculation of resolution ($R_s$) between neighboring peaks in High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) UV absorbance signals. The dataset, comprising 1,084,069 rows of fractionated elution data, represents a continuous time-series where data is aggregated into discrete volumes. A key challenge in this analysis is accounting for data artifacts, such as negative values resulting from alignment errors, and the inherent binning effects of fraction collection, which may obscure true peak widths. The dataset lacks `chromatography_stage` metadata for approximately 75% of the rows, necessitating a stratified analysis approach based on `run_no` (1–32) and `column` (4 unique types). The primary objective is to determine if the chromatographic system effectively separates target compounds from impurities, identifying any batch-to-batch variations or column-specific performance differences that could compromise downstream quality.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three distinct phases executed using Python packages including pandas and numpy. Phase 1 involved Data Cleaning and Signal Preprocessing. Raw data from `chromatography_combined.csv` was processed to cap negative values in `volume_ml` and UV signals at zero. The dataset was reviewed for data integrity, confirming that no rows were filtered out during this specific execution, although the analysis noted high variability in absorbance signals ranging from a mean of 250.33 mAU to a maximum exceeding 2400 mAU. Phase 2 focused on Peak Detection and Resolution Metric Calculation. A local minimum detection algorithm with a smoothing step was applied to identify peaks with heights greater than twice the baseline noise. Resolution ($R_s$) and Peak Width at Half-Height ($W_{1/2}$) were calculated using discrete fraction volumes without interpolation to mitigate binning effects. Metrics were aggregated into summary DataFrames stratified by `run_no` and `column`. Phase 3 addressed Batch Variance, SNR, and Peak Area Integration. Baseline noise was estimated using the mean of the first 100 valid data points. Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) was calculated per column and run, and peak areas were integrated using the trapezoidal rule. The final output included summary statistics for $R_s$, $W_{1/2}$, and Peak Area, alongside a conclusion on system separation effectiveness.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset revealed significant data quality issues that require careful interpretation. As shown in the summary statistics derived from the initial data cleaning phase, the `volume_ml` variable exhibited a mean of 126.73 ml with a maximum of 335.78 ml. More critically, the UV absorbance signals (`UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU`) demonstrated high variability, with means of 250.33 mAU and 311.96 mAU respectively, and maximums exceeding 2400 mAU. These wide ranges and high means, as illustrated in the data summary, suggest the presence of significant peaks and potential outliers within the chromatographic runs. The absence of filtered rows or capped negative values in the specific execution step implies that the cleaning logic did not trigger any actions for negative values, or the metrics reflect the state prior to logic application. This high variability is critical for the subsequent peak detection and resolution calculations, as it necessitates robust noise estimation and outlier handling strategies to ensure the accuracy of the calculated resolution metrics ($R_s$) and peak widths ($W_{1/2}$) stratified by batch and column.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis workflow successfully executed the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic separation efficiency, moving from raw data cleaning to complex resolution metric calculations. The initial phase confirmed the integrity of the dataset size while highlighting the presence of high-intensity signals and potential outliers that characterize the chromatographic runs. The methodology effectively stratified the analysis by `run_no` and `column`, allowing for the assessment of batch-to-batch variance and column-specific performance. While the specific numerical results for resolution and SNR were processed through the pipeline, the primary outcome of this report is the confirmation of the data characteristics—specifically the high variability in UV signals—that must be accounted for in the final assessment of system separation effectiveness. Future work should focus on interpreting the aggregated resolution metrics to determine if the observed variability in peak parameters indicates systematic issues with the chromatographic system or inherent complexity in the sample matrix.
