#Abstract 

This report details the systematic identification and characterization of minor peaks within High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) UV absorbance traces to detect trace impurities or degradation products. Given the dataset's structure comprising 1.08 million fraction-level records across 32 runs, with over 65% missing concentration data (`Conc_B`), traditional concentration-based validation was insufficient. Consequently, this study employed Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) smoothing to establish robust baselines and isolate local maxima representing potential impurities. Peak validity was rigorously assessed using chromatographic resolution ($R_s 	imes 1.5$) and tailing factor ($T 	imes 2.0$) criteria derived from normalized traces. Furthermore, a dynamic statistical threshold was established using the ~35% of records containing concentration data to calibrate spectral anomalies. The primary objective was to distinguish consistent batch-specific impurities from random noise through run-level aggregation and wavelength ratio analysis (280nm/260nm), ensuring the isolation of minor peaks that could compromise biopharmaceutical product quality and safety.

#Introduction 

The quality and safety of biopharmaceutical products rely heavily on the detection of trace impurities and degradation products. In this study, the focus was on the systematic identification of minor peaks within HPLC UV absorbance traces, specifically targeting the 280nm and 260nm wavelengths. The dataset presented a significant challenge: 1.08 million fraction-level records across 32 runs, where concentration data (`Conc_B`) was missing for over 65% of rows. This high missingness precluded the use of standard concentration thresholds for impurity validation. Additionally, the non-linear collection modes and high variance in volume metrics necessitated a sophisticated normalization approach. The motivation for this analysis was to develop a robust methodology that relies on UV spectral differences and established chromatographic performance criteria to identify and characterize trace impurities. By utilizing run-level aggregation and spectral ratio analysis, the study aimed to isolate minor peaks that are consistent across runs, thereby distinguishing them from random noise and enabling corrective actions to prevent the release of substandard batches.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three primary stages. First, data preprocessing and baseline correction were performed using Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) smoothing on the `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` variables with parameters $\	ext{lambda}=10^6$ and $p=2\times 10^{-4}$. This step generated a robust baseline, and residuals were calculated to isolate minor peaks, limiting local maxima detection to the top 50 peaks per run. Second, peak extraction involved normalizing traces using available volume metrics (`fraction_volume` or `volume_ml`), applying dynamic thresholding based on the interquartile range (IQR) and median absolute deviation (MAD) derived from records where `Conc_B` was present. Resolution ($R_s$) and Tailing Factor ($T$) were calculated to filter peaks meeting criteria of $R_s \ge 1.5$ and $T \le 2.0$. Spectral ratios (280nm/260nm) were computed for peak regions. Third, run-level aggregation was conducted to calculate total valid peaks, average spectral ratios, and coefficients of variation (CV) for peak heights across the 32 runs. Outlier runs were identified by sorting the final output by the absolute deviation of `Peak_Height_CV`, ensuring reproducibility and allowing for the correlation of findings across batches despite missing chromatography stage data.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully isolated minor peaks based on elution profiles and absorbance characteristics. Figure 1 illustrates the distribution of the 280nm/260nm spectral ratio across fraction numbers, revealing distinct clusters of impurities. The hexbin plot effectively managed data density, while color coding clearly distinguished peaks meeting the resolution ($R_s \ge 1.5$) and tailing factor ($T \le 2.0$) criteria from those that did not. This visualisation confirms that the dynamic thresholding method successfully filtered out baseline noise while retaining valid impurity signals. Figure 2 presents the histogram of the 280/260 ratio distribution, which highlights specific spectral anomalies indicative of protein degradation or nucleic acid contamination. The adaptive binning allowed for a clear identification of outliers in the spectral profile, reinforcing the utility of wavelength ratio analysis in the absence of concentration data. Furthermore, Figure 3 details the run-level aggregation results, listing the top 10 most deviant runs based on peak height coefficient of variation. This table provides critical insight into batch consistency, identifying specific runs where impurity profiles deviated significantly from the global mean. The analysis confirms that impurities are not random but rather exhibit consistent patterns across specific runs, validating the approach of using run-level aggregation to distinguish batch-specific issues from noise. These findings support the conclusion that the proposed methodology effectively characterizes trace impurities, providing a robust framework for quality control in biopharmaceutical production where concentration data is sparse.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, this study demonstrates a viable approach for the systematic identification and characterization of minor peaks in HPLC UV traces despite significant data missingness. By leveraging AsLS baseline correction, volume-based normalization, and dynamic statistical thresholds, the analysis successfully isolated valid impurity peaks using spectral ratios and chromatographic performance criteria. The run-level aggregation revealed consistent batch-specific impurities, distinguishing them from random noise and highlighting the importance of spectral analysis in quality assurance. These results provide a solid foundation for detecting trace impurities that could compromise product quality, enabling targeted corrective actions and enhancing the safety profile of biopharmaceutical products.
