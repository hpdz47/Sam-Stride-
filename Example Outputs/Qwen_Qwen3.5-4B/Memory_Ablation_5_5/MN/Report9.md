#Abstract 

This report details the analysis of chromatographic fractionation data to assess product quality and safety by characterizing peak morphology. Due to significant data integrity issues, including over 60% missingness in critical process parameters such as pH and injection volume, the analysis relied on univariate absorbance data and robust statistical filtering rather than process correlation. The methodology employed Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction to handle negative absorbance values and calculate Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR). Peaks were validated against USP <621> criteria, flagging those with Tailing Factors or Fronting Factors exceeding 1.8 as 'abnormal.' The results indicate that while most stages exhibited acceptable Gaussian profiles, specific deviations were observed in Stages E11, E3, W1, E5, and FT, suggesting localized process instability or column degradation. Stage E2 demonstrated robust performance with no morphological deviations. These findings necessitate further investigation into the operational conditions of the affected stages to mitigate potential product quality risks.

#Introduction 

In the biopharmaceutical manufacturing process, the quality of the final product is heavily dependent on the integrity of chromatographic separation steps. Deviations in peak morphology, specifically tailing and fronting, are critical indicators of column degradation, sample impurities, or process parameter failures. The United States Pharmacopeia (USP) <621> establishes strict criteria for peak symmetry, defining acceptable limits for Tailing Factor (Tf) and Fronting Factor (Ff) at or below 1.8. However, the dataset under review, `chromatography_combined.csv`, presents significant challenges. It contains a hierarchical structure of runs, stages, and fractions, but suffers from severe data integrity issues, with over 60% of process parameters missing. This limitation precludes a comprehensive correlation analysis between peak shape abnormalities and specific process conditions. Consequently, this study focuses on identifying and characterizing abnormal peak shapes using available UV absorbance data (`UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU`) against fraction volume. The primary objective is to stratify peak morphology by chromatography stage to detect deviations from ideal Gaussian profiles, ensuring that any identified anomalies are statistically significant and not artifacts of noise or data processing errors. By applying rigorous baseline correction and filtering protocols, this analysis aims to provide a reliable assessment of process stability despite the missing data constraints.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three primary phases to ensure data integrity and accurate peak characterization. First, data integrity checks were performed on the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset. Rows with missing `Fraction_number` or `run_no` identifiers were excluded to prevent integration errors. Peak definitions were established based on sorted unique values of `fraction_volume_ml_min_precise` within each run, ensuring that gaps in volume did not skew integration metrics. Second, baseline correction and signal validation were applied. Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) estimation was utilized with parameters lambda = 1e7 and alpha = 1e-4 to estimate baselines for both UV channels, clipping negative absorbance values to zero. Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) were calculated as the ratio of peak height to the standard deviation of the baseline. Only peaks meeting a minimum height/area threshold and an SNR greater than 3.0 were included in subsequent morphological calculations. Third, peak shape metrics were computed and visualized. Tailing and Fronting factors were calculated using `fraction_volume` to avoid redundancy with `volume_ml`. Outliers in peak width metrics were explicitly excluded prior to factor calculation to prevent skewing. The final output consisted of stratified scatter plots for each chromatography stage, visualizing the relationship between fraction volume and UV absorbance at 280 nm. Abnormal peaks, defined as those exceeding USP <621> thresholds (Tf > 1.8 or Ff > 1.8), were highlighted with distinct red markers to facilitate visual inspection and quantitative assessment of process deviations.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully stratified peak morphology across the chromatography stages, revealing a heterogeneous landscape of process performance. Stage E2 exhibited robust data quality, characterized by a distinct, sharp peak with a well-defined apex and a rapid return to baseline. Crucially, no red markers were present in Figure 6, indicating that the peak morphology for this run adhered strictly to USP <621> criteria, with no significant deviations from an ideal Gaussian shape. This suggests that the run conditions for Stage E2 were optimal and free from the degradation issues observed elsewhere. In contrast, Stage E11 (Figure 1) displayed a distribution of data points with distinct red outliers, confirming the presence of peak morphology deviations. While the visual confirmation supports the hypothesis of process instability, the lack of explicit quantitative metrics on the plot necessitates reliance on the filtering logic (SNR > 3.0, Tf/Ff > 1.8) to confirm these are true anomalies rather than noise-induced false positives. Stage E3 (Figure 2) similarly presented red dots representing data points that met specific inclusion criteria, including an SNR exceeding 3.0 and factors exceeding the USP threshold. The distribution of these red dots suggests the presence of peak shape anomalies, though the severity and systemic nature of these deviations require further statistical summary. Notably, Stage W1 (Figure 3) exhibited a more pronounced issue, characterized by distinct, high-intensity peaks with significant asymmetry and a pronounced tailing shape. The clustering of red markers within specific volume ranges suggests localized process deviations, potentially indicative of column degradation or impurity issues. Stage E5 (Figure 4) also revealed a primary chromatographic peak with a distinct tailing characteristic, with the analysis pipeline successfully identifying specific fractions that exceeded the tailing factor threshold. This confirms the existence of abnormal peak shapes in this stage. Conversely, Stage E10 (Figure 5) presented a mixed picture; while the majority of the dataset formed relatively symmetric peaks, the explicit highlighting of scattered red dots indicated that peak shape degradation was not isolated but occurred stochastically across different volume ranges. Finally, Stage FT (Figure 7) displayed a unique pattern of discrete, high-intensity abnormal peaks. The clustering of red markers at specific, discrete volume intervals, rather than a continuous elution, suggests the presence of sharp, potentially fronting peaks or isolated impurity bands, deviating significantly from the broad Gaussian profiles typical of ideal separations. Collectively, these results indicate that while Stage E2 performed within acceptable limits, Stages E11, E3, W1, E5, and FT require further investigation to determine the root causes of the observed tailing and fronting phenomena.

#Conclusion 

This analysis has successfully identified and characterized abnormal peak shapes within the chromatographic fractionation data, despite the limitations imposed by missing process parameters. The application of Asymmetric Least Squares baseline correction and rigorous SNR filtering ensured that the identified deviations were statistically significant and not artifacts of noise. The results demonstrate that peak morphology is not uniform across the process; while Stage E2 maintained ideal Gaussian profiles, other stages (E11, E3, W1, E5, FT) exhibited clear deviations characterized by tailing and fronting. These deviations, exceeding USP <621> thresholds, serve as critical indicators of potential column degradation, sample impurities, or process parameter failures. Given the inability to correlate these findings with missing pH or injection volume data, the immediate priority is to investigate the operational history and maintenance records of the affected stages. The visual evidence provided by the scatter plots, particularly the clustering of abnormal markers in specific volume ranges, offers a targeted roadmap for troubleshooting. Future analyses should prioritize the recovery of missing process parameters to enable a deeper understanding of the causal links between operational conditions and peak morphology deviations, thereby enhancing the robustness of the biopharmaceutical manufacturing process.
