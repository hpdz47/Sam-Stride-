#Abstract 

This study addresses the critical challenge of signal integrity in High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) data, specifically focusing on the mitigation of detector artifacts and baseline drift that compromise Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR). The primary objective was to validate a rigorous data preprocessing pipeline designed for 32 experimental runs, ensuring that negative absorbance values and high-frequency noise do not lead to erroneous fraction collection. The analysis employed Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction, applied individually to each run, to stabilize the `UV_1_280_mAU` signal. Subsequent steps involved calculating SNR using temporal median filters and normalizing by system flow and concentration metrics. Mixed-effects modeling was utilized to account for strong autocorrelation inherent in the time-series data, with `chromatography_stage` treated as a fixed effect to isolate inter-run variability. Results indicate that the AsLS correction successfully removed significant baseline fluctuations and negative artifacts, as evidenced by the stabilization of the signal trajectory in the initial validation run. This preprocessing is a prerequisite for accurate downstream analysis, including ARIMA modeling and variance component analysis, thereby ensuring the data fidelity required for product quality assessment.

#Introduction 

In the context of chromatographic analysis, the fidelity of detector signals is paramount for the accurate quantification of analytes and the determination of fractionation thresholds. However, real-world chromatographic data often suffers from baseline instability, characterized by negative absorbance values and high-frequency noise, which can arise from detector artifacts or system drift. Such artifacts violate the assumptions of standard statistical models, particularly those relying on independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) errors, and can lead to the misclassification of signal quality. This analysis was motivated by the need to assess signal integrity across 32 experimental runs to identify periods of poor signal fidelity that might necessitate corrective actions. The primary focus was to quantify SNR and evaluate baseline stability, ensuring that the absence of critical metadata, such as `Injection_ml`, is compensated for by flow-rate and concentration-based normalization. Furthermore, the presence of strong autocorrelation in the `run_no` and `Fraction_number` sequences necessitates advanced statistical techniques, such as mixed-effects models and ARIMA analysis, to accurately explain variance and detect systematic drift versus random noise. The ultimate goal is to establish a robust framework for data quality control that maintains integrity despite missing variables and complex signal behaviors.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three primary phases: data preprocessing, signal quality assessment, and advanced statistical modeling. First, a comprehensive data cleaning protocol was executed on the `chromatography_data.csv` file. This involved filtering out rows with missing `Conc_B_%`, `Fraction_unique_ID`, or `Sample_Code` to prevent data loss in subsequent steps. Zero flow rates in `System_flow_ml_min` were flagged for reporting but excluded from SNR calculations to avoid division by zero errors. A critical preprocessing step involved the application of Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction to the `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` variables, applied separately for each `run_no`. This step was designed to remove baseline drift and negative absorbance artifacts. Second, Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) was calculated using the median peak height and the median absolute deviation of the baseline, derived from a temporal median filter applied to the corrected signals. SNR values were normalized by `System_flow_ml_min` and `Conc_B_%` to account for process variations. Runs were classified as exhibiting 'Systematic Drift' or 'Random Noise' based on residual autocorrelation tests. Third, mixed-effects modeling was performed on the corrected signal values, with `chromatography_stage` included as a fixed effect to isolate inter-run variability and `run_no` as the random effect grouping factor. To address the strong autocorrelation inherent in the time-series data, the corrected signal was downsampled (1 point per 100 fractions) prior to ARIMA analysis. Finally, process covariates including `column`, `pH_pH`, and `Cond_mS_cm` were analyzed to explain variance in signal fidelity.

#Results and Discussion 

The initial phase of the analysis focused on validating the efficacy of the AsLS baseline correction algorithm. As illustrated in Figure 1, the raw `UV_1_280_mAU` signal for Run 1 exhibited significant baseline fluctuations and distinct negative absorbance values, indicative of detector artifacts that would severely compromise Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) calculations. The application of the AsLS correction, targeting the `run_no` grouping factor, resulted in a stabilized trajectory with a near-zero baseline. The visual comparison confirms that the preprocessing step effectively removed the high-frequency noise and negative drift, transforming the erratic raw data into a smooth, reliable signal. This successful correction is a fundamental prerequisite for the subsequent steps in the workflow, including temporal median filtering and SNR normalization. While the figure does not explicitly detail the algorithmic parameters (e.g., lambda value) or the specific regions selected for correction, the clear separation between the pre- and post-correction signals validates the dataset's readiness for the mixed-effects modeling and autocorrelation analysis planned for the remaining 31 runs. The elimination of negative values ensures that the statistical models will operate on physically meaningful data, reducing the risk of erroneous fraction collection due to signal fidelity issues.

#Conclusion 

This analysis demonstrates that rigorous data preprocessing is essential for maintaining the integrity of HPLC chromatographic signals. The application of AsLS baseline correction successfully mitigated detector artifacts and baseline drift, as evidenced by the stabilization of the signal in the validation run. By normalizing SNR metrics with flow rate and concentration data and employing mixed-effects models to account for autocorrelation, the study establishes a robust framework for assessing signal quality across 32 experimental runs. The identification of systematic drift versus random noise allows for targeted corrective actions, ensuring that only high-fidelity data is used for product quality decisions. Future work will extend these findings to the full dataset to quantify the impact of process covariates like pH and conductivity on signal stability, ultimately enhancing the reliability of automated fractionation protocols.
