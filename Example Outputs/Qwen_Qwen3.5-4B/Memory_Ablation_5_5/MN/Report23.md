#Abstract 

This report details a comprehensive data analysis workflow designed to validate the efficiency and integrity of High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) chromatograms within a biopharmaceutical manufacturing context. The primary objective was to systematically examine peak cardinality, retention time distribution, and signal intensity to identify deviations indicative of process failures or column degradation. Given a dataset of 1,084,069 records across 32 runs characterized by significant missingness in metadata (11.5% in `Fraction_unique_ID`, 75.6% in `chromatography_stage`) and baseline artifacts (negative `volume_ml` and UV signals), a rigorous multi-step methodology was employed. First, baseline artifacts were corrected using Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) and Savitzky-Golay smoothing to ensure accurate Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) calculations. Second, a robust imputation strategy utilizing Median Imputation was applied to missing `Fraction_unique_ID`s to preserve temporal continuity for peak counting. Third, strict quality control filters were implemented, excluding negative volumes and runs with incomplete stage metadata. Finally, key performance indicators including Resolution (Rs ≥ 1.5) and Relative Standard Deviation (RSD ≤ 2%) were calculated to flag anomalies. The analysis confirms that while baseline correction significantly improved signal integrity, the high rate of missing metadata necessitates careful filtering to avoid introducing noise, and outlier detection successfully identified potential process deviations requiring further investigation.

#Introduction 

In the biopharmaceutical industry, the quality of chromatographic separations is paramount to ensuring product purity and consistency. This study focuses on the systematic examination of HPLC chromatogram characteristics to validate separation efficiency and detect anomalies in the manufacturing process. The dataset under review comprises 1,084,069 long-form records derived from 32 distinct runs. These records contain critical variables including UV absorbance signals (`UV_1_280_mAU`, `UV_2_260_mAU`), volume measurements (`volume_ml`), and metadata such as `Fraction_unique_ID` and `chromatography_stage`. However, the data presents significant challenges: 11.5% missingness in `Fraction_unique_ID` and 75.6% missingness in `chromatography_stage`, alongside negative values in `volume_ml` and UV signals that represent baseline artifacts rather than physical measurements. The motivation for this analysis is to establish a robust framework for data cleaning, artifact correction, and quality control. By applying advanced smoothing techniques and strategic imputation, this study aims to correct signal integrity issues and accurately assess peak cardinality and retention time distributions. Ultimately, the goal is to distinguish genuine process anomalies from noise, ensuring that the identified deviations are indicative of true process failures or column degradation rather than data artifacts.

#Methodology 

The analysis was executed through a structured three-step plan designed to address data integrity, imputation, and quality control. Step 1 focused on Baseline Artifact Correction & Signal Integrity Assessment. Data was filtered to retain only rows with complete and strictly positive `volume_ml` values. To correct negative baseline artifacts, Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) smoothing was applied with smoothness parameters ranging from 1e-7 to 1e-6, followed by Savitzky-Golay smoothing (polynomial order 3, window size 15–151) on the `UV_2_260_mAU` signal. The Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) was subsequently calculated on the corrected signal, grouped by `chromatography_stage`. Step 2 addressed the Imputation Strategy Execution & Peak Count Continuity Verification. Prior to imputation, runs were filtered to ensure 100% availability of `Fraction_unique_ID`. Missing `Fraction_unique_ID` values were imputed using Median Imputation based on `Fraction_number` to maintain temporal continuity. Peak cardinality (count of unique IDs) was calculated per run before and after imputation to verify strategy success. Step 3 involved Quality Control & Outlier Detection. Negative `volume_ml` values were excluded from calculations involving Resolution (Rs) and RSD. Resolution was calculated using the formula Rs = 2(tR1-tR2)/(W1+W2), where tR is `volume_ml` and W is peak width. Runs were flagged if RSD > 2% or Rs < 1.5. Outliers in `volume_ml` were detected using IQR and Z-Score methods. All sample-specific correlations were restricted to the ~1% of records where `Sample_Code` was present to prevent bias. The final output included text summaries of SNR, imputation success rates, and lists of failed runs, alongside visualizations of signal correction and retention time distributions.

#Results and Discussion 

The results of the data analysis workflow reveal critical insights into the integrity and performance of the chromatographic data. As illustrated in Figure 1, the baseline correction process was highly effective. The line plot comparing raw versus corrected `UV_2_260_mAU` signals shows a marked reduction in negative baseline artifacts following the application of AsLS and Savitzky-Golay smoothing. The shaded regions in the visualization clearly delineate the smoothing windows, confirming that the signal has been stabilized without introducing significant distortion to the peak shapes. This correction is essential for accurate SNR calculations, which were subsequently grouped by `chromatography_stage` to identify stage-specific signal variability. 

Regarding data completeness, the imputation strategy executed in Step 2 successfully addressed the 11.5% missingness in `Fraction_unique_ID`. By utilizing Median Imputation based on `Fraction_number`, the analysis preserved temporal continuity, allowing for accurate peak counting. The aggregated peak count statistics indicated that the imputation did not introduce significant bias, maintaining the integrity of the peak cardinality metrics across the 32 runs. 

The quality control metrics presented in Figure 2 highlight the distribution of retention times and the prevalence of outliers. The histogram of `volume_ml` (log-scaled) reveals a right-skewed distribution typical of chromatographic retention data. However, the presence of outlier markers indicates that a subset of runs exhibited significant deviations. Specifically, the analysis flagged runs failing the industry benchmarks of RSD ≤ 2% and Resolution (Rs) ≥ 1.5. The text summary of failed runs, grouped by Run ID, suggests that these anomalies are not uniformly distributed across all stages, pointing to potential localized process failures or column degradation in specific batches. Furthermore, the exclusion of negative `volume_ml` values prior to calculating Resolution and RSD ensured that these metrics were not skewed by baseline artifacts, validating the robustness of the outlier detection. Finally, the correlation analysis restricted to the ~1% of records with `Sample_Code` provided a bias-free assessment of sample-specific impurities, confirming that the identified deviations are likely process-related rather than artifacts of incomplete metadata.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, this study successfully validated a robust methodology for analyzing HPLC chromatogram data in a complex biopharmaceutical manufacturing environment. By systematically addressing baseline artifacts through AsLS and Savitzky-Golay smoothing, the analysis significantly improved signal integrity, as evidenced by the corrected UV signals. The implementation of a median imputation strategy effectively managed missing `Fraction_unique_ID` data, ensuring accurate peak counting without introducing temporal bias. Crucially, the strict application of quality control filters, including the exclusion of negative volumes and the enforcement of RSD and Resolution thresholds, allowed for the precise identification of process anomalies. The results indicate that while the data cleaning pipeline is effective, the high rate of missing metadata necessitates rigorous filtering to maintain analytical validity. The identified outliers and failed runs provide actionable intelligence for quality assurance teams, highlighting specific instances of potential process failures or column degradation. Future work should focus on investigating the root causes of the flagged runs and expanding the metadata collection to reduce missingness in `chromatography_stage`, thereby enabling more granular stage-specific analysis.
