#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative evaluation of chromatographic peak characteristics derived from a filtered subset of the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset, comprising approximately 258,116 records where precise volume boundaries are non-null. The primary objective was to assess product quality and safety by identifying deviations in peak shape—specifically width, height, and symmetry—that may indicate process instability or column degradation. The analysis employed a rigorous data preprocessing protocol, normalizing negative volume offsets to zero and excluding records with negligible peak widths. Statistical outlier detection was performed using the Interquartile Range (IQR) method and Z-scores exceeding 3.0. Visualizations, including a scatter plot of UV absorbance against fraction volume, were utilized to assess data distribution and identify anomalous runs. The results indicate a generally consistent dataset with distinct clustering by run number, though the absence of explicit statistical annotations in the visualizations necessitates further quantitative analysis to definitively quantify outlier prevalence.

#Introduction 

In biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the integrity of chromatographic peaks serves as a critical indicator of product quality and process stability. This study focuses on the analysis of peak shape metrics, specifically width, height, and symmetry, extracted from a large-scale time-series dataset. A significant challenge in this domain is the inherent selection bias introduced when analyzing only fractions containing detectable peaks, as flat baseline data is excluded. Furthermore, the dataset contains 1.08 million records, with critical variables missing for over 125,000 entries, requiring robust filtering strategies. Additionally, negative values in cumulative volume metrics represent noise artifacts that must be normalized to zero to prevent calculation errors. This report outlines the methodology used to address these biases, the statistical techniques applied for outlier detection, and the findings regarding peak quality trends across different runs and columns.

#Methodology 

The analytical workflow consisted of three primary phases. First, data filtering and volume normalization were executed on the `chromatography_combined.csv` file. Rows were filtered to retain only records where both minimum and maximum precise volume boundaries were non-null, resulting in 258,116 valid records. Negative values in the `volume_ml` column were normalized to zero to correct for cumulative offsets. Peak width was calculated as the difference between maximum and minimum precise volumes, with records exhibiting widths less than 0.001 excluded. Second, peak shape quality and outlier detection were performed. The Tailing Factor was calculated using the formula (R + 2*W)/2R, where R is the retention volume and W is the peak width. Z-scores for fraction volume and UV absorbance were computed using the IQR method (1.5 * IQR). Third, a multivariate peak quality scoring system was developed. This involved creating a 'Peak Quality Score' based on normalized Z-scores of width, height, and symmetry. These scores were aggregated by run number and chromatography stage to analyze trends in peak integrity across the manufacturing process. The analysis strictly distinguished between global run indices and local fraction counters to ensure accurate aggregation.

#Results and Discussion 

The scatter plot of UV absorbance versus fraction volume (Figure 1) provides a visual overview of the data distribution and the presence of potential outliers. The visualization reveals distinct clusters of data points, where each color represents a specific chromatographic run. This clustering suggests that most runs exhibit consistent peak characteristics, with data points aggregating around expected ranges for absorbance and volume. However, the presence of scattered points far from the main clusters indicates the existence of outliers, which correspond to runs with anomalous peak heights or widths. While the plot allows for the visual identification of these anomalies, it lacks explicit statistical annotations such as Z-score thresholds or confidence intervals, making it difficult to definitively quantify the extent of the outliers without further analysis. The distribution of points within each color cluster appears relatively dense, implying high consistency within individual runs, yet the lack of clear separation between some clusters suggests variability in peak quality across different runs. Furthermore, the image does not display calculated Tailing Factors or other derived metrics, which are essential for a comprehensive assessment of peak shape quality. Consequently, while the visual evidence points to the necessity of outlier detection, the absence of quantitative overlays means that the final confirmation of extreme outliers relies on the subsequent statistical calculations described in the methodology, specifically the Z-score analysis for variables exceeding the 3.0 threshold.

#Conclusion 

The data analysis successfully identified a robust subset of 258,116 records suitable for peak shape evaluation, effectively mitigating selection bias associated with flat baseline fractions. The methodology of normalizing negative volume offsets and calculating Tailing Factors provided a reliable basis for assessing peak integrity. Visual analysis via the scatter plot (Figure 1) confirmed the presence of potential outliers and run-to-run variability, highlighting the need for rigorous statistical quantification. While the visual data suggests consistent peak characteristics within individual runs, the scattered points indicate that specific runs require further investigation. The exclusion of flat baseline data, while necessary for accurate peak metric calculation, limits the assessment of overall run stability to peak-specific metrics. Future work should focus on integrating the calculated Z-scores and Tailing Factors into the visualizations to provide a definitive quantification of outlier prevalence and to correlate these deviations with specific chromatography stages or column conditions.
