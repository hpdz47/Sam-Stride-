#Abstract 

This report evaluates the structural integrity and signal quality of chromatographic time-series data to determine its suitability for automated feature extraction and predictive modeling. The analysis addresses critical data quality issues, including physical impossibilities such as negative cumulative volumes and severe signal noise, while assessing the impact of missing process parameters on signal reliability. Initial data cleaning corrected 32,007 records with negative volumes, resulting in a dataset of 1,084,069 records with a mean volume of 126.73 ml. Pre-correction Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) were found to be low (0.62 and 0.64), indicating significant background noise. Following Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction and Z-score normalization, the analysis focused on signal integrity. Visual assessment of the normalized UV_2_260_mAU signals reveals overlapping distributions between samples with present and missing pH data, suggesting no significant separation in signal quality based on pH availability. However, the analysis highlights extreme signal instability, with a reported Relative Standard Deviation (RSD) of 164.36%, far exceeding the USP <621> precision criteria (RSD ≤ 2%). Furthermore, a Pearson correlation between the mean UV signal and the complete concentration metric (`Conc_B_%`) was not explicitly detailed in the provided visual outputs, though the plan indicates a threshold of 0.85 is required for validation. The findings indicate that while structural errors have been resolved, the signal quality remains compromised by high variance, and the reliance on UV signals as a standalone concentration proxy requires rigorous statistical validation due to the presence of missing process parameters.

#Introduction 

The objective of this study is to evaluate the structural integrity and signal quality of longitudinal chromatography data to support automated feature extraction and subsequent predictive modeling. Chromatographic datasets often contain complex longitudinal time-series data where signal validity is paramount. However, this specific dataset presents significant challenges, including structural data errors such as negative cumulative volumes and severe structural missingness (approximately 11.5%) in key process variables like `Conc_B_ml` and `pH_pH`. These missing process parameters are critical proxies for concentration, and their absence could introduce selection bias if the UV signal is used as the sole basis for modeling. Additionally, the data exhibits high-frequency noise, evidenced by low pre-correction Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) and extreme signal variance. The primary motivation for this analysis is to determine whether the UV signal can serve as a reliable standalone proxy for concentration despite these data quality issues. This requires a multi-step approach: first, correcting physical impossibilities to ensure data consistency; second, applying rigorous baseline correction and normalization to meet USP <621> precision criteria; third, assessing the impact of missing process parameters on signal reliability by checking for selection bias and retention time drift; and finally, validating the UV signal against the complete concentration metric using correlation analysis. The success of the proposed predictive modeling pipeline depends on resolving these data quality hurdles.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three primary phases to address structural integrity, signal quality, and validity assessment. The first phase involved Data Cleaning and Physical Consistency Checks. Negative cumulative volumes (`volume_ml`) were clipped to zero to correct physical impossibilities. Descriptive statistics, including mean and standard deviation, were calculated for the corrected volume data. Pre-correction SNR for UV channels at 260nm and 280nm was computed using signal versus background standard deviations to establish a baseline for noise levels. The second phase focused on Signal Quality Assessment and Missingness Impact Analysis. Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction was applied with a lambda parameter of 1000, followed by Z-score normalization of the UV signals. The impact of missing process parameters, specifically `pH_pH`, was analyzed by comparing mean normalized UV signals between groups with present and missing data. A t-test was performed to assess statistical significance, and visualizations were generated to inspect for retention time drift. The third phase addressed UV Signal Validity as a Concentration Proxy. Runs were pre-aligned by calculating alignment error against the mean trace, excluding runs with errors exceeding 5 mAU. A simple Pearson correlation was then performed between the mean UV signal of each run and the complete `Conc_B_%` metric, ensuring a minimum fraction count of 50 per run. This phase aimed to provide a go/no-go recommendation based on whether the correlation coefficient exceeded 0.85.

#Results and Discussion 

The results of the data analysis reveal significant challenges regarding signal integrity and data consistency. In the initial data cleaning phase, 32,007 records containing physically impossible negative cumulative volumes were identified and corrected to zero. This correction resulted in a final dataset of 1,084,069 records, with a mean volume of 126.73 ml and a standard deviation of 81.72 ml, indicating high variability in the collected fractions. Pre-correction analysis showed low Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) of 0.62 and 0.64 for the UV channels at 260nm and 280nm respectively, suggesting significant background noise relative to the signal. Following the application of ALS baseline correction (lambda=1000) and Z-score normalization, the signal quality assessment provided critical insights into the impact of missing process parameters. As illustrated in Figure 1, the left panel displays a boxplot of normalized UV_2_260_mAU signals split by pH_pH availability. The visualization reveals overlapping interquartile ranges and medians between the 'Present' and 'Missing' groups, suggesting no significant separation in signal distribution based on pH data presence. The right panel of Figure 1 presents a scatter plot of Fraction_number versus UV_2_260_mAU, colored by pH availability. The points form a continuous trend line that appears consistent across both color groups, indicating no obvious retention time drift or signal degradation associated with missing pH measurements. However, despite the visual consistency, the analysis reports an extreme instability in the signal, evidenced by a reported RSD of 164.36%. This value far exceeds the USP <621> threshold of ≤2%, suggesting that the baseline correction or normalization process may not have fully mitigated high-frequency noise, or that the signal variance is inherently high. The absence of explicit statistical annotations, such as t-test p-values, in the figure necessitates reliance on accompanying text for rigorous inference regarding selection bias. While the visual evidence supports the conclusion that missing pH data does not introduce detectable signal drift, the severe signal variability remains a critical barrier to using the UV signal as a reliable concentration proxy. The Pearson correlation analysis, intended to validate the UV signal against `Conc_B_%`, was planned but the specific correlation coefficient and the final go/no-go recommendation are not explicitly detailed in the provided visual outputs, leaving the ultimate suitability of the UV signal for predictive modeling uncertain.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, this analysis has successfully addressed structural data errors, such as negative cumulative volumes, and quantified the pre-correction noise levels in the chromatographic dataset. However, the evaluation of signal quality indicates that the current data processing pipeline, while employing standard baseline correction and normalization techniques, fails to meet the stringent precision criteria required for robust predictive modeling. The extreme signal variability, characterized by an RSD of 164.36%, poses a significant risk to the reliability of the UV signal as a standalone proxy for concentration. Although visual assessments suggest that missing pH data does not induce retention time drift or signal degradation, the high inherent noise levels and the lack of explicit statistical validation regarding the correlation with the complete concentration metric (`Conc_B_%`) prevent a definitive go-ahead for automated feature extraction. Future work must focus on optimizing the baseline correction parameters to reduce signal variance, potentially exploring alternative normalization methods, and conducting a comprehensive statistical analysis to quantify the correlation between the UV signal and the concentration metric. Until these issues are resolved, the reliance on UV signals for predictive modeling remains unsupported by the current data quality metrics.
