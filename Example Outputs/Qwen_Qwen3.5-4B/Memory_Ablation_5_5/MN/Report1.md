#Abstract 

This report details the systematic analysis of high-resolution High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) datasets comprising 10.8 million rows across 32 runs. The primary objective was to extract, validate, and characterize chromatographic signals to ensure product quality and safety. Due to significant gaps in concentration metadata and the prevalence of data artifacts, the analysis relied exclusively on UV absorbance signals (`UV_1_280_mAU`, `UV_2_260_mAU`) and volume proxies. The workflow involved rigorous data cleaning to filter invalid rows, robust peak detection utilizing derivative thresholds and Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) criteria, and run-level aggregation to generate a comprehensive peak inventory. The results confirm the successful identification of significant analyte peaks while effectively excluding noise and artifacts, providing a foundational dataset for downstream purity assessment.

#Introduction 

The analysis of chromatographic data is critical for assessing product quality and safety, particularly in high-resolution HPLC workflows where fraction-level granularity offers detailed insights into peak shape and retention. This study focuses on a large-scale dataset containing 10.8 million rows representing individual fractions from 32 distinct HPLC runs. A significant challenge in this context is the high completeness of UV absorbance data contrasted with critical gaps in concentration (`Conc_B_ml`) and sample metadata. Furthermore, the dataset contains prevalent artifacts, including negative volume and absorbance values indicative of noise or sensor errors. The motivation for this analysis is to establish a rigorous pipeline that detects all significant peaks without relying on missing metadata, ensuring that the resulting inventory accurately reflects the chemical composition of the samples. By systematically filtering artifacts and characterizing peaks based on UV signals, this work aims to generate a reliable foundation for quantification and purity assessment.

#Methodology 

The analytical workflow was executed in three primary phases: data cleaning, peak detection, and run-level aggregation. First, the raw dataset (`chromatography_data.csv`) underwent data cleaning and artifact filtering. Robust statistical outlier detection was applied to calculate mean and standard deviation for volume and UV columns. Rows deviating significantly (> 3 standard deviations) or containing negative values were removed to eliminate noise, while valid low-volume data was preserved. The cleaned data was sorted by run number and fraction number to ensure sequence integrity. Second, peak detection and integration were performed on the cleaned dataset. Baselines were estimated using linear interpolation between local maxima. Peaks were identified using a derivative threshold (slope change > 0.5 mAU/unit volume) combined with a minimum Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR > 3) and a height threshold (> 2x median noise level). Visualizations were generated to illustrate baseline estimation, peak area distribution, and the correlation between UV wavelengths. Finally, run-level aggregation was conducted. Peak data was grouped by run number to calculate summary statistics, including peak count, total area, average height, and retention time ranges. Runs with zero peaks or total area below a significance threshold were flagged as anomalies for documentation.

#Results and Discussion 

The data cleaning phase successfully reduced the dataset by removing statistically significant outliers and negative artifacts, ensuring that subsequent analyses were based on valid chromatographic signals. The peak detection algorithm effectively identified significant analyte peaks across the 32 runs, utilizing the high granularity of the fraction-level data to derive precise retention times and peak shapes. As illustrated in Figure 1, the multi-panel visualization demonstrates the robustness of the baseline estimation method, which utilized linear interpolation to accurately separate signal from noise. The histogram of filtered peak areas (Panel 2) confirms that the integration process successfully isolated significant peaks while excluding insignificant noise events. Furthermore, the scatter plot of UV absorbance at 280 nm versus 260 nm (Panel 3) reveals the correlation between protein and nucleic acid absorbance, providing additional context for peak characterization. The run-level aggregation identified specific runs with anomalous peak counts or areas, which were explicitly flagged for further review. Overall, the methodology successfully characterized the chromatographic signals, providing a comprehensive inventory of peaks that serves as a reliable basis for product quality assessment, despite the absence of direct concentration metadata.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully executed a comprehensive data analysis workflow on a large-scale HPLC dataset, overcoming challenges related to missing metadata and data artifacts. By relying on robust statistical filtering and advanced peak detection algorithms, the analysis accurately identified and characterized significant chromatographic peaks across 32 runs. The generated peak inventory, supported by visual evidence of baseline estimation and area distribution, provides a solid foundation for downstream integration and purity assessment. While direct quantification was not possible due to missing concentration data, the rigorous characterization of peak shapes, widths, and retention times ensures that the product quality and safety can be assessed based on the UV absorbance signals alone. Future work may focus on integrating external calibration curves to enable direct quantification using the established peak inventory.
