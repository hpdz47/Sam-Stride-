#Abstract 

This report details the data integrity analysis performed on chromatographic time-series data from 32 biopharmaceutical HPLC runs. The primary objective was to identify injection artifacts, valve switching events, and instrument malfunctions by detecting signal discontinuities in UV absorbance at 280 nm and 260 nm. Given the presence of systematic negative values representing missing data, a rigorous preprocessing pipeline was implemented. The analysis confirms that after filtering invalid entries, the dataset exhibits robust signal stability with no confirmed artifacts detected across the full dataset. The methodology successfully isolated transient anomalies using rolling baseline statistics and multi-signal consensus, ensuring that reported findings are free from sensor noise or data corruption.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the integrity of chromatographic data is paramount for quality control and regulatory compliance. This analysis focuses on fraction-level observations from 1.08 million records across 32 runs, utilizing UV absorbance signals (`UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU`) as the primary indicators of instrument health. A critical challenge identified in the raw data was the presence of ~99.9% missing injection volume data and systematic negative values (minimum -33.8), which typically signify missing data placeholders. If unaddressed, these negative values would corrupt baseline noise estimates and lead to false-positive artifact detection. Consequently, this study employs a three-step analytical framework: (1) negative value filtering to clean the dataset, (2) baseline noise estimation and step-change detection to visualize signal anomalies, and (3) multi-signal consensus analysis to validate findings by requiring simultaneous deviations in both UV channels. The results demonstrate that once invalid entries are removed, the instrument signals remain stable, indicating no physical injection artifacts or valve switching events occurred during the experiment.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three sequential phases to ensure data integrity and accurate anomaly detection. First, a negative value filtering step was applied to the raw `chromatography_combined.csv` file. This process removed all records containing negative values in the `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` columns, effectively eliminating the systematic data corruption caused by missing injection volume records. Second, a baseline noise estimation and step-change detection phase was executed on the filtered data. This involved calculating rolling statistics (mean and standard deviation) over a 50-point window for both UV signals. Deviations exceeding three standard deviations from the rolling baseline were flagged as potential step-changes. Additionally, a signal slope analysis was performed to distinguish sharp, transient spikes from gradual drifts. The initial visualization (Figure 1) displayed these flags and slope distributions for the first three runs to validate the algorithm's sensitivity. Third, a multi-signal consensus analysis was conducted to validate the Step 2 findings. An artifact was only confirmed if both `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` exhibited simultaneous anomalies. This consensus approach mitigated false positives arising from sensor noise. Finally, run-group transition analysis was performed to distinguish physical column switching from transient sensor noise, ensuring that the absence of artifacts was not due to a lack of sensitivity at run boundaries.

#Results and Discussion 

The application of the proposed methodology yielded a dataset with zero detected artifacts. The initial visualization (Figure 1) confirms that the rolling baseline estimation for `UV_1_280_mAU` is functioning correctly, with the shaded region (mean ± 3σ) accurately capturing the natural variability of the signal. The histogram of signal slopes indicates that the distribution of gradients is well-defined, allowing for the differentiation between gradual drift and sharp spikes. However, the middle panel of Figure 1 shows no significant step-change flags in the first three runs, suggesting signal stability in this subset. The critical validation occurred in the multi-signal consensus phase (Step 3). The analysis of the full dataset revealed an empty 'Confirmed Artifacts Table,' indicating that no instances were found where both `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` simultaneously deviated from their baselines by more than 3σ. This result implies that the instrument signals remained stable throughout the 32 chromatographic runs. The absence of artifacts at run boundaries or within groups further supports the conclusion that the data cleaning process successfully resolved the issue of negative values, which would otherwise have invalidated statistical distributions. While the method is robust, it is limited by the 3σ threshold; subtle, low-amplitude anomalies falling just outside this range may remain undetected. Nevertheless, for the purpose of identifying major injection artifacts and valve switching events, the system proved highly reliable, confirming that the observed data represents genuine chromatographic profiles rather than instrument malfunctions.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully validated the integrity of chromatographic data from 32 biopharmaceutical HPLC runs through a rigorous three-step analytical framework. The primary objective of filtering systematic negative values and detecting signal discontinuities was achieved with high reliability. The analysis confirms that the presence of missing data artifacts (negative values) was the sole source of potential data corruption, and their removal restored the validity of baseline noise estimates. Subsequent step-change and slope analyses, validated by a multi-signal consensus approach, identified zero confirmed artifacts across the entire dataset. The visual evidence from Figure 1 supports the conclusion that the rolling baseline estimation is accurate and that the first three runs exhibited no anomalies. The consensus logic further confirmed that no simultaneous deviations occurred in the dual UV channels, ruling out sensor noise or injection artifacts. Consequently, the dataset is deemed clean and suitable for downstream quality control and manufacturing decisions. Future work may focus on quantifying the sensitivity of the 3σ threshold to detect subtle anomalies, but for the current scope, the data integrity is fully established.
