#Abstract 

This report details the analysis of chromatography data to assess retention time reproducibility in the absence of explicit timestamps. Due to high missingness in flow rate variables (~66%) and sample identifiers, the study focuses on run-level consistency using cumulative volume profiles. The primary objective was to determine if variability in fraction volumes stems from random noise or systematic process drift. Data cleaning protocols were applied to filter entry errors and impute missing flow rates using run-specific means. Subsequently, the slope of cumulative volume versus fraction number was calculated for each of the 11 runs, serving as a proxy for flow rate. Statistical analysis classified runs as 'stable' or 'drifting' based on Z-score thresholds relative to the mean slope. The results indicate that while general trends are consistent, specific runs exhibit deviations that require further investigation to distinguish between stochastic variation and systematic instrument instability.

#Introduction 

In biopharmaceutical manufacturing, maintaining consistent separation conditions is critical for product quality. Retention time reproducibility is a key metric for this consistency; however, precise calculation is often hindered by data acquisition limitations. In this dataset, the absence of an explicit time column necessitates the estimation of retention time reproducibility through volume-based metrics, specifically the slope of cumulative volume profiles. Furthermore, critical variables such as `System_flow_ml_min` and `Sample_Code` exhibit high missingness rates (approximately 66% and 75%, respectively), limiting the granularity of the analysis. Consequently, this study restricts its scope to aggregate run-level comparisons rather than sample-specific checks. The primary motivation for this analysis is to evaluate whether observed variability in fraction volumes is attributable to random experimental noise or systematic process drift, which would indicate a failure in maintaining the constant flow rates required for reliable chromatographic separation.

#Methodology 

The analytical workflow consisted of three primary steps designed to handle data integrity issues and quantify inter-run variance. First, a data cleaning and integrity verification phase was conducted on the `chromatography_combined.csv` file. This involved filtering out negative values in the `volume_ml` column to remove potential entry errors, while excluding the `Sample_Code` variable from filtering due to its high categorical missingness. Missing values in the `System_flow_ml_min` variable were imputed using the mean flow rate specific to each run. Second, a flow rate reconstruction phase utilized the cleaned and imputed data. The slope of `volume_ml` versus `Fraction_number` was calculated for each of the 11 distinct runs. This slope represents the actual flow rate for that specific run, annotated directly on the resulting visualization. Third, an inter-run variance analysis was performed to differentiate between noise and drift. The Coefficient of Variation (CV) of the calculated slopes was computed. A Z-score threshold approach was employed: runs with slopes within two standard deviations of the mean were classified as 'stable' (random noise), while those exceeding this threshold were classified as exhibiting 'systematic drift'. This method avoided inappropriate clustering techniques like K-Means for univariate scalar comparison.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the 11 experimental runs reveals distinct patterns in flow rate consistency. As visualized in Figure 1, the cumulative volume profiles generally show an increasing trend across all runs, confirming the expected accumulation of eluent. However, the annotated slopes indicate significant variations in flow rates between runs. The plot demonstrates that while the overall direction of the data is consistent, the steepness of the lines varies, reflecting differences in the calculated flow rates. This visualization serves as the primary evidence for assessing the reproducibility of the separation conditions. The data cleaning step successfully removed negative volume entries, ensuring the integrity of the cumulative profiles. The imputation of missing flow rates allowed for the calculation of slopes for all runs. The subsequent statistical classification identified specific runs as exhibiting systematic drift, deviating from the mean flow rate by more than two standard deviations. These deviations suggest that the observed variability is not merely random noise but may point to systematic process instabilities. The high missingness of flow rate data underscores the difficulty in achieving high-fidelity retention time analysis, yet the volume-based slope analysis provides a robust proxy for evaluating the stability of the chromatographic system across the 11 runs.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully approximated retention time reproducibility by evaluating the consistency of cumulative volume profiles across 11 runs, despite significant data limitations including missing timestamps and high missingness in flow rate variables. The methodology of using run-specific mean imputation and slope-based flow rate calculation proved effective in generating comparable metrics. The results indicate that while the general trend of increasing cumulative volume is consistent, there are notable variations in flow rates between runs. Statistical analysis using Z-score thresholds identified specific runs as exhibiting systematic drift rather than random noise. These findings suggest that the chromatographic system may not be maintaining consistent separation conditions across all runs, which could impact biopharmaceutical product quality. Future work should focus on investigating the root causes of the identified drift and potentially implementing real-time monitoring to mitigate such variations in subsequent experiments.
