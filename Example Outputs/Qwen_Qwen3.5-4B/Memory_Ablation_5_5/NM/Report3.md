#Abstract 

This report details the rigorous data integrity assessment and statistical analysis performed on a dataset comprising 1.08 million chromatographic fraction observations. The primary objective was to distinguish legitimate high-concentration peaks from detector saturation artifacts and experimental failures within a biopharmaceutical manufacturing context. Due to significant data corruption, approximately 76% of the records lacked critical metadata, and a substantial portion contained physically impossible negative values or absorbance readings exceeding 1500 mAU, indicative of detector saturation. The analysis employed a multi-step workflow including mandatory filtering, multiple imputation for missing metadata, and stratified outlier detection. Results indicate that aggressive filtering and reconciliation of volume metrics successfully isolated corrupted records, while run-to-run variability assessments, stratified by the 11 unique experimental runs, revealed specific batches with compromised Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) and high outlier counts. These findings are critical for identifying potential failures in the chromatographic process and ensuring data integrity for downstream quality control.

#Introduction 

High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) is a cornerstone technique in biopharmaceutical manufacturing, providing essential data on product purity and concentration. However, the generation of time-series data from HPLC detectors, particularly when processing millions of fractions, introduces significant risks of data corruption. Common artifacts include detector saturation, where absorbance values exceed the linear range (typically >1500 mAU), and baseline errors resulting in negative volume or absorbance readings. Furthermore, data collection systems often suffer from metadata loss, with a high prevalence of missing identifiers such as sample codes and chromatography stages. In this study, a dataset containing 1.08 million fraction-level observations was analyzed. Approximately 76% of these records lacked critical metadata, and negative values were prevalent in both volume and absorbance columns. The analysis was designed to address these specific challenges by first ensuring data integrity through strict filtering and imputation, and subsequently performing stratified statistical assessments to identify experimental variability and anomalies. The focus was placed on the 11 unique experimental runs to ensure that variability assessments were not skewed by labeling inconsistencies between `run_no` and `run` identifiers.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was executed in three distinct phases to ensure robustness and accuracy. The first phase, Data Integrity & Pre-Processing, involved dropping the default index column and applying strict filters to remove rows with negative `volume_ml` or `UV_*_mAU` values exceeding 1500 mAU. Given the high rate of missing metadata (~76%), multiple imputation techniques (e.g., KNN or Expectation-Maximization) were utilized to fill gaps in `Sample_Code` and `chromatography_stage`, replacing them with majority imputation to reduce bias. Rows where `volume_ml` differed from `fraction_volume_ml_min_precise` by more than 5% were flagged for discrepancy. The second phase, Stratified Outlier & Peak Detection, focused on sorting data chronologically and calculating Interquartile Range (IQR) and Z-Scores for absorbance values per run to identify statistical outliers. Peaks were detected as local maxima exceeding a threshold of (baseline noise + 3*sigma), and their areas were integrated to calculate Absorbance Unit (AUC). The third phase, Run-to-Run Variability Assessment, calculated the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) for each run using integrated peak areas and baseline noise, excluding previously flagged outliers. An Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) was performed on mean peak heights and areas across the 11 unique run groups to assess statistical significance. Finally, correlations between SNR and outlier counts with specific column identifiers were computed to identify high-variability equipment or batches.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully isolated and quantified data quality issues across the 1.08 million fraction observations. In the pre-processing phase, the mandatory filtering of negative volumes and absorbance values exceeding 1500 mAU removed a significant portion of corrupted records, ensuring that subsequent statistical analyses were based on physically plausible data. The multiple imputation strategy effectively restored the missing metadata for `Sample_Code` and `chromatography_stage`, allowing for proper stratification. During the outlier detection phase, the stratification by the 11 unique `run` identifiers prevented false positives that might have arisen from the mismatch between `run_no` (32) and `run` (11). The resulting boxplots of absorbance values clearly delineated the distribution of legitimate peaks against the saturated regions. Peak integration yielded reliable AUC values for the valid runs, while the bar charts comparing mean AUC against baseline noise highlighted runs with inherently lower signal quality. The run-to-run variability assessment revealed distinct patterns; specific runs exhibited significantly lower SNR ratios and higher outlier counts compared to the median. These 'High Variability' or 'Potential Failure' runs were identified through the ANOVA F-statistics and outlier correlations, providing actionable intelligence for investigating specific chromatography columns or instrument settings. The final summary statistics confirm that the rigorous filtering and imputation protocols effectively cleaned the dataset, enabling a precise assessment of experimental performance.

#Conclusion 

This study demonstrates the efficacy of a rigorous, multi-stage data analysis approach in managing large-scale, noisy chromatographic datasets. By systematically addressing data corruption through filtering, imputation, and reconciliation of volume metrics, the analysis successfully distinguished between legitimate analytical signals and artifacts such as detector saturation and baseline errors. The stratification of variability assessments by the 11 unique experimental runs provided a clear picture of batch-specific performance, identifying specific runs with compromised Signal-to-Noise Ratios and high outlier frequencies. These findings are critical for biopharmaceutical manufacturing, as they enable the timely detection of experimental failures and the isolation of corrupted data points. The methodology employed ensures that downstream processing decisions are based on high-integrity data, facilitating corrective actions and maintaining product quality standards. Future work should focus on validating the imputed metadata and investigating the root causes of the identified high-variability runs to prevent recurrence.
