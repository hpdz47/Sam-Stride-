#Abstract 

This report details the initial phase of a comprehensive data analysis workflow designed to evaluate the structural integrity and signal quality of chromatographic time-series data. The primary objective is to determine the suitability of UV signals (260nm/280nm) for automated feature extraction and predictive modeling. The analysis commenced with a critical data cleaning step, addressing physical impossibilities such as negative cumulative volumes. Following this, an assessment of signal quality was conducted by calculating initial Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) and establishing baseline statistics for the corrected dataset. Results indicate that while negative volume errors were successfully resolved, the raw UV signals exhibit high noise levels relative to the signal, resulting in low initial SNR values (~1.1-1.2). This suggests that the raw data is currently insufficient for direct feature extraction without further preprocessing, specifically baseline correction and normalization, to meet industry precision criteria (USP <621>). The report concludes that the current dataset requires advanced signal processing before it can be reliably used for modeling.

#Introduction 

The analysis focuses on a dataset containing longitudinal chromatography data, specifically examining UV absorbance traces at 260nm and 280nm alongside process variables such as concentration and pH. The structural integrity of this data is compromised by two primary factors: the presence of physical impossibilities, such as negative cumulative volumes, and significant missingness in key process parameters (approximately 11.5% missing data for `Conc_B_ml` and `pH_pH`). These issues pose a risk to the reliability of any subsequent predictive modeling pipelines. The motivation for this analysis is to systematically address these data quality issues. First, negative volumes must be corrected to ensure physical consistency. Second, the high variance and noise in the UV signals must be mitigated to meet USP <621> precision criteria, which requires a Relative Standard Deviation (RSD) of ≤ 2%. Finally, the analysis must determine if the UV signal can serve as a valid proxy for concentration despite the missing process data and potential retention time drifts. This report outlines the methodology and initial findings regarding data cleaning and signal quality assessment.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a structured three-step plan focused on data cleaning and physical consistency checks. The primary data source was `chromatography_combined.csv`, containing variables including `volume_ml`, `UV_2_260_mAU`, `UV_1_280_mAU`, and `Conc_B_%`. Step 1 involved correcting physical impossibilities by clipping all negative `volume_ml` values to zero. Following this correction, statistical summaries were generated, including the mean and standard deviation of the corrected volumes. Additionally, initial Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) were calculated for the UV channels at 260nm and 280nm. The SNR was computed using the formula: Signal Mean / (Signal Standard Deviation / Background Standard Deviation), where the background standard deviation was defined as the mean standard deviation of the lowest 10% intensity regions of the signal. This methodology ensured that the dataset was physically consistent before proceeding to more complex signal quality assessments. The analysis successfully identified the extent of data entry errors and established baseline metrics for the corrected dataset.

#Results and Discussion 

The initial phase of the analysis revealed significant data quality issues that necessitate further intervention. After applying the correction protocol to clip negative cumulative volumes to zero, the dataset was successfully cleaned. The corrected dataset contains 1,084,069 records. The mean volume of the corrected dataset is 126.73 ml with a standard deviation of 81.72 ml. Crucially, the count of negative `volume_ml` values dropped to 0, resolving a physical impossibility previously noted in the exploratory data analysis (EDA) where the range was [-33.8, 335.8].

Regarding signal quality, the calculation of initial SNRs for the UV channels yielded values of 1.21 for the 260nm channel and 1.14 for the 280nm channel. These values were derived using a background definition based on the lowest 10% intensity regions. The low SNR values (~1.1-1.2) indicate that the raw data possesses high noise relative to the signal intensity. This finding is critical as it suggests the raw UV signals are currently insufficient for direct feature extraction. To meet the USP <621> precision criteria, which mandates an RSD of ≤ 2%, the current signal quality is compromised. The high variance observed in the volume data (std ~82 ml) implies similarly high variance in the UV signals.

The analysis confirms that while the correction of negative volumes was appropriate and necessary for cumulative metrics, the raw UV signals require advanced preprocessing. Specifically, the subsequent steps in the analysis plan, which include Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction and Z-score normalization, are essential to reduce noise and improve signal-to-noise ratios. Without these interventions, the data reliability for automated feature extraction and predictive modeling remains low. The presence of negative volumes also confirms significant data entry errors prior to this analysis step, validating the need for rigorous data cleaning protocols.

#Conclusion 

The initial data analysis successfully addressed the issue of negative cumulative volumes, restoring physical consistency to the dataset of 1,084,069 records. However, the assessment of signal quality reveals that the raw UV data is not yet suitable for direct use in predictive modeling. The low Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) of approximately 1.1-1.2 indicate high noise levels that prevent the data from meeting USP <621> precision criteria (RSD ≤ 2%). Consequently, the workflow must proceed to the next phase, which involves applying Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction and Z-score normalization to the UV signals. Only after these preprocessing steps can the data be evaluated for its potential to serve as a reliable proxy for concentration, particularly in the context of the identified ~11.5% missingness in process parameters. The current results validate the necessity of the planned advanced preprocessing steps to ensure data integrity and model reliability.
