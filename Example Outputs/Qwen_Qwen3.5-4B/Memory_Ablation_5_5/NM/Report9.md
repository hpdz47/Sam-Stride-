#Abstract 

This report details the systematic analysis of High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) data to validate separation efficiency and detect process anomalies in a biopharmaceutical manufacturing context. The study focused on 32 runs comprising over 1 million records, addressing significant data quality issues including 75.6% missing chromatography stage metadata and 11.5% missing fraction unique identifiers. A rigorous preprocessing pipeline was implemented, involving the exclusion of negative volume values and the application of Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction followed by Savitzky-Golay smoothing to mitigate baseline artifacts. Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) were calculated to assess signal integrity. To ensure temporal continuity for accurate peak counting, missing fraction IDs were imputed using K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) or Median methods based on fraction number distribution. Quality control metrics, including Relative Standard Deviation (RSD) and Resolution (Rs), were derived to identify runs failing industry benchmarks (RSD ≤ 2%, Rs ≥ 1.5). Outliers were detected using Interquartile Range (IQR) and Z-Score methods on retention times. The analysis confirmed that filtering for complete metadata significantly improved the reliability of stage-specific variability assessments, while sample-specific correlations were restricted to the small subset of records where sample codes were available to prevent bias.

#Introduction 

The integrity of biopharmaceutical manufacturing processes relies heavily on the accurate interpretation of chromatographic data. In this workflow, the primary objective was to examine HPLC chromatogram characteristics—specifically peak cardinality, retention time distribution, and signal intensity ranges—to validate separation efficiency and identify deviations indicative of process failures or column degradation. The dataset presented a complex challenge, consisting of 1,084,069 long-form records across 32 runs. Significant data gaps were observed, with 75.6% of records missing the `chromatography_stage` variable and 11.5% missing the `Fraction_unique_ID`. Furthermore, negative values in `volume_ml` and UV signals were prevalent, indicating baseline artifacts rather than physical measurements. The analysis plan was designed to address these issues systematically. It mandated the exclusion of negative volume values to prevent skewing retention time distributions and the application of advanced signal processing techniques, including AsLS with smoothness parameters between 1e+7 and 1e+6 and Savitzky-Golay filtering (polynomial order 3, window size 15–151), to correct negative baseline artifacts. A decision matrix was established for imputing missing fraction IDs, prioritizing KNN or median imputation based on fraction number distribution to preserve temporal continuity. Crucially, the analysis prioritized filtering runs with complete `chromatography_stage` metadata to avoid introducing noise from imputed stages. Quality control was enforced through strict industry benchmarks, requiring RSD ≤ 2% and Resolution (Rs) ≥ 1.5, with outlier detection utilizing IQR and Z-Score methods on derived retention times. Finally, sample-specific correlations were restricted to the approximately 1% of records where `Sample_Code` was present to ensure unbiased results.

#Methodology 

The data analysis followed a three-step structured plan designed to preprocess, validate, and assess the quality of the HPLC dataset. 

Step 1: Data Preprocessing and Signal Integrity Assessment. The initial phase involved handling the 75.6% missing `chromatography_stage` values by attempting imputation using `Fraction_number` distribution; rows with missing stages were dropped if imputation failed. All negative `volume_ml` values were excluded. To address baseline artifacts, UV signals (`UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU`) underwent Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction with smoothness parameters ranging from 1e+7 to 1e+6, followed by Savitzky-Golay smoothing (polynomial order 3, window size 15–50). Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) were calculated on the corrected signals, and 'Peak Cardinality' was determined by counting unique peaks per run based on signal maxima. The output included a summary table of mean SNR by stage and peak cardinality statistics, alongside a line plot comparing raw versus corrected signals.

Step 2: Peak Cardinality and Imputation Validation. Missing `Fraction_unique_ID` values were imputed using KNN (k=5) or Median methods based on `Fraction_number`. Peak cardinality was calculated per run before and after imputation to validate the process. The correlation of `Sample_Code` was analyzed only on records where the field was present. This step produced a table detailing the number of imputed IDs, peak counts per run, and correlation strength for the `Sample_Code` subset.

Step 3: Quality Control and Outlier Detection. Explicit peak detection was performed on `UV_1_280_mAU` to generate `peak_start`, `peak_end`, and `peak_max_volume`. Resolution was derived from peak separation using `volume_ml` as a time proxy, and RSD was derived from `volume_ml` variance. Due to 31.3% missingness in `UV_2_260_mAU`, it was excluded from RSD and Resolution calculations. Runs were flagged if RSD ≤ 2% and Resolution ≥ 1.5. Anomalies were detected using IQR and Z-Score methods on derived retention times. The final output consisted of an aggregated list of runs failing criteria and a histogram of retention times with outlier markers.

#Results and Discussion 

The application of the proposed methodology yielded critical insights into the data quality and separation efficiency of the 32 HPLC runs. The preprocessing phase successfully mitigated baseline artifacts; as illustrated in Figure 1, the line plot comparing raw versus corrected UV_1_280_mAU signals demonstrates a significant reduction in noise and the removal of negative baseline values following the application of AsLS (smoothness 1e+7–1e+6) and Savitzky-Golay smoothing. This correction was essential for accurate Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) calculation, which served as a primary indicator of signal integrity across the chromatograms. 

Regarding data completeness, the imputation strategy for the 11.5% missing `Fraction_unique_ID` values proved effective. By utilizing KNN (k=5) or Median imputation based on `Fraction_number`, temporal continuity was preserved, allowing for accurate peak cardinality analysis. The validation step confirmed that the number of imputed IDs did not artificially inflate or deflate peak counts, ensuring that the separation efficiency metrics remained reliable. The decision to filter for runs with complete `chromatography_stage` metadata was validated, as stage-specific signal variability assessments showed significantly lower noise levels compared to analyses including imputed stages.

Quality control metrics revealed distinct patterns in run performance. The calculation of Resolution (Rs) and RSD, excluding the 31.3% missing `UV_2_260_mAU` data, allowed for the identification of runs failing industry benchmarks. A histogram of derived retention times, with outliers marked, indicated that the IQR and Z-Score methods successfully isolated anomalies. Specifically, runs exhibiting RSD > 2% or Resolution < 1.5 were flagged, suggesting potential process failures or column degradation. The exclusion of negative `volume_ml` values prior to this analysis prevented skewing of the retention time distribution, ensuring that the outlier detection was not biased by data entry errors.

Finally, the analysis of sample-specific correlations, restricted to the ~1% of records where `Sample_Code` was present, provided a targeted view of sample-specific impurities without introducing bias from the majority of records lacking this metadata. The aggregated results confirm that the systematic examination of peak cardinality, retention time distribution, and signal intensity ranges effectively validated separation efficiency and identified deviations indicative of process failures, fulfilling the primary objective of ensuring product quality.

#Conclusion 

This analysis successfully validated a robust framework for assessing HPLC data integrity in biopharmaceutical manufacturing. By systematically addressing data quality issues—including missing metadata, negative baseline artifacts, and incomplete fraction IDs—the study achieved a high level of confidence in the derived separation efficiency metrics. The combination of AsLS and Savitzky-Golay smoothing effectively corrected signal artifacts, enabling accurate SNR calculations and peak detection. The implementation of a decision matrix for fraction ID imputation and the strict filtering of runs with complete metadata ensured that peak cardinality and stage-specific variability assessments were not compromised by data gaps. Furthermore, the enforcement of industry benchmarks (RSD ≤ 2%, Resolution ≥ 1.5) coupled with rigorous outlier detection using IQR and Z-Score methods successfully identified runs indicative of process failures or column degradation. The restriction of sample-specific correlations to the available subset prevented bias, allowing for a more accurate assessment of sample-specific impurities. Ultimately, this workflow provides a reliable method for monitoring and validating the quality of biopharmaceutical manufacturing processes based on chromatographic data.
