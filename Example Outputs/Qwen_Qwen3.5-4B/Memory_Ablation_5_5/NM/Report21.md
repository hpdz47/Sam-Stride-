#Abstract 

This report evaluates the structural integrity and signal quality of chromatographic time-series data to determine its suitability for automated feature extraction. Initial data cleaning corrected 1,084,069 records with physically impossible negative cumulative volumes. However, preliminary Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) calculations for the UV channels returned 'nan', indicating that baseline correction and noise estimation were not yet completed. Subsequent analysis applied Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction and Z-score normalization to the `UV_2_260_mAU` channel. The resulting SNR was calculated at 1.2109, which is significantly low and fails to meet USP <621> precision criteria (RSD ≤ 2%). Regarding process parameter missingness, the analysis addressed a reported ~11.5% missingness in `pH_pH`. Visualizations confirm that the normalization procedure was successfully applied, displaying standardized signal distributions. However, the dataset analyzed for bias assessment contained 0.00% missingness for `pH_pH`, rendering the t-test for selection bias and the assessment of signal drift incomplete. Consequently, while the signal exhibits high dynamic range, its reliability for automated feature extraction remains unquantified due to the low SNR and the inability to assess the impact of missing process data.

#Introduction 

The primary objective of this study is to assess the viability of using UV absorbance signals as a standalone proxy for concentration in predictive modeling pipelines. Chromatographic data often suffers from high signal variance and structural inconsistencies, which can compromise the accuracy of automated feature extraction. This analysis focuses on a dataset characterized by severe structural missingness in key process variables, specifically approximately 11.5% missing data for `pH_pH` and `Conc_B_ml`. Furthermore, the dataset contains critical data integrity issues, including negative cumulative volume values, which must be resolved prior to any signal processing. The analysis aims to determine if the UV signal quality, after appropriate preprocessing, meets regulatory precision standards and if the absence of process data introduces systematic bias that would invalidate the signal as a concentration proxy.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a three-step workflow designed to ensure data consistency and signal reliability. First, a physical consistency check was performed on the raw chromatography data. This involved identifying and correcting 1,084,069 records containing negative `volume_ml` values by clipping them to zero, thereby establishing a baseline for valid cumulative volume measurements. Second, a signal quality assessment was conducted. Initial attempts to calculate Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) for `UV_2_260_mAU` and `UV_1_280_mAU` yielded 'nan' values, indicating that prerequisite steps such as baseline correction and noise estimation from the lowest 10% intensity regions had not been executed. To address this, Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction and Z-score normalization were applied specifically to the `UV_2_260_mAU` channel. The final SNR was then recalculated using the mean signal divided by the standard deviation of the background (lowest 10% intensity regions). Third, an impact analysis of missing process parameters was performed. The study investigated the correlation between the availability of `pH_pH` and the signal profile. This involved comparing the distribution of normalized signal values between runs where `pH_pH` was recorded versus missing, and visualizing the relationship between fraction number and signal intensity to detect retention time drift. Statistical tests, including t-tests, were planned to quantify selection bias, though data availability limited the execution of these tests in the final report.

#Results and Discussion 

The data cleaning phase successfully resolved physical impossibilities, correcting 1,084,069 negative volume records to zero. However, the signal quality assessment revealed significant challenges. The initial SNR calculations for both UV channels returned 'nan', highlighting a gap in the preprocessing pipeline where baseline correction was not yet applied. Upon implementing ALS baseline correction and Z-score normalization, the SNR for the `UV_2_260_mAU` channel was determined to be 1.2109. This value is critically low, indicating that the signal is dominated by noise and fails to meet the USP <621> precision criteria requiring an RSD of ≤ 2%. This finding suggests that the current signal quality is insufficient for reliable automated feature extraction without further intervention. Regarding the impact of missing process data, the analysis focused on `pH_pH`, which was reported to have ~11.5% missingness in the broader context. Figure 1 presents a boxplot of the normalized UV signals stratified by `pH_pH` availability ('Present' vs. 'Missing'). While the plot confirms the successful application of normalization through the standardized spread of data, it does not explicitly display the t-test p-value. The analysis notes that the visual evidence allows for an immediate assessment of signal quality compromise; however, the specific dataset used for the bias assessment contained 0.00% missingness for `pH_pH`. Consequently, the t-test p-value and the mean normalized signal for the 'Missing' group were reported as 'nan', preventing a definitive conclusion on selection bias. Figure 2 provides a scatter plot of `Fraction_number` versus `UV_2_260_mAU`, colored by `pH_pH` availability. This visualization reveals a dense distribution of data points across the fraction range, suggesting the chromatographic run is well-sampled. The color coding indicates that the 'Present' and 'Missing' groups are intermingled, which would typically suggest random missingness; however, since the 'Missing' category is empty in the analyzed subset, this pattern cannot be fully interpreted. The wide spread of UV values, consistent with a dynamic range up to ~2500 mAU, underscores the necessity of the normalization steps performed. In summary, while the data cleaning and normalization procedures were executed, the resulting low SNR and the inability to assess bias due to the absence of 'Missing' data in the specific analysis subset limit the conclusion that the UV signal is currently suitable for predictive modeling.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the initial phase of the data analysis workflow has identified critical barriers to utilizing the UV signal for automated feature extraction. While negative volume values have been corrected, the Signal-to-Noise Ratio for the `UV_2_260_mAU` channel remains at 1.2109, failing to meet the required USP <621> precision standards. Additionally, the assessment of selection bias regarding the ~11.5% missingness in `pH_pH` is incomplete because the specific dataset analyzed contained no missing values for this variable, resulting in 'nan' values for the t-test and mean signal of the missing group. Therefore, the current data is not yet validated for use as a concentration proxy in predictive modeling pipelines. Further preprocessing steps, specifically those capable of improving the SNR to meet RSD ≤ 2% criteria, and strategies to address the structural missingness of process parameters, are required before the UV signal can be deemed reliable for downstream analysis.
