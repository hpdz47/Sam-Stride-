#Abstract 

This report evaluates the data integrity and feasibility of utilizing HPLC chromatographic records for downstream statistical modeling. The analysis focuses on three critical variables: `Conc_B_ml`, `pH_ml`, and `chromatography_stage`, which exhibit severe data quality issues including over 65% missingness and potential unit mislabeling. While initial imputation validation reports suggest acceptable error rates using simple Mean/Median strategies, a critical review of the analysis plan and data context reveals significant methodological inconsistencies. The reported 0.0000% error rates for `Conc_B_ml` and `pH_ml` are statistically improbable given the massive volume of missing data and the presence of negative values indicative of data corruption. Consequently, this report concludes that simple imputation strategies are insufficient and potentially misleading. The dataset requires a shift towards global modeling approaches or rigorous data correction to address the fundamental issues of missing process metadata and signal integrity before any robust modeling can be attempted.

#Introduction 

The objective of this data analysis workflow was to determine the suitability of high-resolution HPLC and MS chromatographic records for robust quality assurance modeling. The primary focus area involved rigorously evaluating signal integrity, data completeness, and unit consistency against specific domain thresholds. Key concerns included the presence of over 65% missingness in critical variables such as `Conc_B_ml`, `pH_ml`, and `chromatography_stage`, as well as the occurrence of negative absorbance values in UV variables, suggesting potential unit mislabeling. The analysis plan outlined a three-step process: validating signal integrity and unit consistency, assessing the impact of missingness on global modeling feasibility, and validating specific imputation strategies. However, the execution of this plan has yielded results that contradict the underlying data context, necessitating a detailed discussion of these discrepancies.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a structured three-step approach defined in the data analysis plan. First, signal integrity and unit consistency were validated by checking for negative values, calculating Z-scores for outliers, and correlating `_ml` and `_mAU` variants to detect mislabeling. Second, the impact of missingness was assessed by calculating missing percentages, stratifying by run number, and estimating performance degradation for global versus sample-specific modeling. Third, imputation strategies were validated using a hold-out set; simple Mean/Median imputation was applied to `Conc_B_ml` and `pH_ml` due to low cardinality rendering KNN infeasible, while MICE was proposed for `chromatography_stage`. The validation threshold was set at a 5% relative error rate. Despite the plan's recommendations, the execution relied heavily on simple statistical imputation methods without fully addressing the flagged data corruption issues identified in the initial signal validation phase.

#Results and Discussion 

The results presented in Figure 1 indicate that the imputation strategies for `Conc_B_ml`, `pH_ml`, and `chromatography_stage` met the defined threshold with a 0.0000% error rate for the former two variables and a 100% fill rate for the latter. The report recommends accepting these strategies. However, this conclusion is methodologically inconsistent with the broader data context. The analysis plan explicitly noted that these variables had over 65% missingness and that KNN was deemed infeasible due to low cardinality. A 0.0000% error rate on a dataset with 711,786 rows of missing data is highly suspicious and suggests that the imputation process may have failed to capture the true distribution of the missing values or that the hold-out set validation was flawed. Furthermore, the presence of negative values in the `_ml` variants, identified as potential data corruption or unit confusion (e.g., treating volume as concentration), contradicts the assumption that simple Mean/Median imputation is appropriate. The validity of the 0.0000% error rate is questionable as it may mask severe data quality issues, such as the unit confusion and negative absorbance values, potentially leading to biased downstream modeling if these errors are not addressed prior to imputation. The recommendation to use simple imputation contradicts the plan's context, which suggested MICE for `chromatography_stage` and flagged KNN as infeasible, indicating a disconnect between the analysis execution and the strategic requirements of the project.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, the current data analysis workflow has identified critical data quality failures that preclude the immediate use of the dataset for robust statistical modeling. While the imputation validation reports suggest success, the underlying data integrity issues—specifically the 65%+ missingness, negative values indicating unit mislabeling, and the implausibility of zero error rates—render the current findings unreliable. The dataset requires a fundamental shift in approach, likely involving a move away from sample-specific modeling due to the lack of process metadata, and a prioritization of data correction or advanced imputation techniques (such as MICE) that account for the specific nature of the corruption. Until these integrity issues are resolved, the dataset cannot be safely utilized for quality assurance or predictive modeling.
