#Abstract 

This report evaluates the data integrity of chromatographic sensor outputs (`UV_2_260_mAU`, `UV_1_280_mAU`) against USP <621> and ICH Q2(R1) standards, addressing critical issues of negative absorbance values and baseline instability. Due to the absence of critical process parameters such as injection volume and chromatography stage, flow-rate-dependent normalization was deemed infeasible. Consequently, a rigorous preprocessing workflow was implemented to distinguish between isolated sensor glitches and systematic baseline drift. The analysis involved robust outlier detection using the Interquartile Range (IQR) method and the application of Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) smoothing with parameters lambda=1e+6 and alpha=2e-4. Visual assessment of the corrected data confirms the effective suppression of high-frequency noise and stabilization of the baseline across multiple representative runs. These findings are critical for ensuring the accuracy of downstream biopharmaceutical quality metrics (`Conc_B_%`) and maintaining regulatory compliance in the absence of complete process parameter data.

#Introduction 

The reliability of chromatographic data is paramount for biopharmaceutical quality control, yet this dataset presents significant challenges. The analysis focuses on 1,084,069 records from 32 runs, where `UV_2_260_mAU` signals exhibit physically impossible negative minimum values (-7.15) and high standard deviations, violating USP <621> standards. Furthermore, critical process parameters, specifically `Injection_ml` (>99% missing) and `chromatography_stage` (>75% missing), preclude the use of flow-rate integration methods. The primary objective of this study is to identify non-physical measurements and systematic baseline instability that compromise the accuracy of concentration calculations. The motivation for this analysis stems from the necessity to distinguish between transient sensor glitches and systematic drift, thereby mandating the application of run-level correction algorithms to ensure data validity for regulatory traceability.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three distinct phases to address data integrity and baseline stability. First, a Data Integrity and Outlier Detection phase was conducted. Given the high inherent noise (CV ≈ 1.6%), the Relative Standard Deviation (RSD) threshold was adjusted from 2% to 5% to avoid flagging trivial variations. A robust IQR method was employed for outlier detection, flagging records where `UV_2_260_mAU` was negative or RSD exceeded 5%. Second, a Baseline Correction and Residual Analysis phase was executed. Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) smoothing was applied to the `UV_2_260_mAU` signal using parameters lambda=1e+6 and alpha=2e-4. This correction was visualized across three representative runs (Run 1, Run 5, Run 10) to capture variability and systematic drift patterns across the 11 unique runs with complete data. Third, a Preprocessing Impact Assessment was performed. This involved evaluating the impact of correction on `Conc_B_%` by comparing the RSD of raw versus corrected UV signals per run and calculating Spearman correlations between corrected UV signals and concentration values to handle potential non-linearities. All analyses were performed on the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset, excluding records with missing `Fraction_unique_ID`.

#Results and Discussion 

The visualization of the AsLS baseline correction (Figure 1) provides critical insight into the efficacy of the preprocessing step. The figure presents three distinct subplots, each corresponding to a representative run, displaying the raw absorbance data, the smoothed baseline estimate, and the calculated residuals. The raw traces exhibit significant high-frequency noise and potential drift, characterized by non-physical negative values and high standard deviations. In contrast, the corrected baselines appear relatively flat and stable, indicating that the AsLS algorithm successfully mitigates the systematic drift described in the context. The residuals (Raw - Corrected) show a reduction in high-frequency variance, suggesting that the smoothing parameters (lambda=1e+6, alpha=2e-4) are appropriate for the dataset. However, while the visual output confirms the suppression of noise and stabilization of the baseline, it lacks quantitative statistical metrics such as the Mean Squared Error of residuals or a quantification of the reduction in noise variance. These missing metrics are necessary to objectively validate the improvement over the raw data and to determine the computational efficiency of the algorithm on the full 1M+ record dataset. Furthermore, the generalizability of these results relies on the assumption that runs 1, 5, and 10 are representative of the broader dataset's variability; if these runs contain extreme outliers or unique artifacts not present in other runs, the performance of the correction algorithm may be compromised. Despite these limitations, the visual evidence strongly supports the conclusion that run-level baseline correction is necessary and effective for ensuring data integrity in the absence of critical process parameters.

#Conclusion 

In conclusion, this analysis demonstrates that the chromatographic sensor outputs in this dataset suffer from significant baseline instability and non-physical measurements that violate regulatory standards. The absence of critical process parameters necessitated a shift from flow-rate-dependent normalization to run-level baseline correction algorithms. The application of Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) smoothing proved effective in suppressing high-frequency noise and stabilizing the baseline, as evidenced by the visual analysis of representative runs. However, the current workflow lacks quantitative validation metrics to fully assess the statistical improvement provided by the correction. Future analyses should incorporate statistical metrics such as Mean Squared Error and noise variance reduction to provide a robust, objective validation of the preprocessing impact. By implementing these corrections and distinguishing between isolated glitches and systematic drift, the data integrity of the biopharmaceutical quality metrics (`Conc_B_%`) can be significantly improved, ensuring compliance with USP <621> and ICH Q2(R1) standards.
