#Abstract 

This report details a quantitative analysis of biopharmaceutical chromatographic data to characterize compound distributions and impurity profiles. Due to the >66% missingness of concentration data, the analysis relies exclusively on UV absorbance signals at 280 nm and 260 nm, utilizing peak area integration as the primary metric. The study employs a rigorous multi-step preprocessing pipeline, including Neighboring-Value Height Thresholding, Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction, and multi-Gaussian peak fitting, to isolate signals from baseline drift and noise. Results demonstrate a distinct separation between intermediate process stages (E1–E9) and the final formulation (FT) stage based on the 280/260 nm absorbance ratio. Intermediate stages exhibit high ratios (2.24–4.26), indicative of protein-rich environments with minimal nucleic acid interference, whereas the FT stage shows a significantly lower ratio (0.89), suggesting a shift in composition or dilution. Statistical analysis via ANOVA confirms that observed variances are driven by the chromatographic process steps rather than random run-to-run noise. However, the absence of sample-specific identifiers (76% missing Sample_Code) limits the scope to batch-level consistency checks. The developed methodology successfully generates stage-level fingerprints, validating the robustness of the signal extraction techniques despite data limitations.

#Introduction 

In the context of biopharmaceutical manufacturing, ensuring product consistency and identifying impurity profiles are critical for quality control. This analysis focuses on a dataset comprising 1.08 million granular time-series records derived from chromatographic runs. A significant challenge in this dataset is the >66% missingness of concentration data (`Conc_B_ml`), which renders traditional concentration-based normalization impossible. Furthermore, 76% of the records lack specific sample identifiers (`Sample_Code`), restricting profiling capabilities to the batch or stage level. To address these constraints, this study utilizes UV absorbance signals at 280 nm (associated with aromatic amino acids in proteins) and 260 nm (associated with nucleic acids). The primary objective is to quantify compound distributions across experimental runs and chromatography stages to ensure product consistency while explicitly excluding baseline artifacts and pre-run noise. This requires a sophisticated signal processing approach capable of handling high signal dynamic ranges (up to 1921 mAU for 280 nm and 2479 mAU for 260 nm) and distinguishing overlapping peaks through advanced fitting techniques.

#Methodology 

The analysis followed a three-step data processing workflow designed to handle high signal dynamic ranges and missing data constraints. First, data preprocessing involved loading the `chromatography_combined.csv` file and filtering for valid integration windows. Rows were excluded where `volume_ml` was negative or where UV signals were missing. A boolean mask was created to ensure only physically collected fractions, defined by `fraction_volume_ml_min_precise` and `fraction_volume_ml_max_precise`, were analyzed. Second, peak detection, baseline correction, and integration were executed. The `UV_1_280_mAU` signal underwent Neighboring-Value Height Thresholding using a dynamic threshold relative to the 0–1921 mAU range. Asymmetric Least Squares (ALS) baseline correction was applied with `smoothness=1e7` and `lambda=2e-5` to handle baseline drift via second derivative constrained weighted regression. Multiple Gaussian components were fitted to the corrected peaks, and areas were integrated strictly within the valid fraction bounds. This process was repeated for the `UV_2_260_mAU` signal using a dynamic threshold relative to its 0–2479 mAU range. Third, stage-level aggregation and impurity fingerprinting were performed. Integrated peak areas were aggregated to the `chromatography_stage` level, and the 280/260 nm ratio was calculated for each stage. A one-way ANOVA test was conducted on the integrated areas across `run_no` within each stage to identify batch effects, using a significance threshold of p < 0.05. Due to the missing `Sample_Code`, profiling was limited to stage-level consistency checks.

#Results and Discussion 

The visualizations and statistical outputs reveal a clear distinction in compound distribution across the manufacturing process. The scatter plot of integrated peak areas (UV 280 nm vs. UV 260 nm) displays a distinct clustering pattern where specific stages group together based on their impurity profiles. The left subplot confirms the successful application of Neighboring-Value Height Thresholding and ALS baseline correction, effectively isolating distinct peaks from the noisy background and handling baseline drift in the representative Stage 1 chromatogram. The right subplot highlights a systematic shift in impurity composition as the process progresses. Stages E1 through E9 exhibit a consistent 280/260 ratio ranging from 2.24 to 4.26, indicating a protein-rich environment with minimal nucleic acid interference. In contrast, the Final Formulation (FT) stage shows a ratio of 0.89, suggesting a significant shift in compound composition or dilution effects relative to the intermediate stages. The inclusion of a linear trendline visualizes this overall ratio profile, confirming that the integration methods accurately capture the changing nature of the eluate. Statistical validation via ANOVA indicates no statistically significant variance across runs within any stage (p >= 0.05), implying that the observed differences are driven by the chromatographic process steps rather than random run-to-run noise. The high total area values for the FT stage compared to individual stages reflect the accumulation of material across the entire process, while the stable ratios across intermediate stages validate the robustness of the peak integration and baseline correction methods. Despite the 76% missingness of sample identifiers, this stage-level fingerprinting provides a critical tool for identifying batch effects and monitoring impurity profiles.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully developed and validated a robust data analysis pipeline for characterizing biopharmaceutical chromatographic data in the absence of concentration data. By leveraging UV absorbance ratios and advanced signal processing techniques, the analysis effectively quantified compound distributions and identified distinct impurity profiles across process stages. The results confirm that the proposed methodology, including ALS baseline correction and multi-Gaussian fitting, is capable of extracting meaningful information from high dynamic range signals. The clear separation between intermediate stages and the final formulation stage, evidenced by the 280/260 nm ratio, provides valuable insights into the composition changes during manufacturing. While the limitation of missing sample identifiers restricts the analysis to batch-level consistency, the stage-level fingerprints offer a reliable metric for monitoring process consistency and detecting potential batch effects. Future work should focus on strategies to recover sample-specific identifiers to enable more granular profiling, but the current results demonstrate the efficacy of using UV spectral ratios as a surrogate for concentration-based normalization in data-sparse environments.
