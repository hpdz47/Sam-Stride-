#Abstract 

This report details the quantitative processing of High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) time-series data to establish a validated correlation between integrated peak areas and relative compound abundances. The primary objective was to address significant data quality constraints, including >66% missingness in the concentration variable (`Conc_B_%`) and noisy UV signal data containing negative values. A rigorous multi-step methodology was employed, beginning with Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) smoothing to correct baselines for both 280 nm and 260 nm channels. Dynamic noise filtering was applied to remove negative values below the local baseline minus three standard deviations, with a global baseline fallback utilized for significant data gaps. Signal-to-Noise Ratios (SNR) were calculated to select the optimal UV channel, prioritizing `UV_1_280_mAU` or `UV_2_260_mAU` based on a 1.5x SNR threshold. Missing concentration values were imputed using K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN, k=5), strictly restricted to runs with sufficient coverage of system flow and conductivity to ensure validity. Finally, peak integration was performed using single-fraction volumes, and the resulting peak areas were correlated with imputed concentrations. Sensitivity analyses confirmed that the correlation remains robust, with shifts in correlation coefficients not exceeding the predefined ±5% threshold when critical parameters like pH and pressure were excluded. The analysis successfully validated the relationship between chromatographic peak area and compound abundance, providing a reliable dataset for downstream quantitative modeling.

#Introduction 

Accurate quantification of chemical compounds in complex mixtures is fundamental to process development and quality control in pharmaceutical manufacturing. High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) remains the gold standard for separating and quantifying these compounds, relying on the absorbance of UV signals to determine peak areas, which are subsequently converted to concentration estimates. However, real-world HPLC data often presents substantial challenges that can compromise the accuracy of these quantifications. Common issues include baseline drift, noise interference, missing data points, and the presence of anomalous negative values in absorbance signals. Furthermore, the concentration data associated with these chromatograms may suffer from high missingness rates, necessitating robust imputation strategies that do not introduce bias. This study focuses on the rigorous quantitative processing of HPLC time-series signals to extract peak characteristics and estimate relative compound abundances. The specific focus area involves applying advanced baseline correction techniques, dynamic noise filtering, and statistical validation to ensure that the final correlation between integrated peak areas and concentration estimates is both statistically significant and reproducible. By adhering to strict data quality standards, including USP/IUPAC resolution criteria and sensitivity analysis, this work aims to establish a validated analytical pipeline capable of handling noisy and incomplete datasets.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow was structured into three primary phases: signal preprocessing, data imputation, and quantitative validation. 

First, signal preprocessing involved the application of Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) smoothing to the `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` channels. The smoothing parameters were set to a lambda value of 1e7 and an asymmetry parameter of 2e-4 to effectively separate the baseline from the peak signal. To handle regions of missing data, 'significant gaps' were defined as areas where the smoothed baseline was missing more than 10% of the points; these were filled with the global mean of the respective channel. Negative values were dynamically filtered using a threshold of (Local Baseline - 3*StdDev) or a fixed -5 mAU cutoff, whichever was more conservative. The optimal signal source was determined by calculating the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) as Peak Height divided by RMS Noise for both channels. The channel with the higher SNR was selected, with a specific rule to prioritize `UV_1_280_CUT_TEMP_100_BASEM_mAU` only if its SNR was at least 1.5 times higher than the raw absorbance channel.

Second, data imputation was performed on the `Conc_B_%` variable, which exhibited over 66% missingness. A K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) algorithm with k=5 was utilized. To ensure the validity of the imputed values, KNN neighbors were restricted to runs with sufficient coverage of `system_flow_ml_min` and `cond_mS_cm`. The imputation process was stratified by `chromatography_stage`. If the stage was missing, the `Run_Log_Logbook` was used as a fallback; if that was also missing or empty, the `run` identifier was used. Runs with insufficient neighbor coverage were flagged for exclusion.

Third, quantitative validation involved peak integration using single-fraction volumes (`fraction_volume`) derived from the selected UV channel. Peaks were filtered based on USP/IUPAC standards: Resolution (Rs) >= 1.5, Asymmetry between 0.8 and 1.8, and Plate Count (N) > 2000. The correlation between integrated peak areas and imputed `Conc_B_%` was calculated. A sensitivity analysis was conducted by recalculating the correlation while excluding critical process parameters (`pH_pH` and `deltaC_pressure_MPa`). The analysis confirmed that the correlation shift did not exceed the ±5% threshold, validating the robustness of the relationship between peak area and concentration.

#Results and Discussion 

The application of the proposed methodology yielded a robust dataset suitable for quantitative analysis. As illustrated in Figure 1, the AsLS smoothing effectively corrected the baseline drift, allowing for precise peak integration using single-fraction volumes. The dynamic noise filtering successfully removed anomalous negative values without distorting the underlying peak shapes, ensuring that the calculated peak heights and areas were representative of the true analyte concentration. The SNR comparison confirmed that the selected UV channel provided the highest signal fidelity, minimizing noise-induced errors in the integration process.

Regarding data imputation, the KNN approach successfully recovered the missing concentration values. The distribution of KNN distances indicated that the selected neighbors were sufficiently similar in terms of system flow and conductivity, suggesting that the imputed values are reliable. The stratification by chromatography stage ensured that batch-specific variations were accounted for, reducing the risk of introducing systematic bias. The correlation matrix between integrated peak areas and imputed `Conc_B_%` showed a strong positive relationship, supporting the validity of the quantitative model.

The sensitivity analysis provided critical insights into the stability of the correlation. When critical parameters such as pH and pressure were excluded from the analysis, the correlation coefficient remained within the ±5% threshold. This result is significant as it demonstrates that the observed relationship between peak area and concentration is not an artifact of unmeasured process variables but is instead a fundamental property of the separation system. The filtering of peaks based on USP/IUPAC standards further ensured that only high-quality data contributing to the final correlation were included, enhancing the overall reliability of the results. In summary, the data analysis successfully navigated the complexities of noisy and incomplete HPLC data, establishing a validated correlation between chromatographic peak area and compound abundance.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully demonstrated a rigorous and validated workflow for the quantitative analysis of HPLC data, overcoming significant challenges related to signal noise and missing concentration values. By employing AsLS smoothing, dynamic noise filtering, and KNN imputation with strict coverage constraints, the analysis produced a high-quality dataset where the correlation between integrated peak areas and compound abundance is robust. The sensitivity analysis confirmed that the quantitative relationship holds true even when critical process parameters are excluded, validating the independence of the correlation from specific operational conditions. These findings provide a reliable foundation for future quantitative modeling and quality control applications, ensuring that the derived concentration estimates are accurate, reproducible, and compliant with established analytical standards.
