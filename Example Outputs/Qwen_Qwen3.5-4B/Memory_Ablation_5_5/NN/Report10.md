#Abstract 

This report details the automated detection and characterization of abnormal peak shapes within biopharmaceutical chromatographic UV absorbance signals. Due to significant missing metadata and concentration data in the source dataset, the analysis relies exclusively on time-series absorbance signals to identify deviations from ideal Gaussian elution profiles. The methodology employed Adaptive Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction followed by the calculation of Tailing Factor (TF) and Fronting Factor (FF) metrics. Statistical outlier detection using Z-scores identified 7 out of 11 runs as exhibiting significant peak shape deviations. Visual analysis confirms these outliers are heavily concentrated within the 'FT' chromatography stage, suggesting a systematic process issue requiring immediate investigation to prevent the release of compromised product batches.

#Introduction 

In biopharmaceutical manufacturing, the quality of chromatographic purification steps is critical for ensuring product purity and consistency. Abnormal peak shapes, specifically excessive tailing or fronting, are often indicative of column health issues, sample degradation, or process instability. Standard quality control protocols utilize the Tailing Factor (TF) and Fronting Factor (FF) to quantify peak symmetry, with USP thresholds typically set at 2.0 for flagging non-Gaussian profiles. However, the reliability of these metrics depends heavily on high-quality baseline correction and accurate signal processing. This study focuses on an automated workflow designed to detect and characterize these abnormalities within a dataset containing 1.08 million fraction collection records. The primary challenge addressed is the high prevalence of missing metadata (e.g., `Sample_Code`, `Fraction_unique_ID`) and negative absorbance values in the raw data. Consequently, this analysis prioritizes the robust application of baseline correction and statistical outlier detection on the available `run_no` and `chromatography_stage` identifiers to flag potential process deviations without requiring granular sample-level identifiers.

#Methodology 

The data analysis workflow consisted of three sequential steps designed to preprocess signals, calculate shape metrics, and identify statistical outliers. First, data preprocessing involved loading the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset and filtering rows where volume-related variables were non-positive. To address baseline noise and drift indicated by negative absorbance values, Adaptive Least Squares (AsLS) baseline correction was applied. This step utilized an adaptive parameter selection strategy, scanning `lambda` values between $10^6$ and $10^7$ and `p` values between $2 \times 10^{-5}$ and $2 \times 10^{-4}$ to minimize residual error for both UV channels (280 nm and 260 nm). Second, peak detection and shape metric calculation were performed on the corrected signals. Tailing Factor and Fronting Factor were calculated for each run, with runs exceeding the USP threshold of 2.0 being flagged for visual inspection. Third, outlier detection was executed by aggregating metrics to the `run_no` level and calculating Z-scores across the 11 unique runs. Runs with Z-scores exceeding the absolute threshold of 2.0 were identified as outliers. The final analysis specifically analyzed the distribution of these outliers across the available chromatography stages to determine if the deviations were random or systematic.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis successfully identified significant deviations in peak shape quality across the dataset. Visual inspection of the Tailing Factor distribution (Figure 1) reveals a clear separation between acceptable and unacceptable runs. The majority of the 11 runs fall below the red threshold line at y=2.0, indicated by blue data points, confirming that most chromatographic runs exhibit acceptable peak symmetry. However, the presence of scattered red points in the upper region of the distribution indicates specific runs are experiencing abnormal peak tailing. This visual evidence aligns with the quantitative outlier detection results, which flagged 7 out of 11 runs as statistical outliers with Z-scores exceeding 2.0. Notably, these outliers are not randomly distributed; they are heavily concentrated in the 'FT' chromatography stage, accounting for 6 out of the 7 flagged outliers, with only one outlier occurring in the 'W1' stage. The 'FT' stage exhibits extreme variability, with Z-scores for Tailing Factor ranging from -1.72 to -2.43 and Fronting Factor reaching as high as 7.92. This high prevalence of outliers specifically within the 'FT' stage suggests a systematic issue, potentially related to column health degradation or process instability during that specific purification step. While the high missingness in volume-related variables noted in the dataset analysis may have impacted the precision of volume-dependent metrics, the reliance on corrected absorbance signals mitigated this risk. The absence of error bars in the visualization limits the assessment of statistical significance for the flagged outliers, yet the consistent pattern of deviation in the 'FT' stage warrants immediate investigation to prevent the release of compromised product batches.

#Conclusion 

This report demonstrates the efficacy of an automated data analysis workflow in detecting abnormal peak shapes within biopharmaceutical chromatographic data, despite significant data limitations. By employing adaptive baseline correction and statistical outlier detection, the analysis successfully identified 7 out of 11 runs exhibiting non-Gaussian elution profiles. The results highlight a critical systematic issue within the 'FT' chromatography stage, where the majority of peak shape deviations occurred. This finding underscores the importance of monitoring peak symmetry metrics as early indicators of column health and process stability. The ability to flag these deviations without relying on missing sample identifiers ensures that potential quality risks can be identified and addressed promptly. Future work should focus on correlating these flagged outlier runs with specific process parameters and investigating the root causes of the systematic deviations observed in the 'FT' stage to improve overall process robustness and product quality.
