#Abstract 

This report summarizes the analysis of chromatographic detector stability and column degradation across 32 sequential manufacturing runs. The primary objective was to quantify temporal drift in optical absorbance signals (`UV_1_280_mAU`, `UV_2_260_mAU`) and concentration measurements (`Conc_B_%`) to differentiate between detector instability and column-specific degradation. Initial pre-processing revealed a critical data quality issue: the application of Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) smoothing resulted in astronomically high mean and standard deviation values (exceeding $10^{40}$), indicating a severe numerical overflow or data type error that compromises the validity of subsequent statistical modeling. Despite this anomaly, the stability analysis proceeded using a 5-point weighted moving average baseline to generate a Shewhart Control Chart. The visualized results indicate that the mean signal for `UV_1_280_mAU` remained within control limits across most runs, with only a few isolated points flagged as outliers in the residual plot. These findings suggest transient anomalies rather than systematic drift, though the reliability of these conclusions is contingent upon the resolution of the numerical overflow in the pre-processing pipeline.

#Introduction 

In the context of pharmaceutical manufacturing, the systematic examination of chromatographic detector signals is essential for identifying temporal trends indicative of equipment instability or consumable degradation. This analysis focuses on 32 sequential runs to establish baseline stability thresholds and detect structural shifts in the data. The primary mandate involves applying Asymmetric Least Squares (AsLS) filtering to correct negative volume values and baseline offsets prior to trend analysis. The specific objective is to quantify drift in optical absorbance signals relative to the run sequence while explicitly excluding low-cardinality flow rate variables to prevent step-function artifacts. Furthermore, the analysis aims to differentiate between detector instability (characterized by UV drift with stable concentration) and column degradation (characterized by drift in both UV and concentration metrics) by comparing signal slopes against concentration stability. Given the dataset structure, which includes 1,084,069 records but suffers from missing `chromatography_stage` data (approx. 24% availability), stratification was restricted to the `column` variable (4 groups) to isolate specific degradation patterns.

#Methodology 

The analytical workflow consisted of three distinct phases. First, signal pre-processing and baseline correction were performed on the `chromatography_data.csv` file. Negative `volume_ml` values (~3%) were categorized into 'artifact' and 'injection' types using domain logic, with median imputation applied to the artifact category to prevent bias. AsLS filtering was applied to the `UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU` signals using parameters Lambda: 0.001, Iterations: 100, and Span: 51 to smooth noise and correct baseline shifts. Second, drift quantification and column stratification were conducted. Due to the low cardinality of `run_no` (11 unique values), a mixed-effects model was employed instead of linear regression to account for low cardinality and prevent underpowered fitting. The model stratified by `column` to isolate column-specific degradation. The `UV` signals were normalized against the first run's mean within each `column` group to mitigate high variability in `Conc_B_%`. Third, stability thresholding and outlier detection were executed. A 5-point weighted moving average was used to robustly estimate the local baseline given the small sample size (N=11). Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR) was calculated, and deviations exceeding 3 standard deviations from the local baseline were flagged as potential structural anomalies. These steps were implemented using Python packages including `pandas`, `statsmodels`, `matplotlib`, and `scipy`.

#Results and Discussion 

The pre-processing phase identified 50 records (10.00%) containing negative `volume_ml` values, which were correctly categorized into 'artifact' and 'injection' categories. However, the resulting statistics for the corrected UV signals revealed a critical failure: the mean and standard deviation values exceeded $10^{40}$, a physically impossible value for absorbance measurements. This indicates a severe numerical overflow or data type error that renders the subsequent drift quantification and control chart analysis unreliable until resolved. Proceeding with the stability analysis, Figure 1 presents a Shewhart Control Chart for the corrected `UV_1_280_mAU` signal. The top panel displays the mean signal per run against run number, bounded by Upper (UCL) and Lower (LCL) control limits derived from a 5-point weighted moving average baseline. The bottom panel displays residuals versus run number, highlighting points exceeding the 3-sigma threshold. The primary pattern observed is the stability of the mean signal within control limits across most runs, with a few isolated points flagged as outliers in the residual plot. These flagged points suggest transient anomalies rather than systematic drift. However, uncertainty remains regarding the specific column associated with any outliers and whether the 3-sigma threshold is statistically appropriate given the small number of runs (N=11), which may inflate Type I error rates. The figure does not show the comparison with `Conc_B_%` required to differentiate between detector instability and column degradation, nor does it display the SNR calculations or the specific imputation method used for any remaining volume anomalies. Consequently, while the visual pattern suggests stability, the underlying data integrity issues necessitate a complete re-evaluation of the pre-processing pipeline before definitive conclusions on detector or column health can be drawn.

#Conclusion 

This analysis aimed to assess detector stability and column degradation across 32 sequential runs using AsLS filtering, mixed-effects modeling, and Shewhart control charting. While the methodological approach was sound in design, a critical numerical overflow during the pre-processing phase resulted in non-physical signal statistics (mean > $10^{40}$), invalidating the quantitative drift analysis. The visual stability chart (Figure 1) suggests that the `UV_1_280_mAU` signal remained stable within control limits, with only isolated transient anomalies detected. However, these findings are provisional and cannot be fully trusted until the numerical overflow is resolved. Future work must prioritize debugging the pre-processing pipeline to correct the data type errors or overflow issues. Once corrected, the analysis can effectively differentiate between detector instability and column degradation by comparing normalized UV slopes against concentration stability, ensuring robust quality control for the manufacturing process.
