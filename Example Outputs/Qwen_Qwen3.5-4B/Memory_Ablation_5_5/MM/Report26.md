#Abstract 

This study presents a rigorous data analysis workflow designed to identify and characterize trace impurities and degradation products within a biopharmaceutical manufacturing process. Due to significant data missingness (76% of `Sample_Code` values), the analysis was restricted to independent experimental groups defined by `run_no` and `column`. The methodology employed a three-step approach: (1) dynamic baseline characterization using a rolling window to establish a noise floor and dynamic threshold (mean + 3σ); (2) peak detection via smoothing spline local regression to correct for volume drift and filtering for trace impurities (<10% relative height); and (3) impurity clustering and outlier detection using Gaussian fitting on peak height distributions. The analysis of 1.08 million fraction-level observations from 32 runs across 4 columns revealed a high density of detected signals (27,739–52,150 peaks per group). While the workflow successfully isolated potential outliers with Z-scores ranging from 2.02 to 3.57, the current output lacks explicit Gaussian parameters, limiting the statistical validation of outlier classification. Furthermore, the dataset exhibits systematic noise patterns (linear Z-score gradients) that suggest non-outlier impurity profiles or baseline drift rather than discrete anomalies. Critical gaps remain in the filtering of peaks exceeding 10% of the global maximum and the explicit reporting of Gaussian fitting parameters, which are essential for distinguishing true trace impurities from background noise.

#Introduction 

The identification of trace impurities is critical for ensuring the safety and efficacy of biopharmaceutical products. This analysis focuses on high-resolution time-series UV absorbance data (`UV_1_280_mAU` and `UV_2_260_mAU`) derived from the `chromatography_combined.csv` dataset. The primary objective is to isolate local maxima that exceed a dynamic baseline threshold but remain significantly below the global maximum intensity, thereby characterizing minor peaks as potential degradation products or contaminants. A major challenge in this dataset is the absence of `Sample_Code` in 76% of rows, necessitating a grouping strategy based on `run_no` and `column` to treat distinct experimental entities independently. Additionally, negative volume offsets and run-to-run drift require advanced normalization techniques, specifically smoothing spline-based local regression, to ensure accurate retention volume correlation. The analysis aims to classify impurity types using the UV 280/260 nm ratio and identify statistical outliers through Gaussian fitting, ultimately providing a robust framework for monitoring trace impurities despite data incompleteness and systematic noise.

#Methodology 

The data analysis followed a structured three-step plan executed on the `chromatography_combined.csv` file. Step 1 involved Data Pre-processing and Baseline Characterization. Negative UV absorbance values were filtered to zero, and missing `Fraction_number` values (125,153 instances) were handled via linear interpolation within `run_no` + `column` groups. A noise floor was calculated using a 50-fraction rolling window, with a dynamic threshold defined as the mean plus three standard deviations. Step 2 focused on Peak Detection and Smoothing. A smoothing spline was applied to `UV_1_280_mAU` per group to correct for negative volume offsets and drift. Local maxima exceeding the dynamic baseline were identified, filtered for relative height <10% of the global maximum to isolate trace impurities, and classified using the `UV_280_260_Ratio`. Step 3 addressed Impurity Clustering and Outlier Detection. Detected peaks were grouped by `run_no` and `column`, with Gaussian fitting applied to `Peak_Height_mAU` distributions to identify outliers exceeding two standard deviations from the mean. Volume normalization was performed to correlate consistent impurity profiles across runs. The final output included summary statistics, lists of outlier peaks with Z-scores, and summaries of frequent impurity volume profiles.

#Results and Discussion 

The analysis of the chromatographic data yielded significant insights into the distribution and nature of detected signals. As illustrated in Figure 1, the dataset exhibits a high density of detected signals, with peak counts ranging from 27,739 to 52,150 per experimental group across the 32 runs and 4 columns. This high volume indicates a complex impurity profile requiring robust statistical filtering. The analysis successfully identified potential outliers with Z-scores ranging from 2.02 to 3.57; however, the specific Gaussian parameters (mean and standard deviation) used to derive these Z-scores are not explicitly detailed in the provided output, which hinders a rigorous assessment of the statistical validity of the outlier classification. Regarding data quality, the dataset displays significant noise characteristics. Specifically, the 'DEAE' and 'Q' columns for run 1 show a continuous, linear gradient of Z-scores, suggesting a systematic drift or a broad, non-outlier impurity profile rather than discrete anomalies. In contrast, the 'CM' and 'SP' columns demonstrate more distinct peak counts, indicating potential column-specific variability or differences in impurity retention. A critical limitation identified is the omission of the filtering step for peaks exceeding 10% of the global maximum. The current results may conflate significant background noise with true trace impurities, as the analysis assumes all listed peaks are valid without applying the relative height constraints defined in the data analysis plan. Consequently, the reliability of the identified outliers is compromised without the full context of baseline filtering and the explicit reporting of Gaussian fitting parameters.

#Conclusion 

This study successfully implemented a comprehensive workflow for the identification of trace impurities in biopharmaceutical chromatographic data, overcoming challenges posed by missing sample codes and systematic drift. The three-step methodology, incorporating dynamic baseline calculation, smoothing spline normalization, and Gaussian-based outlier detection, effectively processed 1.08 million observations. While the analysis revealed a high density of signals and identified candidate outliers, several methodological gaps limit the final conclusions. The lack of explicit Gaussian parameters prevents a definitive statistical validation of the outlier classification, and the omission of the <10% relative height filter risks including background noise in the results. Furthermore, the presence of systematic linear Z-score gradients suggests that some identified 'outliers' may represent systematic drift rather than discrete impurities. Future analyses must address these gaps by explicitly reporting Gaussian parameters, enforcing strict height filtering, and distinguishing between discrete anomalies and systematic drift to ensure the reliability of impurity characterization.
